The Context

CERN in the 1980s, the internet that already existed, the hypertext that didn't quite, and the explosion that followed

CERN in the 1980s

The European Organisation for Nuclear Research — always known by its French acronym, CERN — is one of the largest and most complex scientific institutions in human history. Founded in 1954, situated on the border between France and Switzerland near Geneva, it operates particle accelerators for experiments at the frontier of fundamental physics. By the 1980s, it employed several thousand staff and hosted tens of thousands of visiting researchers from more than a hundred countries.

The organisational challenge was extreme. Scientists came from different countries, different institutions, different disciplines. They worked on different experiments that connected to other experiments in complex, shifting ways. People arrived, did their work, and left — taking with them everything they knew about which systems connected to which, who the right person to contact was, what documentation existed and where. Institutional memory was fragile. The network of human knowledge at CERN was unmapped.

Berners-Lee arrived at CERN at exactly the right moment: a place with an acute information problem and the technical resources to think about solving it. CERN was not just any institution. Its physicists were among the most analytically rigorous people in the world, accustomed to working with large, complex systems. If a new information architecture was going to be prototyped anywhere, CERN was the place.

The Problem

Institutional Memory

People came and went. Knowledge of who did what, which project connected to which, what documentation existed — constantly being lost. No system to track the web of connections.

The Context

High-Energy Physics

The experiments were massive. The Large Hadron Collider (still decades away) would require thousands of collaborators. CERN needed an information system that matched the complexity of its science.

The Resource

Technical Rigour

CERN had the hardware, the networking expertise, and the intellectual culture to adopt a new technical system if it was good enough. It was the ideal proving ground.

The Internet: Already There

When Berners-Lee proposed the web in 1989, the internet already existed. It had existed, in various forms, for two decades. The distinction between the internet and the web is one of the most commonly confused points in the history of technology, and it matters: they are completely different things.

The internet is a physical and logical network — a system for routing packets of data between computers anywhere in the world. It began as ARPANET in 1969, a project of the US Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency. By the early 1980s, it had acquired the TCP/IP protocols that still govern it today. By 1989, it connected universities, research institutions, and government agencies across the world. Email worked. File transfer worked. Remote login worked.

What the internet lacked, in 1989, was a common way to present and link formatted documents across it. You could send a file — but the recipient needed to know what program to open it with. You could connect to a remote computer — but you had to know exactly which computer and what it contained. There was no universal, human-readable layer on top of the network. That is what Berners-Lee added.

Ted Nelson and Hypertext

In 1965, an American philosopher and sociologist named Theodor Holm Nelson — known universally as Ted Nelson — proposed a concept he called hypertext. Hypertext was text that contained links to other text. Documents that referenced other documents, non-linearly, in a web of interconnections rather than a linear sequence. The idea was powerful, strange, and ahead of its time.

Nelson spent the next several decades working on a system to implement his vision. He called it Xanadu — named after the Coleridge poem. Xanadu was to be a global hypertext system: a single, unified document space in which any text could link to any other text, version control was built in, and authors were compensated for content reuse. It was an extraordinarily ambitious design, and it was never shipped. The first usable public release came decades later, long after the web had made it irrelevant.

Berners-Lee was aware of Nelson's work. He cited it explicitly in his proposal. The influence was real. But where Nelson aimed for a perfect, complete, unified system — with proper versioning, transclusion, micropayment — Berners-Lee built something deliberately simpler. Links in HTML were not bidirectional. There was no versioning. There was no payment system. The "broken link" problem Nelson had designed around was simply accepted as a cost of simplicity.

This pragmatic compromise is why the web exists and Xanadu does not. Berners-Lee later said: "I took Ted Nelson's idea and stripped out everything that made it hard to ship." The web is hypertext, imperfectly implemented, that actually ran.

"Ted Nelson coined the word hypertext. He worked for decades on Xanadu — a perfect hypertext system. It never shipped. I built a simpler version that ran on what was already there. Imperfect but real." — Paraphrase of Berners-Lee on Nelson

The People Around Him

No invention happens in a vacuum. The web required a specific ecosystem of people — collaborators, enablers, and subsequent builders who turned a prototype at CERN into a global phenomenon.

Robert Cailliau

Co-Proposer — CERN

Belgian engineer at CERN who co-wrote the November 1990 "WorldWideWeb: Proposal for a HyperText Project" with Berners-Lee. Cailliau was the internal advocate who helped make the proposal concrete and persuasive to CERN management. Without his partnership, the project might have remained a solo curiosity.

Mike Sendall

Supervisor — CERN

Berners-Lee's boss at CERN who approved the original 1989 proposal with the famous note "Vague but exciting." Sendall gave the project institutional cover at a critical moment. Without his approval, the work could not have proceeded. He died in 1999, before the scale of what he had enabled was fully apparent.

Ted Nelson

Concept Origin — Hypertext

Coined the word "hypertext" in 1965. Proposed linked documents as an organising principle for knowledge. Never shipped a working system. His Xanadu project remains unfinished. But the concept he articulated is what Berners-Lee implemented — imperfectly, simply, and in a form that actually worked.

Marc Andreessen

Mosaic — 1993

Computer science student at the University of Illinois who led the development of Mosaic, the first graphical web browser, in 1993. Mosaic could display images inline with text — a revolution in usability. It transformed the web from a text-based research tool into a medium that anyone could use. Andreessen went on to co-found Netscape.

Nicola Pellow

Line Mode Browser — CERN

A maths student on placement at CERN who wrote the "Line Mode Browser" — the first browser that could run on any computer, not just the NeXT. This was crucial: Berners-Lee's original browser only ran on one platform. Pellow's browser made the web accessible on any terminal and was therefore the web's first truly cross-platform client.

Vint Cerf & Bob Kahn

TCP/IP — the Internet Itself

Co-designed TCP/IP, the fundamental protocol suite of the internet, in the 1970s. The web sits on top of TCP/IP. Without the internet infrastructure Cerf and Kahn created, Berners-Lee would have had no network to build on. Often called the "fathers of the internet" — not the web, but the layer beneath it.

WorldWideWeb: The First Browser

Berners-Lee's first browser was called WorldWideWeb — later renamed Nexus to avoid confusion with the web itself. It ran on the NeXTSTEP operating system, on NeXT computers that CERN had recently acquired. It was both a browser and an editor: it could not only read web pages but write and modify them. This reflected Berners-Lee's vision of the web as a read-write medium, not a read-only one.

The design choice to make the first browser an editor was significant and ahead of its time. Most of the subsequent browsers that made the web popular — Mosaic, Netscape, Internet Explorer — were read-only. The read-write vision did not fully arrive until Wikipedia, blogs, and eventually Web 2.0. In a sense, the web's first browser was more ambitious than anything that followed it for a decade.

The limitation of WorldWideWeb was critical: it only ran on NeXT hardware. CERN had NeXT computers, but most of the world did not. This was addressed in 1991 when Nicola Pellow, a maths student on placement at CERN, wrote a "Line Mode Browser" that could run on any computer with a terminal connection. The Line Mode Browser was text-only, without the graphical elegance of WorldWideWeb, but it worked everywhere. It was the web's first truly universal client.

1991: Released to the World

On 6 August 1991, Tim Berners-Lee posted a short message to several internet newsgroups — alt.hypertext, comp.sys.next.announce, comp.text.hypertext — announcing the WorldWideWeb project and providing access to the software. The post was matter-of-fact and brief. It described what the system did and invited people to try it.

The response was modest at first. The internet of 1991 was not the internet of today: it was a specialist tool used primarily by academics and researchers. But the people who saw the post and downloaded the software were precisely the people who could do something with it — engineers, scientists, academics who understood what they were looking at and could set up servers of their own.

The first websites were not commercial products or media sites. They were what you would expect from researchers: documentation, references, project pages, university directories. The web's first years were quiet and academic. The explosion came later, with Mosaic.

The inflection point was Mosaic in 1993. Before Mosaic, the web was a text-based tool for researchers. After Mosaic, it was a graphical medium accessible to anyone who could use a mouse. Mosaic was free, cross-platform, and easy. It was built at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications (NCSA) at the University of Illinois by a team led by Marc Andreessen, then a 22-year-old undergraduate.

Andreessen went on to co-found Netscape in 1994, bringing in experienced management and Silicon Valley money. Netscape Navigator became the dominant browser for several years, introducing millions of people to the web. The commercial internet era had begun. It had been enabled, entirely, by a decision made by one person at CERN in 1989 to make his work free.

"Mosaic was the first browser that could display images inline with text. Before that, the web was text. After that, it was a medium. That was when everything changed." — Summary of the Mosaic moment, 1993

1 Website in 1991. 1.9 Billion Today.

The most rapid adoption of any technology in history. Driven entirely by protocols that are free to use, free to implement, and free to build on. The web is not a product. It is a standard. And standards that are free spread faster than anything with a toll-booth.
