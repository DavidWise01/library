Volume Zero · The Man

Tim Berners-Lee

The physicist who gave the world the web — and asked for nothing in return

b. London, 8 June 1955

Origins: Born to Computers

Timothy John Berners-Lee was born in London on 8 June 1955, the oldest of four children. His parents — Conway Berners-Lee and Mary Lee Woods — were both mathematicians who had worked on the Manchester Mark 1, one of the earliest stored-program computers. The dinner table conversation in the Berners-Lee household was unlike most: it ran to mathematics, to computation, to what machines might one day be made to do.

That early immersion mattered. Berners-Lee has said he grew up surrounded by the idea that computers were not just calculating machines but something more open-ended — tools for representing and connecting ideas. The notion that information could be structured and linked was, for him, almost a childhood intuition before it was a professional one.

CERN and Enquire

In 1980, Berners-Lee took a software engineering contract at CERN, the European Organisation for Nuclear Research, in Geneva. CERN was — and remains — one of the world's great scientific institutions: a place where thousands of physicists, engineers, and computer scientists from dozens of countries collaborate on experiments at the frontier of human knowledge.

The organisational challenge this created was extraordinary. People came and went. Projects were related to other projects in complex ways. There was no good way to keep track of who was doing what, who knew whom, which system connected to which other system. Every new person who arrived had to rediscover the connections from scratch.

Berners-Lee's solution was personal and practical: he wrote a program for himself, which he called Enquire — short for "Enquire Within Upon Everything," the title of a Victorian domestic encyclopedia he had found as a child. Enquire was a simple hypertext system: it stored information as nodes and links. A node might be a person, a project, a concept, or a machine. A link connected any node to any other node.

This was not yet the web. Enquire ran only on Berners-Lee's own machine, could not link outside its own database, and was never distributed. When he left CERN at the end of 1980, the program was lost. But the idea — of a system where anything could link to anything, where the network was the organising principle — stayed with him. It was the seed of what was coming.

Return to CERN: The Proposal

After leaving CERN in 1980, Berners-Lee spent several years in the private sector, working at Image Computer Systems in Dorset. He returned to CERN in 1984 as a Fellow, and the problem he had tried to solve with Enquire was still there — if anything, it had grown larger. CERN now had thousands of researchers. The internet existed and was growing. The missing piece was a way to link documents across it.

In March 1989, Berners-Lee wrote a document entitled "Information Management: A Proposal" and submitted it to his supervisor, Mike Sendall. The document proposed a distributed hypertext system that would allow documents anywhere on the internet to link to any other document anywhere else on the internet. It described, in outline, what would become the World Wide Web.

Sendall's response — written on the cover of the proposal — has become one of the most famous marginal notes in the history of technology. He wrote: "Vague but exciting." He approved the work. Berners-Lee was given permission to continue.

Mike Sendall's Cover Note — March 1989

"Vague but exciting."

Written on the cover of Berners-Lee's proposal document. The four words that greenlit the World Wide Web.

A revised and expanded version of the proposal, written with Belgian engineer Robert Cailliau, was circulated in November 1990. It was called "WorldWideWeb: Proposal for a HyperText Project." This version was more concrete, more technical, and more persuasive. It described HTML (to structure documents), HTTP (to transfer them), and URLs (to address them). All three still exist. All three still work.

December 1990: The First Web

Between the approval of his proposal in 1989 and the end of 1990, Berners-Lee built the entire stack. He wrote the first web server — the program that receives requests and returns documents. He wrote the first web browser, which he called WorldWideWeb (later renamed Nexus to avoid confusion with the web itself). He published the first web page at the address http://info.cern.ch/.

The first web page was not a commercial product, not a media site, not a social network. It was documentation. It explained what the World Wide Web was, what it was for, and how to use it. It was exactly what you would expect from a physicist and engineer: a clear explanation of a system, written for other people who might want to use it.

The first browser was also an editor. Berners-Lee's original design was that the web would be a read-write medium: anyone could not just read pages, but write them. This vision — of a web where everyone contributes, not just consumes — is one that took decades to partially arrive (with wikis, blogs, social media) and one that Berners-Lee still advocates for in its deeper form today.

The Gift: No Patent, No Royalty

This is the fact that most people, hearing it for the first time, find almost impossible to believe: Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web and gave it away for free. He made no patent claim. He charged no royalty. He did not become a billionaire. He did not start a company to commercialise it.

CERN released the underlying web technology with a declaration making it royalty-free in perpetuity, in April 1993. This was a deliberate decision — one that Berners-Lee lobbied for. His reasoning was clear: a web that anyone could use for free would grow exponentially faster than one encumbered by licences. He estimated that making the web open rather than proprietary meant it grew roughly 1,000 times faster than it would have otherwise. That estimate, if anything, seems conservative.

The web's openness is not accidental. It is the result of a specific ethical choice made by a specific person at a specific moment. Had Berners-Lee patented his inventions and licensed them — as he could have, as many people in his position would have — the web as we know it could not exist. Every website would owe a fee. Every browser would require a licence. The open internet would have been stillborn.

"Had the technology been proprietary, and in my total control, it would probably not have taken off. You can't propose that something be a universal space and at the same time keep control of it." — Tim Berners-Lee

He is not poor. He has a comfortable academic salary from MIT, where he has held a position for many years, and honours beyond counting. But he has no equity in Google, Facebook, Amazon, or any of the trillion-dollar companies whose entire existence depends on what he invented. He made a choice. The rest of us live in the world that choice created.

What Kind of Person Does This?

Berners-Lee is, by all accounts, a quiet person. Not a showman. Not a salesman. He has given countless interviews and lectures over four decades, but he is not someone who speaks in slogans or brands himself. His public persona is defined almost entirely by what he built and what he has chosen to do — and not do — with what he built.

He has been described by colleagues as intensely focused, technically rigorous, and possessed of an unusual capacity to hold complexity in his head — to see how systems connected to other systems. The physicist's training shows: he thinks in terms of architecture, of protocols, of how information flows. He is also, by his own description, a social optimist. He built the web not as a commercial product but as a social infrastructure, because he believed — and still believes — that access to information is a human good.

That belief explains the decision not to patent the web. It also explains his current campaigns: Solid, the Contract for the Web, his advocacy for net neutrality. He did not build the web and walk away. He has spent his entire adult life trying to make sure it stays what he intended it to be.

"The web is more a social creation than a technical one. I designed it for a social effect — to help people work together — and not as a technical toy." — Tim Berners-Lee

The Scale of the Gift

It is worth stating plainly: no individual in the history of technology has given away more than Tim Berners-Lee. James Watt patented the steam engine. Edison patented everything. Bell patented the telephone. Marconi patented radio. Berners-Lee invented a system that restructured the global economy, the global media, and the daily life of five billion people — and asked for nothing. Not a penny. Not a patent. Not a royalty. Not a share.

The reasons are a mix of practicality and ethics. Practically: a web with toll-booths would not have become the web. Ethically: he believed information should be free to flow, that a universal information space must be universally accessible. The two reasons reinforce each other. That combination — the practical and the ethical pointing in the same direction — is what made the decision possible and permanent.
