Volume 01 — The Problems

The 23 Problems

A single 1900 lecture set the agenda for an entire century of mathematics. Roughly sixteen are now solved; some are independent of the axioms themselves; one is worth a million dollars.

Paris ICM · 8 August 1900

The list that defined a century

When Hilbert read out his problems at the Sorbonne, he was not merely listing hard questions. He was making a wager: that mathematics advances by attacking the right problems, and that every well-posed mathematical question has a definite answer.

The 23 problems span number theory, geometry, analysis, logic, and physics. Their fates are wildly different. Some fell within a decade. Some took most of the century. Two of them — the ones about foundations — were answered not with a proof but with a demonstration that the very kind of answer Hilbert wanted is impossible. The headline cases

Four problems that changed mathematics

PROBLEM 1 · INDEPENDENT OF ZFC

The Continuum Hypothesis

Is there a set whose size lies strictly between the integers and the reals? Gödel (1940) showed CH cannot be disproved from ZFC; Cohen (1963), inventing forcing, showed it cannot be proved either. The answer is independent of the standard axioms — a possibility Hilbert never imagined.

PROBLEM 2 · REFUTED

Consistency of Arithmetic

Prove the axioms of arithmetic are consistent. Gödel's second incompleteness theorem (1931) showed no sufficiently strong consistent system can prove its own consistency. Hilbert's central goal was shown to be unreachable on its own terms.

PROBLEM 8 · UNSOLVED · $1M

The Riemann Hypothesis

Do all non-trivial zeros of the zeta function lie on the critical line Re(s)=1/2? Still open after 160+ years — the greatest unsolved problem in mathematics, and a Clay Millennium Prize worth one million dollars.

PROBLEM 10 · SOLVED (NEGATIVE)

Diophantine Decision Problem

Is there an algorithm to decide if any polynomial Diophantine equation has integer solutions? Matiyasevich (1970), building on Davis, Putnam, and Robinson, proved no — no such algorithm can exist. The MRDP theorem.

A space named for him

Hilbert spaces

Beyond the problems, Hilbert's name lives in one of the most important structures in all of mathematics and physics: the Hilbert space. Emerging from his work on integral equations around 1900–1912, a Hilbert space is a complete inner-product vector space — possibly infinite-dimensional — in which lengths, angles, and limits all behave as our geometric intuition expects.

The payoff came in physics. In the 1920s, John von Neumann recognized that quantum mechanics is naturally formulated in Hilbert space: every quantum state is a vector, observables are operators, and probabilities come from inner products. The mathematics Hilbert built to solve analysis problems turned out to be the native language of the subatomic world.

DEFINITION

Inner product

A way to measure length and angle between vectors, generalized to infinitely many dimensions.

DEFINITION

Completeness

Every Cauchy sequence converges — no "holes" in the space, so limits always land somewhere.

APPLICATION

Quantum states

Each physical state is a unit vector; measurement projects onto an eigenvector.

The grand project

Hilbert's Program — and its death by Gödel

Problems 1 and 2 were not isolated puzzles; they were the public face of Hilbert's Program, his plan to secure all of mathematics on finitary foundations. The program had three pillars:

PILLAR I

Completeness

Every true mathematical statement should be provable within the system.

PILLAR II

Consistency

The system should never prove a contradiction — and this should be provable by safe, finitary means.

PILLAR III

Decidability

There should be a procedure that decides, for any statement, whether it is provable.

Gödel's 1931 theorems knocked out pillars I and II in one stroke: any consistent system strong enough for arithmetic is incomplete, and cannot prove its own consistency. The program in its original form was dead. Yet what replaced it — proof theory, model theory, recursion theory — is among the richest mathematics of the century.

"The conviction of the solvability of every mathematical problem is a powerful incentive to the worker." — David Hilbert, 1900
Pillar III falls too

The Entscheidungsproblem → Turing

The third pillar — decidability — had its own dramatic ending. The Entscheidungsproblem ("decision problem") asked for a mechanical procedure to decide the validity of any first-order logical statement. In 1936, working independently, Alonzo Church and Alan Turing proved that no such procedure exists.

To do it, Turing had to invent a precise definition of "mechanical procedure" — the Turing machine — and prove the halting problem undecidable. In answering Hilbert's question with a "no," Turing accidentally founded computer science. The modern computer is, quite literally, a child of Hilbert's failed third pillar.

How to read the list

Why these problems mattered

Hilbert did not choose his problems at random. He chose questions that were hard but not hopeless, that sat at the boundary of the known, and whose pursuit he expected to generate new mathematics regardless of the eventual answer. He famously insisted that a good problem should be clear and easy to understand, yet deep enough to resist easy solution — "for what is clear and easily comprehended attracts; the complicated repels us."

That instinct proved astonishingly accurate. The problems that turned out to be "unsolvable" in Hilbert's intended sense — the continuum hypothesis, the consistency of arithmetic, the Diophantine decision problem — were not failures of the list. They were its most profound successes, because the proofs that they could not be settled the way Hilbert hoped created set theory's independence results, Gödel's incompleteness theorems, and the theory of computation.

The four fates of a problem

SOLVED

A definite answer found

Most of the list — from Dehn's #3 to Gelfond–Schneider's #7 — was resolved with a clear yes, no, or construction.

PARTIAL / VAGUE

Too broad to "close"

Problems like #6 (axiomatize physics) and #16 are research programs, not single questions — progress is continual but open-ended.

UNSOLVED

Genuinely open

#8, the Riemann Hypothesis, remains the great open question — its proof would illuminate the distribution of the primes.

INDEPENDENT

Beyond the axioms

#1 cannot be decided from ZFC at all. The answer is neither yes nor no but "it depends on which axioms you adopt."

Two foundations in one stroke

Problems 1 and 2: the limits of certainty

The first two problems are best understood together, because together they form the heart of Hilbert's foundational ambition — and together they were turned back. Problem 1, the continuum hypothesis, asks a question about the sizes of infinite sets. Problem 2 asks for a proof that the axioms of arithmetic can never lead to contradiction.

Hilbert expected both to have clean, positive answers. Instead, Gödel and Cohen showed that the continuum hypothesis is independent of the standard axioms of set theory: you may assume it true or false without contradiction, and standard mathematics cannot decide between them. And Gödel's second incompleteness theorem showed that no consistent system strong enough for arithmetic can prove its own consistency — so Problem 2, as Hilbert posed it, has no solution within the system itself.

These are not gaps in our knowledge waiting to be filled. They are theorems about the structure of mathematical truth itself — the most surprising results to come out of the entire list, and arguably the most important.

A working method

The questions that built fields

Tracing the descendants of individual problems shows how generative the list was. Problem 10's negative solution required the entire apparatus of recursion theory and gave us deep links between number theory and computability. Problem 18's Kepler conjecture drove decades of work on packing and ultimately a landmark computer-assisted, formally verified proof. Problem 6's call to axiomatize physics helped motivate Kolmogorov's axioms for probability and continues to animate mathematical physics today.

The lesson Hilbert intended is still taught: progress in mathematics comes from posing the right problems clearly, and then following them wherever they lead — even when, as with Problems 1 and 2, they lead to the discovery that some questions have no answer of the kind we first imagined.

"As long as a branch of science offers an abundance of problems, so long is it alive." — David Hilbert, Paris, 1900
