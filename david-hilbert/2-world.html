Volume 02 — The World

Göttingen

For four decades, a small Saxon town was the gravitational center of mathematics. Then, in a single year, the regime tore it apart.

Königsberg → Göttingen

An inheritance of giants

Hilbert's two cities tell the story of nineteenth-century German mathematics. He was formed in Königsberg, the bridge city of Euler's puzzle. He came into his powers in Göttingen, the town of Gauss.

When Hilbert arrived in 1895, Göttingen already carried a staggering lineage. Carl Friedrich Gauss, the "prince of mathematicians," had worked there. Bernhard Riemann, whose geometry would later underpin general relativity, had been his student there. Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet had held the chair between them. To take a position at Göttingen was to stand in that line.

Hilbert and Felix Klein were determined to make the inheritance pay. They recruited aggressively, built institutes, attracted funding, and made Göttingen a destination for the best minds in the world. Students arrived from across Europe, Britain, America, and Japan. "Göttingen" became shorthand for the research frontier itself.

Paris, 8 August 1900

The lecture that became a century

At the second International Congress of Mathematicians, in the heat of a Paris August, Hilbert stood before the assembled mathematicians of the world and gave a long, discursive lecture on the nature of mathematical problems. He spoke of the importance of good problems, of the boundary between the known and the unknown, and of his faith that no problem need remain forever unsolved.

Then he read his list. What might have been a forgettable survey became, almost immediately, the agenda for the next hundred years. The 23 problems organized careers, founded fields, and gave the twentieth century its sense of direction. No other lecture in the history of mathematics has cast so long a shadow.

A fight worth having

Emmy Noether at Göttingen

One of Hilbert's finest acts was a fight he nearly lost. Emmy Noether — arguably the greatest algebraist of the twentieth century — came to Göttingen in 1915. Hilbert and Klein wanted her to teach as a Privatdozent, but the university's philosophy faculty objected: women could not be lecturers.

Hilbert was contemptuous of the objection. At the faculty meeting he is reported to have said: "I do not see that the sex of the candidate is an argument against her admission as a Privatdozent. After all, we are a university, not a bathhouse." For years, while the bureaucracy stalled, lectures were announced under Hilbert's name and delivered by Noether. She finally gained her Habilitation in 1919.

At Göttingen, Noether transformed abstract algebra and proved Noether's theorem, linking symmetries to conservation laws — one of the deepest results in mathematical physics. She was among the first forced out in 1933.

"We are a university, not a bathhouse." — Hilbert, on the objection to hiring Emmy Noether
1933

One year of destruction

On 7 April 1933 the Nazi government enacted the Law for the Restoration of the Professional Civil Service. It removed Jewish and politically undesirable people from public employment — including the universities. Göttingen, with its many Jewish and liberal mathematicians, was devastated.

Within a single year, the greatest concentration of mathematical talent on earth was scattered. Emmy Noether, Richard Courant, Edmund Landau, Hermann Weyl, Paul Bernays, and many others were dismissed or driven into exile. Most went to the United States and Britain, seeding a generation of foreign mathematics with the riches stripped from Göttingen.

The dinner, c. 1934

"There is no mathematics in Göttingen anymore"

The most famous anecdote of Hilbert's last years is also the most desolate. At a banquet, the Nazi Minister of Education, Bernhard Rust, seated beside the old man, asked him a courtier's question — whether the Mathematics Institute at Göttingen had really suffered now that it had been "freed from the Jewish influence."

Hilbert's reply was quiet and total: "There is no mathematics in Göttingen anymore."

It was not bitterness so much as a plain statement of fact. The faculty he and Klein had spent four decades assembling no longer existed. The world center was gone.

"Mathematics in Göttingen? There is really none any more." — David Hilbert to Minister Bernhard Rust
The final years

Isolation and death

Hilbert lived on in Göttingen through the war, increasingly cut off. The colleagues who had once filled his seminars were in America, in Britain, or dead. His own powers had faded. He died on 14 February 1943, aged 81. Wartime conditions and the destruction of his circle meant that only a small number of people — by most accounts fewer than a dozen — attended his funeral.

His tombstone in Göttingen bears the words from his 1930 radio address — the creed of a man who never stopped believing that the unknown could be conquered, even after he had been shown otherwise.

The lineage of a place

Why Göttingen was Göttingen

It is worth pausing on what was actually lost in 1933. Göttingen was not merely a strong department; it was, for a generation, the single most important center of mathematical and physical research on earth. Its strength lay in concentration — the right people, arguing the right questions, in the same corridors, day after day.

The physics side was as formidable as the mathematics. Max Born and James Franck were there; the young Werner Heisenberg, Wolfgang Pauli, and Robert Oppenheimer all passed through. The town's reputation was such that American and British students treated a year at Göttingen as a rite of passage. When the regime emptied the place, it did not just harm German science — it transferred that concentration, almost intact, to Princeton, New York, Cambridge, and beyond.

Hilbert's flat answer to Minister Rust — "There is no mathematics in Göttingen anymore" — was therefore not an exaggeration. The thing that had made the town extraordinary was precisely the community of people, and that community had been deliberately destroyed. The buildings remained. The mathematics had left.

The diaspora

Where the talent went

The mathematicians and physicists driven out of Göttingen and the other German universities did not vanish. They re-rooted, mostly in the English-speaking world, and in doing so they shifted the center of gravity of world science across the Atlantic. The intellectual capital that Germany expelled in 1933 became the seed of American scientific dominance for the rest of the century.

BRITAIN & BEYOND

A scattered generation

Others reached Cambridge, Manchester, Istanbul, and Palestine — every destination enriched, Germany impoverished.

It is one of history's grim ironies that the regime which boasted of purifying German science delivered the gift of a generation of genius to its future adversaries. The atomic age, the computer age, and much of postwar mathematics were built in part by people the Reich had thrown away.

The man in the ruins

Hilbert's last decade

Hilbert himself was not Jewish and was not dismissed. He kept his chair and his home. But he was old — 71 in 1933 — and his great work was behind him. He watched, largely powerless, as the institute he and Klein had built was hollowed out colleague by colleague.

The famous exchange with Minister Rust was not the only sign of his desolation. Friends recalled an old man bewildered that the world he had ordered around mathematics could be dismantled by politics. He had believed, deeply, in the universality and permanence of mathematical truth; he had not reckoned on the fragility of the human institutions that sustain it.

He died on 14 February 1943, in the middle of the war, with most of his circle gone or in exile. The small funeral was a final measure of how completely the community had been destroyed. On the stone they carved the words of his proudest moment: Wir müssen wissen. Wir werden wissen.

"There is no mathematics in Göttingen anymore." — David Hilbert, to the Nazi Minister of Education
What it cost, what it meant

A warning carved into history

The destruction of Göttingen is studied today not only as a chapter in Hilbert's biography but as a parable about the conditions science needs to flourish. Genius is not enough; it requires institutions, freedom, and a community in which ideas can circulate without fear. When a regime decides that the identity of the thinker matters more than the truth of the thought, even the strongest intellectual tradition can be destroyed in months.

Hilbert had built Göttingen on the opposite principle — that mathematics belongs to no race and no nation, that "the cultural world is one country." His fight to admit Emmy Noether was an expression of that creed; so was his refusal to flatter Minister Rust. The town's fall was the defeat of everything he stood for, and he lived just long enough to see it.

The two cities, year by year

A timeline of place

Hilbert's life can be told almost entirely through two cities — Königsberg, where he was formed, and Göttingen, where he reigned and which he watched fall. The arc runs from a confident, expanding mathematical Europe to its catastrophic fracture.
