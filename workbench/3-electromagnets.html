Electromagnets & Solenoids

A coil of wire and a kick of current is half of every gadget you own. They pull, switch, clamp, strike, and sing — and they're hiding in every dead appliance. Here's the family, and what to make it do.

Know your coil

Electromagnet

Wire round an iron core. Power on = magnetic; power off = nothing. The root of all the rest.

coil + core

Solenoid

A coil that yanks an iron plunger in when energized; a spring throws it back. Linear push/pull.

plunger

Relay

A tiny solenoid that flips a switch — a small signal commands a big load.

switches power

Voice coil

The flat coil in a speaker (and a drive's head arm) — fast, precise motion in a magnet's gap.

fast & precise

Solenoid valve

A solenoid that opens or shuts a fluid or air valve.

controls flow

Reed switch

A sealed switch a passing magnet snaps shut — no contact, no wear.

magnet-tripped
Make a magnet
01

Basic electromagnet

Wire round an iron core; current makes it magnetic, off and it isn't.

need enamelled wire · a nail or bolt · a battery

do wind a tight coil, scrape the ends, power it — picks up paperclips.

02

Dial-a-strength

Vary the current and you vary the pull.

need an electromagnet · a pot or PWM · a transistor

do PWM the coil — turn the field up and down on demand.

03

Junkyard crane

A switchable magnet grabs steel, then drops it on cue — the only magnet that lets go.

need a big electromagnet · an arm · a switch

do energize to grab, cut power to drop.

Switch big things relays
04

Switch a big load

A relay's coil throws a switch — a tiny signal controls a lamp, pump, or motor it could never drive directly.

need a relay · a transistor + flyback diode · the load

do pulse the coil; its contacts carry the heavy current.

05

Electric bell

An electromagnet that interrupts its own power chatters a striker — the classic buzzer.

need a coil · a springy contact · a clapper

do run the coil through its own contact — it makes & breaks, and rings.

06

Relay logic

Relays were the first computers — wire them into gates and memory.

need a handful of relays

do cross-wire two relays to hold a state (a latch), or build an AND/OR.

Push & pull solenoids
07

Solenoid striker

Energize and the plunger slams in; a spring throws it back.

need a solenoid · a transistor + flyback diode · a supply

do pulse it — instant kick. Knockers, kickers, stampers.

08

Electric door latch

A solenoid bolt you release electronically.

need a solenoid lock · a button or keypad · a driver

do press to pull the bolt; the spring re-latches it.

09

Solenoid valve

A solenoid opens or closes a fluid or air valve.

need a solenoid valve · a driver · tubing

do switch water or air on and off — irrigation, pneumatics, dosing.

10

Robotic drummer

Solenoids tap drums, bells, or keys on command.

need solenoids · strikers · a microcontroller

do fire each on the beat — a self-playing instrument.

Voice coils & sound
11

Build a speaker

A coil in a magnet's gap pushes a cone to move air.

need a magnet · a coil · a cone or membrane

do drive the coil with an audio signal — it plays.

12

…is also a mic

Run it backwards: move the coil and it generates a signal.

need a small speaker · an amp input

do speak into it; the cone's motion becomes voltage.

13

HDD head actuator

A hard drive's arm is a fast, precise voice-coil mover behind a fierce magnet.

need a dead hard drive

do harvest the actuator for fast pointing — or just keep the magnets.

⚠ very strong magnets
Magnetism tricks
14

Reed switch trigger

A passing magnet snaps a sealed switch shut — no contact, no wear.

need a reed switch · a magnet

do door sensors, bike speedometers, hidden triggers.

15

Shake-to-light

A magnet sliding through a coil makes current (Faraday).

need a coil · a magnet · an LED (+ diode/cap)

do shake it — the LED flickers; store it to glow steady.

16

Wireless power

Two coils pass energy across a gap with nothing touching.

need two coils · an oscillator to drive one · an LED

do drive the primary at high frequency; the LED lights across the air.

17

Magnetic levitator

A coil that pulls just enough, corrected by a sensor, floats a magnet in midair.

need a coil · a hall sensor · a control loop

do sense the magnet's height, trim the coil — it hovers.

18

Mag-lock holder

An energized electromagnet clamps a steel plate hard; release by cutting power.

need an electromagnet · a steel strike plate

do door holders, fail-safe locks, work clamps.

Driving coils
  • The one rule for every coil: a flyback diode. Switch a coil off and its field collapses into a high-voltage spike that kills transistors and chips. Put a diode across the coil, reverse-biased. Always.
  • Don't drive a coil from a logic pin — use a transistor or MOSFET (or a relay/driver module). Coils want real current.
  • Match the rated voltage of the relay or solenoid. Many can pull in hard, then hold at lower power (PWM) so they don't cook.
  • A coil left on gets hot — solenoids are often rated for short pulses, not continuous duty.
  • More turns + an iron core = a stronger field for the same current. The core gathers the flux.
  • AC coils — mains relays, contactors, transformers — are a separate, dangerous category.
Before you let the smoke out
  • Every coil kicks back when switched off — flyback diode, or expect dead parts (and a real zap off a bigger one).
  • Coils and solenoids run hot — mind duty cycles; don't leave them energized.
  • Solenoid plungers move fast and hard — keep fingers out of the path.
  • Salvaged voice-coil, HDD, and speaker magnets are brutally strong — pinch hazard, and keep them clear of cards, drives, and pacemakers.
  • Mains/AC coils, contactors, and capacitor-bank builds are the genuinely dangerous territory — discharge caps, respect mains, same lethal-energy caution as opening a TV or microwave.
