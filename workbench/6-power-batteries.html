Power discipline
17

Measure it

A multimeter is the one tool you can't skip.

need a multimeter

do read volts, check current, test continuity — before you trust anything.

18

Budget the current

Add up what every part pulls at peak.

need each part's draw · arithmetic

do size the supply with headroom; a starved rail browns out.

Fuse & protect

Cheap insurance against smoke and reverse wiring.

need a fuse · a series diode or P-MOSFET · maybe a TVS

do fuse the pack, block reverse polarity, clamp the spikes.

Ground & wiring

One reference, wire thick enough for the current.

need solid grounds · the right gauge

do tie all grounds together; short, fat runs for high current.

Power rules
  • Volts push, amps flow, watts (V × A) are the power. Match the voltage exactly, and make sure the supply can deliver the current.
  • Linear regulators (7805) are simple but burn the extra voltage as heat; switching (buck/boost) converters are efficient — switch when the voltage gap or the current is large.
  • Put a small decoupling capacitor right at every chip's power pins — the cheapest reliability you'll ever buy.
  • Total what everything draws at peak (motors and LED strips spike), and pick a supply with headroom — a starved supply browns out and resets the board.
  • Reverse polarity kills parts instantly — add a series diode or a P-MOSFET — and every subsystem must share one ground.
  • Lithium is unforgiving: Li-ion / LiPo need a proper charger and a protection circuit. Respect them, or they bite.
Before it burns
  • This is the issue where it can hurt you and start a fire — respect it.
  • Lithium cells (Li-ion, LiPo): a damaged, overcharged, shorted, or punctured cell can vent, ignite, or explode. Real charger with protection, never charge unattended, never trust a swollen cell, never short the terminals.
  • Mains voltage can kill. Don't open or rewire mains gear unless you're trained — use a sealed adapter or a proper bench supply for low-voltage work.
  • Shorts and overcurrent start fires — fuse your packs, insulate bare metal, and never let a battery short across a tool or loose in a pocket.
  • Big capacitors and cap banks hold a charge after power-off and can deliver a dangerous jolt — discharge before touching (same caution as TVs and microwaves earlier in the series).
  • Heat: regulators, resistors, and stalled loads run hot — give them airflow or a heatsink, and think before you box them in.
