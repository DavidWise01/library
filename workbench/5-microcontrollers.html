Core moves
  • GPIO — any pin is a digital output (drive it high/low: LED, transistor) or an input (read a switch). You pick the mode in code.
  • PWM — pins fake an analog level by switching fast: dim LEDs, set motor speed, position servos.
  • ADC — analog-input pins turn a voltage into a number; that's how you read most sensors. Not every pin is analog — and the full Raspberry Pi has none built in.
  • Buses — I²C (two wires, many chips), SPI (fast), UART/serial (to your PC): how the board talks to sensors and displays.
  • The loop — most sketches run setup() once, then loop() forever: read, decide, drive, repeat.
  • Current-hungry outputs — motors, coils, big LED strips — never hang off a pin. Use a driver (and a flyback diode for coils), give them their own supply, and tie grounds together.
Before it bites
  • It's low voltage, so you're safe — what's at risk is the board. Most magic smoke is a wiring mistake, not bad luck.
  • Match the logic voltage (3.3 V vs 5 V). Feeding 5 V into a 3.3 V pin can kill it — level-shift.
  • Common ground: every part of the circuit must share a ground, or nothing reads right.
  • A pin sources only a few milliamps — never run a motor, coil, or big strip straight off one.
  • Power hungry parts separately; their current spikes crash a board sharing the supply. Tie everything at ground.
  • Check + and − before powering up — reverse polarity kills boards instantly.
  • The honest one: the board does exactly what you wrote, not what you meant. When it misbehaves, print the values and check them against reality before blaming the hardware — the bug is almost always your assumption.
