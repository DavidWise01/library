Srinivasa Ramanujan

The clerk from Madras who sent God's equations to Cambridge

1887 — 1920  ·  Erode, Tamil Nadu → Trinity College, Cambridge

I. Origins

Born in Erode, Tamil Nadu

On the twenty-second of December, 1887, in the town of Erode in the Tanjore district of Tamil Nadu — then a corner of British India — a boy was born who would, within three decades, produce mathematical work so original and so dense with implication that it is still being fully unpacked a century later. His name was Srinivasa Ramanujan Iyengar.

His family were Brahmin — the priestly, learned caste — but poor. His father, Kuppuswamy Srinivasa Iyengar, worked as a clerk for a cloth merchant in Kumbakonam, sixty miles from Erode. His mother, Komalatammal, was a strong-willed woman who sang devotional songs at the local Namagiri temple. Ramanujan would grow up understanding himself in relation to that goddess — Namagiri Thayar, consort of Narasimha, dwelling in the temple at Namakkal — as the source, the channel, the proof-reader of his mathematics.

Kumbakonam was a town of temples, rivers, and Brahmin learning. Ramanujan attended the Town High School, where by age twelve he had mastered the trigonometry in Loney's Trigonometry, a standard university text. A college student boarding with the family lent him Carr's Synopsis of Pure Mathematics — a dense Victorian compilation of some 6,000 theorems stated without proof. Ramanujan worked through it, supplying his own proofs, and then began generating results far beyond its scope.

The Goddess Namagiri

Ramanujan's relationship to the divine was not metaphorical. He reported that theorems came to him in dreams — specifically, that Namagiri would write equations on his tongue, or hold a scroll of formulae before him in the night. He tested results by computation and intuition, trusting the source.

"An equation for me has no meaning unless it represents a thought of God." — Srinivasa Ramanujan

This is not the statement of a mystic retreating from rigor. Ramanujan computed with ferocious precision. His results — even the ones he could not prove in Western-formal terms — were nearly always correct. The goddess, if we may accept the metaphor on his own terms, had impeccable taste in mathematics.

II. Failure & the Clerk's Desk

The Man Who Failed Everything Except Mathematics

In 1903, Ramanujan won a scholarship to the Government College at Kumbakonam. He lost it the following year. He had neglected every subject but mathematics — failing physiology, English, Sanskrit. He tried again at Pachaiyappa's College in Madras in 1906. He failed again. He never completed a formal university degree.

Between 1904 and 1912, Ramanujan lived in poverty on the margins of Madras. He was married in 1909 to Janaki Ammal, then nine years old (a common arrangement in the period; she moved into his household at twelve). He tutored students for small fees. He was thin, often ill, and filled notebooks — large paper notebooks — with theorems that no mathematician in India could fully evaluate.

He sent work to two established mathematicians in England: both failed to recognize what they were reading and did not respond substantively. His results looked, to eyes trained in conventional mathematics, either wrong, or obvious, or impossible to evaluate.

The Madras Port Trust

In 1912, through the intervention of friends, Ramanujan secured a position as a clerk — Class III, Grade IV — at the Madras Port Trust, earning twenty rupees a month. His supervisor, S. Narayana Iyer, recognized that the man filing accounts was doing something extraordinary in his spare hours. The chairman of the Port Trust, Sir Francis Spring, encouraged him. He also began corresponding with members of the Indian Mathematical Society. Two of those members suggested he write to mathematicians at Cambridge.

III. The Letter

January 16, 1913 — The Most Important Letter in Mathematical History

On the sixteenth of January, 1913, G. H. Hardy — Fellow of Trinity College, Cambridge, already one of the leading mathematicians in the world — received a letter from an unknown clerk in Madras. The handwriting was careful. The English was grammatically correct but formal in the way of colonial self-presentation. The mathematics was extraordinary.

The letter contained eleven pages and approximately 120 theorems: results in infinite series, continued fractions, integrals, prime number theory, hypergeometric series. Many were known — Ramanujan had no way of knowing what had or had not been proved in European mathematics, because he had almost no access to current journals. But many were entirely new. And some, Hardy later wrote, "could only be written down by a mathematician of the highest class."

"I had never seen anything in the least like them before. A single look at them is enough to show that they could only be written down by a mathematician of the highest class. They must be true because, if they were not true, no one would have had the imagination to invent them." — G. H. Hardy, on first reading Ramanujan's letter

Hardy showed the letter to his collaborator J. E. Littlewood. They spent the evening working through the results. Hardy concluded: this man is either a fraud with access to advanced results he has not credited, or — the more likely explanation, as the results were too consistent and too strange to be stolen — a genuine genius working in isolation.

Hardy replied within days. He asked for proofs. He began the bureaucratic and personal work of bringing Ramanujan to Cambridge.

What Was in the Letter

The 120 theorems Ramanujan included covered an enormous range. Among them: results on infinite series converging to values expressible in terms of pi; continued fractions of unusual beauty; results on hypergeometric functions; identities involving what would later be understood as mock theta functions; and results on the distribution of primes — some correct, some approximately correct, some subtly wrong in ways that revealed the boundaries of self-taught intuition touching the edges of the Riemann hypothesis, which Ramanujan had not seen and did not know existed.

Hardy: it was the most unusual letter he ever received in forty years at Cambridge.

IV. Cambridge

Trinity College, 1914–1919

There was a complication: Ramanujan was a Brahmin. Orthodox Brahmin practice prohibited crossing the ocean — the kala pani, the black water. He declined Hardy's first invitation. It was his mother, Komalatammal, who eventually resolved the question — she reported a dream in which Namagiri commanded her to release her son to his destiny. In April 1914, Ramanujan sailed for England on the SS Nevasa.

Cambridge was cold, grey, full of meat Ramanujan would not eat. As an orthodox Brahmin vegetarian, he cooked his own food in his rooms, increasingly inadequately as the war wore on and vegetables became scarce and expensive. The English winter was brutal by Madras standards. He was far from his wife, his mother, the temples he had grown up near.

He worked. For five years — 1914 through 1919 — he and Hardy collaborated in one of the most remarkable partnerships in intellectual history. Hardy provided the formal framework, the proofs, the connection to the European literature. Ramanujan provided the results — the raw, inexplicable, extraordinary identities that Hardy then had to verify and prove.

The Partnership

Hardy described the collaboration carefully. Ramanujan could not always prove his results in the rigorous European sense. He worked by intuition, by a species of mathematical perception that Hardy found impossible to analyze. But he was almost always right — and when wrong, wrong in instructive ways, at the edge of very deep water. Hardy's role was in part to teach Ramanujan the architecture of proof, and in part to follow him into territory Hardy could not have found alone.

V. The Taxi

The Taxicab and 1729

The most famous anecdote in the history of mathematics involves a London taxicab.

Hardy visited Ramanujan in a nursing home in Putney. He came by taxi, and making conversation told Ramanujan the cab number — 1729 — remarking that it seemed to him a rather dull number, and he hoped it was not an unfavorable omen.

"No, Hardy! No, Hardy! It is a very interesting number. It is the smallest number expressible as the sum of two cubes in two different ways." — Srinivasa Ramanujan, from his sickbed

1³ + 12³ = 1 + 1728 = 1729.
9³ + 10³ = 729 + 1000 = 1729.

Hardy's response, recalled years later: "I realized then that I was talking to the greatest mathematical genius I had ever known, and perhaps the greatest of our time." That a man dying of tuberculosis in a London nursing home should hold in immediate recall the number-theoretic properties of arbitrary four-digit integers says something essential about what Ramanujan's mind was.

VI. Return and End

The Return to India, and Death at Thirty-Two

Ramanujan returned to India in April 1919. He was gravely ill. The diagnosis most often cited at the time was tuberculosis; modern scholars have suggested hepatic amoebiasis, complicated by severe vitamin deficiencies from years of inadequate vegetarian diet in wartime England. He was installed in a house in Madras. He continued working.

In his final year, isolated, increasingly weakened, knowing he was dying, Ramanujan produced what would be recognized — sixty years later — as his most original work: the mock theta functions. He described seventeen of them in a letter to Hardy written in January 1920, three months before his death. He did not explain what they were. He gave formulae. It would take mathematicians until 2002 to fully understand them.

"Before I leave England, I would like to write down certain new functions I have discovered that I have called mock theta functions... I have not tried to prove these results yet." — Ramanujan, letter to Hardy, January 1920

Srinivasa Ramanujan died on 26 April 1920, at his home in Chetput, Madras. He was thirty-two years old. He left behind three large notebooks of theorems — the "Ramanujan Notebooks" — and, discovered in a box at Trinity College in 1976, what came to be called the "Lost Notebook": 138 pages of mathematics, produced in his final year, largely dealing with mock theta functions.

What He Left

Three thousand individual formulae and theorems across the notebooks. A letter containing the mock theta functions. A wife, Janaki, who outlived him by seventy-four years, dying in 1994. A name inscribed on the Fellow's roll of the Royal Society. And a question that Hardy articulated and that mathematics has not quite stopped asking: what would Ramanujan have done had he lived to sixty?

Hardy's answer was his own: "He would have been the greatest mathematician of the century."
