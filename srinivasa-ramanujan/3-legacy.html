The Legacy

A century of mathematics built on foundations laid by a clerk from Madras who died at thirty-two

I. What He Left Behind

The Notebooks — Three Volumes and a Box

When Ramanujan died, he left three large paper notebooks. They contained, across thousands of pages, something on the order of 3,000 individual theorems, identities, and results — most stated without proof, in the style of Carr's Synopsis that had been his original model. The notebooks were known; they were difficult; they resisted systematic publication because verifying each result required mathematical work comparable to producing the result itself.

Hardy oversaw the publication of the Collected Papers in 1927 — the papers Ramanujan had published in his lifetime. The notebooks remained largely unpublished, known to specialists, systematically explored by G. N. Watson and B. M. Wilson in the 1930s, and then by Bruce Berndt of the University of Illinois from 1977 onward.

Berndt spent twenty years systematically proving (or disproving, when they were wrong — which was rare) the claims in the notebooks. The result was a five-volume series, Ramanujan's Notebooks, published between 1985 and 1998. A sixth supplementary volume addressed the lost notebook.

"The notebooks of Ramanujan are extraordinary... they contain thousands of results, almost all of them correct, that Ramanujan stated without proof. The task of proving them has occupied mathematicians for a century." — Bruce C. Berndt, University of Illinois, Urbana-Champaign
II. The Discovery

The Lost Notebook — 1976, Wren Library

In the spring of 1976, George Andrews — a number theorist from Pennsylvania State University, in Cambridge for a conference — was given access to a box of papers that had been sitting in the Wren Library of Trinity College since the 1920s. The papers had belonged to G. N. Watson, who had obtained them from Hardy's estate and deposited them at Trinity without fully cataloguing them.

In the box, Andrews found 138 pages of mathematics in Ramanujan's handwriting. The pages contained hundreds of results — primarily involving mock theta functions, which Ramanujan had described (without proof) to Hardy in his January 1920 letter. This was the work of the final year of Ramanujan's life. It had been sitting, unknown, in a box at Trinity for fifty-six years.

Andrews named the pages the "Lost Notebook" — though this is slightly misleading: the pages were never bound as a notebook, and they were not truly lost in the sense of being discarded or destroyed, merely uncatalogued and unread. What they contained was, mathematically, a revelation.

138 Pages

Volume and Density

The lost notebook contains roughly 600 to 650 individual mathematical claims — primarily in the theory of mock theta functions, but also continued fractions, infinite series, and q-series identities. Virtually all have been verified as correct.

1920

Written While Dying

The pages were produced in the last year of Ramanujan's life — while he was bedridden, in severe pain, increasingly weakened. The mathematical density of the work produced under these conditions staggers description.

1987 Publication

Narosa / Springer

The Lost Notebook was published in facsimile in 1987 by Narosa (India) and Springer, on the centenary of Ramanujan's birth. Andrews and Berndt's five-volume proof-by-proof analysis began publication in 2005.

2005–2018

Andrews & Berndt

Five volumes of Ramanujan's Lost Notebook — Andrews and Berndt proving (in modern notation, with full proofs) every claim in the 138 pages. The final volume appeared in 2018.

III. Mathematical Physics

Mock Modular Forms and the Modern World

The resolution of the mock theta function mystery — accomplished by Sander Zwegers in his 2002 Utrecht PhD thesis, and extended by Ken Ono and collaborators — opened a field that connects Ramanujan's deathbed mathematics to some of the deepest problems in theoretical physics.

Zwegers and the Structure (2002)

Sander Zwegers, in his doctoral dissertation under Don Zagier at Utrecht, showed that each mock theta function, when completed by adding a specific non-holomorphic correction term (the "shadow"), becomes a weight-1/2 harmonic Maass form — a function with the correct transformation properties under the modular group.

Completion of a Mock Theta Function
f̂(τ) = f(τ) + R(τ)
where f(τ) is the mock theta function (holomorphic, almost-modular) and R(τ) is a non-holomorphic "shadow" integral. The sum f̂(τ) is a harmonic Maass form — it satisfies the modular transformation laws that f(τ) alone fails to satisfy. The shadow of f is related to a classical theta series.

This was the solution Ramanujan could not supply — he knew something was there, he named it, he computed it, he intuited its properties. He did not have the machinery to explain it. The machinery was built eighty-two years after his death.

Ken Ono and the Extensions

Ken Ono (then at Wisconsin, later Emory, now Virginia) extended Zwegers' work into a systematic theory of harmonic Maass forms and mock modular forms. Ono and his collaborators proved Dyson's partition rank conjecture (1944), explained Ramanujan's partition congruences bijectively, and connected mock theta functions to Borcherds products and the Monster Moonshine.

Ono's 2008 paper with Bringmann unified the theory: every mock theta function of Ramanujan is the holomorphic part of a harmonic Maass form, and its shadow is a unary theta series. The "mock" structure is now understood as a fundamental feature of the theory of modular forms.

IV. The Physics

String Theory, Black Holes, and Moonshine

Ramanujan's work entered theoretical physics through multiple independent routes. Each connection was a surprise; taken together they suggest that what Ramanujan was computing — whatever the source, divine or otherwise — was something fundamental about the mathematical structure of the universe.

String Theory

Bosonic Strings and Partitions

The bosonic string theory requires 26 spacetime dimensions. The proof of this uses the Ramanujan function and the modularity of the partition generating function. The fact that p(n) is generated by a modular form is load-bearing for the consistency of bosonic string theory.

Black Hole Physics

Entropy Counting

Dijkgraaf, Maldacena, Moore, and Verlinde (1997): the degeneracy of BPS black hole microstates in N=4 string compactifications is counted by functions with mock modular symmetry — directly related to Ramanujan's mock theta functions. Black hole entropy = mock theta.

Monster Moonshine

Monstrous and Umbral

Borcherds (1992, Fields Medal 1998) proved the Conway-Norton Monstrous Moonshine conjecture using Borcherds products — which require modular and mock modular machinery. Umbral Moonshine (Cheng-Duncan-Harvey 2012) generalizes this to 23 cases, each involving mock theta functions.

Statistical Mechanics

Hard Hexagon Model

Baxter's hard hexagon model (1980) — exactly solvable in two-dimensional statistical mechanics — has a partition function expressible in terms of the Rogers-Ramanujan identities. The mathematics Ramanujan and Rogers developed appears in crystal lattice physics.

Conformal Field Theory

Characters of Representations

Characters of irreducible representations of Virasoro algebras — the algebra underlying conformal field theory and hence the worldsheet of string theory — are expressible in terms of mock theta functions. Ramanujan's deathbed mathematics is a working tool of quantum field theory.

Cryptography

Elliptic Curves

The connections Ramanujan perceived between theta functions and elliptic curves — perceived intuitively, without the Shimura-Taniyama-Weil conjecture or modern theory — anticipated the mathematical structure underlying elliptic-curve cryptography, the basis of modern secure communications.

The Rogers-Ramanujan Identities in Physics

Rogers-Ramanujan in Statistical Mechanics
The partition function of Baxter's hard hexagon model (1980):

κ(x) = Π(n≥0) (1−x^(5n+2))⁻¹ · (1−x^(5n+3))⁻¹

= Σ(n≥0) x^(n²+n) / (x;x)_n   [Second Rogers-Ramanujan identity]
The hard hexagon model describes atoms on a triangular lattice where adjacent sites cannot both be occupied. Its exact solution requires the Rogers-Ramanujan identities. This was discovered by Rodney Baxter 63 years after Ramanujan's death — and the mathematics was already there, waiting.
V. Century by Century

The Afterlife in Full

  • 1927 Collected Papers published — Hardy edits Ramanujan's published mathematical papers into a collected volume. The notebooks remain unproven, partially explored.
  • 1937 Rademacher's Exact Formula — Hans Rademacher extends the Hardy-Ramanujan asymptotic formula for p(n) into an exact convergent series. The circle method Hardy and Ramanujan invented becomes a major tool of analytic number theory.
  • 1940 Hardy's Ramanujan — G. H. Hardy publishes his personal account of the collaboration, originally delivered as twelve lectures at Harvard. It contains Hardy's famous self-rating and his assessment of Ramanujan as the one mathematician he had known of the "first class." Hardy dies in 1947.
  • 1944 Dyson's Rank Conjecture — Freeman Dyson, then a 20-year-old undergraduate at Cambridge, conjectures the existence of a "rank" of partitions that would explain Ramanujan's partition congruences combinatorially. He cannot prove it. The conjecture is proved in 1987 by Atkin and Swinnerton-Dyer. The "crank" (conjectured by Dyson, defined by Andrews and Garvan in 1988) explains the remaining congruences.
  • 1976 George Andrews discovers the Lost Notebook — In the Wren Library, Trinity College, Cambridge: 138 pages of Ramanujan's mathematics from 1920, containing the mock theta functions and hundreds of other results. Andrews announces the discovery. The mathematical world is astonished.
  • 1977 Bruce Berndt begins the Notebooks project — Berndt at the University of Illinois undertakes the systematic proof of every result in Ramanujan's three notebooks. Twenty years of work. Five volumes. Virtually everything Ramanujan claimed was true.
  • 1985 Gosper's π computation — William Gosper uses Ramanujan's 1914 series to compute π to 17 million decimal places — at that point the world record. The series had been waiting 71 years for computers fast enough to exploit its convergence rate.
  • 1987 Chudnovsky Algorithm — Gregory and David Chudnovsky derive an enhanced version of Ramanujan's π series that adds ~14 digits per term. Used for virtually every subsequent world record computation of π. Ramanujan's 1914 paper becomes the basis of the mathematics underlying modern numerical computation of transcendental constants.
  • 1992 Borcherds proves Monstrous Moonshine — Richard Borcherds wins the Fields Medal in 1998 for, among other things, proving the Conway-Norton Moonshine conjecture — connecting the Monster sporadic group to modular forms via Borcherds products. The structure required is intimately connected to the mock modular machinery traced back to Ramanujan.
  • 2002 Sander Zwegers resolves mock theta functions — In his Utrecht PhD dissertation, Zwegers shows that mock theta functions are the holomorphic parts of harmonic Maass forms. Ken Ono extends this to a full theory. The 82-year puzzle is solved. Mock modular forms become an active research field.
  • 2005–2018 Andrews and Berndt complete the Lost Notebook analysis — Five volumes of Ramanujan's Lost Notebook. Every claim on the 138 pages is proved or explained. The project is complete.
  • 2012 Umbral Moonshine — Miranda Cheng, John Duncan, and Jeff Harvey discover 23 cases of "umbral moonshine" — generalizing monstrous moonshine to 23 Niemeier lattices, with mock theta functions at the center of each case. The mathematical objects Ramanujan described on his deathbed are now central to the deepest structures of moonshine theory.
  • 2015 "The Man Who Knew Infinity" — Feature film directed by Matthew Brown, starring Dev Patel as Ramanujan and Jeremy Irons as Hardy. Based on Robert Kanigel's 1991 biography. Ken Ono serves as mathematical consultant. The film brings Ramanujan's story to the broadest audience it has ever had.
  • Ongoing Ramanujan Machine (2019–) — Israeli researchers at the Technion develop an automated conjecture-generation system named after Ramanujan, which automatically generates new conjectures about mathematical constants (including π) — in the style of Ramanujan's intuitive generation of identities, but driven by algorithms. Several conjectures it generates are subsequently proved by mathematicians.
VI. Honoring the Name

The Ramanujan Prize and the Ramanujan Institute

International Prize

The ICTP Ramanujan Prize

The DST-ICTP-IMU Ramanujan Prize for Young Mathematicians from Developing Countries is awarded annually by the International Centre for Theoretical Physics (Trieste), the International Mathematical Union, and the Government of India's Department of Science and Technology. The prize is specifically for mathematicians from developing countries who are under 45.

The prize carries USD 15,000 and is awarded at a ceremony in Trieste. Recipients have included mathematicians from Iran, Brazil, Vietnam, Argentina, Colombia, India, Mexico, and Nigeria — work in algebraic geometry, combinatorics, number theory, and PDEs.

Indian Institution

SASTRA Ramanujan Prize

The SASTRA Ramanujan Prize, awarded by Shanmugha Arts, Science, Technology and Research Academy (SASTRA University) in Kumbakonam — Ramanujan's hometown — is awarded annually at a conference held on Ramanujan's birthday (22 December) to mathematicians under 32. The age limit of 32 explicitly mirrors the age at which Ramanujan died.

Recipients have included Manjul Bhargava (2005), Kannan Soundararajan (2005), Terence Tao (2006), Ben Green (2007), and Ken Ono (2012). This is one of the most prestigious prizes in number theory and related fields.

Commemorative Institutions

Ramanujan Institute and Museum

The Ramanujan Institute for Advanced Study in Mathematics at the University of Madras (now University of Chennai) was founded in 1957. The Ramanujan Museum in Chennai, operated by the Avvai Home educational charity, preserves documents, photographs, and memorabilia. Ramanujan's birth house in Kumbakonam is maintained as a museum.

The Government of India declared 22 December (Ramanujan's birthday) as National Mathematics Day in 2011. The year 2012 was declared National Mathematics Year on the 125th anniversary of his birth.

VII. The Culture

The Man Who Knew Infinity — Film and Book

1991 Book

Robert Kanigel's Biography

The Man Who Knew Infinity: A Life of the Genius Ramanujan (1991) remains the definitive popular biography. Kanigel spent years in India and England, interviewing those who had known Ramanujan's surviving family and colleagues. The title comes from a phrase Hardy used.

2015 Film

Dev Patel & Jeremy Irons

Directed by Matthew Brown. Dev Patel as Ramanujan, Jeremy Irons as Hardy. The film is faithful to the biographical outline and notably careful with the mathematics. Ken Ono (Emory) served as mathematical consultant and appears in the documentary extras.

Mathematical Accuracy

Ken Ono's Role

Ken Ono — whose own career was shaped by discovering Ramanujan's work — ensured the equations shown on screen are accurate. He has written about the personal significance of Ramanujan's mathematics to his own mathematical development and mental health.

Hardy's Line

The Self-Appraisal

"I have done one thing you could never have done, and that is to have collaborated with both Littlewood and Ramanujan on something like equal terms." This line appears in the film, accurately attributed, in a late scene. Hardy meant it entirely as a statement of pride.

VIII. What Remains

Open Problems and Ramanujan's Unfinished Business

Despite a century of intensive work, Ramanujan's notebooks are not exhausted. Open problems generated by his work include:

Unsolved

Ramanujan's Conjecture on τ(n)

Beyond Deligne's proof of |τ(p)| ≤ 2p^(11/2): the distribution of the values of τ(n) modulo primes, and the precise statistical behavior of the angles arccos(τ(p)/2p^(11/2)) — the "Sato-Tate distribution for weight 12 modular forms" — proved only in 2006 by Barnet-Lamb, Geraghty, Harris, and Taylor, using techniques from the Langlands program.

Active Research

Moonshine Generalizations

Umbral moonshine (2012) remains not fully proved. The connection between the 23 cases and mock modular forms is established; the deeper structural explanation — why exactly these 23 groups and these 23 mock modular forms — is an active research question connecting finite group theory, string theory, and mock theta functions.

Open

Taxicab Numbers Ta(6)

The sixth taxicab number — the smallest integer expressible as the sum of two positive cubes in six distinct ways — is unknown. Ta(5) = 48988659276962496 (found 1999). Ta(6) is unknown; it is known to exist, by probabilistic argument, but has not been identified.

Philosophy

The Source

How did Ramanujan know? Hardy could not answer. No subsequent analysis of his work has produced a mechanical account of how results of such originality and correctness were generated. The question of mathematical intuition — what it is, whether it can be understood — remains open, with Ramanujan as its limiting case.

"He could remember the idiosyncracies of numbers in an almost uncanny way... it was one of his greatest gifts."
— G. H. Hardy
IX. Hardy's Final Word

"The Greatest Mathematician of Our Time"

G. H. Hardy died in 1947 at the age of seventy. His last years were difficult — a heart attack in 1939 left him unable to do serious mathematics. He wrote A Mathematician's Apology (1940) as a farewell to the creative life.

In that book, and in the lectures on Ramanujan that became the 1940 volume, Hardy returned again and again to the collaboration — to what it had meant, what it had cost, what had been produced. He did not regret bringing Ramanujan to Cambridge. He did, privately, blame himself for Ramanujan's illness — for not ensuring better food, better care, for not understanding the depth of the cultural dislocation Ramanujan had undergone.

"I still say to myself when I am depressed, and find myself forced to listen to pompous and tiresome people, 'Well, I have done one thing you could never have done, and that is to have collaborated with both Littlewood and Ramanujan on something like equal terms.'" — G. H. Hardy, A Mathematician's Apology, 1940

Hardy's private rating: he gave himself 25 out of 100 in mathematical ability. He gave Littlewood 30. He gave Hilbert 80. He gave Ramanujan 100.

In a letter written near the end of his life: "Ramanujan's was certainly one of the most romantic of all mathematical careers. From obscurity in the provincial towns of India to a Fellowship of the Royal Society in five years — it is the most extraordinary story I know."

The taxi was number 1729. A dying man, from his sickbed, noted that it was the smallest integer expressible as the sum of two cubes in two ways. A number Hardy had thought dull. What Hardy realized at that moment — what he said he realized — is what we are still realizing: that mathematics, and the minds that produce it, contain depths we have not finished sounding.
