The Numbers

The actual mathematics of Srinivasa Ramanujan — equations that reshaped number theory, analysis, and physics

I. The Taxicab Number

1729 — Hardy-Ramanujan Number

The taxicab number 1729 is the canonical entry point to Ramanujan's number-theoretic sensibility — the anecdote too perfect to be invention but too neat not to feel like mathematics arranged itself to be noticed.

Hardy-Ramanujan Number
1729 = 1³ + 12³ = 9³ + 10³
The smallest positive integer expressible as the sum of two positive cubes in two distinct ways. This property is now used to define the "taxicab sequence": Ta(n) = smallest number expressible as sum of two positive cubes in n distinct ways.

The full taxicab sequence, for completeness: Ta(1) = 2 (= 1³ + 1³), Ta(2) = 1729, Ta(3) = 87539319 (= 167³+436³ = 228³+423³ = 255³+414³). Ta(6) remains unknown. Ramanujan did not find these — he simply knew 1729 cold. That is the point.

Generalizations

Ramanujan was interested in what we now call Diophantine equations — equations in integers — and in taxicab-like problems more broadly. The Hardy-Ramanujan number inaugurated a class of questions about the additive structure of cubes, fourth powers, and higher that occupied number theorists throughout the twentieth century.

II. The Series for π

Ramanujan's Infinite Series for 1/π

In 1914, Ramanujan published a paper containing seventeen extraordinary series for 1/π. They had no precedent. They converged with shocking speed. The most famous, and the one that drove all subsequent computation of π for decades:

Ramanujan 1914 — Series for 1/π
1/π = (2√2 / 9801) · Σ(n=0 to ∞) [(4n)! · (1103 + 26390n)] / [(n!)⁴ · 396^(4n)]
Each term adds approximately 8 correct decimal digits of π. Computed by William Gosper in 1985 to 17 million digits. The Chudnovsky brothers' 1988 variant computed π to over 1 billion digits.

The numbers in this formula — 9801, 26390, 396 — are not guesses or heuristics. They arise from the theory of complex multiplication of elliptic curves and modular forms, a connection Ramanujan perceived intuitively before the theory was formally built. The number 9801 = 99² appears because of a specific modular equation of level related to the Heegner number 163: e^(π√163) ≈ 262537412640768743.99999999999925... — a remarkable near-integer that also drives the structure of the formula.

The Chudnovsky Connection

Chudnovsky Algorithm (1988) — derived from Ramanujan
1/π = (12 / 640320^(3/2)) · Σ(n=0 to ∞) [(-1)^n · (6n)! · (13591409 + 545140134n)] / [(3n)! · (n!)³ · 640320^(3n)]
Adds ~14.18 correct digits per term. Basis of most modern π computation records. 640320³ = 262537412640768000 — related to the same j-function structure Ramanujan used.
III. Mock Theta Functions

The Deathbed Discovery

In January 1920, three months before he died, Ramanujan wrote Hardy a letter. It described seventeen functions he called mock theta functions. He did not explain what they were. He gave their formulae. He said he had "not tried to prove these results yet."

Mathematicians spent eighty-two years not understanding them. The problem: Ramanujan's theta functions looked like modular forms — functions of extraordinary symmetry central to number theory — but they weren't. They behaved like modular forms in some regimes and failed to in others, in a way that seemed almost deliberate.

Mock Theta Function — third order, first type
f(q) = Σ(n=0 to ∞) [q^(n²)] / [(−q; q)_n]²
ω(q) = Σ(n=0 to ∞) [q^(2n²+2n)] / [(q; q²)_(n+1)]²
ν(q) = Σ(n=0 to ∞) [q^(n²+n)] / [(−q²; q²)_(n+1)]
Here (a; q)_n = (1−a)(1−aq)···(1−aq^(n−1)) is the q-Pochhammer symbol. These satisfy near-modular transformation properties that puzzled mathematicians for decades.

Ken Ono's Resolution (2002)

In 2002, Ken Ono at the University of Wisconsin showed that mock theta functions are the "holomorphic parts" of certain weak Maass forms — a class of functions with the correct transformation properties under the modular group, but which fail to be holomorphic. The mock theta functions, it turned out, are half of a larger structure — the other half, the "shadow," supplies exactly the correction needed to restore modularity.

This resolved a seventy-year puzzle and opened an entirely new field. Mock modular forms now appear in:

String Theory

Black Hole Entropy

Dijkgraaf, Maldacena, Moore, and Verlinde (1997) connected mock theta functions to counting of black hole microstates in superstring theory. Elliptic genera of string compactifications.

Moonshine

Umbral Moonshine

Cheng, Duncan, and Harvey (2012–14): 23 instances of "umbral moonshine" connect mock modular forms to sporadic finite simple groups — a vast generalization of the Monster Moonshine conjecture proved by Borcherds.

Combinatorics

Ranks and Cranks of Partitions

Dyson's "rank" and Andrews-Garvan "crank" of partitions — which explain Ramanujan's partition congruences — are intimately related to mock theta functions.

Physics

Quantum Gravity

Witten (2007): the partition function of three-dimensional pure quantum gravity may be expressible in terms of mock modular forms — connecting Ramanujan's last letter directly to quantum gravity research.

IV. The Partition Function

p(n) — Counting the Ways

The partition function p(n) counts the number of ways to write the integer n as an unordered sum of positive integers. The first few values are easily computed by hand. Large values were, before Ramanujan, essentially inaccessible.

The Hardy-Ramanujan Asymptotic Formula (1918)

Hardy-Ramanujan Asymptotic (1918)
p(n) ~ (1 / 4n√3) · exp(π · √(2n/3))   as n → ∞
The first asymptotic result for p(n). For n=200: the formula gives approximately 3.93 × 10¹², vs exact value 3.97 × 10¹² — already remarkable accuracy. Derived using the "circle method" — Hardy and Ramanujan's joint invention.

Rademacher's Exact Formula (1937)

Rademacher Exact Formula
p(n) = (1/π√2) · Σ(k=1 to ∞) √k · A_k(n) · d/dn [ sinh(π/k · √(2/3·(n−1/24))) / √(n−1/24) ]
A_k(n) is a Kloosterman-sum term. This is an exact formula for p(n) as a convergent (not merely asymptotic) series — extraordinary for a combinatorial quantity. It is a direct extension of Hardy-Ramanujan's circle method.

Ramanujan's Congruences

Ramanujan observed, and partially proved, three miraculous congruences:

Ramanujan Partition Congruences
p(5n + 4) ≡ 0 (mod 5)
p(7n + 5) ≡ 0 (mod 7)
p(11n + 6) ≡ 0 (mod 11)
For example: p(4)=5, p(9)=30, p(14)=135, p(19)=490 — all divisible by 5. Dyson (1944) conjectured that a "rank" function on partitions would explain these. The rank and crank functions, now understood via mock theta functions, give bijective proofs.
V. Primes and Sums

Ramanujan Primes and Ramanujan's Sum

Ramanujan Prime

A Ramanujan prime R_n is the smallest prime such that for all x ≥ R_n, the number of primes in the interval (x/2, x] is at least n. Equivalently, R_n is the smallest prime p such that π(x) − π(x/2) ≥ n for x ≥ p, where π is the prime-counting function.

Ramanujan Prime — First Fifteen
2, 11, 17, 29, 41, 47, 59, 67, 71, 97, 101, 107, 127, 149, 151, ...
R₁ = 2 (trivially), R₂ = 11, R₃ = 17, R₄ = 29, R₅ = 41. The sequence was proved by Ramanujan as a consequence of his generalization of Bertrand's postulate: for n ≥ 1, there is always a prime between n and 2n.

Ramanujan's Sum

Ramanujan's sum c_q(n) is defined for positive integers q, n:

Ramanujan's Sum
c_q(n) = Σ_{1≤k≤q, gcd(k,q)=1} exp(2πikn/q)
       = μ(q/gcd(n,q)) · φ(q) / φ(q/gcd(n,q))
μ is the Möbius function, φ is Euler's totient. Ramanujan's sum is a trigonometric sum ranging over integers coprime to q. It appears naturally in the Fourier analysis of arithmetic functions. Used in Vinogradov's proof of the Goldbach conjecture for large odd numbers.

Ramanujan's Conjecture (Tau Function)

Define Ramanujan's tau function τ(n) by the expansion of the discriminant modular form:

Ramanujan Tau Function
Δ(q) = q · Π(n=1 to ∞)(1−qⁿ)²⁴ = Σ(n=1 to ∞) τ(n) · qⁿ
τ(1)=1, τ(2)=−24, τ(3)=252, τ(4)=−1472, τ(5)=4830, τ(6)=−6048...
Ramanujan conjectured (1916) that |τ(p)| ≤ 2p^(11/2) for primes p — now called the Ramanujan conjecture. Proved by Pierre Deligne in 1974 as a consequence of his proof of the Weil conjectures — work for which Deligne received the Fields Medal in 1978.
VI. Composite Structures

Highly Composite Numbers and Rogers-Ramanujan

Highly Composite Numbers

A highly composite number n is a positive integer with more divisors than any smaller positive integer. Ramanujan studied these systematically in 1915 in a paper that began the serious study of what is now called the divisor function d(n).

First Highly Composite Numbers

The Sequence

1, 2, 4, 6, 12, 24, 36, 48, 60, 120,
180, 240, 360, 720, 840, 1260, 1680,
2520, 5040, 7560, 10080, 15120, ...

The number 5040 (= 7!) appears — Plato's ideal city-state size. 360 (= 12! /... degrees). Ramanujan characterized these in terms of prime factorization.

Ramanujan's Result

Structure Theorem

If n = 2^a₁ · 3^a₂ · 5^a₃ · 7^a₄ · ...
is highly composite, then
a₁ ≥ a₂ ≥ a₃ ≥ a₄ ≥ ...

The exponents in the prime factorization must be non-increasing. Also: the largest prime factor of an HCN of size ≈ N is ≈ log N.

Rogers-Ramanujan Identities

The Rogers-Ramanujan identities are among the most beautiful — and most useful — in all of combinatorics and physics:

Rogers-Ramanujan Identities (1894/1917)
Σ(n=0 to ∞) q^(n²) / (q;q)_n = Π(n≥0) 1/[(1−q^(5n+1))(1−q^(5n+4))]

Σ(n=0 to ∞) q^(n²+n) / (q;q)_n = Π(n≥0) 1/[(1−q^(5n+2))(1−q^(5n+3))]
Leonard James Rogers proved these in 1894. Ramanujan discovered them independently around 1913, found them in an old paper of Rogers, and proved them by his own methods. Schur proved them a third time, independently. They appear in: exactly solvable models in statistical mechanics (Baxter's hard hexagon model), conformal field theory, representation theory of affine Lie algebras.
VII. Continued Fractions

Ramanujan's Continued Fractions

Ramanujan had a spectacular facility with continued fractions — infinite expressions of the form a₀ + b₁/(a₁ + b₂/(a₂ + ···)). Some of his results in this area were already known; many were entirely new; some looked so implausible that Hardy needed weeks to verify them.

Rogers-Ramanujan Continued Fraction
R(q) = q^(1/5) · (q; q⁵)_∞ · (q⁴; q⁵)_∞ / [(q²; q⁵)_∞ · (q³; q⁵)_∞] = q^(1/5) / (1 + q/(1 + q²/(1 + q³/(1 + ···))))
Ramanujan to Hardy: "I told him that the continued fraction was related to θ-functions... he had no idea of it." This fraction encodes deep information about modular curves of level 5. Special values include R(e^(−2π)) = (√5−1)/2 · e^(2π/5) — related to the golden ratio φ = (√5−1)/2.
A Startling Identity Ramanujan Sent Hardy
1/(1 + e^(−2π)/(1 + e^(−4π)/(1 + e^(−6π)/(1 + ···)))) = (√5·√(φ√5)−φ²) · e^(2π/5)
Hardy: "If you had told me that [this] was true, I would have doubted you. I should have asked you to prove it. And yet it is true." φ = (1+√5)/2, the golden ratio. Ramanujan's mastery of q-series theory is evident throughout — he perceived structural relationships between continued fractions and theta functions that European mathematicians had not seen.
"The formulae defeated me completely. I had never seen anything in the least like them before."
— G. H. Hardy, on reading Ramanujan's first letter
