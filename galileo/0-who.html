Galileo Galilei

Natural philosopher, mathematician, astronomer — the first modern scientist
PISA, 15 FEBRUARY 1564 — ARCETRI, 8 JANUARY 1642

The Son of a Musician

Galileo Galilei was born on 15 February 1564 in Pisa, in the Duchy of Florence — the same year Michelangelo died and the same year William Shakespeare was born. His father, Vincenzo Galilei, was a lutenist, composer, and music theorist of serious standing, a man who argued against received wisdom in musical theory by appealing to experiment and measurement. The apple, as the saying goes, does not fall far from the tree.

Vincenzo's work on string tension and pitch — published in Dialogo della musica antica et della moderna (1581) — required mathematical reasoning applied to physical phenomena. He measured. He disputed authority. He concluded that tradition was not proof. His son absorbed these habits before he could name them.

The family moved to Florence when Galileo was about ten. Florence was the Medici city — one of the great crucibles of the Renaissance, already past its zenith but still rich with art, patronage, and intellectual ambition. Galileo would orbit the Medici for the rest of his life, eventually becoming their court mathematician and philosopher — a title he carefully negotiated to include "philosopher," because mathematics alone conferred lower social standing than natural philosophy.

The University Dropout

In 1581, at seventeen, Galileo enrolled at the University of Pisa to study medicine — the practical, lucrative choice his father wanted. He was a difficult student in the best sense: persistently curious, unwilling to accept assertions without demonstration. He attended lectures on Aristotle as required and found them increasingly unsatisfying.

According to his first biographer, Vincenzo Viviani (writing decades later, so treat with appropriate skepticism), it was in the cathedral of Pisa in 1583 that Galileo noticed a large bronze lamp — a chandelier — swinging on its chain after being set in motion by a sexton lighting it. Using his pulse as a clock, he timed the swings. Short arc or long arc, fast swing or slow: the period was the same. He had, at twenty, discovered the isochronism of the pendulum.

"All truths are easy to understand once they are discovered; the point is to discover them." — Galileo Galilei

The medical degree never materialized. By 1585 Galileo had left the university without graduating, having spent most of his time studying Euclid and Archimedes — geometry and mechanics, not Galen and Hippocrates. He returned to Florence to study mathematics privately, giving private lessons to support himself. He was broke, in debt (his family's finances were precarious), and completely certain he was on the right path.

In 1589, through patronage and demonstrated ability, he secured the Chair of Mathematics at the University of Pisa — ironically the institution that had failed to grant him a degree. The salary was modest: 60 scudi per year. He earned it anyway.

The Padua Years (1592–1610)

The eighteen years Galileo spent at the University of Padua were, by his own account, the happiest and most productive of his life. Padua was under the sovereignty of the Venetian Republic — arguably the most intellectually tolerant state in Italy, deliberately resistant to papal authority, proud of its independence. The university drew students from all over Europe.

Galileo lectured on Euclid, mechanics, and astronomy (teaching the Ptolemaic system officially, as required, while privately doubting it). He lived with Marina Gamba, with whom he had three children — Virginia, Livia, and Vincenzo. He never married her. This was common enough in his class, but he later ensured his daughters were placed in a convent and his son legitimized. His relationship with Virginia — who took the name Sister Maria Celeste — became one of the great correspondences in the history of science. Her letters, warm and devoted, survived; his to her did not.

During these years he invented the geometric and military compass (1597), a calculating device for artillery and surveying; the thermoscope (an early, non-calibrated thermometer); and began the systematic experiments on motion that would eventually overturn Aristotelian physics. He also made instruments and sold them — he was always short of money.

Invention · 1597

Geometric Compass

A sector-based calculating instrument for artillery elevation, currency conversion, and geometric problems. Galileo sold it with a manual and private lessons.

Instrument · ~1593

Thermoscope

A rudimentary temperature-sensing device — a glass bulb with a long neck inverted in water. It showed temperature change but lacked a calibrated scale.

Experiment · c.1604

Inclined Plane

Rolling balls down grooved ramps to slow free fall enough to measure it. Proved distance increases as the square of time: the first kinematic law.

The Telescope, 1609

In the summer of 1609, Galileo heard a rumor — a Dutch spectacle-maker had invented a device that made distant objects appear close. The original device, probably developed by Hans Lipperhey in 1608, magnified perhaps three times. Galileo, working from the principle alone, built his own instruments with rapidly increasing magnification: 8×, then 20×, then nearly 30 times.

He did not invent the telescope. He made it into a scientific instrument. He pointed it upward. No one, apparently, had thought to do this in any systematic way. What he saw in the autumn and winter of 1609–1610 shattered the cosmology of the ancient world.

Discovery · Nov 1609

Lunar Mountains

The Moon's surface was rough, cratered, mountainous — not the perfect crystalline sphere Aristotle required. Galileo estimated mountain heights from shadow lengths.

Discovery · Jan 1610

Jupiter's Moons

On 7 January 1610, he saw three stars near Jupiter; a fourth appeared days later. They moved with Jupiter but around it. Not everything orbited Earth.

Discovery · 1610

Phases of Venus

Venus showed a full cycle of phases like the Moon. This was impossible under the Ptolemaic system where Venus always stayed between Earth and Sun.

Discovery · 1610

The Milky Way

The faint band across the sky resolved into countless individual stars — not a cloud or nebula but a vast multitude of suns, too faint to see separately.

Discovery · 1611–12

Sunspots

Dark blemishes on the Sun's surface that moved, appeared, and disappeared. The Sun was imperfect — and it rotated. Heresy in stone, written in light.

Discovery · 1610

Saturn's Shape

Saturn appeared as three bodies — he described it as "three-bodied." His telescope could not resolve the rings; they appeared as two flanking blobs.

He published everything in Sidereus Nuncius — Starry Messenger — in March 1610. It was an immediate sensation across Europe. Five hundred copies sold almost instantly. He dedicated Jupiter's moons to his patrons, the Medici, naming them the Medicean Stars. The political calculation worked: Cosimo II de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany, appointed him Chief Mathematician and Philosopher to the court in Florence, at twice his Padua salary.

Triumph in Rome, 1611

In 1611, Galileo went to Rome in triumph. He demonstrated his telescope to cardinals, nobility, and the Jesuit mathematicians of the Collegio Romano. They confirmed his observations. He was elected to the Accademia dei Lincei — the Lincean Academy, one of the first scientific societies in Europe — and was received by Pope Paul V, who showed him honor.

He met a Florentine cardinal named Maffeo Barberini, a cultured humanist who admired him, wrote him a poem, and became his friend. Barberini would later become Pope Urban VIII — and the man who sent Galileo to the Inquisition.

The atmosphere in Rome was not yet hostile. The Church had not formally prohibited heliocentrism as heresy. The Jesuits were intrigued. There was a genuine window in which the new astronomy might have been accommodated. It would not last.

The Dialogo and the Trial

In 1616, the Inquisition declared heliocentrism "foolish and absurd in philosophy and formally heretical." Cardinal Bellarmine, on behalf of the Holy Office, instructed Galileo not to hold or defend the Copernican view. Galileo agreed. He spent the next several years working on other problems — tides, comets, a method of finding longitude using Jupiter's moons.

In 1623, his friend Maffeo Barberini became Pope Urban VIII. Galileo believed the climate had changed. He sought and received permission to write a book comparing the Ptolemaic and Copernican systems — provided he treated them as mathematical hypotheses and did not advocate for either.

The Dialogo sopra i due massimi sistemi del mondo — Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems — was published in 1632. The fiction of impartiality was transparent to anyone who read it. The Aristotelian defender, named Simplicio, was made to look foolish. Urban VIII believed the arguments he had personally given Galileo had been put in Simplicio's mouth. He felt mocked. The book was banned within months of publication.

1632

Dialogo published in Florence. Sensation across Europe. Banned within months by the Holy Office.

1633 · FEB

Galileo, 68 years old and in poor health, ordered to Rome. He travels in a litter, stops to rest in Siena.

1633 · APR–JUN

Tried before the Inquisition. Three examinations. Threatened with torture — historians debate whether it was applied. He recants.

1633 · JUN 22

Kneeling in the Church of Santa Maria sopra Minerva, Rome, Galileo formally abjures "the false opinion that the Sun is the center of the world." Sentenced to house arrest for life.

1634

His beloved daughter Virginia — Sister Maria Celeste — dies. Galileo is devastated. He writes to a friend that he hears her calling to him.

1638

Discorsi e dimostrazioni matematiche smuggled out of Italy and published in Leiden, Holland. His greatest scientific work.

1638

Galileo goes completely blind. He continues to dictate. Students — including Evangelista Torricelli and Vincenzo Viviani — come to Arcetri to work with him.

1642 · JAN 8

Galileo Galilei dies at Arcetri, under house arrest. He is 77. In England, on Christmas Day the same year, Isaac Newton is born.

"And Yet It Moves"

Eppur si muove — "And yet it moves." The phrase is almost certainly apocryphal. No contemporary account records Galileo saying it at the recantation or afterward. The earliest known attribution dates to 1757, more than a century after his death. Yet it has the quality of a distilled truth — a compression of something real into a legend that persists because it captures the man's essence better than any documented utterance.

What is not apocryphal is this: Galileo recanted under duress, spent the remaining nine years of his life under house arrest at Arcetri outside Florence, went blind, and in that blindness produced the Discorsi — a work that founded the science of mechanics and gave Newton the foundation on which to build. He was not broken. He was not silent. He worked until he could not.

"In questions of science, the authority of a thousand is not worth the humble reasoning of a single individual." — Galileo Galilei

The Church denied him a public funeral and a monument in the Basilica of Santa Croce in Florence. A century later, in 1737, those decisions were reversed. His remains were moved to a grand tomb in Santa Croce, beside Michelangelo, across from Machiavelli. Florence, at last, claimed him.

The Catholic Church formally apologized in 1992. Pope John Paul II, addressing the Pontifical Academy of Sciences, acknowledged that the theologians who condemned Galileo had made an error — that they had confused the Bible's religious teaching with scientific questions about the structure of the universe. It took 359 years. The apology was real.

It moves.
