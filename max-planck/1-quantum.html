I — The Problem

The Blackbody Radiation Problem

A blackbody is an idealized physical object that absorbs all electromagnetic radiation that falls on it, and re-emits radiation purely as a function of its temperature. A perfect blackbody is a perfect absorber and a perfect emitter. The sun is approximately a blackbody. A furnace with a small hole through which you observe the interior is approximately a blackbody. A glowing piece of metal approximates a blackbody. The question physicists asked was: what is the exact spectrum of radiation emitted by a blackbody at a given temperature?

This was not a question of purely theoretical interest. The practical applications were immediate — steelmakers needed to know a furnace's temperature by its color, and precision measurement of temperature by spectral emission was commercially and scientifically valuable. The German government had accordingly funded very precise experimental measurements of blackbody spectra at the Physikalisch-Technische Reichsanstalt (Imperial Bureau of Standards) in Berlin, where Otto Lummer and Ernst Pringsheim produced extraordinarily careful data from the 1890s onward.

The data was precise. The theoretical prediction was a disaster.

The Ultraviolet Catastrophe

Classical physics — specifically, the combination of Maxwell's electromagnetism with the equipartition theorem of statistical mechanics — predicted what would later be called the Rayleigh-Jeans law. This law described how energy should be distributed among the different frequencies of radiation inside a blackbody cavity. According to the law, the energy radiated at each frequency is proportional to the square of the frequency, with no upper bound. This means: as you go to higher frequencies (shorter wavelengths, toward the ultraviolet and beyond), the classical prediction rises without limit.

In the real world, of course, nothing of the kind happens. A hot object glows red, then orange, then yellow-white as its temperature rises. The spectrum peaks at a frequency determined by temperature, then falls off at high frequencies. Classical physics predicted that the spectrum should keep rising toward infinity — an impossible result that would mean any hot object radiates infinite energy. This prediction, catastrophically wrong at high frequencies, was named the ultraviolet catastrophe by Paul Ehrenfest in 1911.

"The whole procedure was an act of desperation because a theoretical interpretation had to be found at any price, no matter how high that might be."
— Max Planck, 1931 letter to R.W. Wood

II — The Formula

E = hf: What It Means

Planck's breakthrough was to assume that the energy of each oscillator in the blackbody walls — each atom vibrating and emitting radiation — could not take on arbitrary values. Instead, it could only take values that were integer multiples of a basic quantum of energy proportional to frequency:

E = hf
E = energy of one quantum · h = Planck's constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s) · f = frequency of radiation
Alternatively written: E = hν (using ν, nu, for frequency) or E = ℏω (ℏ = h/2π, ω = angular frequency)

This is a statement of almost shocking simplicity. Energy is proportional to frequency. The proportionality constant is h — a new fundamental constant that Planck had to introduce, which he called the quantum of action (Wirkungsquantum). Its value, measured by fitting the blackbody data, is:

h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s
Planck's Constant — SI units (joule-seconds)
This number is extraordinarily small — a single photon of visible light carries energy of about 3 × 10⁻¹⁹ joules. The smallness of h is why quantum effects are invisible in everyday life and only become apparent at atomic scales, where individual quanta matter.

The quantity h has dimensions of energy × time, which is also the dimension of angular momentum and of action in classical mechanics — hence Planck's name for it. In modern physics, h appears in the uncertainty principle, in the energy of every photon, in the quantization of atomic energy levels, in the spin of particles, in the Compton wavelength, in the fine structure constant. It is, arguably, the most fundamental constant in quantum mechanics — the scale at which the quantum nature of the world becomes visible.

III — The Logic

How the Quantum Resolves the Catastrophe

The catastrophe arises from the classical assumption that all frequencies can hold arbitrary amounts of energy — so high-frequency modes get the same average energy as low-frequency modes. Planck's quantization changes this because it becomes increasingly costly to excite a high-frequency mode. If the energy must be at least hf just to activate the mode at all, and if that minimum energy is much larger than the typical thermal energy available (kT, where k is Boltzmann's constant and T is temperature), the mode simply stays empty. High-frequency modes are frozen out.

The Planck Distribution — Conceptual Derivation
1. E_n = nhf Assumption: energy of a mode can only be 0, hf, 2hf, 3hf, ... — discrete steps, not a continuous range. n is a non-negative integer.
2. P(E_n) ∝ e^(−nhf/kT) From Boltzmann statistics: the probability of finding a mode in energy state E_n is proportional to the Boltzmann factor. Higher energy states are exponentially less likely at temperature T.
3. ⟨E⟩ = hf/(e^(hf/kT)−1) Average energy of a mode: sum over all states (n × hf × probability), divided by sum of probabilities. The geometric series evaluates to this elegant expression — the Planck distribution.
4. hf ≪ kT (low freq) When frequency is low, hf/kT ≪ 1, and e^(hf/kT) ≈ 1 + hf/kT, so ⟨E⟩ ≈ kT. This recovers the classical result — equipartition holds at low frequencies.
5. hf ≫ kT (high freq) When frequency is high, hf/kT ≫ 1, and e^(hf/kT) ≫ 1, so ⟨E⟩ ≈ hf·e^(−hf/kT) → 0. The high-frequency modes are exponentially suppressed. No catastrophe.

The Planck distribution function ⟨E⟩ = hf/(e^(hf/kT)−1) contains within it both limits: it is exactly the classical result at low frequencies, and exponentially suppressed at high frequencies. It fits the Lummer-Pringsheim blackbody data with precision. It also automatically recovers, as special cases, both Wien's displacement law (location of the spectral peak) and the Stefan-Boltzmann law (total power emitted). A single formula — built on a single assumption about discreteness — resolves every outstanding problem with blackbody radiation simultaneously.

IV — The Constant

Planck's Constant: The Scale of the Quantum World

When Planck introduced h, he could compute its value from the experimental blackbody data. This was immediately significant: from h and the then-known value of Boltzmann's constant k, he could derive estimates of Avogadro's number (the number of molecules in a mole) more accurately than any previous method. From Avogadro's number and the charge of the electron (which had just been measured by Thomson), he could derive the fundamental unit of electric charge — again, more accurately than previous methods. A constant introduced to explain light emitted by hot objects was already, on the day it was published, one of the most precisely known numbers in physics.

Value

h = 6.62607015 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s

Exact as of 2019, when the kilogram was redefined in terms of h. The Planck constant now defines the SI unit of mass.

Alternative Form

ℏ = h/2π ≈ 1.055 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s

ℏ (h-bar) appears naturally in quantum mechanics. Heisenberg's uncertainty principle reads: Δx·Δp ≥ ℏ/2. Spin is measured in units of ℏ/2.

Uncertainty Principle

Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ/2

Heisenberg's uncertainty principle, derived by Werner Heisenberg in 1927. Position and momentum cannot both be precisely known — the product of their uncertainties has a minimum fixed by ℏ.

Photon Energy

E = hf (or E = hc/λ)

The energy of a photon is proportional to frequency. Visible light (f ≈ 5×10¹⁴ Hz) has energy ≈ 3.3×10⁻¹⁹ J per photon — about 2 eV. A single photon of gamma radiation carries millions of times more.

Atomic Structure

E_n = −me⁴/8ε₀²h²n²

Bohr's formula for hydrogen energy levels (1913). The discrete energy levels of the hydrogen atom — the quantum ladder — are governed by h. Without h, no stable atoms exist.

SI Definition (2019)

1 kg defined via h

Since 2019, h is defined exactly as 6.62607015×10⁻³⁴ J·s. The kilogram is derived from this. Planck's "temporary mathematical trick" now defines the mass of the international prototype kilogram.

V — The Next Step

Einstein 1905: The Quantum Is Real

Planck had assumed that the energy of the emitters (the oscillators in the blackbody walls) was quantized. He did not assert that light itself was quantized. He thought of the quantum as a property of matter, not radiation. In 1905, Albert Einstein went further: he proposed that light itself exists in discrete packets — photons — each carrying energy E = hf. This was not what Planck intended at all.

Einstein used this hypothesis to explain the photoelectric effect: when light strikes a metal surface, electrons are ejected — but only if the light's frequency exceeds a threshold, regardless of intensity. Classical wave theory could not explain this. If a single photon's energy (hf) exceeds the binding energy of the electron, the electron is ejected. Below the threshold frequency, no individual photon has enough energy, no matter how many photons arrive. This explained every feature of the photoelectric effect quantitatively and correctly.

Planck was not pleased. He respected Einstein enormously — it was Planck who championed Einstein's work to German physicists and who brought him to Berlin. But he thought the photon hypothesis was too radical. Light was a wave; Maxwell had proved it; the interference and diffraction experiments were beyond dispute. Planck accepted Einstein's mathematics but resisted the physical interpretation for years. He was wrong. The photon is real. Wave-particle duality is real. The discomfort Planck felt was the discomfort of a new universe pushing its way through the mathematics he had written.

"Planck is one of the best men in Berlin — he is the one who first recognized the significance of my work. But he still has not fully accepted the photon."
— Albert Einstein, c. 1912

The Revolution Planck Started But Never Fully Accepted

The quantum revolution that followed Planck's 1900 paper was built by other hands. Bohr's atomic model (1913) used Planck's constant to explain the discrete spectral lines of hydrogen. De Broglie's matter waves (1924) gave electrons a wavelength of λ = h/p. Heisenberg's matrix mechanics (1925) and Schrödinger's wave mechanics (1926) completed the formal quantum theory. Born's probabilistic interpretation of the wave function (1926) gave the theory its definitive physical content — and was the step that most disturbed Planck and Einstein alike. Dirac unified quantum mechanics with special relativity (1928), producing the Dirac equation that predicted antimatter.

Throughout all of this, Planck remained the elder statesman — respected, consulted, and increasingly out of step with the generation his paper had made possible. He had opened a door he did not want to walk through. Younger physicists, with no attachment to the classical worldview, walked through it without hesitation and built the physics of the twentieth century on the other side.

VI — The Reckoning

What One Formula Resolved — and Created

Resolved by Planck 1900

The Ultraviolet Catastrophe

Classical physics predicted infinite energy at high frequencies. E = hf terminated this divergence by exponentially suppressing high-frequency modes. The catastrophe vanished.

Resolved by Planck 1900

The Blackbody Spectrum

The exact spectral distribution of radiation from a hot body — matching Lummer and Pringsheim's precision data — was explained for the first time, correctly, by the Planck distribution.

Created: Einstein 1905

The Photon

Einstein's photoelectric paper established that light itself is quantized. The photon — the quantum of the electromagnetic field — became real. Won Einstein the 1921 Nobel Prize.

Created: Bohr 1913

Quantized Atomic Energy Levels

Electrons orbit atoms only at discrete energy levels given by multiples of h. Atoms emit and absorb light only at specific frequencies corresponding to level transitions.

Created: Heisenberg-Schrödinger 1925–26

Quantum Mechanics

The full formal framework — wave functions, operators, probability amplitudes — all written in terms of h. The theory that governs every atom, molecule, and subatomic process.

Created: Dirac 1928

Relativistic Quantum Theory

The Dirac equation combined quantum mechanics with special relativity. It predicted the existence of antimatter — confirmed in 1932 with the discovery of the positron.
