Volume 0 — The Person

Werner Heisenberg

1901 – 1976

The man who told us that the universe, at its root, refuses to be pinned down — and who lived a life of equal ambiguity.

Physicist · Nobel 1932 · Uncertainty Principle · Matrix Mechanics · The Question of the Bomb
Introduction

The Man from Würzburg

Werner Karl Heisenberg was born on 5 December 1901 in Würzburg, Bavaria, the second son of August Heisenberg, a scholar of Byzantine Greek, and Annie Wecklein, daughter of the headmaster of the Maximilian Gymnasium in Munich. He would die in Munich on 1 February 1976, at the age of 74, of kidney and gallbladder cancer. Between those two dates he invented matrix mechanics — the first complete mathematical formulation of quantum theory — derived the uncertainty principle that bears his name, helped lead the German nuclear weapons program during the Second World War, and became one of the most consequential and contested figures in the history of science.

He was also, by every account, an exceptionally gifted pianist, a passionate hiker and mountaineer, a devoted family man, and a man whose wartime choices have never been fully explained. The uncertainty that is written into the fabric of nature, Heisenberg's great discovery, extends also to his own biography. We cannot know with precision what he intended during those years under Hitler, and the more carefully we look, the less certain we become.

Origins

Munich, and the Education of a Prodigy

The Heisenberg family moved from Würzburg to Munich in 1910, when August was appointed professor of Byzantine philology at the University of Munich. Werner was nine years old. He attended the Maximilian Gymnasium — the same school his maternal grandfather had headed — and demonstrated an early and intense aptitude for mathematics. He reportedly learned calculus before it was formally taught, from a book he discovered at home.

The First World War swept through his youth. Bavaria in 1918 and 1919 was in chaos: the revolution, the short-lived Bavarian Soviet Republic, and the violent reaction of the Freikorps. Heisenberg, then seventeen, was involved in the youth brigade that helped keep order during the turbulent interregnum. He was shaped, as were so many of his generation, by the sense that the old certainties had dissolved — a sentiment that would, perhaps not coincidentally, find its scientific expression in a physics of irreducible uncertainty.

He was also shaped by Wandervogel, the German youth movement that turned hiking and romantic communion with nature into something like a spiritual practice. Throughout his life he would return to the mountains — to walk, to think, to recover. Some of his greatest intellectual breakthroughs occurred not at his desk but in transit, in motion, outdoors.

"When I was a boy I fell in love with mathematics, and this love has never left me. It was the beauty of pure mathematics that first drew me to theoretical physics."
— Heisenberg, in conversation with Werner Bloch, 1971
Munich 1920

Sommerfeld and the Old Quantum Theory

In 1920 Heisenberg enrolled at the University of Munich, intending to study pure mathematics under Ferdinand von Lindemann. The meeting was disastrous — von Lindemann was unimpressed and unwelcoming. Heisenberg then sought out Arnold Sommerfeld, the great theoretical physicist who was at that time running the most productive and influential physics seminar in the world. Sommerfeld, who had an extraordinary gift for identifying talent, recognized Heisenberg immediately.

Sommerfeld's institute was at the cutting edge of the old quantum theory — the patchwork of classical mechanics and Bohr's quantum postulates that everyone knew was fundamentally unsatisfactory. The old quantum theory could not explain the intensities of spectral lines, could not handle atoms with more than one electron, and rested on arbitrary rules (the "Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization conditions") without any underlying justification. It was scaffolding over a void, and everyone knew it.

Heisenberg took to theoretical physics immediately and completely. He was so advanced that Sommerfeld brought him as a junior collaborator to the Solvay Conference in 1921, and by 1922 had sent him to Göttingen to attend lectures by Niels Bohr — a visit that would be the hinge of Heisenberg's life.

The Bohr Encounter, 1922

Bohr came to Göttingen in June 1922 to deliver a series of lectures on atomic structure — what became known as the "Bohr Festival." It was at one of these lectures that the twenty-year-old Heisenberg raised his hand and respectfully but firmly challenged one of Bohr's calculations. Bohr was taken aback — and then delighted. After the lecture he approached Heisenberg and invited him for a walk. That walk lasted three hours.

Bohr later said that it was in this conversation that he first felt the problem of atomic theory might genuinely be solved. He saw in Heisenberg someone who combined mathematical facility with genuine physical intuition — someone who was not satisfied by the patched-together compromises of the old theory. He invited Heisenberg to come to Copenhagen.

Göttingen 1922–1923

Born, Göttingen, and the PhD

After the Bohr Festival lectures, Sommerfeld sent Heisenberg to spend a semester in Göttingen under Max Born. Born was one of the great mathematical physicists of the era — more rigorous, more algebraically formidable than the intuitively-driven Bohr. The combination of influences would prove decisive: from Sommerfeld Heisenberg learned physical intuition; from Bohr he learned to question the conceptual foundations; from Born he learned mathematical precision and the power of abstract algebra.

Heisenberg completed his PhD at Munich in July 1923, at age twenty-one — technically twenty-two, since his birthday fell in December. The dissertation concerned turbulence in fluids, a subject that Sommerfeld had suggested. His oral examination famously nearly ended in disaster: the experimentalist Wilhelm Wien, who sat on his committee, was irritated by Heisenberg's inability to explain the resolving power of a microscope or the operation of a storage battery. Wien voted to fail him outright. Sommerfeld and the mathematician Ferdinand von Lindemann voted to pass. Heisenberg scraped through with the lowest possible passing grade.

He did not let it rattle him. Within two years he would do the most important work in physics of the twentieth century.

Helgoland, June 1925

The Island, the Sea, and the Birth of Quantum Mechanics

By 1925 Heisenberg was working with Born in Göttingen and spending extended periods in Copenhagen with Bohr. He was twenty-three. The problem consuming him — consuming everyone — was the failure of the old quantum theory. It gave right answers for some things and wrong answers for others, and its foundations were rotten. The only honest path forward was to throw out the classical framework entirely and build something new from the observed facts alone.

In June 1925 Heisenberg was suffering severely from hay fever. His face was swollen, his eyes were streaming. Born, taking pity, gave him two weeks off. Heisenberg took the train north and crossed by ferry to Helgoland, a small, nearly treeless island in the North Sea, far enough from the mainland that there was no pollen. The air off the sea was clean.

On Helgoland he walked, swam in the cold water, memorized Goethe, and thought. He was attempting to reformulate atomic physics using only observable quantities — the frequencies and intensities of spectral lines that could actually be measured in the laboratory — rather than unobservable electron orbits. The key insight: do not try to picture what the electron is doing between observations. Work only with what can be observed.

The mathematics that emerged was strange. When he tried to compute the energy of a quantum state and ensure that it was conserved, he found that the quantities he was working with did not commute — that is, the order of multiplication mattered: A × B ≠ B × A. He recognized this was an entirely new kind of mathematics. He was alarmed. He was also fairly sure it was right.

"It was about three o'clock in the morning when the final result of the calculation lay before me. At first I was deeply alarmed. I had the feeling that, through the surface of atomic phenomena, I was looking at a strangely beautiful interior, and felt almost giddy at the thought that I now had to probe this wealth of mathematical structures nature had so generously spread out before me."
— Heisenberg, Physics and Beyond, 1971

He walked to the southern tip of Helgoland and climbed a rock formation at the water's edge. He sat there as the sun rose over the North Sea. He was twenty-three years old. He had just invented quantum mechanics.

Life & Chronology

A Life in Time

The three-man paper that casts matrix mechanics in full mathematical form, identifying the commutation relation pq − qp = ℏ/i as the fundamental postulate of quantum theory.

Paper submitted: "Über den anschaulichen Inhalt der quantentheoretischen Kinematik und Mechanik." The inequality Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ/2 is stated for the first time.
1927
Professor in Leipzig
Appointed full professor at Leipzig at age 25 — one of the youngest in Germany's history. Builds a major research school.
1932
Nobel Prize in Physics
Awarded the Nobel Prize "for the creation of quantum mechanics." Schrödinger and Dirac share the 1933 Nobel. Heisenberg is 31.
1933
Hitler Comes to Power
Heisenberg refuses to leave Germany. Attacked by the "Deutsche Physik" movement as a "white Jew" for teaching relativity and quantum theory. Himmler eventually orders the attacks stopped, but the damage to Heisenberg's standing is lasting.
September 1939
Drafted into the German Nuclear Program
Germany invades Poland; Heisenberg is called to Berlin to lead the theoretical side of what becomes the Uranverein — the German uranium project.
September 1941
The Copenhagen Meeting
Heisenberg visits occupied Copenhagen and meets privately with Niels Bohr. What was said between them has never been established with certainty. Bohr leaves the meeting shaken and angry. The meeting is one of the most debated episodes in the history of science.
August 1945
Farm Hall; Hiroshima
Heisenberg and nine other German physicists are interned at Farm Hall, England. Their secretly recorded conversations upon hearing the news of Hiroshima reveal that they had not come close to building a bomb.
1946–1976
Postwar Germany
Heisenberg rebuilds German physics. Directs the Max Planck Institute for Physics in Munich. Pursues a unified field theory. Remains active in public debate on nuclear weapons and science ethics until his death.
1 February 1976
Death in Munich
Werner Heisenberg dies at home in Munich, aged 74, of kidney and gallbladder cancer.
The Central Question

The War, the Bomb, and the Question That Cannot Be Closed

Unresolved History

Did Heisenberg Choose Not to Build the Bomb?

Germany had the knowledge, the physicists, and the industrial capacity to attempt an atomic bomb in the early 1940s. It did not build one. Werner Heisenberg led the theoretical effort. The question — why didn't Germany build the bomb? — has never been definitively answered, and Heisenberg is at the center of the ambiguity.

There are two broad interpretations, and adherents of each consider the other's position historically naive:

The Incompetence Hypothesis

Heisenberg made an early and critical technical error: he miscalculated the critical mass of fissile material required for a bomb, arriving at a figure many times too large. This made a German bomb seem impossibly resource-intensive. The Germans therefore focused on reactors rather than weapons, and never got close to a bomb. On this view, Heisenberg simply got the physics wrong, and Germany's failure was a consequence of that error rather than any moral decision.

The Farm Hall transcripts — recordings made of the German physicists' private conversations after they were told of Hiroshima — lend significant weight to this view. Heisenberg and his colleagues are audibly astonished that the Americans had succeeded. Their conversations show they had not worked out the key physics of a fission bomb. They were not merely pretending ignorance for their captors.

The Sabotage Hypothesis

Heisenberg deliberately suppressed or misled the project. He understood the critical mass calculation. He could have pushed harder, organized better, redirected more resources. He chose not to. He was protecting physics from becoming a weapon — or protecting Germany from winning a war under Hitler — or both.

Heisenberg's own postwar account strongly favored this interpretation. He claimed to have told Bohr in Copenhagen in 1941 that he was deliberately avoiding weapons work. Bohr's response — furious rejection — suggests he heard something very different. The Copenhagen meeting of 1941 remains the hinge on which all interpretation turns.

What the Evidence Permits

The honest answer is: we do not know. The Farm Hall transcripts show German physicists who had not solved the bomb. They do not tell us whether this was by accident or design. Heisenberg's postwar testimony is self-serving. Bohr's letters (released by his family in 2002, long after Bohr's death) suggest that what Heisenberg actually said in Copenhagen was disturbingly close to a claim that Germany would win the war and physicists on both sides had better cooperate. Michael Frayn's 1998 play Copenhagen dramatizes this ambiguity with great precision — and concludes that the ambiguity is not incidental but essential.

Heisenberg's uncertainty principle describes a universe where certain things cannot be simultaneously known with precision. His own history exhibits the same property.

The Man

Character and Dimensions

The Musician

Concert-Level Pianist

Heisenberg was a devoted and technically accomplished pianist. He practiced throughout his life and played Mozart, Bach, and Beethoven. Music was not an affectation but a genuine passion — several friends recalled that it was when playing piano that Heisenberg seemed most completely himself.

The Hiker

Wandervogel to the End

The Wandervogel spirit he absorbed as a teenager never left him. He hiked the Alps regularly throughout his life. After his work on Helgoland, he climbed rocks at the island's tip to watch the sunrise. Physical motion and intellectual breakthrough were deeply linked in his psychology.

The Teacher

Builder of Schools

At Leipzig, Heisenberg built one of the strongest theoretical physics departments in the world. He trained numerous students who went on to important careers. He was known as a patient, thoughtful teacher who could explain complex ideas with unusual clarity — except, notoriously, the resolving power of a microscope to Wilhelm Wien.

The Patriot

Chose to Stay

Many of Germany's Jewish physicists fled after 1933. Heisenberg, not Jewish, chose to remain. He was attacked by the Deutsche Physik movement — Stark and Lenard called him a "white Jew" — but he stayed, considering it his duty to preserve German science and culture through the dark years.

The Philosopher

Physics and Philosophy

Heisenberg took the philosophical implications of quantum mechanics seriously in a way that many physicists did not. His books — Physics and Philosophy, Physics and Beyond — wrestle with questions of epistemology and ontology that were not merely window-dressing but central to his scientific vision.

The Family Man

Elisabeth and Seven Children

Heisenberg married Elisabeth Schumacher in 1937, with whom he had three sons and four daughters. By all accounts the marriage was happy and the household warm. He was a devoted, present father — unusual in an era when many scientists of his generation were largely absent from domestic life.

Closing

What Remains

Heisenberg left behind three things that will survive as long as physics survives. First, matrix mechanics — the first complete formulation of quantum theory, invented at age twenty-three on a North Sea island. Second, the uncertainty principle — the result that tells us that nature itself is not merely opaque to our measurements but is fundamentally indeterminate in a way that has no classical analogue. Third, the question of who he was during the war — a question that remains open, and which may be the most human thing about him.

Physics is full of geniuses. The particular kind of genius that Heisenberg represented — synthetic, architectural, willing to throw away all of classical physics and rebuild from scratch — is rarer. He did not merely extend what came before. He broke with it entirely, and built something new from observable facts alone. The quantum mechanics we use today, the quantum mechanics on which every transistor and laser is based, flows directly from those weeks on Helgoland in June 1925.

"Natural science does not simply describe and explain nature; it is part of the interplay between nature and ourselves."
— Werner Heisenberg, Physics and Philosophy, 1958
