The Signal Carries On

1879 – Present · Hertz to Feynman to You

Maxwell died in 1879. Everything transmitted wirelessly since then — every radio broadcast, every telephone call, every WiFi packet, every photon from every screen — travels by his equations. His afterlife is larger than his life, and it is still happening right now.

Died 1879  Still propagating
I.

The Eight-Year Gap: Maxwell to Hertz

Maxwell died on 5 November 1879. His equations had been published in 1865. For fourteen years they were accepted by a growing number of mathematical physicists and resisted by an equal number who wanted mechanisms, not fields. The key prediction — that electromagnetic waves should exist and should travel at the speed of light — had not been experimentally verified.

In 1879, the year Maxwell died, a prize was announced by the Berlin Academy of Sciences for the experimental detection of electromagnetic waves. A young German physicist named Heinrich Hertz was in the audience when the prize was announced. He was 22 years old. He would not succeed for another eight years — but when he did, he would confirm everything Maxwell had predicted.

1887
Heinrich Hertz Detects Electromagnetic Waves
Karlsruhe, Germany

Hertz built a spark-gap transmitter — essentially a high-voltage device that produced a rapidly oscillating electric current — and detected the resulting electromagnetic waves across his laboratory with a loop of wire. He measured the wavelength. He calculated the frequency. He multiplied them: the product was 3 × 10⁸ m/s. The speed of light. Maxwell's prediction was confirmed, eight years after Maxwell's death. Hertz then showed that the waves could be reflected, refracted, and polarized — exactly like light. They were the same thing. Maxwell was right.

"I feel that these sparks have to do with Maxwell's theory."

Heinrich Hertz, 1887 — in a letter describing his first successful electromagnetic wave experiments

Hertz also did something Maxwell had left undone: he provided physical intuition for the mechanism of radiation. Maxwell had shown mathematically that accelerating charges must radiate electromagnetic waves. Hertz showed it experimentally. The spark gap was a device that accelerated charges — electrons oscillating in the antenna — and the result was a wave propagating outward at c. Every radio transmitter in history is a more sophisticated version of Hertz's spark gap. The unit of frequency — the Hertz — is named for him. Every Hertz of frequency is a tribute to a man who was himself verifying Maxwell.

II.

What Grew From Maxwell's Equations: The Electromagnetic Spectrum

Maxwell's equations describe all electromagnetic radiation. When Hertz verified radio waves in 1887, he had opened a door. Beyond the visible spectrum — the narrow sliver of electromagnetic radiation that human eyes detect — lay a vast territory that Maxwell had predicted but no one had explored. The exploration of that territory is the technological history of the twentieth century.

Maxwell did not live to see any of it. But it all runs on his equations.

III.

Einstein: The Photograph on the Wall

Albert Einstein kept three portraits on the wall of his study: Isaac Newton, Michael Faraday, and James Clerk Maxwell. This was not decoration. It was a statement of intellectual lineage. Einstein understood, with unusual clarity, that his own work grew directly from Maxwell's.

Einstein's Study Wall — Princeton
N
Isaac Newton
1643–1727
F
Michael Faraday
1791–1867
M
James C. Maxwell
1831–1879

Einstein wrote that Maxwell's work was "the most profound and fruitful that physics has experienced since the time of Newton." Three men on a wall. The arc from Newton to Maxwell to Einstein is the arc of classical physics.

The connection between Maxwell and special relativity is direct and technical. Maxwell's equations have a remarkable mathematical property: they are invariant under the Lorentz transformation — they take the same form for all inertial observers. But Newtonian mechanics is invariant under the Galilean transformation, not the Lorentz. The two were inconsistent.

Einstein's special relativity (1905) resolved the inconsistency by keeping Maxwell's equations and revising Newtonian mechanics. Newtonian mechanics was the approximation; Maxwell's equations were the deeper truth. Every consequence of special relativity — time dilation, length contraction, E = mc² — follows from taking Maxwell's equations seriously and asking what mechanics has to look like for them to hold in all inertial frames.

"The special theory of relativity owes its origin to Maxwell's equations of the electromagnetic field. Since Maxwell's time, physical reality has been thought of as represented by continuous fields, governed by partial differential equations, and not capable of any mechanical interpretation. This change in the conception of reality is the most profound and fruitful that physics has experienced since the time of Newton."

Albert Einstein, 1931 — centenary tribute to Maxwell

The "field concept" Einstein refers to — the idea that space itself contains physical quantities (field values) that vary continuously from point to point and obey differential equations — was Faraday's intuition and Maxwell's formalization. It became the template for all of twentieth-century physics. General relativity is a field theory. Quantum mechanics is reformulated as a field theory. The Standard Model is a collection of field theories. Every one of them inherits its conceptual structure from Maxwell's electromagnetic field.

IV.

Quantum Electrodynamics: Maxwell at Quantum Scale

Maxwell's theory is classical — it describes fields as continuous entities, not made of discrete particles. Quantum mechanics, developed in the 1920s, showed that at small scales, energy comes in discrete packets: quanta. This raised a problem: how do you make Maxwell's electromagnetic field theory consistent with quantum mechanics?

The answer is quantum electrodynamics (QED), developed by Paul Dirac (1927), and then completed by Richard Feynman, Julian Schwinger, and Sin-Itiro Tomonaga in the late 1940s. QED is the quantum version of Maxwell's theory. The electromagnetic field, in QED, is made of photons — the quanta of light. Every interaction between charged particles happens via the exchange of virtual photons. Every photon that has ever touched your retina was a quantum of Maxwell's electromagnetic field.

QED — The Most Precisely Tested Theory in Physics

QED predicts the magnetic moment of the electron — a quantity called g/2 — to be approximately 1.00115965218128. The measured value is 1.00115965218059. These numbers agree to eleven decimal places. This is the most precise agreement between theory and experiment ever achieved in physics. It is a precision equivalent to measuring the distance from New York to Los Angeles to within a hair's width. Maxwell's equations, promoted to the quantum level, describe reality with extraordinary precision.

QED Pioneer
Paul A.M. Dirac
1902–1984

Combined Maxwell's electromagnetism with quantum mechanics and special relativity (1927). His equation for the electron predicted antimatter. Dirac called Maxwell's equations the most profound achievement in physics prior to his own era.

QED Completion
Richard Feynman
1918–1988

Co-developed the complete form of QED, Nobel Prize 1965. Feynman's path integral formulation of quantum mechanics generalizes Maxwell's field concept to the quantum level. He described QED as the jewel of physics. The jewel is Maxwell's gem, cut quantum-sharp.

QED Completion
Julian Schwinger
1918–1994

Independently developed a rigorous formulation of QED, Nobel Prize 1965 shared with Feynman and Tomonaga. Schwinger explicitly described his work as extending Maxwell's program into the quantum domain.

Standard Model
Yang, Mills, et al.
1954–present

The Yang-Mills theory (1954) generalized Maxwell's gauge structure to non-abelian symmetry groups, producing the mathematical framework for the weak and strong nuclear forces. The entire Standard Model is a generalization of Maxwell's mathematical structure.

V.

The Long Afterlife: A Timeline

1879

Maxwell Dies

Cambridge, 5 November. His equations are 14 years old. Hertz has not yet confirmed them. Einstein is not yet born. The telephone is three years old. The light bulb has just been invented by Edison. The electromagnetic world Maxwell described is about to begin arriving.

1887

Hertz Confirms Electromagnetic Waves

Karlsruhe, Germany. Hertz detects radio waves with a spark-gap transmitter and loop antenna. Measures wavelength and speed. Product = c. Maxwell was right. The unit of frequency (Hz) is named for Hertz. Every FM radio station broadcasts at frequencies measured in hertz.

1895

Marconi Sends Radio Signals

Guglielmo Marconi uses Maxwell-Hertz waves to send signals over increasing distances. By 1901 he transmits across the Atlantic. Wireless telegraphy — and ultimately all wireless communication — is born. Maxwell's equations are doing practical work.

1895

Röntgen Discovers X-rays

Wilhelm Röntgen observes penetrating radiation from his cathode ray tube. X-rays are electromagnetic radiation at very high frequency — described by Maxwell's equations, unknown to Maxwell himself. Medical imaging transforms within years. The EM spectrum, whose existence Maxwell predicted, starts being explored.

1905

Einstein's Special Relativity

Built directly on Maxwell. Einstein asks: what does mechanics look like if Maxwell's equations hold for all inertial observers? The answer is special relativity: time dilation, length contraction, E = mc². Maxwell's equations were right; Newtonian mechanics needed revision.

1927

Dirac Equation — Quantum Electromagnetism Begins

Paul Dirac combines Maxwell's electromagnetism with quantum mechanics and special relativity. The result predicts antimatter and becomes the foundation of quantum electrodynamics — the quantum version of Maxwell's theory.

1948

Feynman, Schwinger, Tomonaga Complete QED

Quantum electrodynamics reaches its final form — the most precisely tested theory in physics. Every photon, every electromagnetic interaction, described by Maxwell's field promoted to quantum status. Nobel Prize 1965.

1954–1970s

Yang-Mills and the Standard Model

Maxwell's mathematical gauge structure — the mathematical symmetry underlying his equations — is generalized to non-abelian groups. The result is the Yang-Mills framework, the mathematical backbone of the entire Standard Model of particle physics. Maxwell's insight into gauge invariance describes not just light but the weak and strong nuclear forces.

1973

WiFi's Mathematical Ancestor — ARPANET and Wireless

ARPANET, the internet's ancestor, goes live. The eventual wireless internet — WiFi, 4G, 5G, Bluetooth — are all applications of Maxwell's equations. The standard governing WiFi (IEEE 802.11) encodes Maxwell's physics in silicon.

2015

LIGO Detects Gravitational Waves

The LIGO detector — mirrors separated by 4 km, detecting displacements smaller than a proton's width — uses laser interferometry. The lasers are governed by Maxwell's equations. The detection method relies on Maxwell's optics. The theoretical framework of gravitational waves is modeled on Maxwell's electromagnetic waves: Einstein's field equations for gravity were written in explicit analogy to Maxwell's field equations for electromagnetism.

Today

Every Signal Is Maxwell

The device you are reading this on communicates wirelessly via radio waves. Its processor synchronizes via clock signals that propagate electromagnetically. Its screen emits visible light — electromagnetic radiation at 430–770 THz. The GPS signal locating it travels at c, described by Maxwell. The X-ray that imaged your last broken bone, the MRI that scanned your brain, the microwave that heated your food — all Maxwell. He is the most ubiquitous dead physicist in the world.

VI.

The Problem of His Obscurity

Maxwell is virtually unknown to the general public. Ask a non-scientist to name a great physicist. Newton. Einstein. Hawking. Feynman. Ask them to name five. You might get Bohr, Heisenberg, Curie. Maxwell rarely appears before the tenth name, if he appears at all.

This is a historical accident with a specific cause. Maxwell's equations, unlike the falling apple or the mushroom cloud or the famous equation E = mc², do not have an accessible visual or narrative hook. They are four equations in vector calculus. They reward the person who studies them but resist the person who wants a quick summary. Moreover, Maxwell lived between the age of the great natural philosophers (Newton, Faraday) and the age of mass communication (Einstein's fame was amplified by the 1919 eclipse observations and intensive press coverage). Maxwell had neither: no memorable celebrity experiments to observe and no press corps to amplify him.

"From a long view of the history of mankind — seen from, say, ten thousand years from now — there can be little doubt that the most significant event of the 19th century will be judged as Maxwell's discovery of the laws of electrodynamics."

Richard Feynman — The Feynman Lectures on Physics, Vol. 2

Feynman says this seriously. He is ranking Maxwell above Darwin, above Marx, above Lincoln, above the entire industrial revolution. His argument: the electromagnetic equations are still exactly right, still exactly used, still the operating framework for a technology that did not exist in Maxwell's lifetime and now constitutes a third of the global economy. No other discovery from the 19th century can claim that status.

Why He Is Not Famous — And Why It Matters

The people who know Maxwell's equations best — working physicists and engineers — tend not to write popular books about him. The people who write popular science books tend not to know the equations well enough to explain what Maxwell actually did rather than just asserting it was important. The result is a strange void: a physicist of the first rank, universally recognized within his field as Newton's peer and Einstein's inspiration, essentially unknown to the culture that runs on his work. Every time you connect to WiFi, you are connecting to something Maxwell predicted. He deserves to be remembered.

VII.

The Accounting

Maxwell was 48 when he died. He had been doing serious mathematical physics for about 25 years — from his first paper on oval curves at fourteen to his death in Cambridge. In that span he:

The Unified Theory

Unified Electricity, Magnetism, and Light

Three domains of physics revealed as one. The governing equations of wireless communication, optics, and radiation all follow from four equations written by one man in 1865.

The First

Founded Statistical Mechanics

The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution was the first statistical law in physics. It opened the door to Boltzmann's entropy, Gibbs's ensemble theory, and the entire thermal physics of the 20th century.

The Prediction

Solved Saturn's Rings from Mathematics

No telescope could see ring particles in 1859. Maxwell proved they had to be there from the equations of mechanics. The Cassini probe confirmed it 145 years later.

The First Image

Created the First Color Photograph

1861. Three filter images, three projectors, one superimposed color image. The ancestor of every color photograph, color film, color screen, and color sensor in the world.

The Institution

Built the Cavendish Laboratory

Designed from nothing. Opened 1874. Site of the discovery of the electron, the neutron, and the structure of DNA. The greatest physics laboratory of the 20th century was Maxwell's project.

The Question

Founded Information Thermodynamics

Maxwell's Demon — an 1867 thought experiment — became the founding question of the thermodynamics of information. Landauer's principle, Shannon entropy, and the physics of computation trace back to a question Maxwell posed as a casual aside.

The Signal That Did Not Stop

He was forty-eight years old. He died before Hertz confirmed his waves, before Marconi sent his signals, before Einstein built relativity on his foundations, before Feynman made his fields quantum. Every photon you have ever seen has obeyed his equations. Every radio wave, every WiFi packet, every X-ray, every microwave. Every color on this screen. The electromagnetic field Maxwell described is still propagating. It has never stopped. It never will.
