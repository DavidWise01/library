Volume 1  ·  The Four Equations

The Laws That Unified Light

Electricity · Magnetism · Light · One Theory

In 1865, Maxwell published twenty equations (later compressed to four) that unified all of classical electromagnetism. They predicted that light is an electromagnetic wave — and gave its speed from first principles. Nothing in physics has been more consequential.

Published 1865  Treatise 1873
Preface

Before the Equations

Before Maxwell, electricity and magnetism were two separate sciences. Electricity was studied by Coulomb, Ampère, and Gauss. Magnetism by Ørsted, Ampère, and Faraday. Light was studied by Newton, Young, and Fresnel. Three domains, three sets of laws, three kinds of apparatus.

Faraday had suspected they were connected. His experiments on electromagnetic induction — changing magnetic fields producing electric currents — showed a relationship. But Faraday had no mathematics to describe it rigorously. He thought in pictures: lines of force, field lines, tubes of flux. The pictures were right. They lacked the quantitative spine that would make prediction possible.

Maxwell's project, begun in 1855 with his first paper "On Faraday's Lines of Force," was to give Faraday's pictures that spine. He succeeded beyond anyone's expectation, including his own. When the mathematics emerged complete, it contained something no one had put in: a prediction of electromagnetic waves traveling at the measured speed of light.

"We can scarcely avoid the conclusion that light consists in the transverse undulations of the same medium which is the cause of electric and magnetic phenomena."

James Clerk Maxwell, 1865 — "A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field"

He did not say this tentatively. He had calculated the speed of propagation of electromagnetic disturbances from his equations and obtained 3.107 × 10⁸ m/s. The measured speed of light was 3.14 × 10⁸ m/s. The agreement was not a coincidence. It was identification.

Notation Note

Maxwell's original 1865 paper contained 20 equations in 20 unknowns. Oliver Heaviside reformulated them in the 1880s using vector calculus, compressing them to the four equations in the form we use today. The physics is Maxwell's. The notation is Heaviside's. The four equations shown here are in Heaviside's SI form — the standard presentation in modern physics and engineering.

Notation

Equation I

Gauss's Law for the Electric Field

Maxwell I Gauss's Law — Electric Electric charges create fields
Integral Form
∮ E · dA = Q_enc / ε₀
Differential Form
∇ · E = ρ / ε₀

What it says: The electric flux passing through any closed surface is proportional to the total electric charge enclosed within that surface.

In the differential form: the divergence of the electric field at any point equals the charge density at that point, divided by the permittivity of free space. Wherever charge exists, electric field lines either originate (positive charge) or terminate (negative charge). No charge, no net flux through a closed surface.

Physical consequence: Electric field lines begin on positive charges and end on negative charges. An isolated positive charge creates a field that radiates outward in all directions like a hedgehog. An isolated negative charge draws field lines in. This is why opposite charges attract and like charges repel — their fields interact.

This equation is a reformulation of Coulomb's law (the inverse-square law for electric forces) but in a form that extends naturally to dynamic situations and arbitrary charge distributions. It remains valid in all inertial frames — a fact Einstein used in special relativity.

Equation II

Gauss's Law for the Magnetic Field

Maxwell II Gauss's Law — Magnetic No magnetic monopoles
Integral Form
∮ B · dA = 0
Differential Form
∇ · B = 0

What it says: The magnetic flux through any closed surface is always zero. Magnetic field lines never begin or end — they always form closed loops.

The contrast with Equation I is deliberate and deep. Electric fields can have sources and sinks (charges). Magnetic fields cannot. There is no magnetic equivalent of a lone electric charge. Every magnetic north pole comes with a south pole. Cut a magnet in half — you get two magnets, each with a north and south. You cannot isolate one.

Physical consequence: If you draw any closed surface in a magnetic field — a sphere, a cube, a crumpled bag — every field line that enters must exit. The net flux is always zero. This is experimentally confirmed to extraordinary precision.

The open question: Maxwell's second equation forbids magnetic monopoles — isolated north or south poles — in classical electromagnetism. Whether monopoles exist in quantum field theory remains an open question. Dirac showed in 1931 that a single monopole anywhere in the universe would explain the quantization of electric charge. None has ever been detected. If one is ever found, this equation must be modified: the right-hand side would contain a magnetic charge density term analogous to ρ in Equation I.

Equation III

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

Maxwell III Faraday's Law Changing B creates E
Integral Form
∮ E · dl = −dΦ_B / dt
Differential Form
∇ × E = −∂B / ∂t

What it says: A changing magnetic field creates an electric field. The curl of the electric field at any point equals the negative of the rate of change of the magnetic field at that point.

Faraday discovered this experimentally: move a magnet through a loop of wire, and a current flows. The current flows because the changing magnetic flux through the loop induces an electric field that drives electrons around the circuit. Maxwell captured this in a field equation — without requiring any wire, any loop, or any current. The electric field is induced whether or not any conductor is present to detect it.

The minus sign: The negative sign encodes Lenz's law — the induced electric field always opposes the change that created it. Nature resists change. Push a magnet into a loop; a current flows that creates a field opposing the motion. Pull it out; the current reverses to try to keep the flux constant. This is how all electric generators work: rotating coils in magnetic fields, changing flux continuously, inducing continuous current.

Physical consequence: Every transformer, every generator, every electric motor, every induction cooktop, every wireless charger operates on this equation. Faraday's law is the working principle of the entire electrical power industry.

Equation IV

The Ampère-Maxwell Law — Maxwell's Great Addition

Maxwell IV Ampère-Maxwell Law Changing E creates B — Maxwell's own term
Integral Form
∮ B · dl = μ₀(I + ε₀ dΦ_E/dt)
Differential Form
∇ × B = μ₀J + μ₀ε₀ ∂E / ∂t

What it says: Magnetic fields are created by two things: (1) electric currents J, and (2) changing electric fields. The second term — μ₀ε₀ ∂E/∂t — is Maxwell's original contribution. Ampère's original law had only the first term.

Why Maxwell added the displacement current: Ampère's original law (just the J term) was mathematically inconsistent in dynamic situations. Consider a capacitor being charged: current flows in the wires, but no current flows between the plates — there is a gap. Ampère's law without the extra term would give contradictory answers for different surfaces spanning the same loop. Maxwell resolved this by adding the displacement current — the time derivative of the electric field — which is non-zero between the capacitor plates where the electric field is changing.

Why this changes everything: The displacement current term was not measured by any experiment. It was added for mathematical consistency. But its consequences were revolutionary. With this term in place, changing electric fields create magnetic fields — exactly symmetrically with how changing magnetic fields create electric fields (Equation III). The two fields can sustain each other without any charges or currents at all, propagating indefinitely through empty space.

This is the equation that predicts electromagnetic waves.

Synthesis

From the Equations to Light

With all four equations in hand, Maxwell combined equations III and IV in a vacuum (where ρ = 0 and J = 0). The algebra is mechanical but the outcome is astonishing. Differentiating and substituting produces, for each component of E and B separately, the wave equation:

The Wave Equations — In Vacuum
∇²E = μ₀ε₀ ∂²E / ∂t²
∇²B = μ₀ε₀ ∂²B / ∂t²
These are wave equations with wave speed v = 1/√(μ₀ε₀)

The wave speed falls out directly from the electromagnetic constants. Maxwell did not put the speed of light into his equations. It emerged. He then calculated:

Maxwell's Calculation — 1865
c = 1 / √(μ₀ · ε₀)
c = 1 / √(4π × 10⁻⁷ × 8.854 × 10⁻¹²)
c = 2.998 × 10⁸ m/s
Measured speed of light (Fizeau, 1849): 3.13 × 10⁸ m/s

The agreement was not numerical coincidence. Maxwell recognized immediately that the speed of electromagnetic waves predicted by his equations matched the measured speed of light. Light is an electromagnetic wave. This was not a hypothesis. It was a mathematical derivation.

"The agreement of the results seems to show that light and magnetism are affections of the same substance, and that light is an electromagnetic disturbance propagated through the field according to electromagnetic laws."

Maxwell, 1865 — "A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field"
The Key Symmetry

The reason electromagnetic waves can propagate through empty space is the symmetry between equations III and IV. A changing magnetic field creates an electric field (III). A changing electric field creates a magnetic field (IV). Each field, changing in time, regenerates the other. The two fields bootstrap each other through space — no medium required. Maxwell initially thought a medium (the "luminiferous aether") was required. Special relativity, forty years later, would show that no medium is needed: the fields themselves are the entities propagating through spacetime.

Beyond Electromagnetism

Maxwell's Other Mathematical Contributions

Kinetic Theory · 1860

Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

f(v) = 4π(m/2πkT)^(3/2) v² e^(−mv²/2kT)

The probability distribution of speeds of molecules in a gas at temperature T. The first rigorous statistical law in physics. Later extended by Boltzmann into the full statistical mechanics program.

Saturn · 1857–1859

Ring Stability Analysis

rings = discrete particles
(not solid, not liquid)

Maxwell proved by perturbation analysis that a solid or liquid ring around Saturn would be dynamically unstable and would break apart. Only a swarm of independent particles can be stable. Confirmed by Cassini 145 years later.

Thermodynamics · 1867

Maxwell's Demon

ΔS ≥ 0 — but can
information defeat entropy?

A thought experiment: a tiny intelligent being sorting fast from slow molecules. Could it violate the second law? Resolution: measurement has thermodynamic cost. Information is physical. Maxwell's Demon founded information thermodynamics.

Color Vision · 1855–1861

Trichromatic Color Theory

C = rR + gG + bB
(color as vector sum)

Maxwell quantified color perception as a three-dimensional space spanned by red, green, and blue primaries. The CIE 1931 color space, used in every digital display standard, is a direct descendent of Maxwell's color triangle.
