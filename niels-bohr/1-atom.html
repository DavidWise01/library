I — The Bohr Model (1913)

Electrons in Fixed Orbits

In 1911 Rutherford had established the nuclear atom: a tiny dense positive nucleus surrounded by electrons. But classical physics predicted catastrophe. An orbiting electron, by Maxwell's equations, constantly accelerates and should therefore continuously radiate electromagnetic energy, losing speed and spiraling into the nucleus in less than a microsecond. Matter could not exist. And yet it obviously did.

Bohr's solution was audacious: he simply postulated that classical physics did not apply inside the atom. Electrons could only occupy certain specific orbits, which he called stationary states. In these orbits, contrary to all classical expectation, electrons did not radiate. An electron only emitted or absorbed energy when it jumped from one orbit to another — and the energy emitted or absorbed was exactly the difference in energy between the two orbits, expressed as a single photon with frequency given by Planck's relation.

Ephoton = hf = Eupper − Elower Bohr's quantum condition · h = Planck's constant · f = photon frequency

The allowed orbits were those in which the electron's angular momentum was a whole-number multiple of h/2π — the quantum condition. This was a rule imposed on nature, not derived from anything deeper. Bohr knew it. He was not explaining why the rule held; he was showing that if it held, everything followed.

The Hydrogen Spectrum: A Perfect Match

The proof was in the spectrum. Hydrogen, when excited by an electric discharge, emits light at very specific wavelengths — a discrete series of colored lines. The Swiss mathematician Johann Balmer had found a formula describing these lines in 1885, but no one understood why the formula worked. It was empirical pattern without physical basis.

Bohr's model, when applied to hydrogen (a single electron orbiting a single proton), produced the Balmer formula from first principles. The energy levels of hydrogen were:

En = −13.6 eV / n² n = 1, 2, 3 … · Energy in electron-volts · Ground state is n=1 at −13.6 eV

When an electron dropped from n=3 to n=2, it emitted a photon of 656 nanometers — the red Hα line of the Balmer series. When it dropped from n=4 to n=2, the 486nm blue Hβ line. The match was exact, to the limits of experimental measurement. Physicists who had spent years unable to explain these lines saw Bohr's paper and felt something close to vertigo. The atom had suddenly become legible.

The Bohr model was not complete — it worked perfectly for hydrogen but failed for multi-electron atoms, and it mixed classical and quantum ideas in ways that were logically uncomfortable. Bohr himself acknowledged this openly. He was not building a final theory; he was finding the seam in nature where old physics ended and new physics had to begin.

II — Complementarity (1927)

Wave and Particle: Both True, Never Both at Once

By 1925–1926 two complete mathematical frameworks for quantum mechanics had emerged: Heisenberg's matrix mechanics and Schrödinger's wave mechanics. They gave the same predictions but looked completely different. More troublingly, neither gave a clear physical picture of what an electron actually was. Was it a particle following a definite path? Or a wave spread through space? Experiments gave contradictory answers depending on what you measured.

Bohr's response was the principle of complementarity, announced at the Como conference in September 1927. His argument: the wave description and the particle description are both correct, but they apply to mutually exclusive experimental situations. You cannot design an experiment that reveals both simultaneously — not because of technological limitation, but because of the structure of reality itself.

The experimental setup that reveals wave behavior (the double-slit experiment) necessarily destroys the information that would reveal particle behavior. If you try to determine which slit the electron went through, the interference pattern disappears. Nature enforces a boundary between descriptions: each is complete and correct in its domain, and the domains do not overlap.

"There is no quantum world. There is only an abstract quantum physical description. It is wrong to think that the task of physics is to find out how nature is. Physics concerns what we can say about nature."
— Niels Bohr, recorded by Aage Petersen, 1963

This was more than a technical claim. It was a philosophical revolution. The notion that a physical object has simultaneously real properties — that an electron has both a definite position and a definite momentum simultaneously whether or not we observe them — was, Bohr argued, simply wrong. The classical concept of trajectory does not apply. Nature at the quantum level is irreducibly contextual: what a system "is" depends on what you measure.

III — The Copenhagen Interpretation (1927–1930)

The Standard Model of Quantum Reality

Working with Heisenberg through the autumn of 1926 and into 1927, Bohr refined the framework that became known — initially by its critics, then by everyone — as the Copenhagen interpretation. Its core claims:

Claim 1

The Wave Function Is Complete

The quantum state vector (wave function, ψ) gives the most complete possible description of a system. There are no hidden variables that, if known, would allow prediction of individual measurement outcomes.

Claim 2

Measurement Collapses the State

Before measurement, a system exists in a superposition of possible states. Measurement collapses the wave function to a definite outcome. The probability of each outcome is given by Born's rule: |ψ|².

Claim 3

No Definite Properties Between Measurements

It is meaningless to ask what value a quantum observable has between measurements. The concept of a particle having a definite position or momentum independent of observation simply does not apply.

Claim 4

Uncertainty Is Fundamental

Heisenberg's uncertainty principle (Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ/2) is not a measurement artifact — it reflects an irreducible complementarity in the concepts of position and momentum themselves. Both cannot simultaneously be precise.

Claim 5

Classical Language Is Necessary

The results of any quantum experiment must ultimately be described in classical terms — because the measuring apparatus is classical. This is the "cut" between quantum and classical that Bohr insisted was unavoidable.

Claim 6

Complementarity Is Universal

The complementarity of wave and particle descriptions is not limited to physics. Bohr later extended the concept to biology (vitalism vs. mechanism), psychology (free will vs. determinism), and ethics.

The Copenhagen interpretation remains the standard interpretation taught in physics curricula worldwide, nearly a century after its formulation. It has never been experimentally refuted. Competing interpretations — Many Worlds, Pilot Wave, QBism — exist but none has achieved Copenhagen's empirical and pedagogical dominance.

IV — The Bohr–Einstein Debates (1927–1935)

The Greatest Scientific Argument of the Twentieth Century

Einstein was not convinced. He accepted the mathematical formalism of quantum mechanics — he had himself contributed to it — but rejected the Copenhagen interpretation's insistence that quantum mechanics was complete and that physical quantities have no definite values before measurement. His objections were not those of a man who failed to understand quantum mechanics. They were those of a man who understood it and found it philosophically untenable.

Year Einstein's Challenge Bohr's Response Verdict
1927
Solvay 5th		 Proposes thought experiments showing an electron passing through a double slit could be tracked without disturbing the interference pattern — undermining complementarity. Shows in each case that accounting for the recoil of the apparatus (using uncertainty principle) destroys the which-way information before it can be recorded. Bohr wins the battle. Einstein accepts the defeat — and returns with heavier weapons.
1930
Solvay 6th		 The photon-box thought experiment: a box containing photons is weighed before and after releasing one; both the energy and the time of release seem precisely determinable, violating the energy-time uncertainty relation. Works through the night. In the morning shows that Einstein had forgotten to apply his own general relativity: the clock's position in gravity affects its rate, and accounting for this precisely restores the uncertainty relation. Bohr wins, using Einstein's own theory against him. Einstein reportedly shaken. A diagram of the photon box was on Bohr's blackboard when he died in 1962.
1935
EPR Paper		 With Podolsky and Rosen: if two entangled particles are separated and one is measured, the state of the other is immediately determined — either nature is nonlocal (spooky action at a distance) or quantum mechanics is incomplete (there must be hidden variables). Within six weeks, publishes a rebuttal: the EPR argument assumes that physical quantities have definite values prior to and independent of measurement — exactly the assumption quantum mechanics denies. The apparent incompleteness is an artifact of a classical assumption. Bohr: the description is complete. A draw on arguments. The empirical question required new physics to resolve.
1964
Bell's Theorem		 John Bell shows that any local hidden variable theory makes experimentally distinguishable predictions from quantum mechanics. The debate can be decided by experiment. Bell's theorem transforms a philosophical debate into a physics problem.
1982
Aspect Experiment		 Alain Aspect and colleagues in Paris measure the correlations between entangled photons. The results violate Bell inequalities — ruling out local hidden variables — and confirm quantum mechanical predictions exactly. Bohr vindicated. Quantum mechanics is complete and nonlocal correlations are real. Einstein's intuition about a deeper deterministic reality was wrong.

V — Nuclear Fission (1939)

The Theory Behind the Bomb

On January 16, 1939, Bohr arrived in New York aboard the SS Drottningholm, carrying news from Europe: Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann had split the uranium nucleus — nuclear fission had been achieved. Lise Meitner and her nephew Otto Frisch had supplied the theoretical interpretation: the uranium nucleus, on absorbing a neutron, becomes unstable and splits, releasing enormous energy — as Meitner calculated, about 200 million electron volts per fission.

Bohr immediately recognized the theoretical problem: why should uranium-235 fission readily on absorbing a slow neutron, while uranium-238 — the far more abundant isotope — did not? Working with John Wheeler at Princeton over the following months, Bohr developed the liquid drop model of fission: the nucleus behaves like a charged liquid drop; absorbing a neutron causes it to oscillate and, if the neutron energy is right, to elongate and split.

Bohr and Wheeler's paper, published in September 1939 — the same week Germany invaded Poland — explained fission theoretically and correctly predicted that U-235, not U-238, was the fissile isotope. This distinction was crucial: it meant that a practical weapon would require isotope separation to concentrate U-235, which drove the entire industrial program of the Manhattan Project. The Bohr-Wheeler paper was the theoretical foundation on which the bomb was built.

²³⁵U + n → fission fragments + 2–3 neutrons + ~200 MeV Nuclear fission · Bohr & Wheeler 1939 · E=mc² · chain reaction

Bohr knew what he had helped to explain. His response was not to suppress the knowledge — it was too late for that — but to argue for the rest of his life that the weapon's existence demanded international institutions capable of controlling it. Science had created the problem; only politics, elevated to science's level of seriousness, could solve it.
