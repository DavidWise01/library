Charles Darwin

Born 12 February 1809 in Shrewsbury, England — the same morning Abraham Lincoln drew his first breath in a Kentucky log cabin. Died 19 April 1882, buried beside Newton in Westminster Abbey. In between: the most consequential idea in the history of biology.

Family & Origins

The Darwin Line

Charles Robert Darwin was born on 12 February 1809 in The Mount, Shrewsbury — a red-brick Georgian house on a hill above the River Severn. His father, Robert Waring Darwin, was a prominent physician and financier, tall, physically imposing (reportedly over 300 pounds), and by all accounts an affectionate if domineering parent. Robert's own father — Charles's paternal grandfather — was Erasmus Darwin, poet, physician, inventor, and early speculative evolutionist whose 1794 work Zoonomia had already floated the idea that all warm-blooded life might share a common filament of descent.

Erasmus Darwin was famous enough in his day that people joked: would his grandson Charles merely verify what grandfather Erasmus had theorized? The answer, of course, is that Charles did far more — he found the mechanism.

The Wedgwood Line

Charles's mother, Susannah Wedgwood, was the daughter of Josiah Wedgwood I, the famous potter and industrialist who had built the Wedgwood ceramics empire. The Darwin and Wedgwood families were deeply intertwined — both descended from Dissenting (non-Anglican) Protestant stock, both intellectually progressive, both wealthy. Susannah died when Charles was only eight years old; he recalled almost nothing of her.

This Wedgwood connection would resurface with transformative personal consequences: in January 1839, Darwin married his first cousin, Emma Wedgwood, Josiah Wedgwood II's daughter. The marriage was tender, intellectually honest, and enduring, though strained at times by their differing views on religion and immortality.

Education & Misdirections
01

Shrewsbury School

From age nine, Charles attended Shrewsbury School under Headmaster Dr. Samuel Butler (grandfather of the novelist). He was an indifferent student by classical measures — rote Greek and Latin bored him. He spent his time collecting beetles, minerals, and bird eggs, burning chemicals in a garden shed with his brother Erasmus.

Years: 1818–1825  ·  Verdict: Collector, not scholar
02

Edinburgh Medical School

His father Robert, having watched Charles idle at Shrewsbury, enrolled him at Edinburgh to study medicine. Darwin found anatomy lectures dull, and — crucially — witnessed two surgical operations without anesthetic (the norm pre-1847). The sight of blood and patient screaming sickened him. He abandoned medicine after two years, never completing the degree.

Years: 1825–1827  ·  Verdict: Decisive failure
03

Christ's College, Cambridge

Redirected by his father toward the Anglican ministry — a common respectable option for a gentleman's son — Darwin enrolled at Cambridge for a theology degree. He hunted beetles obsessively (once holding two rare specimens in his hands and popping a third in his mouth to free a hand). He rode, he shot, he socialized. He also took mathematics and read John Herschel's Preliminary Discourse and Alexander von Humboldt's Personal Narrative — books that ignited a longing for grand scientific adventure.

Years: 1828–1831  ·  Verdict: Beetle-maniac, future parson
04

John Stevens Henslow

The Cambridge connection that changed everything. Henslow was a professor of botany who ran weekly open evenings where he invited promising students regardless of discipline. Darwin became so constant an attendee that locals called him "the man who walks with Henslow." When a spot opened on HMS Beagle for a naturalist-companion, Henslow recommended Darwin above all others.

Role: Mentor & recommender  ·  Impact: Everything
HMS Beagle, 1831–1836

Darwin was twenty-two years old when HMS Beagle left Plymouth Sound on 27 December 1831. He had nearly not gone: his father initially refused permission (the voyage seemed an idle extravagance), relenting only after Darwin's uncle Josiah Wedgwood II wrote Robert a persuasive letter in Charles's favor. Captain Robert FitzRoy — twenty-six, brilliant, devout, and prone to severe depression — wanted a gentleman companion on the long voyage to prevent the isolation that had driven his predecessor to suicide.

"The voyage of the Beagle has been by far the most important event in my life, and has determined my whole career." — Darwin, Autobiography, c. 1876
1831

Cape Verde Islands

First landfall. Darwin observed volcanic geology, coral, and tidal life. He began keeping meticulous notebooks — geology, zoology, observations — that would eventually fill fourteen volumes.

1832

South America

Brazil, Argentina, Patagonia. Darwin observed tropical forests, fossils of giant extinct mammals (Megatherium) in Patagonian cliffs — closely resembling living armadillos. He met the Fuegians (Jemmy Button, York Minster, Fuegia Basket) whom FitzRoy was returning to Tierra del Fuego.

1835

Galápagos Islands

Five weeks in September–October. Darwin collected birds, tortoises, lizards, plants. The Vice-Governor of the islands mentioned that tortoise shells varied recognizably by island. Darwin did not yet grasp the full implication — that insight would come later, back in London, when ornithologist John Gould told him his "wrens" and "blackbirds" were all finches.

1835–36

Pacific & Indian Ocean

Tahiti, New Zealand, Australia, the Cocos Islands, Mauritius, South Africa. Darwin worked out his coral reef theory (subsidence, not uplift) at the Cocos Islands. The Beagle arrived back in Falmouth, Cornwall, on 2 October 1836.

Return: London & the Secret Notebooks

Darwin returned to England a minor celebrity of the natural history world — Henslow had circulated his geological letters from the voyage. He moved to London, was elected to the prestigious Geological Society, and began working through the Beagle specimens with London's leading experts. John Gould identified his Galápagos birds as thirteen distinct species of finch. Richard Owen catalogued the fossil mammals. Everything pointed toward a pattern Darwin was only beginning to articulate.

In July 1837 — less than a year after returning — Darwin opened what he called his "Transmutation Notebook" (Notebook B). On page 36 he sketched a branching diagram and wrote above it, in his cramped hand: "I think." It was the first tree of life.

For the next two years Darwin read voraciously, corresponded widely, and kept his evolving theory almost entirely secret. In October 1838 he read Thomas Malthus's Essay on the Principle of Population — and the mechanism clicked into place. Malthus had described how human populations outrun food supply, producing a struggle for existence. Darwin saw immediately that this applied to all nature: variation plus struggle equals selection.

Emma, Marriage & Down House

The Marriage Question

Before proposing, Darwin made a characteristically systematic list of the pros and cons of marriage. On the pro side: "Children (if it please God), constant companion (and a friend in old age) who will feel interested in one, object to be beloved and played with, better than a dog anyhow, home, and someone to take care of the house." He married Emma Wedgwood on 29 January 1839. She was thirty, he thirty. They had ten children, seven of whom survived to adulthood.

Emma was devoutly religious; Darwin was moving toward agnosticism. Their correspondence on the subject — tender, honest, unresolved — is one of the great documents of Victorian intellectual life. Emma worried for his soul; Darwin worried about causing her pain. They remained devoted until his death.

Down House

In September 1842 the family moved to Down House in the village of Downe, Kent — sixteen miles from London but sufficiently rural to keep Darwin from the city's social exhaustions. He would spend the remaining forty years of his life there. He designed a "thinking path" — a gravel walk he called his "Sandwalk" — where he would pace in loops, counting circuits with flint stones, thinking through his ideas.

Down House became a working laboratory: greenhouses for plant experiments, worm beds in the study, barnacle specimens in the drawing room. Darwin ran experiments on seedling dispersal, insectivorous plants, and orchid pollination from the comfort of his garden. The great naturalist scarcely needed to travel at all — the whole of nature, it turned out, was available in one English garden.

Chronic Illness

From roughly 1837 onward, Darwin suffered from a debilitating and never-fully-explained chronic illness. Symptoms included persistent vomiting and nausea (especially after meals), palpitations, trembling, extreme fatigue, skin conditions (eczema), and episodes of near-collapse. He could typically work only two to four hours a day; any social engagement left him bedridden for days afterward.

The cause was disputed in his lifetime and remains debated. Leading hypotheses include: Chagas disease (caused by Trypanosoma cruzi, contracted from the bite of a benchuca bug in Argentina — he describes the bug feeding on him in 1835); anxiety disorder (possibly linked to the psychological weight of his secret theory and its anticipated reception); or a combination of both. The illness was, paradoxically, one reason he remained so productive: forced to rest, he wrote.

Death & Westminster Abbey

Darwin died of heart failure on the morning of 19 April 1882. His final reported words were addressed to Emma and his children: "I am not the least afraid of death. Remember what a good wife you have been to me."

His family had intended a quiet country burial in the churchyard at Downe. Within days, however, a campaign led by T. H. Huxley, the MP John Lubbock, and twenty members of Parliament convinced the family and the Abbey authorities that Darwin belonged in Westminster. He was buried on 26 April 1882, in the nave of Westminster Abbey, a few feet from John Herschel, near the monument to Isaac Newton. The man who had dethroned humanity from special creation was given the most reverential burial Victorian England could confer.

The Man Himself
★

Methodical

Darwin worked slowly, systematically, and exhaustively. He spent eight years on barnacles before returning to natural selection. He filled 14 fieldwork notebooks on the Beagle voyage and maintained meticulous correspondence — over 15,000 surviving letters.

Habit: Sandwalk pacing, flint-stone counting
★

Self-Doubting

Darwin was perpetually anxious that his theory was wrong or incomplete. He repeatedly called Origin "one long argument" and worried at every inconsistency. He spent much of his career writing detailed responses to critics he thought might have valid objections.

Letters: Often: "I fear I am wrong about..."
★

Gentle

Colleagues and children alike described Darwin as extraordinarily kind, patient, and warm. He treated servants and gardeners with unusual consideration for a Victorian gentleman. He played with his children on the Sandwalk; he remembered the names of every correspondent's family members.

Contemporary verdict: "The most lovable of men"
★

Anti-Slavery

Darwin witnessed slavery in Brazil in 1832 and was morally appalled. His notebooks record scenes of brutality that sickened him. He clashed with FitzRoy over the subject. His conviction that all human races shared a common ancestor was directly connected to his opposition to slavery — unity of descent meant unity of humanity.
