Dennis Ritchie

He invented the C programming language and co-created Unix. He spent his whole career at one address — Bell Labs, Murray Hill — and changed the world so thoroughly that the world stopped noticing. He won the Turing Award in 1983. He died in 2011, the week after Steve Jobs, and almost no one noticed.

Who he was
01

Bronxville boy, Harvard student

Dennis MacAlistair Ritchie was born September 9, 1941, in Bronxville, New York. His father Alistair was a switching circuit engineer at Bell Labs — a lineage that reads like fate.

study Harvard, physics undergrad

then Harvard applied mathematics PhD — dropped out

+1 the PhD abandonment is a footnote. What he did next rendered it irrelevant to everyone except the degree committee.

02

Bell Labs, 1967 — forever

He joined Bell Labs' Computing Science Research Center in 1967. He stayed until 2007. Forty years. One address. The entire history of modern software was written in that time and that building.

where Murray Hill, New Jersey

culture researchers given enormous freedom, no quarterly targets

+1 his father worked there too. Bell Labs was, in a sense, his inheritance and his life's work simultaneously.

03

A man of notable quiet

Those who knew him describe a man who was gentle, precise, and almost constitutionally humble. He gave credit easily. He deflected acclaim. He was happier writing code than giving speeches.

style precise, self-effacing, quietly funny

said "Unix is very simple, it just needs a genius to understand its simplicity"

+1 the joke is also a philosophy: he believed complexity was a failure, not a feature.

04

Turing Award, 1983

He and Ken Thompson shared the ACM Turing Award — the Nobel Prize of computing — for their work on C and Unix. The committee called it "one of the most far-reaching technical achievements in the history of computing."

honor ACM Turing Award, shared with Thompson

also National Medal of Technology and Innovation, 1999 (with Thompson)

+1 President Clinton awarded the Medal. By then C and Unix had been running the world for twenty-five years.

What made him unusual
05

The most influential unknown

By any measurable standard, Ritchie's work underlies more software than any other person's. And yet almost no one outside of computing knows his name. He is the invisible foundation.

scope C → every OS kernel, every systems language, CPython, JVM

unix → Linux, macOS, iOS, Android, every server

+1 the phrase "standing on the shoulders of giants" was coined for Newton. It applies more literally to Ritchie: the entire industry stands on his two inventions.

06

Physics to computer science

He studied physics — not programming — at Harvard. He arrived at computing sideways, through applied mathematics. This outside path may have given him the outsider's view that produced such clean abstractions.

path physics → applied math → computing

note there was no CS degree to get in 1963

+1 he belonged to a generation that invented the discipline by doing the discipline. There was no textbook because he wrote the textbook.

07

The long collaboration

His partnership with Ken Thompson was one of the great collaborations in the history of technology. Thompson provided creative fire; Ritchie provided theoretical clarity. Together they were more than the sum of their parts.

thompson wrote the first Unix kernel; wrote B (predecessor to C)

ritchie extended B into C; gave Unix its portable foundation

+1 they shared every major award. They appear to have been genuinely fond of each other across a forty-year working friendship.

08

The overlooked death

He died October 12, 2011, at home in Berkeley Heights, New Jersey — alone, after illness. Steve Jobs had died six days earlier. The global outpouring for Jobs lasted weeks. Ritchie's death was a footnote.

irony every Apple device Jobs was mourned on ran Ritchie's code

said Rob Pike: "Ritchie's influence dwarfs [Jobs's], and almost nobody knows it"

+1 the invisibility is structural: Jobs built products you could hold. Ritchie built the substrate everything ran on. Substrate is invisible by nature.

The shape of his career
  • Dennis MacAlistair Ritchie (1941–2011) — American computer scientist, Bell Labs Computing Science Research Center, Murray Hill, NJ.
  • Harvard undergraduate (physics), Harvard applied mathematics graduate student — left without completing the PhD to join Bell Labs in 1967.
  • With Ken Thompson: co-created Unix (1969–1973) and designed the C programming language (1972). Wrote the canonical book with Brian Kernighan: The C Programming Language (1978).
  • ACM Turing Award (1983, shared with Thompson); National Medal of Technology (1999, shared with Thompson); IEEE Richard W. Hamming Medal (1990); Japan Prize (2011).
  • Died quietly at home, October 12, 2011. His death was barely covered. The irony was total: every device used to mourn Steve Jobs ran on Ritchie's Unix and C.
His awards and honors
09

ACM Turing Award — 1983

The Turing Award — computing's Nobel Prize — was shared by Ritchie and Thompson. The citation: "the development of generic operating systems theory and specifically for the implementation of the UNIX operating system."

shared with Ken Thompson

significance highest award in computer science

+1 by 1983 Unix had already been running for fourteen years and C had been the dominant systems language for a decade. The award recognized a decade-and-a-half of demonstrated impact.

10

National Medal of Technology — 1999

President Clinton awarded the National Medal of Technology to Ritchie and Thompson jointly in 1999. By then the internet was booming — all of it running on Unix and C — and the award recognized the scope of what they had built.

shared with Ken Thompson

occasion White House ceremony, 1999

+1 other awards include the IEEE Richard W. Hamming Medal (1990) and the Japan Prize (2011) — also shared with Thompson, awarded the year both men died.

11

A career of papers

Beyond C and Unix, Ritchie wrote careful, precise papers throughout his career documenting the evolution of the languages and systems he built. "The Development of the C Language" (1993) and "The Evolution of the Unix Time-sharing System" (1984) are the definitive primary sources.

paper "The Development of the C Language" (HOPL-II, 1993)

paper "The Evolution of the Unix Time-sharing System" (1979)

+1 his writing style — spare, precise, slightly self-deprecating — is unmistakable. He was as good at prose as at code.

12

The Japan Prize — 2011

The Japan Prize in Information and Communications was awarded to Ritchie and Thompson in 2011 — the year Ritchie died. It was one of the last honors he received and he was reportedly too ill to attend the ceremony.

shared with Ken Thompson

year 2011 — the year Ritchie died

+1 the Japan Prize is given for contributions to science and technology that benefit mankind. The committee's reasoning was simple: C and Unix underlie all modern information technology.

Contemporaries and context

Ken Thompson

His closest collaborator. Co-inventor of Unix. Author of B. Later co-designed the Go language at Google.

partner

Brian Kernighan

Bell Labs colleague. Co-authored K&R. Wrote "Hello, World." Documented the Unix philosophy.

co-author

Claude Shannon

Bell Labs predecessor. Founded information theory (1948). Ritchie's C ran on Shannon's mathematical model.

precursor

Rob Pike

Bell Labs colleague. Co-designed Plan 9 (Unix successor) and Go. Wrote the most-cited tribute to Ritchie in 2011.

colleague
What we know — and how
  • Ritchie's career is thoroughly documented through his own published papers, Bell Labs technical reports, and the oral histories preserved by colleagues including Brian Kernighan and Rob Pike.
  • The biographical details here — Bronxville, Harvard, the PhD dropout, Bell Labs — are unambiguous facts from his own accounts and standard references.
  • The circumstances of his death are confirmed but sparse: he was found alone at home; he had been ill. He was intensely private in life and remained so in death.
  • The Jobs/Ritchie contrast is real and well-noted by the computing community — not an exaggeration. Rob Pike's widely-read post from 2011 captures the consensus view.
  • Books 1–3 cover the work, the world, and the legacy in turn.
