Who He Was

The Greatest Scientist of Antiquity

He proved the mathematics of the lever. He computed pi more accurately than anyone before him. He derived calculus eighteen centuries before Newton — and the evidence was hidden inside a medieval prayer book until 1906. He died defending his city with war machines he had built himself, drawing circles in the sand.

The Historical Person

A Genius With a Biography

Unlike his predecessor Euclid, Archimedes is not a ghost. We know when and where he was born, roughly what he looked like in legend, who his father was, which king he served, how he died, and what he asked to have carved on his tombstone. He is one of the most vivid personalities of the ancient world — not despite the sparse record, but because the record that survives is so specific and so strange.

He was born around 287 BCE in Syracuse, the great Greek city-state on the eastern coast of Sicily. His father was Phidias, an astronomer. He studied in Alexandria — then the intellectual capital of the world — under the successors of Euclid, probably at the Museum. He returned to Syracuse and spent the rest of his life there, serving the court of King Hiero II as a mathematician, engineer, and inventor. He died in 212 BCE when a Roman soldier killed him during the sack of Syracuse — against the direct orders of the Roman general Marcellus, who admired Archimedes and wanted him taken alive.

What makes Archimedes distinctive is not just the scale of his achievement but his self-awareness about it. He was proud. He published work he considered preliminary. He wrote letters to colleagues presenting results as puzzles before revealing proofs. He had a favorite theorem — the relationship between a sphere and its circumscribed cylinder — and he asked for a diagram of it to be carved on his tomb. We know this because Cicero, visiting Syracuse 137 years after Archimedes's death, found the tomb neglected and overgrown but still bearing the image: a sphere inside a cylinder.

"Do not disturb my circles."

— Attributed to Archimedes, to the Roman soldier who killed him, 212 BCE. Reported by Valerius Maximus. He was reportedly drawing geometric figures in the sand when interrupted.
Life in Time

The Arc of a Life

c. 287 BCE

Born in Syracuse, Sicily

Syracuse was the largest and most powerful Greek city-state in the western Mediterranean. Founded by Corinthians in 734 BCE, it had repelled an Athenian invasion in 415–413 BCE — one of the great military disasters of the ancient world — and now thrived under the rule of Hiero II, a pragmatic and culturally ambitious king. Archimedes's father Phidias was an astronomer, suggesting an educated household. The date 287 BCE derives from the historian John Tzetzes, writing in the 12th century CE, who gives his age at death as 75.

c. 270–260 BCE

Study in Alexandria

As a young man, Archimedes traveled to Alexandria — the only place in the ancient world where you could encounter all prior mathematical knowledge in one place. He studied under the successors of Euclid, particularly Conon of Samos, who became a close friend. The relationship mattered: Archimedes's surviving letters mention Conon repeatedly and with evident affection. He also corresponded with Eratosthenes, the librarian who measured the circumference of the Earth. Alexandria gave Archimedes access to every mathematical idea that had come before him.

c. 260 BCE

Return to Syracuse — Court Mathematician

Archimedes returned to Syracuse and remained there for the rest of his life — never, so far as we know, leaving again. He served King Hiero II in a relationship that was more partnership than servitude. Hiero was genuinely interested in practical ingenuity: he commissioned the largest ship ever built in antiquity (the Syracusia), and he asked Archimedes to verify whether his crown was pure gold without melting it down. That last commission led to the Eureka moment. Archimedes also designed war machines, irrigation systems, and astronomical instruments.

c. 250–215 BCE

The Mathematical Works

During this period Archimedes wrote the surviving treatises — On the Sphere and Cylinder, Measurement of a Circle, On Conoids and Spheroids, On Spirals, On the Equilibrium of Planes, The Sand Reckoner, The Method, On Floating Bodies, and others. He was in correspondence with mathematicians in Alexandria throughout, sending problems and results across the Mediterranean. Several texts survive only because he sent them as letters: The Method is addressed directly to Eratosthenes. He was not a reclusive genius; he was an active participant in the international scholarly community of his era.

214 BCE

Rome Besieges Syracuse

The Second Punic War had drawn Syracuse into conflict with Rome. After the death of King Hiero II, Syracuse allied with Carthage. Rome responded by sending a fleet under Marcus Claudius Marcellus to take the city. Archimedes — by now in his seventies — designed the defensive weapons that held the Roman forces off for two full years. The Roman soldiers, according to Plutarch, became so terrified of Archimedes's machines that they would flee if they saw a piece of rope or timber appearing over the city walls, fearing some new device.

212 BCE

Death During the Sack of Syracuse

Syracuse fell in 212 BCE. Marcellus had given explicit orders that Archimedes was not to be harmed. A Roman soldier — accounts differ on the circumstances — killed him anyway. The most famous account, reported by Valerius Maximus, says Archimedes was working on a geometric problem, drawing figures in the sand, and when the soldier interrupted him he said: "Do not disturb my circles." He was approximately 75 years old. Marcellus, reportedly, wept.

The Man Behind the Mathematics

Character and Legend

Archimedes is unusual among ancient scientists in that we have a clear sense of his personality — partly from his own writings, partly from accounts of people who knew or knew of him. He was, by all evidence, both a genuine theorist of the first rank and a consummate engineer. He appears to have delighted in both modes. He was also, by his own admission, proud.

His surviving prefaces reveal a mathematician who understood his own significance. He sends problems to colleagues before revealing the solutions — partly to give them the pleasure of discovery, but partly, he admits, to catch those who might claim credit for results they did not find. He describes his method as something entirely new and describes specific results as the most beautiful he has found. He is not falsely modest.

CHARACTER

The Proud Geometer

Archimedes identified most with his pure mathematics — the work on spheres and cylinders, spirals, parabolas. He considered his mechanical inventions beneath his dignity, remarkable as they were. Plutarch reports that he "looked upon the work of an engineer and every art that ministers to the needs of life as ignoble and vulgar." Whether this is accurate or an Athenian prejudice Plutarch projected onto him, we cannot say. But his request for the geometric tomb marker speaks for itself.

LEGEND

Eureka — The Bath and the Crown

King Hiero II suspected his crown was not pure gold. He asked Archimedes to determine the truth without melting it. The story — recorded by Vitruvius two centuries later — says Archimedes solved it in his bath: noticing that water displaced by his body was proportional to his volume, he leapt out and ran naked through the streets crying "Eureka!" (Greek: I have found it). The word entered every European language.

LEGEND

Give Me a Place to Stand

"Give me a place to stand and I will move the Earth." Archimedes is said to have made this boast about the lever, and proved it — at least partially — by using a compound pulley system to haul the fully loaded Syracusia (the largest ship of the ancient world) across the harbor single-handed, reportedly while seated, pulling a rope. The story is reported by Plutarch. The physics is correct; whether the demonstration happened exactly this way is uncertain.

DEATH

Do Not Disturb My Circles

Three accounts survive of Archimedes's death; all agree a Roman soldier killed him against orders. The version with the circles and the last words has become the defining image of the mathematical temperament: a man so absorbed in a problem that he cannot be bothered by conquest. Whether it is literally true, it is spiritually accurate. His work shows a mind capable of exactly that absorption.

TOMB

The Sphere and Cylinder

Of all his discoveries, Archimedes was proudest of the proof that a sphere inscribed in a cylinder has a volume equal to 2/3 the volume of the cylinder, and a surface area equal to 2/3 the total surface area of the cylinder. He asked for this diagram to be carved on his tomb. Cicero found it, overgrown but legible, when he served as quaestor in Sicily in 75 BCE — 137 years after Archimedes's death.

CORRESPONDENCE

Letters Across the Mediterranean

Archimedes maintained active correspondence with mathematicians in Alexandria — Conon of Samos (his closest intellectual friend), Dositheus (who received several treatises after Conon's death), and Eratosthenes (to whom The Method is addressed). This network of correspondence preserved his work and situated it within the broader mathematical culture of the Hellenistic world.

The Corpus

Nine Works, No Originals

We have nine surviving works by Archimedes — or rather, nine surviving manuscript traditions. No original manuscript by Archimedes exists. Everything we have is copies of copies, the oldest surviving ones dating from the Byzantine era (roughly 9th–13th centuries CE). Most of what we know survives through a single manuscript tradition from Constantinople, and much of the corpus was nearly lost.

The most dramatic case is The Method. This text, in which Archimedes explains the private heuristic reasoning that let him discover results before he could prove them formally — a method that amounts to a form of integral calculus — was completely unknown until 1906. That year, the Danish philologist Johan Ludvig Heiberg examined a 10th-century Byzantine manuscript in Constantinople and found, beneath a 13th-century Christian prayer book, traces of erased mathematical text. The prayer book was a palimpsest: monks had scraped off the parchment and written over it. The text beneath was Archimedes.

"Since I see, however, as I said, that you are a capable scholar and a prominent teacher of philosophy, and also that you understand how to value a mathematical method of investigation when the opportunity is offered…"

— Archimedes, opening of The Method, addressed to Eratosthenes, Head of the Library of Alexandria. c. 250 BCE.

The treatises range from pure mathematics (areas and volumes of curved figures) to applied physics (floating bodies, lever equilibrium) to a kind of cosmic arithmetic (the Sand Reckoner). They are unified by an approach: mathematical proof from first principles, often via the "method of exhaustion" — enclosing a curved figure between polygons whose areas are known and compressing them together. It is the direct ancestor of the limit concept in calculus.
