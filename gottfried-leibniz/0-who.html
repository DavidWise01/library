Origins

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz was born in Leipzig on 1 July 1646, one year after the Peace of Westphalia ended the Thirty Years' War — a war that had killed perhaps a third of the German-speaking population. He entered a world still smoldering, still counting its dead, still trying to remember what it meant to reason carefully about anything.

His father, Friedrich Leibniz, was a professor of moral philosophy at the University of Leipzig. Friedrich died in 1652, when Gottfried was six years old. The boy was left to his father's library — and he read all of it. By eight he was reading Latin without instruction. By twelve he had worked through the Greek historians. By fourteen he had exhausted the holdings of the library he'd been born beside.

There is a particular kind of mind that forms when an isolated, brilliant child is left alone with a large library and the tacit permission of a dead father to go wherever the books lead. Leibniz's mind was that kind: restlessly horizontal, unable to specialize, constitutionally unable to find any question genuinely separate from any other question.

"It is unworthy of excellent men to lose hours like slaves in the labor of calculation."

— Leibniz, on inventing the mechanical calculator
Life in Sequence
  • 1646
    Born Leipzig. Father Friedrich Leibniz teaches moral philosophy at the University of Leipzig. The Thirty Years' War ended the previous year. Saxony is exhausted.
  • 1652
    Father dies. Gottfried is six. He inherits access to the library — thousands of volumes in Latin, Greek, German. He will teach himself from these books for the next decade.
  • 1661
    Enters the University of Leipzig at age fifteen. Studies philosophy, mathematics, and law. Already encountering Kepler, Galileo, Descartes, and Francis Bacon — the scaffolding of modern thought.
  • 1666
    Dissertatio de Arte Combinatoria. Age twenty. His first major work proposes a combinatorial calculus of concepts — an early sketch of the universal symbolic language he will pursue his whole life. The University of Leipzig refuses him a doctorate (possibly because of his age). Nuremberg's University of Altdorf awards it instead, and offers him a professorship. He declines.
  • 1667
    Enters the service of the Elector of Mainz. Political work: legal reform, diplomacy, statecraft. He is useful to powerful men, which will both fund and constrain his genius for the rest of his life.
  • 1672
    Arrives in Paris. The intellectual capital of Europe. He meets Christiaan Huygens, the greatest physicist then living. Huygens gives him mathematics problems. Leibniz is embarrassed to discover how much he does not know. He learns, frantically, for the next four years.
  • 1673
    Visits London. Demonstrates his mechanical calculating machine — the Stepped Reckoner — to the Royal Society. Elected a Fellow. He sees Oldenburg, Collins, perhaps others with access to Newton's unpublished manuscripts. This visit becomes relevant, and disputed, later.
  • 1675–76
    Invents calculus. In Paris, working through the foundational problems of tangents, areas, and rates of change, he develops his own complete notation: the ∫ symbol (November 1675), the d notation for differentials. He is unaware that Newton completed a similar invention in Cambridge a decade earlier — but never published it.
  • 1676
    Returns to Germany via London and Amsterdam. In Amsterdam he spends several hours with Baruch Spinoza, the only known meeting between the two greatest philosophers of the age. What they said to each other is not fully recorded. He takes the post of court counselor and librarian to the House of Hanover.
  • 1684
    Publishes calculus. "Nova Methodus pro Maximis et Minimis" in the Acta Eruditorum — the first published account of differential calculus in history. Newton had priority of invention; Leibniz had priority of publication. This asymmetry will poison the rest of his life.
  • 1686
    Discours de Métaphysique. The seeds of the Monadologie. He proposes that the universe consists of simple, indivisible substances — monads — each one a self-contained mirror of the whole universe, pre-harmonized by God.
  • 1694
    Demonstrates the Stepped Reckoner. His mechanical calculator — capable of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division — is demonstrated at the Berlin Academy. The first machine that could perform all four arithmetic operations.
  • 1703
    "Explication de l'Arithmétique Binaire." Published in the Mémoires de l'Académie Royale des Sciences. Binary arithmetic, developed from an idea he'd had since 1679. He sees in it a connection to the I Ching — the ancient Chinese system of 64 hexagrams, which he takes as confirmation that all things resolve to 0 and 1, Being and Nothing.
  • 1710
    Théodicée. His only book-length philosophical work published in his lifetime. Argues that God, being perfect, necessarily created the best of all possible worlds. Voltaire will spend a career mocking this conclusion.
  • 1712
    The Newton controversy reaches its climax. The Royal Society publishes its Commercium Epistolicum — a report on the calculus priority dispute, almost certainly written by Newton himself. It finds in Newton's favor. Leibniz is implicitly accused of plagiarism. He will not recover his reputation in England before his death.
  • 1714
    Monadologie. Written in Vienna, published posthumously. The distilled statement of his philosophy: the universe is composed of monads, each a windowless microcosm, pre-established by God to harmonize with all others. It is strange, original, and not easily dismissed.
  • 1716
    Dies 14 November, Hanover. His employer, the Elector Georg Ludwig (now King George I of Great Britain), does not attend the funeral. His secretary is the only mourner. He is buried in an unmarked grave at the Neustädter Hof- und Stadtkirche. It stays unmarked for fifty years.
The Man in the Room
01

The Self-Teacher

He had no single teacher who shaped him. At eight he taught himself Latin. At twelve, Greek. He moved through the library of a dead father and came out the other side conversant with every tradition Western thought had produced. This gave him a particular freedom — and a particular isolation.

Formation
02

The Courtier

For forty years he served the House of Hanover as librarian, historian, and court counselor. He was paid to write the genealogy of the House of Brunswick — a project he never finished. The work he wanted to do was mathematics and philosophy. The work they paid him for was pedigree research.

Circumstance
03

The Correspondent

He wrote 15,000 letters to approximately 1,000 correspondents across Europe. Philosophers, theologians, mathematicians, princes, scientists, diplomats. He was the switchboard of the Republic of Letters — the informal network of European intellectuals who worked in Latin and wrote endlessly.

Network
04

The Universalist

He did serious work in mathematics, logic, philosophy, physics, law, history, linguistics, engineering, and theology. He founded the Berlin Academy of Sciences. He drafted plans for a Latin dictionary, a universal legal code, and a mechanical calculating machine. None of this was dilettantism.

Scope
05

The Diplomat

He drafted a plan to redirect Louis XIV's imperial ambitions toward Egypt (instead of Germany). He attempted, repeatedly, to reunite the Catholic and Protestant churches. He tried to prevent the War of Spanish Succession. He failed at all of these — but the attempts tell you something about the scale of his ambitions.

Politics
06

The Unmarked Grave

He died in Hanover in 1716, aged seventy. His employer had gone to London as King George I and forgotten him. His secretary attended the funeral; no one else came. The grave remained unmarked for fifty years. The man who invented calculus, binary arithmetic, and one of the first mechanical computers was buried like a clerk.

End
Paris, 1672–1676

The Paris years are the hinge of Leibniz's intellectual life. He arrived in 1672 as an able but not yet great thinker — a philosophical prodigy and promising lawyer, but someone who had not yet touched the front edge of European mathematics. Within four years he was among the best mathematicians alive.

The catalyst was Christiaan Huygens, the Dutch physicist who had invented the pendulum clock, analyzed centrifugal force, and developed the wave theory of light. Huygens gave Leibniz a problem — the sum of a particular series of fractions — and Leibniz solved it. Then Huygens gave him another. Within months, Leibniz had taught himself the mathematics of Descartes, Pascal, and Barrow; had rediscovered infinitesimal methods independently; and had begun constructing a notation for calculus that would prove, over the next three centuries, to be the most useful mathematical language ever devised.

He also visited London in 1673, demonstrating a prototype of his mechanical calculator to the Royal Society. He was elected a Fellow. He met Oldenburg and Collins, who had access to Newton's correspondence. He may have seen references to Newton's fluxional method. Whether this influenced his later independent invention of calculus is the question that consumed the last decade of his life — and the first great intellectual-priority dispute in the history of science.

Hanover: The Long Decline

30 Years in Service

From 1676 until his death in 1716, Leibniz served the House of Hanover. He was officially their librarian, historian, and court counselor. He founded the Berlin Academy of Sciences in 1700. He corresponded with every major thinker in Europe. He produced the Théodicée, the Monadologie, and thousands of unpublished manuscripts on mathematics, physics, and philosophy.

But his employers cared about their genealogy. They wanted proof that the House of Brunswick descended from a line of sufficient nobility to justify their claim to the English throne. Leibniz spent thirty years on this project. He never finished it. The dynastic ambition it served succeeded anyway — Georg Ludwig became King George I of Great Britain in 1714 — and immediately left Hanover for London, leaving Leibniz behind.

Leibniz followed, writing letters, seeking permission to come to London, to continue his work, to be remembered. The permission never came. He died two years later in the city where he had spent his life, tended only by his secretary.

One Afternoon with Spinoza

In November 1676, passing through Amsterdam on his way back from Paris to Hanover, Leibniz visited Baruch Spinoza. He spent several days at Spinoza's house, reading the manuscript of the Ethics and speaking at length. It is the only recorded meeting between the two greatest philosophers of the seventeenth century.

Spinoza would be dead within three months — tuberculosis, probably complicated by the glass dust he inhaled as a lens grinder. Leibniz knew he was meeting a dying man.

What they said to each other is partly recorded in Leibniz's notes. They disagreed about God, substance, and the nature of mind. Leibniz was a believer in a personal God who created the world with intention; Spinoza believed God and Nature were identical, a single infinite substance admitting no personality, no creation, no special providence. These were not reconcilable positions.

Leibniz later distanced himself from Spinoza — Spinoza was considered dangerous, his philosophy associated with atheism. But the notes Leibniz took suggest genuine engagement, not dismissal. He was, as always, trying to learn from anyone who knew something he did not.
