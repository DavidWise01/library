The Notation Wars: Why Leibniz Won

When Leibniz died in 1716, the calculus dispute was not yet settled. British mathematicians, loyal to Newton and to Newton's dot notation, refused to use Leibniz's dy/dx. Continental mathematicians — the Bernoullis, Euler, d'Alembert, Lagrange — used Leibniz's notation exclusively, because it worked better.

The difference was not merely aesthetic. Leibniz's notation is compositional: it carries information about what variables are involved, what is being differentiated with respect to what. The chain rule in Leibniz notation reads as a fraction cancellation: dy/du · du/dx = dy/dx. It's not rigorously a fraction, but it behaves like one, and this behavior is exactly right. British mathematicians using Newton's ẏ notation had to keep track of what each dot meant verbally, separately, outside the notation.

For about a century after Newton's and Leibniz's deaths (roughly 1720–1820), British mathematics fell behind the continent. The great advances in calculus, differential equations, and mathematical physics came from France, Switzerland, and Germany — from Euler, Lagrange, Laplace, and Cauchy — all working in Leibniz's notation.

By the early nineteenth century the situation had become embarrassing. In 1812, Cambridge undergraduate students Charles Babbage, John Herschel, and George Peacock founded the Analytical Society, explicitly dedicated to introducing Leibniz's d-ism to replace Newton's dot-age. They succeeded. By 1820 Cambridge had switched to Leibniz notation. The switch was over.

Newton had priority of invention. Leibniz won the notation. And notation, in mathematics, is everything: it shapes thought, enables discovery, determines what can be said clearly and what must be said clumsily. Leibniz understood this. He spent years on notation, revising, testing, thinking about what symbols would make mathematics most powerful.

Newton's Notation (Dots)

  • ẋ, ẍ — velocity, acceleration
  • Still used in classical mechanics and engineering
  • Opaque: doesn't show the variables explicitly
  • Cannot be extended to partial derivatives cleanly
  • Chain rule requires verbal statement
  • British mathematics stagnated for ~100 years using it

Leibniz's Notation (d, ∫)

  • dy/dx, d²y/dx² — derivative, second derivative
  • Used in every calculus textbook on earth
  • Explicit: variables and operations stated in the symbol
  • Extends to ∂y/∂x for partial derivatives naturally
  • Chain rule: dy/du · du/dx = dy/dx (visually)
  • Enabled 18th-century mathematical revolution on the continent
Binary: 1703 to Everywhere

Leibniz published his binary arithmetic in 1703. He believed it was philosophically significant — a demonstration that all things resolved to 0 and 1, Being and Nothing, creation from the void. He tried to interest the Emperor of China with it, proposing that the I Ching's sixty-four hexagrams were binary combinations and therefore that Chinese and European thought shared a common mathematical foundation.

He did not know that binary would become the operating language of all modern computation. He could not have known — the concept of an electronic computer was more than two centuries away. But he did know that binary had a particular property decimal lacked: all operations in binary reduce to combinations of the simplest possible moves. Addition is just carrying 1s. Multiplication is shifting and adding. There is no simpler arithmetic.

John von Neumann, one of the architects of the modern computer, knew Leibniz's binary work. He cited it. He understood that the binary choice — 0 or 1, off or on, false or true — was the natural basis for the digital machines he was designing at Princeton in the 1940s.

The transistor implements binary: it is either on (1) or off (0). A modern CPU contains billions of transistors, all switching between 0 and 1 billions of times per second. Every file on every computer is a sequence of 0s and 1s. Every pixel of this page is encoded in binary. Leibniz's 1703 paper runs on every device that has ever run software.

"Binary arithmetic seems useless, but it will serve in the future for machines that do calculations by running, since it is in binary that its results can be expressed most easily."

— Leibniz, letter to Duke Rudolf August, 1697
The Chain: Leibniz to the Computer
  • Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz
    1646–1716  ·  Leipzig / Paris / Hanover
    Binary arithmetic (1703). Characteristica Universalis — the dream of a universal symbolic language that could settle all arguments by calculation. "Calculemus". Also the first design for a general-purpose mechanical calculator performing all four operations.
    Foundation: 0 and 1, and the dream of mechanical reasoning
  • George Boole
    1815–1864  ·  Lincoln / Cork
    The Laws of Thought (1854). Boolean algebra: a formal system for logical reasoning using symbols 0 and 1 and operations AND, OR, NOT. The first concrete realization of Leibniz's project in a specific domain. Boole knew of Leibniz's combinatorial logic and cited him as an inspiration.
    Leibniz connection: Boole explicitly models his algebra on Leibniz's combinatorial calculus
  • Gottlob Frege
    1848–1925  ·  Jena
    Begriffsschrift — Concept Script (1879). Formal predicate logic: a complete notation for logical relationships that could express any mathematical argument. Frege dedicates his work to the project Leibniz envisioned. His preface explicitly names Leibniz's Characteristica Universalis as the motivation.
    Leibniz connection: Frege names the Characteristica Universalis as his direct inspiration
  • Bertrand Russell & Alfred North Whitehead
    1872–1970; 1861–1947  ·  Cambridge
    Principia Mathematica (1910–1913). The attempt to derive all of mathematics from logical axioms — the full implementation of Leibniz's dream for the domain of mathematics. It took three volumes and 362 pages to prove that 1 + 1 = 2. Leibniz would have approved of the ambition.
    Russell also wrote a book on Leibniz's philosophy (1900), rating him among the greatest logicians before Frege
  • Kurt Gödel
    1906–1978  ·  Vienna / Princeton
    Incompleteness Theorems (1931). Proved that any formal system powerful enough to express arithmetic contains true statements that cannot be proved within the system. The Characteristica Universalis cannot be completed — not because of a technical failure, but because of a fundamental limit. Leibniz's dream hits a wall. The wall is real.
    Gödel's proof uses techniques Leibniz would have recognized: encoding statements as numbers, then reasoning about them arithmetically
  • Alan Turing
    1912–1954  ·  Cambridge / Manchester
    "On Computable Numbers" (1936). The theoretical universal computer — a machine that can simulate any other machine that operates on symbols according to rules. Leibniz's Calculemus becomes a formal specification. Turing doesn't solve the Entscheidungsproblem (decision problem); he proves it unsolvable, and in doing so defines the computer.
    The Turing machine is the direct theoretical heir of Leibniz's dream of mechanical reasoning
  • John von Neumann
    1903–1957  ·  Budapest / Princeton
    First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC (1945). The architecture of the modern stored-program computer. Running on binary arithmetic (Leibniz, 1703). Executing Boolean logic (Boole, 1854). Von Neumann knew Leibniz's binary work and acknowledged it. The machine runs. The Calculemus calculates.
    Von Neumann cited Leibniz's binary arithmetic explicitly in discussions of computer design
  • The Digital Computer
    1945–present  ·  Everywhere
    Every computer ever built. Runs on binary (Leibniz, 1703). Executes Boolean logic (Boole, 1854). Designed on Turing's theoretical framework (1936). Programmed with formal languages descended from Frege's predicate logic (1879). The Characteristica Universalis runs. Not completely — Gödel showed it cannot be complete. But it runs.
    The computer is Leibniz's Calculemus, finally running. He is the silent architect of every digital calculation on earth.
The Calculus Wars: Aftermath

The Newton-Leibniz priority dispute is the founding moment of the modern concept of intellectual ownership — the first great controversy about who discovered what first, carried out in the full glare of international correspondence and institutional investigation.

The modern historical consensus: Newton invented calculus first (c. 1666), using his method of fluxions. Leibniz invented it independently (1675–1676), using a completely different approach and notation. Both were genuine, original discoveries. Leibniz was not a plagiarist. The Royal Society's 1712 Commercium Epistolicum — which found against Leibniz — was not impartial; it was organized and largely written by Newton himself, who used his position as President to stack the investigating committee.

The poisoned fruit of this dispute: British mathematics spent roughly a century (1720–1820) isolated from the continental advances in calculus, differential equations, and mathematical physics, because British mathematicians refused to use Leibniz's notation out of loyalty to Newton. The calculus war cost Britain a century of mathematical progress.

When Cambridge finally adopted Leibniz notation in the 1820s, British mathematics rejoined the continental tradition and flourished again. The list of great nineteenth-century British mathematicians — Cayley, Sylvester, Hamilton, Maxwell, Boole — are products of the world after the switch.

Newton: Priority of Invention

Developed fluxional calculus c. 1666 in Cambridge during the plague years. First to the idea. Did not publish for two decades. His priority was real — and was used against Leibniz in a dispute Newton essentially organized himself.

1666

Leibniz: Priority of Publication

Published first — Nova Methodus in 1684. The first public account of calculus in history. His notation — ∫, dy/dx — is what every mathematician uses. He lost the priority battle and won the notational war permanently.

1684

The British Century of Loss

1720–1820: Continental mathematicians advance calculus, differential equations, and mechanics rapidly using Leibniz notation. British mathematicians, using Newton's dot notation, fall behind. 100 years of relative stagnation, reversed only when Cambridge adopts Leibniz notation.

Cost of Loyalty

Modern Verdict

Independent co-discovery. Newton first in time; Leibniz first in print; Leibniz's notation universal. The Royal Society's investigation was compromised. There is no evidence of plagiarism. The dispute was not about mathematics — it was about national pride and personal honor.

Historiography
The Monadologie and Its Critics

Leibniz argued in the Monadologie and the Théodicée that this is the best of all possible worlds. God, being omniscient and perfectly good, surveyed all possible configurations of the universe and chose the best one. The evil and suffering we observe are necessary features of the best achievable arrangement — because a world without them would be worse overall.

Voltaire's response was Candide (1759), written partly in reaction to the Lisbon earthquake of 1755, which killed between 30,000 and 60,000 people. Dr. Pangloss — Voltaire's Leibniz stand-in — continues to insist through disaster after disaster that "all is for the best in this best of all possible worlds." The satire is devastating and funny. It made Leibniz's optimism into a byword for willful blindness.

Leibniz's actual argument is more subtle than Voltaire's parody. He does not claim that every individual event is good. He claims that the total configuration of the world — including its free rational creatures, their freedom, their ability to choose good or evil — is the best overall arrangement possible given the constraints of logic and the goodness of God. This is a coherent, if disputable, theological position. Voltaire was not interested in disputing it carefully; he was interested in destroying it entertainingly.

The Monadologie's actual influence on subsequent philosophy is significant. Kant studied Leibniz closely and engaged critically with his metaphysics throughout his career. Hegel's notion of the Absolute unfolds some of Leibniz's ideas about the relationship between parts and wholes. Russell's early philosophy of logical atomism is partly a response to Leibniz's monads. The monads are strange — but they are productive strangeness.

The Final Accounting

What Leibniz Got Right

  • ∫ and dy/dx notation — universal in all calculus
  • Binary arithmetic — runs every computer
  • Calculus independently — confirmed by historians
  • The dream of formal reasoning — realized by Boole→Turing
  • Principle of Sufficient Reason — still in philosophy
  • Mechanical calculator design — used for 280+ years
  • Berlin Academy of Sciences — still exists
  • Principle of Identity of Indiscernibles — philosophical canon
  • Pre-established harmony — addressed by every subsequent philosopher
  • The Republic of Letters — sustained European intellectual life

What Leibniz Got Wrong (or Left Unfinished)

  • The Characteristica Universalis — not completable; Gödel proved why
  • The House of Hanover genealogy — never finished, thirty years of work
  • "Best of all possible worlds" — Voltaire made it a punchline; the argument has serious problems
  • The calculus priority dispute — he spent his last decade fighting it instead of doing mathematics

— Leibniz, 1685  ·  The machines are now built. They run on his mathematics.
