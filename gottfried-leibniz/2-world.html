His World

The Thirty Years' War left Germany. Louis XIV filled the vacuum.
The Republic of Letters connected Europe across borders and creeds.
And at the center of it, a man who wrote to everyone — and was forgotten by all.

Born in the Ruins: Post-War Germany

Leibniz was born on 1 July 1646. The Peace of Westphalia, which ended the Thirty Years' War, was signed on 24 October 1648 — when he was two years old. He grew up in a landscape of devastation: the war had killed between four and eight million people, perhaps a third of the entire German-speaking population. Cities had been razed, universities depleted, trade routes severed.

The war had been religious, dynastic, and territorial all at once — Catholic vs. Protestant, Habsburg vs. Bourbon, German princes vs. the Emperor. It had turned central Europe into a laboratory for systematic cruelty. When it ended, it left behind a patchwork of weakened, traumatized states. There was no unified Germany. There would not be for two more centuries.

Into this vacuum, Louis XIV of France poured French power. The Sun King reigned from 1643 to 1715 — essentially the entire span of Leibniz's adult life. France was the dominant power in Europe: culturally, militarily, linguistically. Leibniz wrote most of his philosophical and scientific correspondence in French. The Monadologie was written in French. The Théodicée was written in French. Latin was for the scholars; French was for the conversation that mattered.

The Stages of His World
Leipzig1646–1667
Formation. Born here, educated here, first degrees here. A Protestant university city in Saxony — the intellectual milieu of Lutheran Germany, still rebuilding from the Thirty Years' War. His father's library. The University of Leipzig. He absorbs everything available, and learns that everything available is not enough.
Mainz1667–1672
Political apprenticeship. Enters service of the Elector of Mainz, one of the seven German princes who elected the Holy Roman Emperor. Works on legal reform, diplomacy, and statecraft. Drafts a plan to redirect Louis XIV's aggression toward Egypt — the Consilium Aegyptiacum — to spare Germany. Travels to Paris to present it. Louis XIV never receives him.
Paris1672–1676
Transformation. The intellectual center of Europe. He meets Huygens, Arnauld, Malebranche. He learns serious mathematics from Huygens — the geometry of Descartes, the method of Cavalieri and Barrow. He invents calculus. He builds the Stepped Reckoner. He is elected to the Royal Society. In four years he goes from promising to great.
London1673, 1676
The Royal Society. Visits in 1673 to demonstrate his calculator; elected a Fellow. Returns briefly in 1676 en route to Hanover. Meets Henry Oldenburg and John Collins, who have seen Newton's unpublished mathematical manuscripts. Whether Leibniz saw anything relevant to calculus during these visits is the question at the heart of the priority dispute.
AmsterdamNovember 1676
Spinoza. Passes through Amsterdam and spends several days with Baruch Spinoza. They discuss the Ethics — still in manuscript. Spinoza will die of tuberculosis three months later. The meeting is the apex of seventeenth-century philosophy: the two greatest minds of the era, face to face, disagreeing about God and substance.
Hanover1676–1716
Forty years of service. Court counselor, librarian, and historian to the House of Hanover. He writes philosophy and mathematics in the margins of genealogical research. He founds the Berlin Academy of Sciences. He fights the priority dispute. He watches the court he served move to London and forget him. He dies here, in an unmarked grave.
Berlin1700
The Academy. He founds the Berlin-Brandenburg Academy of Sciences in 1700, becomes its first president. It is the German counterpart to the Royal Society (London) and the Académie des Sciences (Paris). His institutional ambition — to build structures for knowledge — is, in this case, successful.
Vienna1712–1714
Last great work. At the Habsburg court in Vienna, working for Charles VI, he writes the Monadologie and the Principes de la Nature et de la Grâce. His philosophical system reaches its final form. He returns to Hanover in 1714 to find his employer has gone to London and left no instructions for what to do with the court philosopher.
The Republic of Letters

Before journals, universities, conferences, and preprint servers, the European intellectual community sustained itself through letters. Latin letters, French letters, German letters — sent across borders, between Catholic and Protestant, between Jesuit and Lutheran, between philosopher and prince. This was the Respublica Literaria: the Republic of Letters, a community without territory, governed only by argument.

Leibniz was its most prolific citizen. He wrote approximately 15,000 letters to roughly 1,000 correspondents over his lifetime. He wrote to Spinoza and to Newton. To the Queen of Prussia and to Jesuit missionaries in China. To Dutch scientists and French theologians and English mathematicians. He was the switchboard of European intellectual life: the man who knew what everyone was thinking, and made sure they knew what everyone else was thinking.

Many of his most important ideas exist only in letters — never systematized into books, never organized into treatises. His philosophical system is reconstructed largely from correspondence. His mathematics is scattered across letters and unpublished manuscripts in the Hanover Archive. He wrote as he thought: continuously, to everyone, about everything.

Christiaan Huygens
Paris, from 1672
Leibniz's mathematical mentor. Huygens set him the problems that forced him to learn the mathematics of his era. Without Huygens, there may have been no calculus.
Isaac Newton
London / Cambridge
They exchanged letters in 1676, mediated by Oldenburg. Newton's letters (the Epistola Prior and Epistola Posterior) described his methods in coded anagrams. The correspondence became Exhibit A in the later priority dispute.
Baruch Spinoza
Amsterdam, 1676
One meeting in person; prior correspondence mediated by Oldenburg. Their philosophical differences were radical. Leibniz later distanced himself from Spinoza publicly, fearing the association with "atheism."
Queen Sophie Charlotte
Berlin / Hanover
The Electress of Brandenburg and later Queen of Prussia. An unusually accomplished intellectual — she studied philosophy directly with Leibniz. The Théodicée grew out of discussions with her. She died in 1705; Leibniz wrote that he had lost his greatest patroness.
Antoine Arnauld
Paris / Rome
The Jansenist theologian. His correspondence with Leibniz on the Discours de Métaphysique (1686) is one of the most important philosophical exchanges of the century — rigorous, extended, and mutually challenging.
Joachim Bouvet, S.J.
China (Jesuit Mission)
The Jesuit missionary who connected Leibniz's binary arithmetic to the I Ching hexagrams. Leibniz used this connection to argue for the universality of binary — and for the potential unity of Chinese and Christian thought.
Samuel Clarke
London, 1715–1716
The Leibniz-Clarke correspondence is the last and one of the greatest philosophical exchanges of Leibniz's life. Newton's proxy debated space, time, God, and gravity. The exchange was ongoing when Leibniz died.
Henry Oldenburg
Royal Society, London
Secretary of the Royal Society and the hub of the pan-European correspondence network. Leibniz's letters to Oldenburg form an important part of the record of his mathematical development — and of the later calculus dispute.
Louis XIV and the French Threat

The dominant geopolitical fact of Leibniz's lifetime was the expansionism of Louis XIV. The Sun King invaded the Spanish Netherlands, attacked the Dutch Republic, threatened the Rhineland. He had the most powerful army in Europe and a court at Versailles that radiated cultural prestige in every direction.

Leibniz was not merely an observer of this. He was an active, if minor, diplomatic actor. In 1672 he drafted the Consilium Aegyptiacum — a detailed proposal for France to redirect its imperial energy toward Egypt and North Africa rather than Germany. He traveled to Paris specifically to present this plan. Louis XIV never granted him an audience. He was not important enough.

Later, as the War of the Spanish Succession approached (1701–1714), Leibniz made further diplomatic efforts to prevent or limit it. A conflict over who would rule Spain threatened to engulf all of Europe. Leibniz wrote to princes, nobles, and emperors. He was, again, ignored. He was a court philosopher and librarian, not a statesman. The war happened.

"Nothing is more important to the common welfare than to show people the true interests of princes and states, so that no injustice is done."

— Leibniz, in correspondence on the Spanish Succession
The Newton Priority Dispute

The calculus priority dispute is one of the ugliest episodes in the history of science. It began as a genuine question about independent invention, became a proxy war between English and Continental science, and ended with a Royal Society investigation widely considered to have been rigged.

The historical record, reconstructed carefully by twentieth-century historians, supports this conclusion: Newton invented calculus first (c. 1666, using the method of fluxions) but left it almost entirely unpublished. Leibniz invented calculus independently (1675–1676) and published it (1684). Both inventions were genuine. Neither man copied the other. The fact that Leibniz visited London in 1676 and met people who had seen Newton's manuscripts creates a shadow of suspicion that cannot be definitively resolved — but there is no evidence of plagiarism.

  • 1666
    Newton develops his method of fluxions in Cambridge, during the plague years. He writes it up in manuscripts that circulate to a small number of colleagues. He does not publish.
  • 1675–76
    Leibniz develops calculus independently in Paris, building on Huygens, Barrow, and Pascal. His notation (dy/dx, ∫) is developed in notebooks now preserved in Hanover. The development is documented in detail.
  • 1676
    The exchange of letters. Newton writes the Epistola Prior (June) and Epistola Posterior (October) to Leibniz, via Oldenburg. These describe Newton's method obliquely, with a key result given as an anagram. Leibniz visits London in October. This is the crux of the dispute.
  • 1684
    Leibniz publishes first. "Nova Methodus" in the Acta Eruditorum. It is the first published account of calculus in history. Newton's prior invention is not publicly known.
  • 1699
    Accusations begin. Nicolas Fatio de Duillier, a Swiss mathematician and Newton associate, accuses Leibniz of plagiarism in print. Leibniz requests a retraction. The Royal Society declines to get involved.
  • 1711
    Leibniz complains to the Royal Society about John Keill's public accusations of plagiarism. He expects the Society to adjudicate fairly. He does not know that Newton has become its President.
  • 1712
    The Commercium Epistolicum. The Royal Society appoints a committee to investigate. The committee is stacked with Newton allies. The report — almost certainly drafted by Newton himself — finds in Newton's favor. Leibniz is not invited to testify. He is not shown the report before publication.
  • 1716
    Leibniz dies with the dispute unresolved, his reputation damaged in England. He had spent his final years trying to rebut the accusations in correspondence. He never cleared his name in British circles during his lifetime.
The Hanover Court: Thirty Years of Service

What They Wanted

The House of Hanover wanted a genealogy proving their descent from the Guelf dynasty and, ultimately, from a sufficiently noble Italian lineage. This would support their claim to the British throne (which they eventually received in 1714). They employed Leibniz as their historian. He worked on this project for thirty years and never finished it.

What He Gave Them

He gave them the genealogy they needed, eventually. He also gave them calculus, binary arithmetic, the first mass-production calculator design, the Berlin Academy of Sciences, the Théodicée, the Monadologie, 15,000 letters maintaining the Republic of Letters, and some of the most important unfinished manuscripts in the history of philosophy.

They gave him a room, a salary, and the freedom to think — when he wasn't doing genealogy.
