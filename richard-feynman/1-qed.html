The Masterwork

Quantum Electrodynamics: The Theory of Everything Electromagnetic

12 Decimal places of precision
1948 Shelther Island / Pocono Conferences
1/137 Fine-structure constant α
∞→∞ Infinities tamed by renormalization

Quantum Electrodynamics is the quantum field theory of electromagnetism — the theory that describes how light and matter interact at the most fundamental level. Every electromagnetic phenomenon ever measured — light, electricity, magnetism, chemistry, biology, every photon that has ever struck every retina — is described by QED. It is the bedrock of the physical world as we know it.

Feynman developed QED between 1946 and 1950, during his years at Cornell and his first years at Caltech. He was not alone: Julian Schwinger at Harvard and Sin-Itiro Tomonaga in Japan developed mathematically equivalent formulations independently and simultaneously. But Feynman's approach was different in kind: where Schwinger's was a tour-de-force of abstract operator mathematics, Feynman's was built on physical intuition, pictures, and a new computational method that made previously intractable calculations tractable.

The central problem QED solved was renormalization: when physicists calculated quantum corrections to electromagnetic quantities, they kept getting infinities. A bare electron, surrounded by a cloud of virtual particles continuously popping in and out of existence, seemed to have infinite energy, infinite mass. Feynman, Schwinger, and Tomonaga — each in their own way — showed that these infinities could be systematically absorbed into measured physical quantities (the mass and charge of the electron) and removed, leaving finite, calculable, experimentally testable predictions.

"It is what I would call a dippy process. Having to resort to such hocus-pocus has prevented me from being able to define clearly what is fundamentally wrong with the present methods of quantum field theory."
— Feynman on renormalization, in "QED: The Strange Theory of Light and Matter," 1985

He was never fully comfortable with renormalization. He called it "sweeping the infinities under the rug." He believed the framework was incomplete — that a deeper theory would eventually replace it. He was probably right, but sixty years later, no one has found that deeper theory, and QED remains the most precisely confirmed scientific theory in existence. The magnetic moment of the electron, predicted by QED to twelve decimal places, agrees with measurement to twelve decimal places. This is equivalent to measuring the distance from Los Angeles to New York to within the width of a human hair.

Visual Language

Feynman Diagrams: A Grammar for the Invisible

Feynman invented his diagrams in 1948, and they constitute perhaps the most influential visual notation in the history of science. Every line in a Feynman diagram has precise mathematical meaning. They are not illustrations — they are computational tools. Each diagram corresponds to a specific mathematical term in a perturbation expansion; summing over diagrams gives the total quantum probability amplitude for a physical process.

The rules: straight lines represent fermions (electrons, quarks, neutrinos). Wavy lines represent photons — force-carrying bosons of the electromagnetic field. Vertices — the points where lines meet — represent interactions. The amplitude for each vertex is proportional to the coupling constant (for electromagnetism, roughly 1/137). The fewer vertices, the larger the contribution.

Electron Emitting a Photon

An electron (e⁻) interacts with the electromagnetic field, emitting a photon (γ). The vertex factor is proportional to √α ≈ 1/11.7.

Electron-Positron Annihilation

An electron and positron annihilate via a virtual photon, producing two real photons. The basis of PET scanning and pair production physics.

Compton Scattering

A photon scatters off an electron, changing direction and losing energy. Arthur Compton's 1923 experiment demonstrated the particle nature of light.

When Feynman first presented his diagrams at the Pocono Conference in 1948, the audience — including Niels Bohr and Paul Dirac — was baffled or hostile. Dirac asked repeatedly how the diagrams were "derived." Feynman tried to explain that they were not derived from anything — they were a new way of organizing calculations, a visual language whose grammar was given by the rules of QED.

Freeman Dyson proved in 1949 that Feynman's pictorial method was mathematically equivalent to the more rigorous approaches of Schwinger and Tomonaga. Within five years, Feynman diagrams were the universal language of particle physics. Within twenty years, they were used in nuclear physics, condensed matter physics, and statistical mechanics. Today, billions of Feynman diagrams have been drawn, and every one of them descends from Feynman's 1948 invention.

Foundations

Path Integrals: Every Particle Takes Every Path

Path Integral: Particle travels from A to B via every possible route simultaneously

Feynman's path integral formulation of quantum mechanics, developed in his 1942 Princeton thesis and published in 1948, is one of the most radical and fruitful ideas in theoretical physics. In classical mechanics, a particle moves along a single definite path from point A to point B — the path of least action. In Feynman's formulation, a quantum particle travels along every possible path simultaneously.

Each path contributes a quantum amplitude — a complex number with magnitude and phase. The phase of each path's contribution is proportional to the classical action along that path. When you sum the contributions from all paths (the "path integral"), the paths that are close to the classical trajectory add coherently — their phases nearly align. Paths far from the classical trajectory oscillate rapidly in phase and cancel each other out. The result: the classical trajectory emerges from quantum mechanics as the path of constructive interference, not as a fundamental axiom.

This is not merely a different way to write the same mathematics. The path integral formulation opened doors that the standard operator formalism could not easily enter: quantum field theory, quantum gravity, statistical mechanics, string theory. Modern physics could not exist in its current form without the path integral.

"It doesn't matter how beautiful your theory is, it doesn't matter how smart you are. If it doesn't agree with experiment, it's wrong."
— Richard Feynman
Prophecy

Quantum Computing and Nanotechnology: Two Fields He Invented

1959

"There's Plenty of Room at the Bottom"

Feynman's landmark Caltech lecture proposing that machines could be built at the atomic scale — that the laws of physics did not prevent it. He offered cash prizes for first demonstration of microscale writing and tiny motors. The prizes were eventually claimed, years later. The lecture is considered the founding text of nanotechnology.

1981

"Simulating Physics with Computers"

Feynman argued that quantum systems cannot be efficiently simulated by classical computers — that the Hilbert space grows exponentially with the number of particles. The solution: build a computer that operates on quantum principles. This is the founding lecture of quantum computing as a field.

1985

The Universal Quantum Computer

Feynman published "Quantum Mechanical Computers," describing a theoretical model of a quantum computer — reversible logic gates, quantum bits, unitary evolution. He showed in principle how any quantum computation could be implemented.

Legacy

The Fields He Founded

Nanotechnology: a multi-trillion-dollar industry. Quantum computing: IBM, Google, IonQ, D-Wave, and dozens of startups are building what Feynman described. Both fields trace their intellectual origin to his Caltech lectures.

The Origin Story

The Wobbling Plate: How QED Began

In 1945, Feynman arrived at Cornell after the war. He was depressed. He felt physics was finished — all the important problems solved. He could not work. The weight of Los Alamos, the death of Arline, the atomic bombings — he had withdrawn.

One day in the Cornell cafeteria, a student tossed a plate into the air. As it wobbled, Feynman noticed the blue Cornell medallion on the rim rotating and wobbling at different rates. On a whim, he worked out the ratio of wobble to rotation — a classical mechanics problem. He found the ratio was 2:1 for small wobbles. He showed his result to Hans Bethe. Bethe asked: "What's the importance of that?" Feynman said: "It has no importance. I just found it interesting."

But the exercise broke the paralysis. Feynman began playing again. He began working on the electromagnetic interaction of the electron "for the fun of it." He wrote later: "The diagrams and the whole business that I got the Nobel Prize for came from that piddling around with the wobbling plate." The plate-wobble led to QED. The "fun" led to the most precise scientific theory ever constructed.

"Physics is like sex: sure, it may give some practical results, but that's not why we do it."
— Richard Feynman
The Greatest Textbook

The Feynman Lectures on Physics

Volume I — 1963

Mechanics, Radiation, Heat

Kinematics, dynamics, energy, thermodynamics, wave mechanics. Feynman's treatment of energy conservation alone is considered a masterclass in physical thinking. Famously begins: "When we say we understand a group of phenomena, we mean that we've found a model that explains all of them."

Volume II — 1964

Electromagnetism and Matter

Maxwell's equations, electromagnetic waves, magnetic materials, electrical circuits. Contains Feynman's famous meditation on what it means that a physical equation can predict phenomena not yet observed.

Volume III — 1965

Quantum Mechanics

The only introductory quantum mechanics text that starts from the actual physics — double-slit experiments, spin, the Schrödinger equation emerging from physical reasoning. Considered by many the most illuminating introduction to quantum mechanics ever written.

The lectures are freely available at feynmanlectures.caltech.edu. They are read by physics students worldwide, by working physicists who want to understand something they thought they already knew, and by people with no physics training who simply want to understand what Feynman was trying to say. There is no other textbook in science that has served all three audiences equally well.
