The Person

Physicist · Bongo Player · Safe-Cracker · Nobel Laureate

He was the most original physicist since Einstein, and possibly the most fully alive human being in the history of science.

Far Rockaway, Queens · 1918 — Los Angeles, California · 1988 · Age 69 · Nobel Prize 1965
Vitals

Richard Phillips Feynman

1918 Born, Far Rockaway
1942 PhD, Princeton, age 24
1965 Nobel Prize in Physics
12 Decimal places: QED precision
1988 Died, Los Angeles, age 69

Richard Phillips Feynman was born on May 11, 1918, in Far Rockaway, a beachfront neighborhood in Queens, New York. His father, Melville Feynman, was a uniform salesman with a deep love for science; his mother, Lucille, gave him his irrepressible sense of humor. From childhood, Feynman was unusual: he rebuilt radios from scratch, taught himself trigonometry and calculus before high school, and became the kind of person who asked not "what is this?" but "why does it do that?"

He enrolled at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 1935, where he scored a perfect 100 on the Putnam mathematics competition — a feat so exceptional that a professor assumed he had cheated. He completed his undergraduate degree in physics in 1939 and went to Princeton for graduate school, submitting a doctoral dissertation that contained the seed of what would later become the path integral formulation of quantum mechanics. He was 24 years old. John Wheeler was his advisor. Albert Einstein, Wolfgang Pauli, and others attended his thesis defense.

What followed was one of the strangest, most productive, most human scientific careers of the twentieth century.

Origins

Far Rockaway and the Self-Taught Tinkerer

Feynman's father was not a scientist, but he was something better: a man who thought like one. He taught young Richard to see patterns, to question, to refuse mere naming in favor of actual understanding. They would walk in the woods and his father would say: "That bird — do you know what it's called in fourteen languages? But none of that tells you anything about the bird. Do you see how it searches for food? How it holds its head?" This mode of thinking — never satisfied by labels, always driving toward mechanism — became Feynman's defining intellectual character.

The garage in Far Rockaway became a laboratory. Feynman repaired neighbors' radios, redesigned circuits, built amplifiers. He read textbooks ahead of school. When he encountered calculus, he invented his own notation — some of which he had to unlearn. By the time he reached MIT, he was already doing physics, not just learning it.

"When I was a kid, I had a lab. It wasn't a place where I made things. It was a place where I found out things."
— Richard Feynman, "Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!"

His score of 105 out of 100 (with extra credit) on MIT's physics entrance exam was, in the folklore of the department, simply what Feynman had done. He graduated in 1939 and arrived at Princeton that fall in a rumpled suit, unable to navigate a formal tea party but entirely capable of solving any physics problem in the building.

Love

Arline Greenbaum

Arline Greenbaum Feynman (1921–1945)

They had known each other since childhood on Long Island. Feynman loved her completely and knew she was ill — diagnosed with tuberculosis — before they married. His family opposed the marriage; his father had died. His mother worried. Richard married Arline anyway, in a civil ceremony on June 29, 1942, the day before he reported for war work. She was already a patient; they arranged the ceremony around her wheelchair.

Arline was placed in a sanitarium in Albuquerque while Feynman worked at Los Alamos, forty miles north in the mountains. Every weekend he could, he hitchhiked or cadged rides to see her. She was funny, fierce, and completely unintimidated by his brilliance. She sent him puzzles. She decorated her room. She refused to be pathetic about dying.

Arline died on June 16, 1945 — seven weeks before the Trinity test, before the bombing of Hiroshima. She was twenty-five. Feynman drove back to Los Alamos and told no one for hours. That night he worked. He later said he did not cry until a year later, walking past a dress shop in Oak Ridge and seeing a dress she would have liked.

"I find I am not able, I can make the words, but not that much depth of feeling comes into them. I know you would want me to go on and enjoy life. I am going to try."
— Feynman's unsent letter to Arline, written October 17, 1946 — sixteen months after her death

The letter was found among his papers after his own death, forty-two years later. It was still sealed. It ended: "Please excuse my not mailing this — but I don't know your new address."

War & Physics

Los Alamos and the Bomb

In 1943, Feynman joined the Manhattan Project at Los Alamos, New Mexico, recruited by Hans Bethe to work in the Theoretical Division. He was twenty-four. Around him: J. Robert Oppenheimer, Enrico Fermi, Niels Bohr (working under the alias "Nicholas Baker"), Bethe, John von Neumann, Edward Teller. Feynman was by far the youngest senior physicist. He was also, by common account, the most disruptive.

He cracked safes. Not as a party trick — as a philosophical project. The base was theoretically highly secure; Feynman demonstrated it was not. He picked the combination locks on filing cabinets containing atomic bomb secrets, sometimes leaving notes inside: "I borrowed document LA-4." The security officers hated him. Oppenheimer quietly found it funny.

Intellectual Sport

Safe-Cracking

Feynman cracked the filing cabinet safes of senior Los Alamos physicists using a combination of social engineering, observed habits, and simple mathematics. He kept a written log of his methods.

Counterintelligence

Coded Letters

He and Arline exchanged coded and censored letters, deliberately designed to baffle and frustrate military censors. He included ciphers she had to decode. The censors required him to stop, in writing.

Computation

Human Computers

Feynman organized the human computer teams at Los Alamos, managing teams of people with mechanical calculators performing iterative numerical calculations. He optimized their efficiency like a factory floor.

Trinity, July 1945

The Test

Feynman watched the Trinity nuclear test without protective goggles, reasoning that the windshield of the truck he sat in would block the ultraviolet radiation. He was likely correct. He was the only person at the test to watch it with naked eyes.

After Hiroshima and Nagasaki, Feynman experienced a delayed moral crisis. He later wrote that walking through New York City after the war, watching people build bridges and plan futures, he could only think: it's all pointless, the bomb will destroy it all. The crisis passed. But it left a permanent scar in his thinking about the relationship between science and its consequences.

The Man

Character: Bongos, Bars, Paintings, and Pure Curiosity

Feynman played bongo drums seriously — not as an affectation but because he genuinely loved percussion. He performed at venues in Pasadena. He played in a ballet production. He played late at night in his office at Caltech until colleagues complained. The physics and the bongos came from the same source: pure, unmediated engagement with the sensory world.

He taught himself to draw and painted under the pseudonym "Ofey" — a name derived from a French obscenity. He sold paintings and held exhibitions. His subjects were often nudes; he was unapologetic. Several of his paintings depicted the stripper Cleo who performed at a Pasadena bar that Feynman frequented — not because of the strippers but, he claimed sincerely, because it was the only genuinely quiet place he could think and write.

Music

Bongo Drums

Feynman learned to play bongos from a professional percussionist and took it seriously enough to perform publicly. He also learned frigideira (a Brazilian percussion instrument) and played with samba schools in Rio.

Visual Art

Painter "Ofey"

Self-taught painter who sold work under the pseudonym Ofey. Exhibited in Pasadena. Believed that science and art arose from the same capacity for wonder, not from separate faculties.

Security

Safe-Cracker

Cracked safes at Los Alamos, Princeton, and Caltech as an intellectual hobby. Read extensively on lock-picking, safecracking, and social engineering. Was asked, politely, to stop doing it to colleagues' offices.

Languages

Tuva & Portuguese

Learned conversational Portuguese for a sabbatical in Brazil. Spent years trying to visit Tuva (a tiny Soviet republic) simply because he found the name "Kyzyl" irresistible. He died before he could go.

Three Marriages

He married three times after Arline. His second marriage, to Mary Louise Bell in 1952, lasted only two years; she reportedly cited his obsession with mathematics as grounds for divorce. His third and lasting marriage was to Gweneth Howarth, an English woman he met in Geneva in 1958. They had two children. Gweneth, by all accounts, was the person who finally gave him a stable home — and he gave her, she said, a life she could never have imagined.

The Lectures

"The Feynman Lectures on Physics"

In 1961, Caltech asked Feynman to redesign the introductory physics curriculum for freshmen and sophomores. What followed was one of the great teaching experiments in the history of science. Feynman lectured twice weekly for two years. The students often could not follow him in real time. Professors and graduate students filled the back rows, taking notes. The lectures were recorded on audio tape and photographed.

The resulting three-volume textbook — The Feynman Lectures on Physics — is still in print, still the most widely read advanced physics text in the world, and still considered by many physicists to be the finest presentation of physics ever written. Volume I covers mechanics, radiation, and heat. Volume II covers electromagnetism. Volume III covers quantum mechanics.

"What I cannot create, I do not understand."
— Written on Feynman's blackboard at Caltech, found after his death, February 1988

The lectures are now freely available online at feynmanlectures.caltech.edu, placed there by the California Institute of Technology. They have been read by millions of people who never met Feynman, in countries he never visited, in languages he never learned.

1986–1988

Challenger and the End

In January 1986, the Space Shuttle Challenger broke apart 73 seconds after launch, killing all seven crew members. President Reagan appointed the Rogers Commission to investigate. Feynman, by then battling kidney cancer, was asked to serve. He nearly declined — he hated committees, ceremonies, and bureaucracy. He agreed only after persuasion by his wife and by former NASA administrator William Graham.

The Commission was investigating in an unfocused way, circling around the real cause. Feynman, characteristically, went around the formal process. He called engineers directly, visited contractors, read internal documents. He identified the O-ring seals in the solid rocket boosters as the likely failure point — and identified the key variable that the Rogers Commission was not looking at: temperature. The O-rings became significantly less pliable at low temperatures. The launch had occurred on one of the coldest mornings in Cape Canaveral history.

At a formal televised Commission hearing, Feynman asked for a glass of ice water, produced a piece of O-ring material, submerged it, and squeezed it with a clamp. When he removed it, the material had not returned to its original shape. He set it on the table and said: "I believe this has some significance for our problem."

"For a successful technology, reality must take precedence over public relations, for Nature cannot be fooled."
— Feynman's personal appendix to the Rogers Commission Report, 1986

NASA management had not wanted this finding. Feynman's appendix was nearly excluded from the report. He threatened to withdraw his name from the report entirely if his appendix was not included. It was included.

By 1987 the cancer had spread. He had already survived one operation for kidney cancer in 1986 and a second rare cancer, myxoid chondrosarcoma, was progressing. On February 15, 1988, his kidneys failed. He was offered dialysis; he declined. Richard Feynman died on February 15, 1988, in Los Angeles. He was sixty-nine years old. His last words, reported by those at his bedside: "I'd hate to die twice. It's so boring."
