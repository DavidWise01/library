I — The World He Was Born Into

Wilhelmine Germany

Albert Einstein was born eight years after German unification — in the new German Empire of Kaiser Wilhelm I, in the kingdom of Württemberg, in the city of Ulm. He grew up in Munich under Kaiser Wilhelm II, attended school during the years of Germany's rapid industrialization and imperial ambition, and graduated from university just as the great powers were building the political and military machinery that would produce the First World War.

The Germany of Einstein's youth was simultaneously the most intellectually productive country in the world — leading in chemistry, physics, mathematics, medicine, and philosophy — and a society organized around hierarchical authority, military tradition, and a Prussian culture of obedience that Einstein found constitutionally alien. He was a Swabian by birth, Jewish by heritage, and cosmopolitan by temperament. None of these fit comfortably in Wilhelmine Germany.

His decision, at seventeen, to renounce his German citizenship was partly practical — to avoid military conscription — and partly temperamental. He did not want to be a German in the Prussian sense. He spent five years stateless, became a Swiss citizen in 1901, and never sought German citizenship again until the Prussian Academy of Sciences insisted upon it in 1914 as a condition of his appointment. He accepted reluctantly. He surrendered it again in 1933, by his own initiative, the week Hitler took power.

The Scientific Culture of German Physics

By 1900, German universities dominated world physics. The Annalen der Physik, where Einstein published his 1905 papers, was the most prestigious physics journal in existence. The Prussian Academy of Sciences, which Einstein joined in 1914, was the formal summit of German scientific life. Germany's investment in pure science was, in Einstein's productive years, unmatched anywhere in the world — and would be destroyed within a generation.

II — The Hatching Ground

The Swiss Patent Office — A Productive Cloister

Einstein arrived at the Swiss Federal Patent Office in Bern in June 1902, hired at the lowest technical grade. He evaluated mechanical and electrical patent applications: understanding devices, identifying prior art, assessing the precision of technical descriptions. The work suited him in ways that academic life had not. It required focused analytical attention for fixed hours, with clear deliverables and no political maneuvering.

He worked efficiently and was promoted — to Technical Expert, Second Class, in 1906. But the deeper value of the patent office was what it was not. It was not a university department. It carried no research expectations, no faculty meetings, no pressure to publish on schedule, no obligation to conduct experiments. Einstein had evenings and mornings free, he had an income, he had intellectual solitude, and he had a circle of friends — the Akademie Olympia, a reading group he formed with Maurice Solovine and Conrad Habicht — who met to read Hume, Mach, Poincaré, and debate the foundations of physics and philosophy.

"That worldly cloister where I hatched my most beautiful ideas, and where we so happily wasted our time."
— Einstein on the Bern Patent Office, letter to Heinrich Zangger, 1917

The thought experiments that Einstein conducted in those years — imagining riding alongside a light beam, imagining an observer falling in a gravitational field, imagining clocks synchronized by light signals — required not laboratories but concentrated, uninterrupted imagination. The patent office provided the conditions. He was promoted to First Class in 1906, but by then the academic world had noticed the 1905 papers, and he was beginning the slow process of being absorbed into the institution he had failed to enter eight years earlier.

III — The Universities

ETH Zurich, Prague, Berlin

Einstein's academic career moved in a rapid arc once it began. His appointment to the University of Zurich in 1909 was followed by full professorships at the German University in Prague (1911) and then back to ETH Zurich (1912). He was, by 1912, one of the most sought-after physicists in Europe. The problem was that he was still working on General Relativity — and the mathematics was resisting him.

His collaboration with Marcel Grossmann, his mathematician friend from ETH student days, produced the Entwurf (draft) theory in 1913 — a precursor to General Relativity that turned out to be incorrect. Einstein spent two years chasing errors in his own equations, periodically convinced he had found the final theory, periodically discovering that he had not. The intellectual strain of those years was considerable. He later said it was the hardest sustained effort of his intellectual life.

In April 1914 he moved to Berlin, appointed to the Prussian Academy of Sciences at the instigation of Max Planck and Walther Nernst — the two most powerful men in German science. Berlin was the center of the German scientific world. It was also, for Einstein, the beginning of a difficult personal period. The move to Berlin destroyed his marriage to Mileva; she took the boys to Zurich and they never lived together again. The First World War began three months after he arrived. He signed the pacifist Manifesto to Europeans while almost every other German intellectual signed the nationalist Manifesto of the Ninety-Three.

Berlin 1914–1933: Physics in the Weimar Republic

Einstein's Berlin years spanned the First World War, the chaos of the 1918 revolution, the short-lived Weimar Republic, economic collapse, hyperinflation, and the political violence that preceded Hitler's rise. He produced General Relativity in 1915, received the Nobel Prize in 1921, was the target of anti-Semitic attacks by the "Deutsche Physik" movement, survived an assassination plot (the killers who murdered Walther Rathenau in 1922 were said to be planning to kill Einstein next), and navigated a political and social landscape in collapse while maintaining, somehow, an extraordinary scientific output.

IV — The Moment of Fame

The 1919 Eclipse — Overnight Immortality

General Relativity predicted that light from distant stars, passing close to the sun, would be deflected by the sun's gravitational field — by a specific, calculable amount (1.75 arc-seconds). Newtonian gravity also predicted a deflection, but exactly half as large. The difference was measurable, but only during a total solar eclipse, when stars near the sun's limb become visible.

Arthur Eddington, a Quaker pacifist and one of the few British physicists who had followed Einstein's wartime German publications, organized two expeditions to observe the solar eclipse of May 29, 1919 — one to Sobral, Brazil, and one to Príncipe Island in the Gulf of Guinea. The conditions were difficult. Cloud threatened the Príncipe observation. The photographs were measured for months after the expeditions returned.

On November 6, 1919, the results were presented jointly to the Royal Society and the Royal Astronomical Society in London. The measured deflection matched Einstein's prediction, not Newton's. Sir J.J. Thomson, discoverer of the electron and president of the Royal Society, called it "one of the greatest achievements in the history of human thought." The next morning, The Times of London ran the headline: "Revolution in Science — New Theory of the Universe — Newtonian Ideas Overthrown." The New York Times picked it up. Einstein was fifty-six years old — actually, forty years old — and he woke up the most famous scientist on earth.

Deflection of Light — The Numbers

Newton's theory predicted 0.875 arc-seconds deflection of starlight by the sun. Einstein's General Relativity predicted 1.75 arc-seconds — exactly double. Eddington measured 1.61 ± 0.30 arc-seconds at Sobral (consistent with Einstein). Modern measurements using radio telescopes put the deflection at 1.7512 ± 0.0012 arc-seconds — matching General Relativity with extraordinary precision.

Einstein's reaction to his sudden fame was characteristically wry. Asked what he would have done if Eddington's measurements had contradicted his theory, he replied: "Then I would have been sorry for the dear Lord. The theory is correct."

V — The Prize and Its Politics

The Nobel Prize — Why Not Relativity?

Einstein was nominated for the Nobel Prize in Physics almost every year from 1910 onward. The Nobel Committee declined repeatedly, primarily because several influential committee members — particularly Allvar Gullstrand, a Swedish ophthalmologist on the Physics committee — believed that General Relativity was speculative and possibly incorrect. The 1919 eclipse results shifted opinion, but not immediately.

The 1921 prize was not awarded that year — it was held over. In 1922, it was retroactively awarded to Einstein "for 1921," specifically for the photoelectric effect. The citation carefully stated "in recognition of his services to Theoretical Physics, and especially for his discovery of the law of the photoelectric effect" — not relativity, and notably not the Einstein field equations. The Nobel Committee was unwilling to award the prize for a theory that had not yet been fully experimentally verified, despite the 1919 eclipse result.

Einstein was on a ship to Japan when the announcement was made and received a telegram informing him. He gave his Nobel lecture — on the theory of relativity, not the photoelectric effect — in Gothenburg in 1923, a year after the award was formally presented (in absentia) to the Swedish minister in Berlin. He sent the prize money to Mileva, as promised in their 1919 divorce settlement.

Deutsche Physik — "Jewish Physics"

From the early 1920s, a nationalist movement within German physics — led by Nobel laureates Philipp Lenard and Johannes Stark — promoted "Aryan physics" and attacked Einstein's work as "Jewish physics." Lenard, in particular, argued that abstract theoretical physics was racially degenerate, and that real German physics was experimental and concrete. The campaign was politically motivated but scientifically incoherent. After 1933, it had state support — and damaged German physics for a generation, as Jewish physicists fled and their replacements were selected for ideology rather than competence.

VI — The Third Reich

His Books Are Burned

On May 10, 1933, across Germany — in Berlin, Munich, Frankfurt, Cologne, and dozens of other cities — students organized by the Nazi German Student Union held public bonfires. Books by Jewish authors, Marxist writers, pacifists, and others deemed "un-German" were thrown into the flames. The works of Sigmund Freud, Karl Marx, Magnus Hirschfeld, Heinrich Heine, Erich Maria Remarque — and Albert Einstein.

Einstein was in California when this happened, had already resigned from the Prussian Academy, had already had his German citizenship revoked by the state of Bavaria (later extended by the Reich). His books were being burned in a country that had called him its greatest living intellectual just fourteen years earlier. His properties in Germany were confiscated. A reward of 20,000 Deutschmarks was offered for his capture — "not yet hanged," said the caption under his photograph in a Nazi magazine listing enemies of the Reich.

He was, by the autumn of 1933, at Princeton, New Jersey, employed by the Institute for Advanced Study. The IAS had been founded in 1930 with a gift from department store heir Louis Bamberger and his sister Caroline Bamberger Fuld, partly inspired by the model of a pure-research institution where scholars worked without teaching or administrative obligations. Its founding director, Abraham Flexner, recruited Einstein as the institution's first and most famous faculty member. Einstein's salary was $15,000 per year — a figure he reportedly found excessive and tried to negotiate downward.

VII — The Atomic Age

The Letter to Roosevelt

In the summer of 1939, Hungarian physicist Leo Szilard was alarmed. He knew, from conversations with European colleagues, that German physicists — including Werner Heisenberg — were investigating the military potential of nuclear fission, which Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann had demonstrated in December 1938. Szilard also knew that the Belgian Congo held the world's largest known uranium deposits, and he feared Germany might move to secure them.

He went to see Einstein at his summer cottage on Long Island. Einstein later said he had not previously considered the military implications of nuclear fission — his work on mass-energy equivalence had been pure theory. "I never thought of that," he reportedly said, when Szilard explained the chain reaction mechanism. They drafted a letter together — Szilard wrote, Einstein reviewed and signed — to be delivered to President Franklin D. Roosevelt through an intermediary, economist Alexander Sachs.

The Einstein–Szilard Letter, August 2, 1939

"...it may become possible to set up a nuclear chain reaction in a large mass of uranium, by which vast amounts of power and large quantities of new radium-like elements would be generated... This new phenomenon would also lead to the construction of bombs... A single bomb of this type, carried by boat and exploded in a port, might very well destroy the whole port together with some of the surrounding territory... I understand that Germany has actually stopped the sale of uranium from the Czechoslovakian mines which she has taken over..."

Roosevelt received the letter in October 1939. The response was cautious — a small committee, modest funding. The full Manhattan Project did not begin until 1942, after Pearl Harbor. Einstein played no role in it. The FBI considered him a security risk due to his pacifism, his socialism, and his left-wing associations; his application for a security clearance was denied. J. Edgar Hoover maintained a file on Einstein for decades. The man whose equation had made the bomb possible was not permitted to know what the bomb was.

When the bombs fell on Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945, Einstein was at Saranac Lake, New York, on a summer vacation. He said, simply: "Oy vey." He spent the remaining decade of his life advocating for nuclear disarmament and international governance of atomic weapons, and was one of the founders of the Emergency Committee of Atomic Scientists.

VIII — The Princeton Years

112 Mercer Street — Einstein and Gödel

Einstein's Princeton years — 1933 to 1955 — were those of a man who had become a monument before his own death. He walked to work, ate simply, played violin badly, refused socks, and maintained an enormous correspondence that occupied a significant part of his time. His secretary, Helen Dukas, managed the avalanche of mail, distinguished the serious correspondence from the cranks, and organized his public life with fierce efficiency.

The most significant intellectual friendship of his Princeton years was with the Austrian mathematician Kurt Gödel, who arrived at the IAS in 1940. Gödel had proved, in 1931, the Incompleteness Theorems — showing that any consistent formal mathematical system powerful enough to describe arithmetic must contain statements that are true but unprovable within that system. It was, in its way, a finding as vertiginous as Einstein's own: mathematics could not be a closed, complete description of truth.

Einstein and Gödel walked together almost daily in Princeton, in conversations no one else fully heard. They were, by common account, the two men on the IAS faculty most capable of challenging each other. In 1949, Gödel presented Einstein with a birthday paper — a new solution to Einstein's own field equations, showing that the equations permitted a universe in which closed time-like curves existed: time loops, mathematically legal, physically intriguing. Einstein accepted the paper with delight and acknowledged that Gödel had found something genuinely disturbing.

Colleague, IAS Princeton

Kurt Gödel

Mathematician of the Incompleteness Theorems. Daily walking companion. Contributed to the foundations of Einstein's own field equations, discovering solutions permitting time travel. Died in 1978 of self-starvation after Adele, his wife and food-taster, was hospitalized.

Colleague, IAS Princeton

John von Neumann

Mathematician, physicist, founding architect of computer science. Also at the IAS. Worked on the Manhattan Project, unlike Einstein. A different kind of universal mind — faster, more applied, less philosophically patient. The two were respectful but not close.

Longtime Friend, Berlin

Max Born

Physicist, co-developer of quantum mechanics, Nobel 1954. The long correspondence between Einstein and Born — running from 1916 to 1955 — is the record of their fundamental disagreement about the interpretation of quantum theory and one of the great intellectual friendships in the history of science.

Friend and Antagonist

Niels Bohr

Danish physicist, architect of the Copenhagen interpretation. Einstein and Bohr debated the foundations of quantum mechanics at every Solvay Conference from 1927 onward. Bohr invariably answered Einstein's challenges. Einstein never conceded. The debate remains, philosophically, unresolved.

The EPR Paradox and "Spooky Action"

In 1935, Einstein, Boris Podolsky, and Nathan Rosen published a paper arguing that quantum mechanics was incomplete. The argument — the EPR paradox — ran as follows: quantum mechanics predicts that if two particles interact and then separate, measuring the state of one particle immediately determines the state of the other, regardless of the distance between them. Either (a) the particles had definite states all along (hidden variables, and quantum mechanics is incomplete), or (b) measuring one particle instantaneously affects the other — "spooky action at a distance," which Einstein found physically unacceptable because it seemed to violate relativity's prohibition on faster-than-light influence.

Einstein expected option (a) to be correct. John Bell proved in 1964 that the two options make different experimental predictions. Alain Aspect's 1982 experiments confirmed option (b): quantum entanglement is real, nonlocal correlations exist, and Einstein's intuition about hidden variables was wrong. Paradoxically, this means Einstein's EPR argument — designed to discredit quantum mechanics — instead identified one of quantum mechanics' most important and useful features. Quantum entanglement underpins every serious proposal for quantum computing and quantum cryptography.

IX — Key Dates in Context

A World Timeline

1871
German unification under Bismarck. The German Empire is proclaimed at Versailles. Einstein will be born eight years later into this new state.
1895–96
Einstein fails and retakes the ETH entrance exam. He is 16–17. He renounces German citizenship. He formulates the light-beam thought experiment.
1902
Joins the Bern Patent Office. Hermann Einstein dies in Milan three weeks before the appointment is confirmed. Einstein will call this the greatest loss of his life.
1905
Annus Mirabilis. Four papers. Brownian motion, photoelectric effect, special relativity, E=mc². He is 26, a third-class patent clerk, unknown outside a small circle.
1914
Moves to Berlin; World War I begins. He signs the pacifist counter-manifesto while 93 German intellectuals sign a declaration supporting German war aims. His marriage collapses.
1915
General Relativity completed. The field equations presented to the Prussian Academy in November. Mercury's orbital precession is explained. The theory is correct.
1919
Eddington eclipse expedition confirms light-bending. Announced November 6. Einstein becomes the most famous scientist in the world overnight. He marries Elsa the same year.
1921–22
Nobel Prize for the photoelectric effect. Tours Japan, Palestine, USA. World lecture tours. The fame is immense and unrelenting. He finds it exhausting and absurd.
1933
Hitler takes power. Einstein never returns to Germany. His citizenship is stripped. His property is confiscated. His books are burned. He is placed on a Nazi liquidation list.
1935
EPR paper published. Einstein, Podolsky, Rosen argue quantum mechanics is incomplete. Designed as a refutation of Bohr; becomes instead a founding document of quantum information theory.
1936
Elsa Einstein dies. He is devastated despite the distance of their relationship in its later years. He does not remarry. Helen Dukas and his stepdaughter Margot remain his household.
1939
Einstein–Szilard letter to Roosevelt. Warns of German atomic bomb program. Triggers the Manhattan Project. Einstein is denied security clearance and plays no role in the project.
1945
Hiroshima and Nagasaki. Einstein learns of the bombings on vacation. Spends the remaining decade advocating for nuclear disarmament. "The unleashed power of the atom has changed everything save our modes of thinking."
1952
Declines presidency of Israel. He is deeply moved and deeply certain he cannot accept. "I lack both the natural aptitude and experience to deal properly with people."
April 1955
Death in Princeton. Aortic aneurysm. He refuses surgery. His last words are spoken in German to a nurse who spoke only English; they are not recorded. His brain is removed without consent.
