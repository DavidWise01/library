The Person

Patent Clerk. Refugee. Genius. Human.

A child born in Ulm who failed an entrance exam, sat in a Swiss patent office, and rewrote the laws of the universe — then watched his country burn his books.

I — Origins

Born in Ulm, Raised in Munich

On the fourteenth of March, 1879, in the southwestern German city of Ulm, a boy was born to Hermann Einstein and Pauline Koch. The family was German Jewish, secular in observance, and commercially minded — Hermann ran a small electrical engineering company. The Einsteins relocated to Munich before Albert's first birthday, and it was there, in the beer-hall capital of Bavaria, that his earliest curiosity was kindled.

At five, his father gave him a compass. The needle's invisible allegiance to magnetic north struck the boy as something close to miraculous — "a deep and lasting impression," he later recalled. The universe was hiding something, and he intended to find it. A family friend, Max Talmud, gave the twelve-year-old a geometry textbook. He devoured it in a single week, calling it the sacred little geometry book.

He was not a prodigy in the conventional mold. He spoke late, repeated phrases to himself, and sat outside the social life of his school. His classical gymnasium education bored him deeply — he resented rote learning, authoritarian instruction, and the Prussian insistence on discipline over understanding. These were complaints he would carry for the rest of his life.

The Family Moves — And He Does Not Follow

When Albert was fifteen, Hermann's business failed and the family relocated to Pavia, Italy, seeking fresh commercial ground. Albert was left behind in Munich to complete his schooling. He lasted six months before convincing a doctor to certify nervous exhaustion, dropped out, and followed his family to Italy. He would never receive his German secondary diploma. He renounced his German citizenship voluntarily in 1896 — at seventeen, stateless by choice, to avoid military conscription. He would spend years as a citizen of nowhere.

II — The Exam

Failing the Door In

In 1895, at sixteen, Einstein applied to the Swiss Federal Polytechnic School in Zurich — known today as ETH Zurich. He failed the entrance examination. His French and botany were inadequate. His mathematics and physics were exceptional, noticed by the physics professor Heinrich Friedrich Weber, who suggested he stay and attend his lectures informally. But the failure was the failure.

He spent a year at a cantonal school in Aarau, Switzerland, boarding with the Winteler family, studying in an atmosphere that was the opposite of Munich — progressive, discussion-based, intellectually open. He flourished. It was here, during long walks in the Swiss foothills, that he began to formulate a thought experiment that would occupy him for a decade: what would the world look like if you could ride alongside a beam of light?

He retook the examination in 1896 and was admitted to ETH. He was seventeen years old, and the only student in his cohort without a secondary school diploma. He was also, by most accounts, an exasperating student — brilliant, argumentative, and constitutionally unable to submit to authority he hadn't earned.

"The secret to creativity is knowing how to hide your sources."
— Albert Einstein (attributed)
III — The Patent Office

Third-Class Technical Expert, Bern

He graduated from ETH in 1900, but could not find an academic post. His professors — particularly Weber, who had once admired him — declined to write recommendations. He was too insolent, too likely to challenge, too unwilling to perform the customary deference. He tutored, substitute-taught, and applied to every university position in the German-speaking world. Every application was rejected.

In 1902, a friend's father pulled a favor at the Swiss Federal Patent Office in Bern. Einstein was hired as a technical expert, third class — the lowest rung. He evaluated patent applications for electrical devices, learned to assess mechanical feasibility quickly, trained himself to find the kernel of an idea beneath layers of technical language.

He was, by his own later testimony, ideally placed. The work engaged just enough of his mind to keep him alert but left the deeper machinery free. He worked eight hours a day, went home, and spent his evenings and early mornings on the physics he could not stop thinking about. He called the patent office "that worldly cloister where I hatched my most beautiful ideas."

He married Mileva Marić in 1903, his Serbian classmate from ETH — one of the few women to study physics at a European polytechnic in that era. They had a daughter, Lieserl, born before the marriage, whose fate remains unknown — likely given up for adoption or dead in infancy. They would have two sons together: Hans Albert and Eduard.

IV — The Miracle Year

1905: Annus Mirabilis

In the spring of 1905, Albert Einstein was twenty-six years old, a patent clerk in Bern, with no university affiliation, no laboratory, no graduate students, no institutional support. Between March and September of that year, he submitted four papers to the journal Annalen der Physik — any one of which would have secured a major scientific reputation. Together, they rebuilt the foundations of physics.

March 1905

The Photoelectric Effect

Light travels as discrete packets of energy — quanta. This paper launched quantum mechanics and won the 1921 Nobel Prize in Physics. Not relativity — this.

May 1905

Brownian Motion

The random jiggling of pollen grains in water is caused by collisions with individual molecules. This paper proved, definitively, that atoms exist.

June 1905

Special Relativity

There is no absolute frame of reference. The speed of light is constant for all observers. Time dilates. Length contracts. The universe is not what it seemed.

September 1905

Mass-Energy Equivalence

E = mc². Energy and mass are interchangeable. A small quantity of mass contains an enormous quantity of energy, locked in the fabric of matter itself.

He submitted his doctoral dissertation to the University of Zurich the same year, on the determination of molecular dimensions from the viscosity of sugar solutions in water. It was accepted. He was awarded a Ph.D. He remained a patent clerk for three more years before academia could absorb what it had missed.

V — Academic Life

From Clerk to Continent's Greatest Mind

Recognition came slowly, then all at once. In 1908 he became a Privatdozent at the University of Bern — an unsalaried lecturer permitted to charge students directly. In 1909 he was appointed associate professor at the University of Zurich. In 1911, full professor at the German University in Prague. In 1912, back to ETH Zurich as full professor. By 1914 he had been called to the Prussian Academy of Sciences in Berlin — the most prestigious scientific position in the German-speaking world — at a salary requiring no teaching obligations whatsoever. He was thirty-five.

His marriage to Mileva had been deteriorating for years. The isolation of his intellectual world, his growing international celebrity, his affairs — the marriage could not hold. In 1919, two years after their legal separation, he married his cousin Elsa Löwenthal, a warm, socially capable woman who organized his life and shielded him from the administrative demands of fame. He relied on her enormously, even if his deeper emotional world remained largely closed to her.

In November 1919, the results of Arthur Eddington's eclipse expedition were announced at the Royal Society in London: starlight bent by the sun's gravity, exactly as predicted by General Relativity. Einstein woke up the next morning the most famous scientist in the world. He found it faintly ridiculous, and said so.

VI — Exile

Germany Burns His Books

Adolf Hitler became Chancellor of Germany on January 30, 1933. Einstein was at the California Institute of Technology in Pasadena when the news reached him. He knew immediately that he would not return. He resigned from the Prussian Academy by letter before they could expel him. In April, Nazi Germany enacted laws stripping Jewish civil servants of their positions. In May, students and SA troops burned books in public squares across Germany — including Einstein's works. The headlines: "Jewish physics."

In October 1933 he arrived at Princeton, New Jersey, to take up a position at the newly founded Institute for Advanced Study. He lived at 112 Mercer Street for the remaining twenty-two years of his life. He was granted U.S. citizenship in 1940. He never returned to Europe.

In 1952, following the death of Israel's first president Chaim Weizmann, Einstein was offered the presidency of the State of Israel. He declined: "I am deeply moved by the offer from our State of Israel, and at once saddened and ashamed that I cannot accept it." He was seventy-three, in poor health, and knew that political administration was not a role he could fill with competence or honesty.

VII — Private Life

The Man Behind the Equations

Mileva Marić remains a complicated figure in the Einstein story. She was one of the first women to study physics at ETH Zurich. She and Einstein corresponded intensely during their student years, and some historians have argued — controversially — that her contributions to the 1905 papers were greater than acknowledged. The historical consensus holds that the ideas were Einstein's, but that the intellectual partnership was real and that she was his most significant sounding board in the crucial years.

Their divorce settlement in 1919 included a remarkable clause: Einstein pledged his future Nobel Prize money to Mileva if and when he received it. He was that confident — and in 1922, he paid. She used it to buy three apartment buildings in Zurich, which she managed until her death in 1948.

His younger son Eduard developed schizophrenia and spent much of his adult life in a psychiatric institution in Zurich. Einstein never saw him after leaving Europe in 1933. It was a grief he carried quietly. He was not, by his own admission, a particularly attentive father. His attention lived elsewhere, always.

He played violin — Beethoven, Mozart — throughout his life, badly enough to irritate and well enough to play chamber music at informal gatherings. He sailed small boats on lakes with conspicuous lack of navigational skill. He walked everywhere he could, thinking as he walked. He rarely wore socks.

"I live in that solitude which is painful in youth, but delicious in the years of maturity."
— Albert Einstein, 1932
VIII — The End

April 18, 1955

An aortic aneurysm had been known about since 1948. On the night of April 17–18, 1955, it ruptured. Einstein refused surgery: "I want to go when I want to go. It is tasteless to prolong life artificially. I have done my share, it is time to go. I will do it elegantly." He died at 1:15 a.m. on April 18 at Princeton Hospital, age seventy-six.

Thomas Harvey, the pathologist on duty, removed Einstein's brain during the autopsy — without the consent of his family or his own stated wishes. He kept it in a jar. He took it with him when he left Princeton, eventually driving it across the United States in a Buick in 1997, with a journalist named Michael Paterniti, looking for Einstein's granddaughter. Harvey was eighty-four. The brain eventually reached the Princeton University Medical Center and the National Museum of Health and Medicine.

Over forty years of study, researchers reported a higher density of glial cells in certain regions, an unusual inferior parietal lobe — the area associated with spatial and mathematical reasoning — and a missing groove (the lateral sulcus) that may have allowed greater neural connectivity. The findings remain contested. What the jar could not contain was the method: to observe carefully, to think without constraint, to trust the imagination more than received authority.

His ashes were scattered at an undisclosed location. He left no grave marker. He had asked for none.

IX — Chronology

A Life in Time

14 March 1879
Born in Ulm, Kingdom of Württemberg
Second child of Hermann Einstein and Pauline Koch. The family is secular German-Jewish.
1880
Family relocates to Munich
Hermann and his brother Jakob establish an electrical engineering firm. Albert grows up in a scientifically curious household.
1895
Fails ETH Zurich entrance examination
Inadequate in French and botany. Spends a year at school in Aarau, Switzerland. Formulates the light-beam thought experiment.
1896
Renounces German citizenship; admitted to ETH
Stateless by choice until Swiss citizenship in 1901. Meets Mileva Marić at ETH.
1900–1902
Academic rejection; patent office appointment
All academic applications fail. Joins the Swiss Federal Patent Office as Technical Expert, Third Class, in June 1902.
1903
Marries Mileva Marić
A daughter, Lieserl, had been born the previous year; her fate is unknown. Sons Hans Albert (1904) and Eduard (1910) follow.
1905
Annus Mirabilis — four papers, one year
Photoelectric effect, Brownian motion, Special Relativity, and E=mc². Doctoral dissertation accepted by the University of Zurich. Age: 26.
1909–1914
Academic ascent: Zurich, Prague, Berlin
Rapidly climbs academic ranks. Appointed to the Prussian Academy of Sciences, Berlin, 1914 — the pinnacle of German science.
1915
General Relativity completed
After ten years of work, the field equations of General Relativity are published in November. Gravity is the curvature of spacetime.
1919
Eclipse confirms General Relativity; world fame overnight
Eddington's solar eclipse expedition measures the bending of starlight. The Times of London: "Revolution in Science — Newtonian Ideas Overthrown."
1921
Nobel Prize in Physics
Awarded for the photoelectric effect, not relativity, which the Nobel Committee considered too speculative. Prize money goes to Mileva per divorce agreement.
1933
Germany strips his citizenship; Einstein emigrates permanently
Already in California when Hitler takes power. His books are burned. He joins the Institute for Advanced Study, Princeton.
1939
Einstein–Szilard letter to Roosevelt
Warns the President that Germany may be developing atomic weapons. Triggers the Manhattan Project — though Einstein himself plays no role in it.
1940
U.S. citizenship granted
Takes the oath of citizenship on October 1, 1940, in Trenton, New Jersey.
1952
Declines presidency of Israel
"I lack both the natural aptitude and the experience to deal properly with people and to exercise official functions."
18 April 1955
Dies in Princeton, age 76
Ruptured aortic aneurysm. Refuses surgery. His brain is removed without consent. His ashes are scattered at an undisclosed location.
