Maria Skłodowska-Curie

The woman who measured the invisible, named the unnamed, and refused to stop.

1867 – 1934

Warsaw, Russian Empire

Born Under Occupation

Maria Skłodowska came into the world on November 7, 1867, in Warsaw — a city then under the iron grip of the Russian Empire. Poland as a political entity had not existed since 1795, partitioned among Russia, Prussia, and Austria. Warsaw was Russian. The Polish language was suppressed in schools by decree. To be educated in one's own tongue was an act of resistance.

Her father, Władysław Skłodowski, was a physics and mathematics teacher — a man who kept a barometer, a spectroscope, and mineral specimens in the family apartment, objects of curiosity that a small girl named Maria could examine. Her mother, Bronisława, ran a boarding school for girls until tuberculosis forced her to give it up. Maria was the youngest of five children. By the time she was ten, her mother was dead. By eleven, her eldest sister Zofia had died of typhus. Grief and intellect shaped her in equal measure.

The Russian educational system in Warsaw tolerated secondary education for girls in Polish but barred them from university. Higher education — real higher education — meant leaving Poland. And leaving Poland required money that the Skłodowski family did not have.

"I was taught that the way of progress was neither swift nor easy."

— Marie Curie

The Flying University

Before Paris, there was the Floating University — in Polish, Uniwersytet Latający. A clandestine network of educators and students who met in private apartments, rotating locations so the Russian authorities could not find them. Maria Skłodowska attended, studying chemistry, anatomy, and sociology in secret. The penalty for participants was deportation to Siberia. They came anyway.

This was not merely education. It was an assertion that Polish minds belonged to Polish people. It shaped in Marie a conviction she would carry for the rest of her life: that knowledge is a form of freedom, and that its restriction is a form of violence.

The Skłodowska Pact

An Agreement Between Sisters

Maria's elder sister Bronisława — Bronia — wanted to study medicine in Paris. The École de Médecine at the Sorbonne was one of very few universities in Europe that formally admitted women. But a passage to Paris and years of tuition and lodging cost more than either sister possessed.

They made an agreement. Maria would work as a governess in Poland — a position she found socially diminishing but economically necessary — and send the bulk of her wages to Paris to support Bronia through medical school. Once Bronia qualified, she would support Maria's own studies in turn.

For five years, from 1886 to 1891, Maria Skłodowska worked in the houses of the wealthy, teaching their children, fighting the loneliness of rural Poland, and studying on her own in the evenings. She wrote letters to Bronia. She read mathematics by lamplight. She kept a promise.

The Governess Years — What She Taught Herself

During her years in service, Maria studied sociology, anatomy, and physics from textbooks. She also ran informal lessons for the children of local peasants — this too was a punishable act under Russian law. She did it anyway. "I describe my situation as it really is," she wrote to her brother, "so that you do not think I am satisfied, while in reality I feel a great emptiness and depression."

In 1891, Bronia wrote: come now. She was married, qualified, practicing medicine in Paris. She had a room. Maria, twenty-four years old, packed everything she owned and boarded a train for a four-day journey west across Europe, sitting in fourth class to save money, wrapped in a blanket she had brought from Warsaw.

Paris, 1891

The Sorbonne

She enrolled in the Faculty of Sciences at the University of Paris — known universally as the Sorbonne — in November 1891, registering as "Marie" rather than "Maria," a small Gallicization she adopted and kept. She was one of only twenty-three women in the entire Faculty of Sciences. Many of her male classmates were younger than her. None of them had lived in secret education.

She moved into a sixth-floor garret in the Latin Quarter, closer to the university than Bronia's apartment in La Villette. In winter the water in her wash basin froze. She sometimes fainted from hunger during lectures because she had forgotten to eat. She earned her licence in physics in 1893, first in her class. She earned her licence in mathematics in 1894, second in her class.

She had intended to return to Poland. A fellowship had been established to bring Polish students to Paris for a year; she had used it. She had planned to go home, to find a teaching position in Warsaw, to contribute to the national resistance by educating Polish children. She stayed in Paris because of a steel manufacturer's commission — and because of Pierre Curie.

A Scientific Marriage

Pierre Curie

They met in the spring of 1894, introduced by a Polish physicist named Józef Kowalski who knew that Marie needed laboratory space and that Pierre, head of the laboratory at the School of Physics and Chemistry of the City of Paris, had it. Pierre was thirty-four. Marie was twenty-six. He had already discovered piezoelectricity with his brother Jacques. He was, like Marie, singularly devoted to science — and, by the standards of his time, unusually uninterested in the social performance of masculinity that surrounded it.

He wrote to her: "It would be a beautiful thing, a thing I dare not hope, if we could spend our life near each other, hypnotized by our dreams: your patriotic dream, our humanitarian dream, and our scientific dream." She went back to Poland that summer. He wrote more letters. She returned.

They married on July 26, 1895, in a civil ceremony in Sceaux. Marie wore a dark blue dress — chosen deliberately for practicality, so she could return to the laboratory in it afterward. No wedding rings. No church. A bicycle trip in Brittany for a honeymoon, funded by a small inheritance. They rode every day, stopped at farms for bread and milk, and carried scientific papers in their panniers.

The Collaboration

Pierre and Marie Curie were not a scientist and his wife. They were two scientists who chose each other as partners in work and in life, which in their case were largely the same thing. When Pierre's notebooks and Marie's are compared — as historians have done — the handwriting shifts mid-experiment, mid-thought. They worked simultaneously on the same datum, checked each other's readings, argued in the margins. It was a genuinely shared scientific enterprise, rare in any era.

Family

Irène, Ève, and the Cart Horse

Their first daughter, Irène, was born in September 1897. Marie took eleven days away from the laboratory. She returned to weighing pitchblende. Irène was raised in part by Pierre's father, Eugène Curie, who moved in with them when Pierre's mother died. The household was a scientific commune: a grandfather who had been a physician, two parents who spent their evenings reading research papers, a child who grew up assuming that science was simply what adults did.

Their second daughter, Ève, was born in December 1904. Ève did not become a scientist. She became a journalist and writer, and she wrote what remains the most intimate biography of her mother — Madame Curie, published in 1937, three years after Marie's death.

On April 19, 1906, Pierre Curie walked across the Rue Dauphine in heavy rain, preoccupied, possibly blinded by his umbrella, and was struck by a horse-drawn wagon loaded with military supplies. He died instantly of a fractured skull. He was forty-six.

"Everything is over. Pierre is dead. Dead. Dead. What is there to say? I am nothing but a wretched creature, lost, blind, feeble, who can only say: I am suffering. I suffer so much. I never knew how much Pierre meant to me."

— Marie Curie, private journal, April 1906

The University of Paris offered Pierre's chair in physics to Marie. It was unprecedented. No woman had ever held a professorship at the Sorbonne. She accepted. Her first lecture, on November 5, 1906, continued Pierre's last lecture — at exactly the point where he had stopped. She did not mention him by name. The hall was packed with journalists, photographers, curious Parisians, students who had come to witness history, and a woman in black who stood at the blackboard and began discussing ionization theory.

Material Consequence

The Radioactive Notebooks

Marie Curie died on July 4, 1934, in a sanatorium in Passy, in the French Alps. The cause of death was aplastic anemia — a bone marrow failure caused by decades of exposure to ionizing radiation. Her notebooks, her personal belongings, her cookbooks, the doorknobs of her laboratory: all remain radioactive today. The primary isotope responsible is radium-226, with a half-life of 1,601 years.

Her research notebooks are stored at the Bibliothèque nationale de France in lead-lined boxes. Researchers who wish to examine them must sign a liability waiver acknowledging the health risks. They are provided with protective equipment. The notebooks glow with an energy that Marie Curie spent her life measuring, naming, and deploying — and that her body absorbed without her knowing the mechanism of its lethality.

1st Person to win two Nobel Prizes

She never wore gloves in the laboratory. She carried test tubes of radioactive isotopes in her coat pockets. She described radium as having "a beautiful glow in the dark" — she kept a piece of it on her bedside table to look at at night. She was not ignorant of the dangers in the abstract; she knew radiation burned the skin, knew it caused the persistent sores on her hands. She did not know, and could not have known in the scientific framework available to her, that it was destroying her marrow.

First Person — Two Nobel Prizes

Marie Curie is the only person in history to have won Nobel Prizes in two different scientific disciplines. She won the Physics Prize in 1903 — shared with Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel — for the discovery of radioactivity. She won the Chemistry Prize in 1911, alone, for the isolation of radium and polonium. The Nobel Committee attempted in 1903 to award the prize only to Pierre and Becquerel. Pierre refused, writing to the committee that Marie's contribution was foundational. She was included. In 1911, with Pierre dead, there was no question of attribution. The prize was hers.

The Second Nobel

Irène Joliot-Curie

Irène Curie grew up in a household that treated science as a natural language. She assisted her mother with the mobile X-ray units during the First World War, beginning at age seventeen. She studied physics and chemistry at the Sorbonne. She married Frédéric Joliot in 1926; they worked together, as her parents had, and took the hyphenated name Joliot-Curie.

In 1935, one year after Marie's death, Irène and Frédéric Joliot-Curie received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for the synthesis of new radioactive elements — artificial radioactivity. Marie Curie had won two Nobel Prizes. Her daughter won one. No other family in history has produced two Nobel laureate generations in the sciences.

Irène Joliot-Curie died in 1956 of leukemia, almost certainly caused by radiation exposure. She had assisted her mother with the X-ray units. She had worked with radioactive materials her entire adult life. The radiation that her mother had discovered and named, that had killed her mother, killed her too.
