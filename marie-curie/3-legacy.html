Measurement — A Name in Every Lab

The Curie — Unit of Radioactivity

The curie (symbol: Ci) is a unit of radioactivity defined as the activity of one gram of radium-226 — the element Marie Curie isolated and whose properties she spent her career measuring. One curie equals 3.7 × 1010 disintegrations per second. It was adopted as the standard unit of radioactivity by the Radiology Congress of 1910 and named in honor of Pierre Curie — though the selection of radium-226's specific activity as the reference point was inseparable from Marie's work in isolating and characterizing it.

The unit was formally redefined after Pierre's death, and the naming was intended as a tribute to him alone. Marie Curie did not comment publicly on this. The irony — that the unit named for her husband was defined by the substance she had discovered — was not lost on historians, though it was apparently invisible to the physicists who made the naming decision.

Today, the curie remains in use alongside the SI unit becquerel (Bq), defined as one disintegration per second. One curie equals 37 gigabecquerels. In nuclear medicine, reactor physics, and radiological protection, both units appear. The curie is especially common in the United States and in medical contexts. Every time a clinician orders a millicurie of technetium-99m for a bone scan, the unit traces back to a gram of radium isolated by hand in a leaking shed in Paris between 1898 and 1902.

Still Operating — 2026

The Curie Institutes Today

The Institut Curie in Paris, co-founded by Marie Curie in 1920, is one of Europe's leading cancer research and treatment centers. Its hospital treats thousands of patients annually for cancers of the breast, eye, brain, and other sites. Its research laboratories occupy the same block as the historic building where Marie worked, now a UNESCO-listed historical monument. Researchers at the Institut Curie today work in molecular oncology, immunotherapy, and radiobiology — in some respects the direct continuation of the physics-medicine bridge that Marie deliberately built into the institution's architecture.

The Maria Skłodowska-Curie National Research Institute of Oncology in Warsaw — the descendant of the Radium Institute she founded in 1932 with her sister Bronia as director — is Poland's national cancer center. It operates in multiple locations across Poland, trains oncologists, and maintains the scientific mission Marie articulated at the cornerstone laying: that the treatment of cancer with radiation is a technical problem requiring both scientific rigor and clinical application, and that these cannot be separated.

BnF Archives — Still Dangerous

The Radioactive Notebooks

Marie Curie's research notebooks, her personal papers, her cookbooks, and her furniture are stored at the Bibliothèque nationale de France (BnF). The notebooks are kept in lead-lined boxes. This is not a precaution against metaphorical danger. The pages of the notebooks are genuinely, measurably radioactive. Radium-226, with a half-life of 1,601 years, is the primary contaminant. The notebooks will remain too dangerous to handle without protective equipment for approximately the next 1,500 years.

Researchers who wish to consult the notebooks must sign a liability waiver acknowledging the radiation hazard. They are provided with gloves and protective equipment. The waiver is not a formality. The notebooks emit ionizing radiation at levels that exceed permissible occupational limits for unprotected exposure.

Bibliothèque nationale de France

The notebooks are not merely dangerous artifacts. They are also among the most historically significant scientific documents in existence — the primary record of the discovery of radioactivity, polonium, and radium, written in Marie Curie's own hand, with data, errors, corrections, and the accumulating evidence of one of the most consequential chains of reasoning in the history of physics.

The fact that they cannot be touched without gloves, and cannot be read without signing a health waiver, is itself a kind of final annotation: she absorbed what they contain. The books are still emitting what she absorbed. She died of it. They endure.

The Half-Life of Her Work

Ra-226 has a half-life of 1,601 years. In the year 3627 CE, the notebooks will have lost half their radioactivity. In the year 5228 CE, half of that. The work Marie Curie performed between 1897 and 1934 created a physical artifact whose danger will outlast all current human institutions, languages, and probably nations. The manuscripts are time capsules of a different kind: not preserved against the future, but dangerous to it.

Nobel 1935 — Year After Marie Died

Irène Joliot-Curie

Irène Curie was born in September 1897. She assisted her mother with the mobile X-ray units during the First World War starting at seventeen. She studied physics and chemistry at the Sorbonne — as her mother had. She married physicist Frédéric Joliot in 1926, and they took the combined name Joliot-Curie — a deliberate honoring of the lineage, and a statement that the name Curie would continue in science.

In 1934, working together, Irène and Frédéric Joliot-Curie created the first artificially radioactive isotopes: by bombarding aluminum foil with alpha particles from polonium (itself discovered by Marie), they produced radioactive phosphorus-30, which did not occur naturally. They had created radioactivity where none had existed. The results were announced in January 1934. Marie Curie, dying in her laboratory at the time, read the paper. She held the glowing test tubes in her hands. She said — according to Frédéric — that it was the greatest moment of scientific discovery she had witnessed since the early days with Pierre.

Irène Joliot-Curie died on March 17, 1956. She was fifty-eight. The cause was leukemia. She had been exposed to radiation throughout her life — during the wartime X-ray units at seventeen, and through decades of work with radioactive materials in the laboratory. The same exposure that created her scientific career ended it, and her life, at the same age her mother was when she won her second Nobel Prize.

Periodic Table — Element 96

Curium

In 1944, Glenn Seaborg, Ralph James, and Albert Ghiorso at the University of California, Berkeley produced element 96 — a transuranic actinide synthesized by bombarding plutonium-239 with alpha particles. They named it curium, symbol Cm, in honor of Marie and Pierre Curie. It was announced in 1945.

Curium is a radioactive metal. It does not occur naturally on Earth. All of its isotopes are radioactive. The most stable, Cm-247, has a half-life of 15.6 million years. Cm-244, the most commonly produced, has a half-life of 18.1 years and is used as an alpha particle source in instruments including the Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometers aboard the Mars Exploration Rovers and the Curiosity and Perseverance rovers. The element named for Marie Curie has been on Mars since 2004, characterizing the Martian soil.

Marie Curie on Mars

The Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometers (APXS) mounted on the Mars rovers Spirit, Opportunity, Curiosity, and Perseverance use curium-244 as an alpha particle source to measure the elemental composition of Martian rocks and soil. The technique is directly related to the alpha particle bombardment experiments Irène and Frédéric Joliot-Curie used to create artificial radioactivity in 1934. The element named for Marie Curie, using a technique descended from her daughter's work, has been operating on the surface of another planet for more than twenty years.

Direct Ancestor — Modern Oncology

Radiotherapy & Nuclear Medicine

Every cancer patient who receives radiation treatment — external beam radiation, brachytherapy, radioiodine therapy, stereotactic radiosurgery, proton therapy — is receiving a treatment whose intellectual and institutional lineage runs directly to Marie Curie's laboratory.

The chain of descent is specific. Curie proved that radioactivity was an atomic property and measured it quantitatively. She established that it could be measured precisely, which meant it could be dosed. She co-founded an institution — the Institut du Radium, 1920 — explicitly designed to connect radiation physics with cancer treatment. That institution developed the first systematic protocols for radiation therapy. Those protocols were refined, quantified, and spread globally through the second half of the twentieth century into the treatments that are now standard worldwide.

  • 1898 Discovery of radioactivity as measurable atomic property. Establishes that ionizing radiation can be quantified — a precondition for any therapeutic dosing.
  • 1901 First clinical observations of radium's effect on tumors — by Henri-Alexandre Danlos at the Hôpital Saint-Louis, Paris, using radium supplied by the Curies. This was the first clinical application of radioactivity.
  • 1914–18 Mobile radiography (petites Curies). Establishes that diagnostic radiation can be deployed in clinical settings outside hospitals — the infrastructure of mobile and emergency radiology.
  • 1920 Institut du Radium co-founded. The explicit institutional bridge between radiation physics and oncology. Claudius Regaud's biological wing pioneered fractionated radiotherapy.
  • 1934 Artificial radioactivity (Irène and Frédéric Joliot-Curie). Opens the possibility of producing custom radioisotopes for specific therapeutic applications — the basis of nuclear medicine.
  • 1951 First medical linear accelerator for radiation therapy deployed at Hammersmith Hospital, London. Built on decades of dosimetry and clinical protocol developed at institutions Marie Curie founded or inspired.
  • 2026 Approximately half of all cancer patients receive radiation therapy at some point in their treatment. Globally, tens of millions of radiation treatments are performed annually. Each one is a direct clinical descendant of Marie Curie's work.

Institutional Honor

UNESCO Marie Curie Actions

The Marie Skłodowska-Curie Actions (MSCA) are the European Union's flagship fellowship and mobility programme for researchers. Named for Marie Curie and managed under the European Commission's Horizon research funding framework, the MSCA fund postdoctoral fellowships, doctoral networks, and staff exchanges for researchers across all disciplines, prioritizing cross-border and cross-sector mobility.

Since 1996, the Marie Curie Actions have funded over 200,000 researchers. The fellowship is competitive and prestigious — in many fields, a Marie Curie Fellowship is the most sought-after early-career research award in Europe. The naming was deliberate: a program supporting individual researchers to cross national boundaries and institutional borders, carrying their knowledge from one context to another, was an appropriate tribute to a woman who had crossed from Warsaw to Paris carrying little but capability and discipline.

Panthéon — 1995

First Woman Honored on Her Own Merit

In 1995, Marie and Pierre Curie were reinterred at the Panthéon in Paris — the national mausoleum of distinguished French citizens. Marie became the first woman to be honored there on her own merit (not by association with a man). French President François Mitterrand attended the ceremony.

Currency

Polish Banknotes

Marie Curie appeared on Polish 20,000 złoty notes in the 1980s and on the current 20 złoty note. She is the most depicted Polish person on Polish currency — a distinction she would likely have found amusing, given that Poland did not exist when she was born.

The Price of the Work

The Real Cost

Marie Curie absorbed lethal doses of ionizing radiation over a career spanning thirty-seven years. She did not stop. Not because she did not know there was a risk — she had seen radiation burns on her hands and Pierre's hands since 1899. Not because she was reckless — she recommended precautions for other researchers that she did not consistently apply to herself. She did not stop because the work required not stopping, and because in the early decades of radioactivity research there was no theoretical framework available to anyone that adequately explained the mechanism of radiation injury at the cellular level.

The X-rays she had used to image soldiers' bones during the war — approximately one million procedures, many of which she was present for — added substantial cumulative exposure on top of decades in the radium laboratory. Her blood counts in her final years showed the progressive failure of bone marrow that characterizes aplastic anemia. She suffered persistent fatigue, weight loss, and visual disturbances. She continued working. Her last major publication appeared in 1933. She entered a sanatorium in March 1934. She died on July 4, 1934.

Aplastic Anemia

Aplastic anemia is a condition in which the bone marrow fails to produce sufficient blood cells — red cells, white cells, and platelets. Without red cells, the blood cannot carry oxygen. Without white cells, the immune system fails. Without platelets, blood does not clot. It can be caused by ionizing radiation destroying the hematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow. Marie Curie's death certificate lists aplastic anemia as the cause of death. Her physicians noted that her blood showed "multiple lesions." There was no effective treatment. There still is not, except bone marrow transplantation — which did not exist in 1934.

The Curie family's exposure did not end with Marie. Irène Joliot-Curie died of leukemia in 1956. Frédéric Joliot-Curie died in 1958 of liver disease, possibly related to radiation exposure. The laboratory at the Institut Curie that Marie built was not adequately decontaminated for decades after her death — researchers who worked there after her continued to be exposed to elevated levels of residual radioactivity. The cost of the original work continued to be paid in the bodies of those who worked in the spaces it had created.
