The Three Laws

Ellipses · Equal Areas · Harmonic Periods

The Mathematical Grammar of the Solar System

Three laws. Published between 1609 and 1619. Together they constitute the most precise mathematical description of planetary motion ever produced — a description so exact that Isaac Newton, sixty years later, was able to prove all three as consequences of a single underlying principle: the inverse-square law of gravity. Every spacecraft ever launched, every satellite orbit ever planned, every GPS signal ever triangulated has been calculated using Kepler's laws as its foundation.

What makes Kepler's laws remarkable is not that they are elegant — though they are. What makes them remarkable is that they are correct. They are not approximations or models that work most of the time. They are exact descriptions of orbital mechanics under Newtonian gravity, which is itself an excellent description of nature at planetary scales. Kepler found physical truth through a combination of exhausting calculation, mathematical mysticism, and the willingness to trust observation over inherited philosophy.

The First Law: Ellipses (1609)

Kepler's First Law · 1609
The orbit of each planet is an ellipse, with the Sun at one of the two foci.

An ellipse is not merely a squashed circle. It is a specific geometric figure defined as the set of all points such that the sum of the distances to two fixed points (the foci) is constant. Every ellipse has two foci; when both foci coincide, the ellipse becomes a circle. The degree of "squashing" is described by the eccentricity (e): a circle has e = 0, a very elongated ellipse has e approaching 1.

The Sun sits at one focus — not at the center

The key conceptual revolution in the First Law is not the ellipse itself — ellipses had been studied by Greek mathematicians for centuries. The revolution is that the Sun is at a focus of the ellipse, not at its center. In every previous astronomical model, the Earth (or the Sun in Copernicus) was at or near the center of the planetary orbits. Kepler showed that the true organizing point of the solar system was a focus — an asymmetric position. The planet is closest to the Sun (perihelion) at one end of the ellipse and furthest (aphelion) at the other.

Earth's orbit has an eccentricity of about 0.017 — nearly circular. Mars, which Kepler studied, has an eccentricity of 0.093. Mercury has the most eccentric orbit of the eight planets at 0.206. These differences are small but they were exactly what Tycho's data revealed and what previous circular models could not accommodate.

Why It Matters

Not at the Center

The Sun at a focus (not the center) explains why planets move unevenly in their orbits — they slow down far away and speed up close in.

The Second Law: Equal Areas (1609)

Kepler's Second Law · 1609
A line segment joining a planet to the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal intervals of time.

This law explains why planets move faster when closer to the Sun and slower when further away. Imagine drawing a line from the planet to the Sun. As the planet moves along its orbit, that line sweeps out a triangular "wedge" of area. Kepler's Second Law says that these wedges — regardless of where in the orbit the planet is — always have the same area for the same time interval.

Equal areas swept in equal times — regardless of position in orbit

The physical significance of the Second Law is immense. It is, as Newton later proved, a consequence of the conservation of angular momentum — one of the most fundamental principles in physics. In any system where the force on an object is directed toward a fixed center (a "central force"), the object will sweep equal areas in equal times. Gravity is exactly such a force: it acts along the line connecting the planet to the Sun. Kepler discovered this empirically; Newton proved it as a theorem.

Physical Meaning

The Second Law means Earth moves fastest in January (perihelion, ~147 million km from Sun) and slowest in July (aphelion, ~152 million km). The difference: about 1 km/s — from 30.3 km/s at perihelion to 29.3 km/s at aphelion. This is why northern hemisphere winters are slightly shorter than summers.

The Third Law: Harmonic Periods (1619)

Kepler's Third Law · 1619
The square of a planet's orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis of its orbit.
T² ∝ a³

Published ten years after the first two laws, in the mystically framed Harmonice Mundi, the Third Law connects two separately observable quantities — how long a planet takes to orbit the Sun (its period T) and how far its orbit extends (its semi-major axis a). The ratio T²/a³ is the same for every planet in the solar system.

If T is measured in Earth years and a is measured in astronomical units (AU, the average Earth-Sun distance), then T²/a³ = 1 for every planet. This is one of the most powerful and surprising regularities in observational astronomy — a single dimensionless constant connecting objects from Mercury to Neptune, from tiny close-in orbits to vast outer ones.

The deviations from exactly 1.000 are due to the gravitational influence of the planets on each other (which Kepler's law ignores, assuming only Sun-planet gravity) and to measurement precision. For the Sun-planet two-body system alone, T²/a³ is exactly constant. The match across six orders of magnitude of orbital size — from Mercury at 0.387 AU to Neptune at 30 AU — is one of the most striking empirical confirmations of a physical law in the history of science.

What the Third Law Means Physically

Newton proved that the Third Law follows from the inverse-square law of gravity: F = GMm/r². If you work through the mathematics of circular orbits (and then extend to ellipses via the semi-major axis), you get T² = (4π²/GM) × a³, where M is the Sun's mass and G is the gravitational constant. The ratio 4π²/GM is the same for all planets orbiting the Sun — hence T²/a³ = constant.

The Third Law therefore contains, buried within it, information about the mass of the Sun. If you know G (which Newton didn't, but 18th-century physicists eventually measured), you can extract the Sun's mass from the orbital data of any planet. This is now the standard method for determining the masses of stars, black holes, and galaxies — measure the orbits of things around them, apply Kepler's Third Law generalized to any central mass.

First Law Summary

Orbits Are Ellipses

Not circles. Sun at one focus. Planets move between perihelion (closest) and aphelion (furthest) on a smooth elliptical path.

Second Law Summary

Equal Areas, Equal Times

Conservation of angular momentum. Planets speed up near the Sun, slow down far away. The product of speed × distance from Sun is constant.

Third Law Summary

T² Proportional to a³

The ratio T²/a³ is identical for every object orbiting the Sun. Extends to any gravitational system: moons around planets, stars around black holes.

Newton's Proof

Consequences of Gravity

Newton proved all three laws as mathematical consequences of F = GMm/r². Kepler found the pattern; Newton explained why.

"I have attested it as true in my soul and I rejoice inestimably... The die is cast, the book is written, to be read either now or by posterity, I care not which; it may well wait a century for a reader, as God has waited six thousand years for an observer." — Johannes Kepler, on discovering the Third Law (Harmonice Mundi, 1619)

From Observation to Law: The Method

What makes Kepler's achievement methodologically extraordinary is the process. He had Tycho's data — precise angular positions of Mars against the background stars, recorded over twenty years. He had to convert these into a three-dimensional orbit shape. He tried circular orbits with various positions, circular orbits with equant points (the Ptolemaic device that made circular orbits appear non-uniform), oval orbits, and finally — in what he described as a moment of desperation and revelation — ellipses.

The total number of distinct calculations he performed in the process of deriving the first two laws from the Mars data has been estimated at nearly 900. Each calculation was done by hand. He made errors; he corrected them. He held an eight-minute discrepancy between his best circular model and two of Tycho's observations as evidence that circular orbits were simply wrong. Eight arcminutes — less than a quarter of the angular diameter of the Moon — was enough for him to reject two millennia of circular astronomy.

This is the standard he set: that a theory must agree with observation precisely, not approximately. If it is off by eight arcminutes, it is wrong. This methodological commitment — more than any specific discovery — is perhaps Kepler's deepest contribution to science.
