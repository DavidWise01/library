Johannes Kepler

Born Weil der Stadt 1571 · Died Regensburg 1630

The Man Who Found the Shape of the Sky

Johannes Kepler was born premature and sickly, survived smallpox at age four (which damaged his eyesight and his hands), grew up in poverty, watched his father disappear into mercenary war, and spent six years of his scientific career defending his mother against accusations of witchcraft. He worked for a decade on a single astronomical problem — the orbit of Mars — filling reams of paper with calculations before discovering that the answer was not a circle but an ellipse. He is one of the greatest scientists in history.

Kepler's three laws of planetary motion did not merely describe the solar system accurately. They were the first physical laws ever discovered in a modern sense: precise mathematical relationships between observable quantities that held everywhere, always, without exception. When Newton proved these laws as consequences of his theory of gravity, the framework of modern physics was essentially in place. Every spacecraft trajectory, every satellite orbit, every GPS calculation traces back to what Kepler discovered by covering pages with arithmetic in a cold study in Prague.

Childhood and Early Life

Kepler was born on December 27, 1571, in the small German town of Weil der Stadt in the Duchy of Württemberg. His family was not prosperous. His father, Heinrich Kepler, was a mercenary soldier who left the family permanently when Johannes was five, likely dying in a military campaign somewhere in the Netherlands. His mother, Katharina, ran an inn. She was, by contemporary accounts, a difficult personality — sharp-tongued, argumentative, and deeply interested in herbal medicine and folk remedies. This last characteristic would bring her to trial for witchcraft decades later.

When Johannes was four, he contracted smallpox. He survived, but the disease left lasting damage: his eyesight was significantly impaired (he described his vision as distorted and weak for the rest of his life), and his hands were partially crippled. For a man who would spend his career doing observational astronomy and endless arithmetic calculations, these were serious handicaps. He overcame them through sheer intellectual effort and, when he had the resources, by employing assistants.

Despite his family's poverty, Kepler showed exceptional intellectual ability and received a formal education through the scholarship system of the Duchy of Württemberg, which sponsored the education of promising Protestant boys. He attended the Adelberg monastery school, then the Maulbronn seminary, then the University of Tübingen — all on scholarship. He had intended to become a Lutheran minister.

Tübingen and the Turn to Astronomy

At Tübingen, Kepler encountered Michael Mästlin, professor of mathematics and astronomy — and a secret Copernican. In public, Mästlin taught the Ptolemaic system (Earth at center) as the curriculum required. In private, he explained the Copernican heliocentric system to selected students. Kepler was one of those students, and the heliocentric system immediately made sense to him in a way that Ptolemy never had.

In 1594, before completing his theology degree, Kepler was offered a position teaching mathematics and astronomy in Graz, Austria. He accepted, somewhat reluctantly — he still planned to complete his theology training and become a minister. He never did. Mathematics consumed him.

In 1596, Kepler published his first major work: Mysterium Cosmographicum — "The Cosmographic Mystery." It was a strange book: he proposed that the distances of the planetary orbits from the sun could be explained by the five Platonic solids nested inside each other (cube, tetrahedron, dodecahedron, icosahedron, octahedron). The planets, he argued, traveled on spheres separated by these solids. The model was wrong. The actual orbital distances do not match the Platonic solid arrangement with any precision. But the attempt revealed something crucial about Kepler: he was looking for mathematical order in the cosmos, for deep structural relationships between geometric forms and physical reality. The laws he eventually discovered arose from exactly this kind of search.

"I wanted to become a theologian; for a long time I was restless. Now, however, behold how through my effort God is being celebrated in astronomy." — Johannes Kepler, in a letter to his former teacher Michael Mästlin

Tycho Brahe and the Data That Mattered

In 1600, Kepler received an invitation that would change his life: Tycho Brahe, the most famous astronomer in Europe, asked Kepler to join him in Prague. Kepler accepted.

Tycho Brahe (1546–1601) was an extraordinary figure — a Danish nobleman who had spent twenty years on the island of Hven, in the Danish Sound, building and operating the greatest astronomical observatory in pre-telescopic history. His instrument: large, precisely calibrated mechanical instruments — quadrants, sextants, armillary spheres — with which he measured the positions of stars and planets night after night, year after year, with an accuracy of about one arcminute. He accumulated twenty years of precise planetary position data. No one before him had anything comparable.

Tycho had his own cosmological model — a geo-heliocentric system in which the planets orbited the sun but the sun orbited the Earth — but he lacked the mathematical talent to turn his observations into physical laws. Kepler had exactly the mathematical talent Tycho lacked, but no data of sufficient precision. The partnership should have been perfect. It was not, personally.

The Tycho-Kepler Relationship: Necessary and Tense

Tycho was secretive about his data. He did not want Kepler to have free access to it; he was afraid Kepler would use it to vindicate Copernicus rather than Tycho's own model. He parceled out observations one planet at a time, keeping Kepler on a short leash. The two men had very different personalities: Tycho was flamboyant, aristocratic, and vain (he had lost part of his nose in a duel over a mathematical argument and wore a prosthetic copper nose); Kepler was intense, mystical, and obsessive. Their collaboration lasted eighteen months before Tycho died.

Tycho Brahe died on October 24, 1601. He had been taken ill suddenly after a long dinner at a nobleman's banquet. The immediate cause was most likely bladder rupture — he reportedly could not leave the table during the extended feast, and contemporary accounts suggest he was in agony in his final days. Centuries later, analysis of hair samples from his exhumed body (he had been reburied in a more prominent location) showed elevated levels of mercury — possibly from medicinal treatments, possibly from something more sinister. The poisoning theory has been debated but not definitively established. What is certain is that Tycho died before he could prevent Kepler from accessing his life's data.

Kepler was appointed Imperial Mathematician in Tycho's place. He now had the data.

The Imperial Mathematician in Prague

As Imperial Mathematician under Holy Roman Emperor Rudolf II (1576–1612), Kepler had official status, a salary (often unpaid or delayed), and access to Tycho's observations. He spent the next decade working through the data with ferocious intensity.

The problem he focused on was Mars. Mars was the most difficult planet to fit into a circular orbit model — its position deviated from Ptolemaic or Copernican circular predictions by amounts too large to ignore. Kepler tried circle after circle, adding and adjusting parameters, filling hundreds of pages with calculations. He estimated he worked through seventy separate attempts before the answer came to him.

The answer was that the orbit was not a circle. It was an ellipse. This seems like a small change but it was conceptually revolutionary. For two thousand years, from Aristotle through Ptolemy through Copernicus, circular orbits had been assumed to be necessary — partly from astronomy (the planets seemed to move in circles), partly from philosophy (the circle is the perfect form, and the heavens must be perfect). Kepler abandoned this assumption because the data required it. He trusted the data over the philosophy.

Collaborator

Tycho Brahe

20 years of precise planetary observations — the data Kepler needed. Died in 1601, leaving Kepler as Imperial Mathematician with access to the archive.

Patron

Emperor Rudolf II

Eccentric, science-loving emperor who paid Kepler's salary (irregularly). Prague under Rudolf was unusually hospitable to intellectual heterodoxy.

Method

Trust the Data

When circle after circle failed to match Tycho's Mars observations, Kepler abandoned the philosophical requirement for circular orbits. The data pointed to ellipses.

The Key Work

Astronomia Nova (1609)

"The New Astronomy" — the first publication of the First and Second Laws. One of the most important books in the history of science.

Personal Catastrophe: His Mother's Witch Trial

While Kepler was working through the most demanding scientific problems of his career, his personal life was collapsing. In 1615, his mother Katharina was accused of witchcraft in Leonberg, Württemberg.

The accusation had its origins in a neighborhood dispute. Katharina had always been an unusual woman — interested in herbal medicine, sharp in disputes, socially unconventional. A former friend, Katherine Reinbold, accused her of giving her a drink that had made her ill. The accusation of witchcraft followed. In the climate of the Thirty Years' War era, with religious tensions running high and witch trials epidemic in the Holy Roman Empire, such accusations were genuinely dangerous. Between 1615 and 1621, the case was prepared, investigated, and tried.

Kepler dropped everything to defend her. He spent six years managing her legal defense from a distance, writing detailed legal briefs, traveling to consult with officials, and advocating on her behalf through official channels. He was hampered by the fact that he himself was politically compromised — a prominent Copernican in a Catholic-dominated legal environment, operating during a war that was partly a religious conflict between Protestants and Catholics. He could not appear in person without risk.

Katharina Kepler was imprisoned in 1620. She endured more than a year in chains in a prison cell. She was subjected to territio verbalis — the formal showing of torture instruments with a description of what would be done if she did not confess — but she did not confess. In 1621, after Kepler had filed an extensive and ultimately successful legal defense, she was released. She died six months later, aged about seventy-four.

Six Years of His Life

Kepler spent six years defending his mother against witchcraft charges during the period when he should have been consolidating his scientific work. She was imprisoned for over a year, shown torture instruments, and survived. He drafted her legal defense himself — a 128-page document. She died six months after her release.

The Later Years: Linz and Sagan

After the loss of his first wife Barbara (1611), Kepler remarried in 1613 — Susanna Reuttinger, a young woman he had selected with unusual care from a list of eleven candidates he considered (he wrote about the selection process in letters with a mathematician's analytical detachment that many readers have found chilling). The marriage appears to have been reasonably happy; they had six children together.

In Linz, where Kepler served as district mathematician under Emperor Matthias, he published his Third Law in Harmonice Mundi (1619) — "The Harmony of the World" — a book combining genuine astronomical insight with an elaborate mystical theory about the musical harmonies of the planetary spheres. The Third Law (T² ∝ a³) was embedded in this mystical framework; Kepler found it by searching for mathematical harmonies in the data. The mystic and the scientist were never separable in him.

When Matthias died and Ferdinand II — a far more rigidly Catholic emperor — came to power, Kepler's position became precarious. He was a Lutheran in an increasingly intolerant Catholic court. His books were placed on the Index of Forbidden Books. He moved to Sagan in Silesia under the protection of the Duke of Friedland, Albrecht von Wallenstein.

Death in Regensburg

In November 1630, Kepler rode to Regensburg on horseback to try to collect a portion of the large salary debt the Imperial Treasury owed him. He had been owed the money for years; the Thirty Years' War had left the treasury effectively bankrupt. He arrived in Regensburg exhausted and became feverish within days. He died on November 15, 1630, aged 58.

He left behind three unpublished volumes of the Epitome of Copernican Astronomy and an unfinished science fiction story, Somnium ("The Dream"), about a journey to the moon — arguably the first work of science fiction. His grave was destroyed in the wars that followed. Nothing physical of him survived.
