The Central Fact

The Schrödinger Equation Is Quantum Mechanics

There is a sense in which the entire edifice of non-relativistic quantum mechanics — and through it, the whole of chemistry, most of condensed-matter physics, and the working theory behind every modern electronic device — reduces to a single statement: find the wavefunction, and you know everything that can be known. The instrument for finding the wavefunction is the Schrödinger equation. It is not one tool among many in quantum theory. It is the engine itself.

Classical mechanics has a comparable centre. Given the forces on a system, Newton's second law tells you how the state of the system evolves, and from that evolution every classical prediction follows. Quantum mechanics has exactly one equation playing this role, and it bears Schrödinger's name. The analogy is not loose or rhetorical: the Schrödinger equation occupies the same logical position in quantum mechanics that F = ma occupies in classical mechanics. It is the dynamical law of the microscopic world.

iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = Ĥψ
The time-dependent Schrödinger equation — the dynamical law of the quantum world

The time-independent form, used to find the stationary states — the orbitals, energy levels, and bound states that make up the visible structure of matter — is written Ĥψ = Eψ. This is an eigenvalue equation: the allowed energies E are the eigenvalues of the Hamiltonian operator Ĥ, and the corresponding wavefunctions ψ are its eigenfunctions. Solving it for the hydrogen atom reproduces the entire observed spectrum and gives the familiar shapes — the spherical s-orbitals, the dumbbell p-orbitals, the cloverleaf d-orbitals — that are taught in every introductory chemistry course on Earth.

Those orbital shapes are not decorative diagrams. They are literally the squared magnitudes of solutions to Schrödinger's equation, |ψ|², interpreted (following Max Born) as probability densities for finding the electron. The animated cloud at the top of this page is a depiction of exactly this: a p-orbital, a region of space where the wavefunction's amplitude is concentrated, drawn as a fog of probability rather than a particle on a track.

The Analogy Made Precise

What F = ma Is to Classical Mechanics, Ĥψ = Eψ Is to Quantum Mechanics

The comparison with Newton is worth making precise, because it captures why physicists and chemists treat the Schrödinger equation as the foundation rather than as a topic. In classical mechanics, F = ma is a second-order differential equation for the trajectory of a particle. Specify the forces and the initial conditions, integrate, and you obtain the entire future and past of the system. Everything else in classical mechanics — orbits, oscillations, collisions, the motion of fluids and solids — is an application of this single dynamical law to particular Hamiltonians or force laws.

In quantum mechanics, the Schrödinger equation is a first-order differential equation in time for the wavefunction. Specify the Hamiltonian — which encodes the kinetic and potential energy of the system — and the initial wavefunction, and the equation propagates the state deterministically forward in time. The wavefunction evolves with perfect determinism; only measurement introduces probability. Everything else in non-relativistic quantum mechanics — atomic structure, molecular bonding, the band structure of solids, tunnelling, scattering — is an application of this single equation to particular Hamiltonians.

F = ma

Specify the Hamiltonian and initial wavefunction; the state evolves. The dynamical law from which all quantum predictions descend.

The State

Trajectory vs Wavefunction

Newton tracks a point in phase space; Schrödinger tracks an amplitude over all of configuration space — a far richer object.

The Output

Certainty vs Probability

The wavefunction evolves deterministically; Born's rule converts |ψ|² into the probabilities we actually measure.

The difference, of course, is profound. Newton's trajectory is a definite path; Schrödinger's wavefunction is a complex-valued amplitude spread over all of configuration space, whose squared magnitude gives probabilities. But the structural role is identical. When a chemist computes a reaction pathway, or a device engineer models electron transport in a transistor, or a pharmacologist screens a candidate molecule, they are — directly or through layers of approximation — solving the Schrödinger equation. It is the law that everything else stands on.

The Reduction of Chemistry to Physics

Every Orbital, Every Bond, Every Molecule

Paul Dirac put the consequence with characteristic bluntness in 1929: with the discovery of quantum mechanics, "the underlying physical laws necessary for the mathematical theory of a large part of physics and the whole of chemistry are thus completely known." The Schrödinger equation, he meant, contains chemistry. The chemical bond, the periodic table, the geometry of molecules, the rates of reactions — all of it is in principle the working-out of the equation for systems of electrons and nuclei.

This was not an idle boast. Within a few years of the equation's appearance, Walter Heitler and Fritz London (1927) had used it to explain the covalent bond in the hydrogen molecule — showing how two hydrogen atoms lower their energy by sharing electrons, a result no classical theory could produce. Linus Pauling built on this to write The Nature of the Chemical Bond (1939), translating Schrödinger's mathematics into the structural language that chemists still use: hybridization, resonance, bond order. Pauling's Nobel Prize in Chemistry was, at bottom, a prize for applying the Schrödinger equation to molecules.

Every electron configuration, every Lewis structure refined into a molecular orbital diagram, every prediction of why oxygen is paramagnetic or why benzene is unusually stable — these are conclusions drawn, ultimately, from the equation. The periodic table's structure, the lengths of its rows, the existence of the noble gases — all follow from solving Schrödinger's equation for atoms and combining the solutions with the Pauli exclusion principle. Chemistry did not merely borrow from quantum mechanics; large parts of it became applied quantum mechanics.

"The underlying physical laws necessary for the mathematical theory of a large part of physics and the whole of chemistry are thus completely known."
— Paul Dirac, 1929, on the consequences of Schrödinger's equation
Solving the Unsolvable

Quantum Chemistry and Density Functional Theory

There is a catch in Dirac's pronouncement, and he stated it himself in the same breath: the equations are exactly soluble only for the simplest systems. The hydrogen atom yields to pen and paper. The helium atom — two electrons — already does not. For any molecule of real interest, the Schrödinger equation is far too complicated for an exact analytic solution, because every electron interacts with every other electron, coupling all their motions together in a high-dimensional problem.

The history of computational quantum chemistry is the history of clever, controlled approximation to a fundamentally intractable equation. The Hartree-Fock method treats each electron as moving in the averaged field of all the others. Post-Hartree-Fock methods — configuration interaction, coupled cluster, Møller-Plesset perturbation theory — systematically restore the correlations that the averaging discards, climbing toward the exact answer at ever-greater computational cost. These methods power the molecular modelling software used across chemistry and pharmaceutical research.

The most consequential breakthrough was Density Functional Theory (DFT). Building on theorems by Pierre Hohenberg and Walter Kohn (1964) and a practical scheme by Kohn and Lu Jeu Sham (1965), DFT reformulates the problem in terms of the electron density rather than the full many-electron wavefunction — collapsing an impossibly high-dimensional problem into a tractable one. Walter Kohn shared the 1998 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for it. DFT is today the single most widely used method in computational science: it is the workhorse of materials discovery, surface chemistry, catalysis, and battery research, run billions of processor-hours per year. Every one of those calculations is an approximate solution of Schrödinger's equation.

The Equation in Your Pocket

Semiconductors, Drugs, and Designed Matter

The reach of the Schrödinger equation into the material world is total and largely invisible. Consider the semiconductor. The behaviour of electrons in a crystal — why silicon is a semiconductor while copper is a conductor and diamond an insulator — is explained by band theory, which is the Schrödinger equation solved for an electron in the periodic potential of a crystal lattice. The band gap, the single number that determines whether and how a material conducts, is an eigenvalue structure of Schrödinger's equation. Every transistor in every processor, every LED, every solar cell, every laser diode is engineered using this quantum-mechanical understanding of electrons in solids.

The device you are reading this on contains billions of transistors whose operation — including the quantum tunnelling of electrons through thin barriers that both enables flash memory and threatens the miniaturization of chips — is described by Schrödinger's equation. Tunnelling, the ability of a quantum particle to pass through a barrier it classically could not surmount, is a direct and unmistakable consequence of the wave nature of matter encoded in the equation. It is exploited in the scanning tunnelling microscope, in tunnel diodes, and in the operation of modern memory.

In drug discovery, computational chemists model how a candidate molecule binds to a protein target, computing binding energies and conformations using methods that descend from the Schrödinger equation. Structure-based drug design, quantum-mechanics/molecular-mechanics (QM/MM) hybrid simulations, and the rapidly growing use of machine-learning potentials trained on quantum-chemical data all rest on the same foundation. When a pharmaceutical company designs a molecule that fits a binding pocket, the electronic structure governing that fit is, in the end, a solution of Schrödinger's equation.

Physics Reaches Into Biology

"What Is Life?" and the Aperiodic Crystal

In February 1943, Schrödinger delivered a series of public lectures at Trinity College Dublin, published the following year as the slim book What Is Life? In it, a physicist asked a biological question: how can the laws of physics account for the events in space and time that take place within the boundary of a living organism, especially the astonishing fidelity with which hereditary information is copied across generations?

Schrödinger's central argument was that the carrier of heredity must be a particular kind of molecule, which he called an aperiodic crystal. Ordinary crystals are periodic — the same small unit repeated endlessly, like wallpaper — and therefore carry very little information. A gene, he reasoned, must instead be a structure that is solid and stable like a crystal (so that hereditary information survives the thermal jostling of warm matter for decades or centuries) yet aperiodic, with its atoms arranged in a non-repeating sequence that could encode an enormous variety of instructions. The information of life, he proposed, is written in the precise sequence of an irregular molecular chain.

This was an extraordinary anticipation. The aperiodic crystal is, in retrospect, a description of DNA — a stable molecular structure whose information is carried in the non-repeating sequence of its bases. What Is Life? was read by a generation of physicists and biologists turning toward the new field of molecular biology. James Watson and Francis Crick, who would determine the double-helix structure of DNA in 1953, both credited the book as an influence that drew them toward the question of the physical basis of heredity. Maurice Wilkins, the third Nobel laureate of the DNA story, likewise cited it. A physicist's thought experiment about stability and information helped set the agenda for the molecular biology revolution.

"The most essential part of a living cell — the chromosome fibre — may suitably be called an aperiodic crystal."
— Erwin Schrödinger, What Is Life?, 1944

The book also introduced, in accessible form, the idea that living systems maintain their order by feeding on "negative entropy" — drawing order from their environment to stave off the equilibrium decay demanded by the second law of thermodynamics. The phrase was imprecise and Schrödinger later qualified it, but the underlying insight — that life is a thermodynamically open system sustaining low-entropy order at the expense of its surroundings — became foundational to biophysics and to the study of self-organizing systems.

The Most Famous Thought Experiment in Science

Schrödinger's Cat

In 1935, in a paper titled "The Present Situation in Quantum Mechanics," Schrödinger described the thought experiment that would, against his own intentions, become the single most recognizable image in all of modern physics. A cat is sealed in a steel chamber with a radioactive atom, a Geiger counter, a hammer, and a flask of poison. If the atom decays — a quantum event governed by probability — the counter triggers the hammer, which shatters the flask and kills the cat. After an hour there is, say, a fifty-fifty chance the atom has decayed.

The radioactive atom, before observation, is described by quantum mechanics as a superposition of "decayed" and "not decayed." Schrödinger's point was that the experimental arrangement entangles the fate of the macroscopic cat with this microscopic superposition. Taken literally, the formalism then requires the cat itself to be in a superposition of alive and dead until someone opens the box and looks. Schrödinger found this absurd — and that was precisely his point.

The cat is almost universally misremembered as a celebration of quantum weirdness. It was the opposite. Schrödinger intended it as a reductio ad absurdum — a demonstration that something was wrong, or at least deeply unsatisfying, about taking the Copenhagen interpretation's account of measurement at face value. If the equations seriously imply that a cat can be smeared between life and death, then either the equations need an additional ingredient (a genuine physical collapse, a many-worlds branching, a hidden variable) or our interpretation of what the wavefunction means is incomplete. The cat was an objection, not an endorsement.

That the objection has outlived its target is one of the ironies of scientific culture. The cat is now invoked to sell quantum computers, to title popular books, and to ornament a thousand explanations of superposition. But its enduring scientific value is exactly the one Schrödinger gave it: it sharpens the measurement problem — the still-unresolved question of how and when the smooth, deterministic evolution of the wavefunction gives way to the definite, single outcomes we observe. Modern work on decoherence has clarified how macroscopic superpositions become unobservably fragile, but the foundational question the cat poses remains live. It is the most famous unsolved problem in physics dressed up as a parable about a cat.

The Word He Coined

Entanglement: The Other Legacy of 1935

The same 1935 paper that gave the world the cat also gave it a word. Reflecting on the Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen argument, Schrödinger introduced the term Verschränkung — translated as entanglement — to describe the correlations between quantum systems that have interacted and can no longer be described independently of one another. He called it "not one but rather the characteristic trait of quantum mechanics, the one that enforces its entire departure from classical lines of thought."

He was right in a way that took decades to confirm. Entanglement, the phenomenon Schrödinger named and analyzed, is now the central resource of quantum information science. It is what makes quantum computers more than fast classical computers; it is what quantum cryptography exploits to guarantee security; it is what quantum teleportation transmits. John Bell's 1964 theorem turned the EPR debate into an experimental question, and decades of increasingly tight experiments — culminating in the work recognized by the 2022 Nobel Prize in Physics — confirmed that entanglement is real and that no local hidden-variable theory can reproduce it.

Thus the two great conceptual offspring of Schrödinger's 1935 paper — the cat and entanglement — frame the two frontiers of contemporary quantum science: the foundational problem of measurement, and the technological promise of quantum information. Both grew from his refusal to be satisfied with the standard story.

The Equation's Newest Frontier

Quantum Computing and the Simulation of Nature

The latest field to rest squarely on Schrödinger's equation is quantum computing — and the connection is more intimate than for any prior technology. A quantum computer is, at its heart, a device that prepares a quantum state and lets it evolve under a Hamiltonian according to the Schrödinger equation, then measures the result. The qubits, the gates, the circuits — all are ways of engineering and steering the unitary evolution that the time-dependent Schrödinger equation describes.

Richard Feynman's original 1981–1982 motivation for the quantum computer was precisely this: classical computers struggle to simulate quantum systems because the wavefunction lives in an exponentially large space, but a quantum machine, being itself governed by the Schrödinger equation, could simulate other quantum systems naturally. The most anticipated "killer application" of quantum computing remains quantum simulation — using one controllable quantum system to solve the Schrödinger equation for another, intractable one, with direct applications to chemistry, materials, and drug design. The circle closes: the field aims to compute the very equation it is built from.

From quantum chemistry to density functional theory, from semiconductor band structure to drug binding, from molecular biology's founding metaphor to the qubits of a quantum processor, the through-line is a single first-order differential equation in time. Schrödinger wrote it during a holiday in the Alpine village of Arosa over the Christmas season of 1925–1926. Nearly a century later, it remains the equation against which the microscopic world is read.

"I don't like it, and I'm sorry I ever had anything to do with it." — and yet it became the most-used equation in the physical sciences.
— Erwin Schrödinger, on the probabilistic interpretation of his own equation
The Measure of a Legacy

The Equation Beneath the Modern World

It is difficult to name a piece of mathematics with a wider footprint on twentieth- and twenty-first-century life than the Schrödinger equation. F = ma built the world of bridges and engines and orbits. The Schrödinger equation built the world of atoms understood, molecules designed, materials engineered, semiconductors fabricated, and information processed at the quantum scale. The first governs the world we can see; the second governs the world that the visible one is made of.

Schrödinger himself never reconciled with the probabilistic interpretation that his equation, in Born's hands, made inevitable. He remained, with Einstein, a dissenter — convinced that nature could not really be as the Copenhagen school described it. The cat was his protest. But the equation transcended its author's discomfort. It does not require us to settle what the wavefunction is in order to compute, with staggering precision, what nature does. That is the mark of a foundational law: it works whether or not we have understood it.

Every chemistry student who draws an orbital, every engineer who designs a chip, every biologist who reads a genome as an aperiodic crystal of information, every physicist who entangles a pair of qubits, is an heir of a holiday in Arosa in the winter of 1925. The Schrödinger equation is not a chapter in the history of physics. It is the grammar of the physical world.
