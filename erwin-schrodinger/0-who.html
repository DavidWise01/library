Origins

Vienna, 1887: Austrian-English Beginnings

Erwin Rudolf Josef Alexander Schrödinger was born on 12 August 1887 in Vienna, the only child of Rudolf Schrödinger, a botanist and oil-cloth manufacturer, and Georgine Emilia Brenda Bauer, whose own mother was English. The Austro-English family connection gave young Erwin bilingual childhood fluency in German and English — a facility that would serve him well across a career that took him to Scotland, England, Ireland, and back. He spoke both languages without an accent in either, a rarity in the physics world of the time.

Vienna at the turn of the twentieth century was one of the greatest intellectual cities in Europe. The Habsburg capital hosted psychoanalysis (Freud was practicing there), atonal music (Schoenberg was composing), the Vienna Circle (Wittgenstein, Carnap, Schlick), modernist architecture (Loos, Wagner), and the emerging traditions of Austrian economics (Menger, Böhm-Bawerk). It was a city in which intellectual ambition of every kind was in the air, and where the educated middle classes regarded cultural and scientific engagement as part of daily life. Schrödinger absorbed this atmosphere deeply — throughout his career he wrote philosophy, history of science, and speculative biology as well as physics, and he never regarded these as separate from his scientific work.

He attended the Akademisches Gymnasium in Vienna — one of the most rigorous secondary schools in the Empire — where he excelled in mathematics, physics, and classical languages. He was equally comfortable with Goethe and Maxwell. He entered the University of Vienna in 1906 and was taught by Friedrich Hasenöhrl, one of the leading physicists in German-speaking Europe, who introduced him to the Austrian tradition of theoretical physics — one strongly influenced by Ludwig Boltzmann's statistical mechanics and by a sophisticated engagement with the philosophy of science.

The Academic Wanderer

Graz, Stuttgart, Breslau, Zurich: A Career in Motion

Schrödinger's academic career before the wave equation was marked by genuine scientific productivity but also by the dislocations of World War I (in which he served as an artillery officer on the Italian front) and by the instability of postwar Central European academia. He held positions at Jena, Stuttgart, and Breslau before accepting a chair at the University of Zurich in 1921. His work in this period ranged widely: atomic spectra, colour theory (he wrote serious papers on the theory of colour vision — a lifelong interest inherited from his reading of Schopenhauer and Helmholtz), the theory of specific heat, and mathematical physics.

At Zurich he encountered the new quantum mechanics being developed by Heisenberg, Born, and Jordan in Göttingen. The physicists meeting in Zurich formed an important secondary node in the quantum revolution — the Swiss-German tradition of Debye, Weyl, and Einstein intersecting with the Northern German tradition of Bohr and Heisenberg. Schrödinger attended the weekly physics colloquium at the Swiss Federal Technical University (the ETH) and was appointed to give a seminar on Heisenberg's matrix mechanics in the autumn of 1925. In preparing the seminar, he found the formalism deeply unsatisfying — it was mathematically correct but physically opaque, a table of numbers without visualisable meaning.

The dissatisfaction was creative. He was also reading de Broglie's 1924 thesis, which proposed that particles had wave properties (as Einstein had noted in correspondence). If particles were waves, then there should be a wave equation governing them. Schrödinger set out to find it.

December 1925 – January 1926

Arosa: The Christmas Inspiration

The story of how the Schrödinger equation was discovered has become one of the great scientific legends, partly because of its setting and partly because of what the setting implies. In late December 1925, Schrödinger went to the Alpine resort of Arosa in Switzerland for a Christmas holiday. He brought with him the de Broglie thesis, his notes on quantum theory, and — according to the best historical evidence — a lover whose identity remains unknown. His wife Anny did not accompany him; she knew of and apparently accepted his extramarital relationships as part of his nature.

Over roughly two weeks at Arosa, Schrödinger wrote down the equation that bears his name. He returned to Zurich in January 1926 and presented it at the ETH colloquium, reportedly to Peter Debye's amused comment that a proper wave equation needed a wave function — and now it had one. Between January and June 1926, Schrödinger published four landmark papers in the Annalen der Physik developing the equation, applying it to the hydrogen atom, proving its equivalence with Heisenberg's matrix mechanics, and working out its physical interpretation. These four papers, published within six months, constitute one of the most concentrated and consequential bursts of scientific output in the history of physics.

"I discovered it working with a very beautiful girlfriend. I always work better with a woman around."
— Erwin Schrödinger (reported), on the Arosa inspiration of the wave equation
Berlin and Exile, 1927–1938

Berlin, Oxford, Graz: Fleeing the Nazis

In 1927 Schrödinger accepted the most prestigious chair in German physics — the position at the University of Berlin that had belonged to Planck. It placed him at the centre of German theoretical physics alongside Einstein. But the republic that made such appointments possible was already under pressure, and by 1933 it was over. When Hitler became Chancellor in January 1933, Schrödinger was appalled. He was not Jewish (he had Jewish colleagues and friends whom he valued), but he was a convinced anti-Nazi and could not remain in Berlin under the new regime. In the autumn of 1933, he left Germany for Oxford, declaring publicly that he found the situation intolerable.

At Oxford, Schrödinger learned that he and Dirac had been jointly awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics for 1933. The Nobel citation honoured him for the discovery of new productive forms of atomic theory — the wave equation. He was forty-six years old. The prize brought both recognition and the practical problem of where to settle: Oxford's college system was not well suited to his unconventional domestic arrangements (he had proposed bringing both his wife and his mistress to live with him, which the Magdalen College authorities found irregular).

He spent periods in Oxford and then, after the 1934 Anschluss — Germany's annexation of Austria — he could not safely return to his homeland. He was briefly back in Graz in 1936–1938, then fled immediately when the Anschluss made Austria German. He went first to Rome, then was invited to Dublin by Éamon de Valera — then Taoiseach and himself a mathematician of some accomplishment — to help found the Dublin Institute for Advanced Studies.

Dublin, 1940–1956

Dublin: Seventeen Productive Years

The Dublin Institute for Advanced Studies, founded in 1940 partly to attract Schrödinger, became his intellectual home for seventeen years. Ireland, neutral during the war, was a calm haven compared to the continental catastrophe. Schrödinger flourished there. He gave public lectures, wrote philosophy, corresponded with physicists and biologists worldwide, and produced his most widely read work: What is Life? (1944), delivered as a series of lectures at Trinity College Dublin and published in 1944.

What is Life? is a remarkable book — a theoretical physicist speculating about the molecular basis of heredity, asking what kind of physical structure could carry the information needed for biological reproduction and development. Schrödinger argued that genetic information must be stored in an "aperiodic crystal" — a molecule with a regular chemical structure but variable information content, like a code. This was nine years before Watson and Crick's double helix. Both Watson and Crick have stated that reading What is Life? was a direct inspiration for their quest for the structure of DNA. The book's influence on mid-twentieth-century biology was profound and traceable.

"We have to ask: is there perhaps some physical structure that is stable enough to carry hereditary information through many generations? I believe the answer is an aperiodic solid — what we might today call a code-script."
— Erwin Schrödinger, "What is Life?" (1944)
The Person

An Unconventional Life

Schrödinger married Annemarie ("Anny") Bertel in 1920, and the marriage lasted until his death in 1961 — despite, or perhaps because of, a complete understanding that both parties maintained independent emotional and romantic lives. Anny had relationships of her own. Schrödinger had a series of significant relationships with other women, some of which produced children. His domestic arrangements were unusual by almost any convention of the time, but they were openly acknowledged within his social circle and apparently workable for those involved.

He was a man of wide culture: he read Sanskrit (and translated Vedantic texts — his engagement with Eastern philosophy influenced his interpretation of quantum mechanics), knew Greek, Latin, English, French, and Spanish, wrote poetry, and engaged seriously with philosophy, history, and biology throughout his life. His book My View of the World (1961) is a philosophical memoir exploring consciousness, the nature of the self, and the relationship between science and human experience. His Nature and the Greeks (1954) traces the intellectual genealogy of science back to pre-Socratic Greek thought.

Thought Experiment · 1935

Schrödinger's Cat

Designed to show the absurdity of the Copenhagen interpretation applied to macroscopic objects. Became the most famous thought experiment in science.

Book · 1944

"What is Life?"

Predicted genetic information is stored in an "aperiodic crystal." Watson and Crick cited it as a direct inspiration. Nine years before the double helix.

Return · 1956

Vienna in Old Age

Returned to Vienna in 1956 as a triumphant figure, appointed professor at the University of Vienna. Died 4 January 1961, aged 73.
