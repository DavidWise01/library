Volume 0 of 5 — The Neo Omnibus

NEO

“I know you’re out there. I can feel you now. I know that you’re afraid… afraid of us.” — The man who was born twice: once as Thomas Anderson, once as the question itself.

Dossier

Who He Was

By day he was Thomas A. Anderson — an unremarkable software developer at a respectable software company called Metacortex, one cubicle among thousands, a man who paid his taxes, helped his landlady carry out her garbage, and lived an ordinary life inside an ordinary 1999.

By night he lived under another name. Neo — a hacker. He wrote programs that were illegal, sold them quietly, and spent his hours at a glowing monitor searching for something he could not name. He was guilty of virtually every computer crime there is a law for. But the crimes were never the point. The searching was the point.

What he was looking for was an answer — and he did not even fully know the question. The question had been with him his whole life, like a splinter in the mind, driving him mad. It was the question that brought him to Morpheus. It was the question that every awakened soul in this story is asked, sooner or later:

“What is the Matrix?”The question Neo could not stop asking
Subject File

Two Names, One Man

Identity — Daylight

Thomas A. Anderson

Resident of room 101. Employee of Metacortex. Has a social security number, pays his bills, and is — on paper — a perfectly ordinary citizen of a world that does not exist. The name the system gave him.

Identity — Midnight

Neo

The hacker alias. An anagram-adjacent rearrangement of “One.” The name he gave himself. Under it he chased rumors of a truth too large to fit inside the life he was living.

Diagnosis

The Splinter

“You’ve felt it your entire life — that there’s something wrong with the world. You don’t know what it is, but it’s there, like a splinter in your mind, driving you mad.” He felt it. He could not see, smell, or taste the bars of his cell.

The Prison

A Cell for the Mind

The deepest horror of Neo’s early life is that it was not a life at all. He had been born into bondage — born into a prison that he could not smell, or taste, or touch. A prison for his mind. The walls were not made of stone. They were made of information: a seamless, total simulation pumped directly into his nervous system, a dream he had mistaken for the waking world since the day he was decanted.

And yet some part of him knew. That is the engine of the whole story. The Matrix is perfect, but no prison is perfectly perfect to the prisoner who suspects the walls. Neo suspected. He stayed up all night at his screen because the dream had a flaw in it, and he could feel the flaw even if he could never quite find it.

wake up, neo...
the matrix has you...
follow the white rabbit.
knock, knock, neo.

The words appeared on his own screen, unbidden, in the dead of night — a message from someone who already knew his name. Then a knock at the door.

The Summons

The White Rabbit

Behind the door were buyers for his illegal software, and one of them had a tattoo — a small white rabbit on a girl’s shoulder. They invited him out to a club. He almost declined. Then he saw the rabbit, and remembered the words on his screen, and went. He followed the white rabbit — the oldest signal in the Western imagination for a person about to fall out of one world and into another, straight out of Alice in Wonderland.

At the nightclub, through the noise and the strobe, a woman approached him. He knew her name before she spoke it. Everyone knew it — she was a legend among hackers, the one who had cracked the I.R.S. database. Trinity.

“It’s the question that drives us, Neo. It’s the question that brought you here. You know the question, just as I did.”Trinity, at the club

She leaned close and told him she knew why he barely slept, why he lived alone, why night after night he sat at his computer. She knew because she had once been looking for the same man he was now looking for. And she told him the man would find him — if he wanted to be found.

The Bug

The Interrogation

Before the rescue came the warning. The morning after the club, at his desk at Metacortex, Neo received a delivery — a cellphone — and a voice on the line: Morpheus, the most dangerous man alive according to the authorities, the man Neo had spent his life searching for. Morpheus tried to guide him out of the building. The Agents were already there.

The Agents — men in suits and earpieces and mirrored sunglasses, faceless and identical, the immune system of the simulation itself. They took him. And in the interrogation room they did something that should have been impossible: they erased his mouth. His lips sealed over with skin. They held him down and planted a writhing, mechanical bug — a tracking organism — into his navel, into his body, through a belly that in the waking world did not exist.

He woke gasping in his own bed and told himself it had been a nightmare. It was not a nightmare. It was the first time the rules of the dream had been broken in front of him, deliberately, by the things that ran it. The world he trusted had reached into him and proven it could rewrite him at will. After that, there was no going back to sleep.

The Threshold

The Man Who Would Become The One

This is where Volume 0 ends — at the threshold, with Neo poised between two lives. He is not yet a hero. He is not yet anything. He is a tired man with a question, standing in the rain, deciding whether to get into a car driven by strangers because a legend named Trinity told him to.

Everything that follows — the pills, the pod, the war, the powers, the sacrifice — flows from this one quality in him: he could not stop wanting to know what was real. Morpheus believed that hunger marked him as The One, the prophesied figure who could reshape the Matrix at will. The Oracle would complicate that belief. But the belief began here, with an ordinary programmer who refused to accept an ordinary world.

“You’re here because you know something. What you know, you can’t explain — but you feel it. You’ve felt it your entire life.”Morpheus, to Neo

He had felt it his entire life. In Volume 1, he finally gets to choose whether to find out why.
