Post-War Netherlands

Dijkstra grew up in a Netherlands that had been humiliated and occupied — Rotterdam bombed flat in 1940, the country liberated only in May 1945 — and that was now rebuilding itself with characteristic Dutch practicality and determination. This was not a country for nostalgia. The rebuilt Rotterdam, rising from rubble, would become one of Europe's great modernist cities. The Netherlands would become one of the world's most prosperous and technically sophisticated societies.

Computing fitted the Dutch national character: precise, pragmatic, resistant to nonsense, interested in systems that worked reliably. The Mathematisch Centrum in Amsterdam, founded in 1946 as a research institute for mathematics and computer science, became one of Europe's first serious computing centers. It was here that Dijkstra came to work in 1952, and it was here that European computing — if there was such a thing as a European tradition — began to develop its own distinct character.

"I think the letterhead matters; the title matters; the paper matters. What is the use of a beautiful building if one cannot afford the proper stationery?" — Edsger W. Dijkstra, on why he handwrote and photocopied the EWDs rather than using a word processor
The Mathematisch Centrum, Amsterdam

The Mathematisch Centrum — now CWI, Centrum Wiskunde & Informatica — was one of a small number of institutions in Europe that took computing seriously as a research discipline from the very beginning. It had a computer — the ARRA, a Dutch-built machine — and it had mathematicians willing to think rigorously about what computing was and could be. This was the environment in which Dijkstra learned to program and in which he began to ask the questions that would define his career.

The early computing community was tiny. The people doing serious work could fit in a conference room — and often did. Dijkstra was in contact with British computing pioneers (he spent time in Cambridge), with the ALGOL committee, and with the small international network of people who were trying to understand what programs were. This smallness mattered: ideas spread quickly, controversy was personal and direct, and there was no insulating layer of management or academic politics between thinkers.

The Cold War Context

Unlike many of his contemporaries, Dijkstra had no significant involvement with military computing. He was not at Los Alamos. He was thinking about what programs could mean — not what weapons they could guide.

ALGOL 60 — The Language That Changed Everything

In the late 1950s, an international committee of European and American computer scientists set out to design a universal algorithmic language — a common notation for describing algorithms that could be implemented on any machine. The result was ALGOL 60: a language that introduced block structure, recursive procedures, and pass-by-value and pass-by-name parameter conventions. It was, by the standards of its time, a masterpiece of design.

Dijkstra was a contributor to ALGOL 60 and wrote the first ALGOL 60 compiler — for the Electrologica X1, a Dutch machine. This was not a small matter. Compiling ALGOL 60 required solving problems — particularly around recursive procedures and dynamic memory allocation — that nobody had solved before. Dijkstra solved them. The techniques he used (particularly for handling recursive procedure calls on a stack) became standard and are still the basis of compiler design today.

ALGOL 60 was also the language that made Dijkstra's career-long argument about structured programming concrete. Here was a language with proper block structure, proper scope, proper procedure calls. GOTO still existed in it — but now you could see what a program without GOTO would look like. The structured programming debate was, in part, a debate about whether to follow ALGOL's good instincts all the way to their logical conclusion.

The GOTO Wars

When Dijkstra's letter "Go To Statement Considered Harmful" appeared in CACM in March 1968, the response was extraordinary. For modern readers, accustomed to structured programming as the only option and GOTO as a historical curiosity, it is difficult to appreciate how controversial this was. The GOTO was not just a programming convenience — it was, for many programmers, the tool that made efficient code possible. Dijkstra was telling an entire profession that they had been thinking badly.

The counterattacks came quickly. Letters to CACM disputed his argument. Frank Rubin's 1987 letter in the same journal, provocatively titled "'GOTO Considered Harmful' Considered Harmful," argued that there were legitimate uses for GOTO in real programs. Donald Knuth — arguably the other leading theorist of algorithms — wrote a more nuanced paper arguing that GOTO could sometimes be the clearest way to express certain control flows, and that the ban should not be absolute.

Dijkstra was not persuaded. He had proved his point to his own satisfaction, and in his view, the counterarguments were largely motivated by inertia. What he found most irritating was not disagreement — he could respect a well-formed counterargument — but the assumption that existing practice was the standard against which his proposals should be measured. That, for him, was backwards. The question was not "what do programmers currently do?" but "what should programs be?"

"It is practically impossible to teach good programming to students that have had a prior exposure to BASIC: as potential programmers they are mentally mutilated beyond hope of regeneration." — Edsger W. Dijkstra, EWD 498, "How Do We Tell Truths That Might Hurt?"
Eindhoven — The Professor

In 1962, Dijkstra was appointed professor of mathematics at Eindhoven University of Technology. He would spend more than twenty years there, and this period produced his most systematic work: the THE operating system, the semaphore, the Dining Philosophers, the guarded command language, and the predicate transformer calculus.

Eindhoven was not a glamorous posting. It was a technical university in a provincial Dutch city, dominated by the electronics company Philips and by a culture of practical engineering. Dijkstra's appointment as a mathematician in a faculty of engineers put him in a permanently adversarial relationship with the institution — which suited him fine. He believed that engineers who did not understand mathematics were dangerous, and that programmers who did not understand proofs were dangerous, and that the most efficient way to make this argument was to demonstrate it by doing the mathematics.

The Tuesday Afternoon Club — his seminar, held weekly — became legendary. Dijkstra would sit at the head of the table. A student or visiting colleague would present work. Dijkstra would listen in silence. Then he would ask one question. The question was always devastating in the best sense: it identified the point where the speaker's understanding had actually stopped, and where the interesting problem actually began. Colleagues described these sessions as among the most intellectually intense experiences of their careers.

The People Around Him
Adriaan van Wijngaarden Mentor · Mathematisch Centrum
Director of the computing department at the MC. Persuaded Dijkstra that programming was a legitimate scientific career. Supervised the ALGOL project. Described by Dijkstra as the most intellectually rigorous person he had ever met.
Niklaus Wirth Colleague · ALGOL / Pascal
Swiss computer scientist; designed Pascal and later Oberon. Wirth and Dijkstra shared the view that programming languages should enforce discipline. Wirth added the famous title "Considered Harmful" to Dijkstra's 1968 letter when Wirth was editor of CACM.
C.A.R. (Tony) Hoare Ally · Structured Programming
British computer scientist; invented Quicksort; developed Hoare Logic for program verification. Hoare and Dijkstra were natural allies in the structured programming movement. Hoare's axiomatic semantics and Dijkstra's predicate transformers are complementary frameworks.
Donald Knuth Peer — sometimes adversary
Author of The Art of Computer Programming. Broadly agreed with Dijkstra's goals but took a more measured view of GOTO. Their relationship was respectful but intellectually tense. Dijkstra thought Knuth was too willing to accommodate bad practice for the sake of performance.
Ria Debets Dijkstra Wife · Companion
Maria (Ria) Debets, a colleague in computing, became Dijkstra's wife in 1957. She accompanied him to Austin and back. By accounts she provided the domestic stability that enabled his intellectual severity — a warmth that his professional persona did not always display.
The EWD System — A Deliberate Choice

Dijkstra began writing the EWDs — Edsger W. Dijkstra memos — in the early 1960s. He wrote them by hand, in his distinctive small cursive, on lined paper. He photocopied them himself. He maintained a list of recipients around the world — colleagues, former students, computing researchers — and mailed the copies to them. No email. No word processor. No publishing intermediary.

He had a specific reason for the handwriting: it made him slow down. Word processors allowed you to produce prose quickly, revise endlessly, never commit to a formulation. Dijkstra believed that the discipline of handwriting forced precision — you thought a sentence through before writing it, because revision was costly. The EWDs are accordingly spare, exact, and without wasted words.

By the time he died in 2002, he had written EWD 0 through EWD 1318 — 1,318 documents, covering algorithms, programming methodology, mathematics, computing education, philosophy of science, academic culture, and occasional personal reflection. The entire archive is now held at the University of Texas at Austin and is freely available online. Scholars, students, and practicing programmers still read them.

UT Austin
Austin, Texas — The American Years

In 1984, Dijkstra accepted the Schlumberger Centennial Chair in Computer Science at the University of Texas at Austin — one of the most prestigious endowed chairs in American computer science. He was sixty-four. He moved to Austin with his wife, and he spent the next fifteen years there.

Austin was a shock in some ways. Texas was not Eindhoven. The culture was more expansive, more boisterous, less precise. Dijkstra kept his habits: he wrote EWDs by hand, he rode his bicycle, he held seminars that resembled interrogations. He found students who were eager and able, and he taught them. He also discovered, in the informality of American academic culture, a freedom he enjoyed: nobody in Austin expected him to be diplomatic.

The Austin seminars — his "Tuesday Afternoon Clubs" transplanted to Texas — continued to be legendarily rigorous. One faculty colleague recalled that Dijkstra's questions in a seminar were so precisely aimed at the weakest point in an argument that some speakers were reduced to silence within minutes. He was not unkind. He was exact. In his view, the kindest thing you could do for a student or a colleague was to identify where their thinking actually stopped — not to congratulate them for the thinking they had done.

"Simplicity is a great virtue but it requires hard work to achieve it and education to appreciate it. And to make matters worse: complexity sells better." — Edsger W. Dijkstra, EWD 1243
The World's Timeline
  • 1945ENIAC operational — the first general-purpose electronic digital computer. Dijkstra is fifteen. The world has no idea what computing will become.
  • 1949EDSAC operational at Cambridge. Maurice Wilkes builds one of the first stored-program computers in Europe. Dijkstra learns of it during his 1951 Cambridge visit.
  • 1954FORTRAN development begins at IBM. The first high-level programming language. Dijkstra: FORTRAN was, at best, a useful accident.
  • 1959COBOL designed by Grace Hopper and committee. Dijkstra would later write that its teaching "should be regarded as a criminal offense."
  • 1960ALGOL 60 report published. Dijkstra wrote the first ALGOL 60 compiler. The language defines European computing's aesthetic for a generation.
  • 1964IBM System/360 announced. Commercial computing scales up. The gap between industrial practice and Dijkstra's ideals begins to widen.
  • 1968NATO Software Engineering Conference, Garmisch. The term "software engineering" coined. The "software crisis" declared. Dijkstra argues for mathematical rigor. Many attendees are skeptical.
  • 1970sThe structured programming movement spreads. BASIC and COBOL remain dominant in practice. Dijkstra becomes increasingly vocal about the failures of computing education.
  • 1972C language developed at Bell Labs. Practical, powerful, full of GOTOs. Dijkstra did not approve.
  • 1982Commodore 64 ships with BASIC. A generation of home programmers learns to program with GOTO. Dijkstra considers this a catastrophe.
  • 1990sJava rises. Dijkstra critiques it as a language that teaches poor thinking habits. "Computer Science is not about computers, and it is not about science, and its name is unfortunate."
  • 1999Dijkstra retires from UT Austin. Returns to Nuenen, Netherlands. Continues writing EWDs.
