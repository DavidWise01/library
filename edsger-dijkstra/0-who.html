Dijkstra

Born in Rotterdam, 1930. Died in Nuenen, 2002. He invented the shortest-path algorithm in twenty minutes at a café, banished the GOTO from civilized code, and wrote 1,300 handwritten memos that defined the ethics and aesthetics of computer science.

Rotterdam, 1930

Edsger Wybe Dijkstra was born on 11 May 1930 in Rotterdam, in a Netherlands still largely confident in its neutrality and prosperity. His father, Douwe Dijkstra, was a chemist — the president of the Dutch Chemical Society. His mother, Brechtje Cornelia Kluijver, was talented in mathematics. The household was intellectually saturated: scientific reasoning was not something done elsewhere by specialists; it was the texture of daily life.

The war came when Dijkstra was nine. Rotterdam was bombed in May 1940 — one of the most destructive air raids of the war, destroying the historic city center and killing nearly a thousand people. The family survived. But Dijkstra grew up in an occupied country, and this formative experience — of institutions that could be corrupted, of authoritative nonsense that killed people — would leave a lifelong suspicion of intellectual complacency dressed as consensus. He would spend his career asking: are we actually sure?

After the war he studied physics at Leiden University, the oldest university in the Netherlands. He was good. But physics at that moment was discovering computing, and computing was discovering itself. Dijkstra attended a short course in Cambridge in 1951 and encountered a stored-program computer for the first time. He returned to the Netherlands changed.

"The use of COBOL cripples the mind; its teaching should therefore be regarded as a criminal offense." — Edsger W. Dijkstra, EWD 498

The First Programmer

In 1952, Dijkstra joined the Mathematisch Centrum in Amsterdam — one of Europe's earliest computing research institutions — as what he would later describe as one of the first people in the world to call himself a programmer. This was not a modest self-assessment. It was a statement about the state of the art. There was no established discipline of programming. There were no textbooks. There were almost no precedents. You encountered a machine and you figured it out.

The machines were enormous, slow by any modern standard, unreliable, and deeply strange to reason about. But Dijkstra had a physicist's habit of reducing things to first principles and a mathematician's intolerance for vagueness. He began to formulate questions that nobody had yet articulated: what does it mean to write a correct program? How do you know a program does what you think it does? These questions would drive his entire career.

He was still nominally a physics student at Leiden, and in 1956 he went to his supervisor — the physicist A. Haantjes — and said he wanted programming to be his profession. Haantjes was blunt: programming was not a recognized field; a physicist would not hire him as a programmer. If Dijkstra intended this seriously, he would have to leave physics. He did. Officially, he dropped his physics studies in 1956, the same year he invented his most famous algorithm.

The Man

Dijkstra was famously difficult in seminars — but difficult in a specific way. He would sit through a presentation in complete silence, which was unnerving enough. Then he would ask a single question. The question was always precise. It usually revealed that the speaker had not quite understood their own work. He was not cruel; he was exact. He believed that precision in language and precision in thought were the same thing, and that sloppy language was therefore not just aesthetically offensive but intellectually dangerous.

He wrote everything by hand. Not as an affectation — or not entirely — but because he believed the act of writing slowly was itself a form of thought. He would draft an EWD memo, revise it carefully, and then photocopy it and mail it to a list of colleagues around the world. No email. No word processor. The EWD system ran from the early 1960s until his death in 2002: over forty years of hand-distributed thought.

He rode a bicycle everywhere. In Eindhoven, colleagues would see him pedaling to the university every morning regardless of weather. In Austin, Texas — a city built around the car — he still rode. The bicycle was, for him, a vehicle of appropriate scale: human-powered, human-speed, honest about what it was. He had no patience for systems that obscured their own workings.

"Computer science is no more about computers than astronomy is about telescopes." — Edsger W. Dijkstra

The Hostility to Nonsense

Opinions
05

Precision as Ethics

For Dijkstra, vague thinking was not an intellectual weakness — it was a moral failure. Programmers who could not reason precisely about their programs were dangerous. Imprecision in software kills people.

Philosophy
06

The Physicist's Training

Never completed his formal physics degree, but kept the physicist's habit of reduction to first principles. His programs were not guessed at; they were derived from specifications, like theorems.

Private Life

Dijkstra married Maria (Ria) Debets in 1957, and they had three children. By all accounts the domestic sphere was warm and stable — a counterweight to the intellectual severity that colleagues encountered. In Nuenen, the village where he spent his final years and where van Gogh had painted The Potato Eaters, he tended a garden and maintained the same disciplined daily rhythms he had kept his entire life.

He was diagnosed with renal cell carcinoma in the late 1990s. He retired from UT Austin in 1999 and returned to the Netherlands. His last EWD — number 1,318 — was written in April 2002, four months before his death. It was about teaching, and about beauty. He never stopped thinking about what computing should be.

A Dutch Sensibility

Dijkstra's Dutch identity is not incidental. The Netherlands has a long tradition of pragmatic intellectual rigor — a country that built itself against the sea through engineering and collective discipline. Dutch culture prizes directness to the point of bluntness; there is a word, doe maar gewoon, for the social code of not putting on airs, of being straightforwardly what you are.

Dijkstra was Dutch to his marrow in this respect. He said what he thought. He did not smooth over intellectual errors to be polite. When he wrote that COBOL "cripples the mind," he was not being provocative for effect — he was stating, in Dutch directness, what he actually believed to be true. His writings have no wasted words, no diplomatic hedging, no appeals to authority. They begin from axioms and proceed to conclusions, and they trust the reader to follow.

"The question of whether machines can think is about as relevant as the question of whether submarines can swim." — Edsger W. Dijkstra, EWD 898
What Computing Is

Dijkstra believed computing was a mathematical discipline — not an engineering trade, not a craft, not a form of applied physics. Programs are mathematical objects. Their correctness can be proved. Their complexity can be analyzed. The relationship between a specification and an implementation is one of logical entailment. If you cannot prove your program correct, you do not know that it is correct, regardless of how many tests it passes.

This view made him a radical — and made him enemies. Much of industrial software practice in the 1960s and 1970s was exactly what he said it was: guessing, testing, patching. He was asking programmers to do something much harder: to think before writing, to derive programs from specifications rather than patching until the output looked right.

"Testing shows the presence, not the absence, of bugs," he wrote — a statement so compressed and so true that it is still quoted in every software engineering course. His point was not that testing was worthless but that it was insufficient. Testing can never give you certainty. Proof can. And certainty was what he wanted.
