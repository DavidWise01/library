His World Restoration England · The Royal Society · The Rivals

Newton did not emerge from a void. He inhabited a specific, turbulent, extraordinary century — plague, civil war, the Restoration, and the birth of organized science. The men around him shaped him, and he shaped the century.

Restoration England — The World Newton Inherited

Newton was born into a country that had just executed its King. The English Civil War (1642–1651) had convulsed England throughout his infancy. By the time he reached Cambridge, Charles II had been restored to the throne (1660) and the cultural life of the nation was experiencing a peculiar, energetic renaissance — theaters reopened, scientific societies formed, and the old certainties of Aristotelianism began crumbling.

The Restoration was both libertine and intellectually explosive. The same decade that produced Nell Gwyn and Samuel Pepys's diaries also produced the Royal Society, the first serious English scientific journal, and the atmosphere in which the Principia would eventually be possible.

The Royal Society — Founded 1660

The Royal Society of London for Improving Natural Knowledge was founded in 1660 by a group of philosophers and gentlemen who had been meeting informally since the 1640s. Its motto — Nullius in verba ("Take nobody's word for it") — was a deliberate rejection of scholastic authority in favor of experiment and observation.

Newton was elected Fellow in 1672, when he presented his reflecting telescope. He was not an enthusiastic participant — he found the Society's meetings provincial and its members meddlesome — but he dominated it utterly from 1703, when he became President, until his death in 1727.

1660 Founded

Gresham College & then Crane Court

The Royal Society met first at Gresham College in London. Its early Fellows included Christopher Wren (architect of St Paul's Cathedral), Robert Boyle (Boyle's Law), John Evelyn, and Samuel Pepys. The Society commissioned, debated, and sometimes suppressed the great scientific papers of the era. Its Philosophical Transactions — first published 1665 — is the world's oldest continuously published scientific journal.

The Places That Shaped Him

1643 Birthplace

Woolsthorpe Manor, Lincolnshire

A modest limestone manor house in the village of Woolsthorpe-by-Colsterworth. The apple orchard where Newton reportedly observed the famous apple still exists. The house is now owned by the National Trust and preserved as a museum. Newton returned here in 1665–1666 during the plague closure of Cambridge and performed the most productive thinking of his life in its rooms and gardens.

1661 University

Trinity College, Cambridge

The largest of Cambridge's colleges, founded by Henry VIII. Newton entered as a sizar, doing menial work for richer students. He studied in his rooms for years at a time with almost no human contact. He was elected to a Fellowship in 1667 and succeeded Isaac Barrow as Lucasian Professor in 1669. He occupied the same set of rooms for decades. The Wren Library at Trinity holds his annotated copy of Euclid's Elements.

1696 London

The Royal Mint — Tower of London

Newton moved to London in 1696 as Warden (later Master) of the Royal Mint, housed within the Tower of London. He oversaw the Great Recoinage of 1696–1699, replacing England's badly degraded silver coinage. He applied his analytical mind to the task of detecting counterfeiters and prosecuted them with relentless energy. He personally attended interrogations and provided evidence at trials.

The People Around Him

Allies · Rivals · Contemporaries
H
Ally · Patron
Edmond Halley
1656 – 1742

Astronomer, mathematician, and the essential catalyst for the Principia. Halley visited Newton in Cambridge in 1684 to ask whether an inverse-square gravity would produce elliptical orbits. Newton said yes — he had already proved it. Halley recognized what he was holding and immediately pushed Newton to write it up. When the Royal Society declined to fund publication, Halley paid for it himself. He computed the orbit of the comet that now bears his name using Newtonian mechanics, predicting its return. It did return, in 1758.

H
Rival · Antagonist
Robert Hooke
1635 – 1703

Curator of Experiments at the Royal Society and one of the era's great scientists — Hooke's Law (elasticity), the compound microscope, coining the word "cell." Hooke and Newton clashed first over optics (1672) when Hooke dismissed Newton's theory of light as derivative of his own work. Later Hooke claimed credit for the inverse-square law of gravity. Newton, who loathed any suggestion of having borrowed an idea, never forgave him. After Hooke died in 1703, Newton became President of the Royal Society and Hooke's portrait — the only known image of him — disappeared.

L
Rival · Parallel Inventor
Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz
1646 – 1716

German polymath who independently invented calculus in the 1670s and published in 1684 — eight years before Newton. Leibniz's notation (dy/dx, ∫) is what we use today. Newton had priority by a decade but told almost no one. The dispute over credit became one of the most bitter in the history of science. The Royal Society investigated in 1712 and found in Newton's favor — but Newton had engineered the investigation himself. Modern historians credit both men independently. Leibniz died in 1716, largely ostracized; Newton eulogized him to his face then proceeded to poison his reputation.

B
Teacher · Predecessor
Isaac Barrow
1630 – 1677

First Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at Cambridge and Newton's teacher and patron. Barrow recognized Newton's extraordinary ability and, in 1669, resigned the Lucasian chair so that Newton — then only 26 — could be appointed in his place. He then became chaplain to Charles II. Without Barrow's extraordinary generosity, Newton might not have had the institutional security to produce the Principia.

F
Priority Dispute
John Flamsteed
1646 – 1719

England's first Astronomer Royal, stationed at the Royal Observatory at Greenwich. Newton needed Flamsteed's observational data to test his lunar theory but Flamsteed was protective of his unpublished observations. Newton eventually obtained and published Flamsteed's star catalog without permission. Flamsteed spent years buying up copies and burning them. Their relationship was one of sustained mutual contempt.

L
Friend · Philosopher
John Locke
1632 – 1704

The political philosopher and one of Newton's closest friends — one of very few people Newton trusted. They shared heterodox views on Christianity (both rejected the Trinity, a deeply dangerous position). Newton sent Locke a disturbing letter during his 1693 breakdown, accusing him of trying to "embroil" him with women. Newton later wrote a contrite apology. Locke understood him and forgave him.

The Secret Newton — Alchemy and Theology

Newton the Alchemist

Newton spent more time on alchemy than on physics. His surviving manuscripts include over a million words on alchemical and theological subjects — roughly ten times the length of the Principia. He maintained a laboratory in his Cambridge rooms where he worked through the night conducting alchemical experiments, often emerging at dawn red-eyed and exhausted.


He studied the Emerald Tablet, corresponded with alchemists across Europe, and collected every alchemical text he could find. He was searching for the prisca sapientia — the ancient wisdom — believing that the ancients had encoded the structure of nature in symbolic language. He referred to this search, in his notebooks, as the "quest for the vegetable spirit" that was the root of all matter.


The economic historian John Maynard Keynes, who purchased Newton's alchemical papers at auction in 1936, called him "the last of the magicians." Newton's 1693 nervous breakdown is sometimes attributed to mercury poisoning from years of alchemical experiments. Analysis of his surviving hair confirms elevated mercury levels.

Newton the Theologian

Newton was privately, profoundly heterodox. He rejected the doctrine of the Trinity — a position that was not merely eccentric but illegal in England under the Blasphemy Act. Had he been known as an Arian (as he was), he would have been stripped of his positions and possibly prosecuted.

He wrote extensively on biblical chronology, attempting to reconstruct the exact dates of ancient history using astronomical observations recorded in the Bible. He believed Daniel and the Book of Revelation were literal prophecies of historical events, and he spent decades trying to decode them. He believed the prophecies pointed to a final conflagration around the year 2060.

"Gravity explains the motions of the planets, but it cannot explain who sets the planets in motion. I see no reason to explain the wonderful order of the universe by anything but the wisdom and skill of an intelligent Being." — Newton, letter to Richard Bentley, 1692

Master of the Mint — The Great Recoinage

By 1696, England's silver coinage was in crisis. Clipping — shaving silver from coin edges — had become so pervasive that the official coins of the realm often contained less than half their face value in silver. Trade was collapsing. The government decided on a complete recall and recoinage.

Newton, appointed Warden in 1696 and Master of the Mint in 1699, applied his analytical mind to the problem with the same intensity he had brought to the motions of the planets. He oversaw the introduction of milled edges on coins (making clipping obvious), streamlined the minting process, and personally hunted counterfeiters.

Newton was particularly aggressive in pursuing William Chaloner, a notorious counterfeiter and informer who had embarrassed the Mint before Parliament. Newton personally gathered testimony and built the case that sent Chaloner to the gallows at Tyburn in 1699. He did not hesitate.
