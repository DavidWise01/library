Isaac Newton Who He Was · 1643 – 1727

A premature infant born on Christmas Day in a Lincolnshire farmhouse who became the architect of the modern scientific world — re-ordering the cosmos with pen, prism, and a singular, solitary mind.

Origins — Woolsthorpe, Lincolnshire

Isaac Newton was born on 25 December 1642 (Old Style, Julian calendar) — or 4 January 1643 in the Gregorian calendar now in use — at Woolsthorpe Manor, a modest limestone farmhouse in Lincolnshire, England. His father, also named Isaac Newton, had died three months before his birth; his mother, Hannah Ayscough, described the infant as so small he could fit inside a quart pot. He was not expected to survive.

He survived. More than survived: when Hannah remarried the Reverend Barnabas Smith in 1645 and moved to the next village, three-year-old Isaac was left in the care of his maternal grandmother, Margery Ayscough. This separation marked him. He later confessed, in a catalogue of his sins, to threatening to burn down his stepfather's house — with his mother and stepfather inside it.

"I do not know what I may appear to the world, but to myself I seem to have been only like a boy playing on the sea-shore, and diverting myself in now and then finding a smoother pebble or a prettier shell than ordinary, whilst the great ocean of truth lay all undiscovered before me." — Isaac Newton, reported by Joseph Spence, c. 1730

He attended the King's School in Grantham from around 1655, lodging with a local apothecary. He was, by most accounts, a mediocre student who came alive only when mechanics and mathematics captured his attention. He built elaborate mechanical models — sundials, wind-powered mills — and reportedly obsessed over the geometry of a comet's tail. His uncle recognized something and recommended Cambridge.

Cambridge — Sizar to Lucasian Professor

Newton entered Trinity College, Cambridge in June 1661 as a sizar — a student who earned his keep by performing menial services for wealthier classmates and fellows. He was eighteen. The curriculum was still largely Aristotelian. Newton quietly discarded it and taught himself Descartes, Kepler, Galileo, Wallis, and Barrow from the texts he found in the bookshops of Cambridge.

In 1664 he purchased a copy of Descartes' Géométrie and plunged into mathematics. Within a year he had extended it beyond what Descartes had done. He also purchased a prism at Stourbridge Fair in 1664 — the beginning of everything with light.

Trinity College

Entered 1661 as a sizar. Received his BA in 1665. Elected Fellow of Trinity in 1667.

📚

Self-Directed Study

Skipped the curriculum. Mastered Descartes, Galileo, Kepler, and Wallis independently by age 22.

🌬

Lucasian Professor

Appointed Lucasian Professor of Mathematics in 1669 at age 26, succeeding Isaac Barrow who vacated the chair for him.

👑

Royal Society

Elected Fellow in 1672. Presented his reflecting telescope to the Society — they were astonished.

The Miracle Year — Annus Mirabilis, 1665–1666

In the summer of 1665, plague swept England. Cambridge University closed and sent its students home. Newton, twenty-two years old, returned to Woolsthorpe Manor. Over the next eighteen months he worked in near-total isolation — and produced what may be the greatest intellectual achievement in the history of science.

He had no laboratory, no colleagues, no institution. He had notebooks, a prism, the apple orchard outside the window, and a mind that would not rest.

1665 — Summer
Calculus Develops the method of fluxions — what we now call differential calculus. He frames the derivative as the "fluxion" of a quantity changing in time. He extends this to the integral (the "fluent") and proves the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus: that differentiation and integration are inverse operations.
1665 — Autumn
Binomial Theorem Generalizes the binomial expansion to fractional and negative exponents. This becomes the foundation for infinite series analysis and power series expansions. He is twenty-two years old.
1666 — January
Optics Purchases prisms and sets up his darkened room experiment. Passes white light through a prism and discovers it is not pure but composed of a spectrum of colors. He invents the term "spectrum." He demonstrates the prism does not add color — it reveals color already present in white light.
1666 — Spring / Summer
Gravity Sitting in the Woolsthorpe orchard — and perhaps observing an apple fall — he poses the decisive question: if the force that pulls an apple to Earth diminishes with distance, could the same force reach the Moon? He calculates. The numbers almost fit. He sets the problem aside and returns to it twenty years later.

Newton later recalled: "In the two plague years of 1665 and 1666, I was in the prime of my age for invention, and minded Mathematicks and Philosophy more than at any time since." This is characteristic understatement: those two years produced calculus, the spectral theory of light, and the seed of universal gravitation.

The Man Himself — Character and Rivals

Newton was famously difficult — solitary, secretive, obsessive, and capable of sudden and ferocious vindictiveness toward those he perceived as having wronged him. He published reluctantly and only under pressure. He carried grudges for decades.

⚔

Robert Hooke

Hooke claimed priority in the inverse-square law of gravity. Newton's rage was bottomless. After Hooke's death in 1703, Newton became President of the Royal Society and — according to some accounts — ensured Hooke's portrait was lost.

✋

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz

Leibniz independently invented calculus and published first (1684). Newton, who had the priority by two decades but published nothing, launched a war of attrition. The Royal Society — stacked with Newton's allies — officially declared Newton the sole inventor in 1712. History gives credit to both.

📅

Edmond Halley

One of the very few men Newton seemed to trust. Halley personally funded the publication of the Principia, cajoled Newton into finishing it, and corrected the proofs. Without Halley, the Principia may never have been published.

🔎

The Secret Notebooks

Newton wrote more on alchemy and theology than on physics. He believed the prisca sapientia — the ancient wisdom — had encoded the structure of the cosmos. He wrote over a million words on biblical prophecy. He was privately, deeply, heterodox.

"Nature and Nature's laws lay hid in night: God said, Let Newton be! and all was light." — Alexander Pope, epitaph proposed for Newton's tomb

Later Life — The Mint, the Royal Society, and Death

After the Principia (1687) and Opticks (1704), Newton's creative scientific work was essentially complete. But his public life was only beginning.

1689
Parliament Elected Member of Parliament for Cambridge University. He served quietly; according to legend his only recorded contribution to debate was a request to close a drafty window.
1693
Breakdown Suffered a severe nervous breakdown — possibly mercury poisoning from his alchemical experiments. Sent disturbing letters to John Locke and Samuel Pepys. He later claimed he could not remember writing them.
1696
The Mint Appointed Warden of the Royal Mint in London. Oversaw the Great Recoinage — replacing clipped and debased coinage with milled, machine-made coin. Prosecuted counterfeiters relentlessly, sending at least twenty to the gallows. Became Master of the Mint in 1699.
1703
Royal Society Elected President of the Royal Society. Re-elected every year until his death. Ran it with iron authority. Published Opticks in English (rather than Latin) the following year — a deliberate move for a wider audience.
1705
Knighthood Knighted by Queen Anne at Trinity College, Cambridge — the first scientist to be knighted for scientific work.
1727
Death Died 20 March 1727 (NS) in Kensington, aged 84. Buried with full state honors in Westminster Abbey. He never married. He reportedly died a virgin. Voltaire attended the funeral and marveled at England's treatment of its great mind.

What He Was

Isaac Newton was not a warm man or an easy one. He was solitary to an extreme, driven to a pathological degree, and capable of sustained cruelty toward rivals. He was also, quite simply, the most powerful scientific mind that had yet lived on Earth — and one of a very small number who reshaped not just a discipline but the entire framework of human knowledge.

He re-ordered the sky. He revealed what light is made of. He invented a mathematics sufficient to describe motion in the universe. He did most of this alone, in his twenties, in a farmhouse during a plague.

The rest, as they say, is the modern world.
