The Work Principia · Opticks · Calculus · 1666–1704

Three laws that moved the heavens. One equation that unified the cosmos. A prism that shattered the myth of pure white light. The Principia Mathematica is the most consequential scientific text ever written.

Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica — 1687

The full title means Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy. Published on 5 July 1687, it was written in Latin — the universal language of scholarship — and presented as a rigorous geometric deduction from first principles. Edmond Halley, astronomer and friend, paid for its publication from his own pocket after the Royal Society demurred.

The Principia has three books. Book I establishes the mathematics of motion in a void. Book II treats motion through resisting media. Book III — The System of the World — applies the mathematics to the actual solar system, deriving the motions of planets, moons, comets, and tides from a single force law.

"I have not been able to discover the cause of those properties of gravity from phenomena, and I frame no hypotheses… It is enough that gravity does really exist, and act according to the laws which we have explained." — Newton, General Scholium, Principia (3rd ed., 1726)

The Three Laws of Motion

Newton opens the Principia with three axioms — the Laws of Motion — from which all of classical mechanics follows. They remain the foundation of engineering and physics for every problem at human scales.

First Law · Lex I · Inertia

The Law of Inertia

Every body perseveres in its state of being at rest, or of moving uniformly straight forward, except insofar as it is compelled to change its state by forces impressed upon it.

An object at rest stays at rest. An object in motion stays in motion — in a straight line at constant speed — unless acted on by a net external force. This demolished Aristotle's physics, which required continuous force to maintain motion.

v = constant if F_net = 0
Second Law · Lex II · Force

The Law of Acceleration

A change in motion is proportional to the motive force impressed and takes place along the straight line in which that force is impressed.

Force equals the rate of change of momentum. When mass is constant, this becomes Force equals mass times acceleration. This is the master equation of mechanics — every rocket, bridge, and projectile calculation rests on it.

F = ma
Third Law · Lex III · Reaction

The Law of Equal and Opposite Forces

To every action there is always an equal and opposite reaction; the mutual actions of two bodies upon each other are always equal, and directed to contrary parts.

Forces always come in pairs. When Earth pulls you downward, you pull Earth upward with equal force. When a rocket engine pushes exhaust backward, the exhaust pushes the rocket forward. Every propulsion system in history obeys this law.

F₁₂ = −F₂₁

Universal Law of Gravitation

The crown of the Principia: a single equation that describes the force of attraction between any two masses in the universe — from an apple to a galaxy. The force is proportional to the product of the masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

Universal Law of Gravitation · Newton, 1687
F = Gm₁m₂ / r²

The gravitational force between two bodies equals the gravitational constant times the product of their masses, divided by the square of the distance between them.

F = gravitational force (Newtons) G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg² (gravitational constant) m₁, m₂ = masses of the two bodies (kg) r = distance between centers (meters)

The inverse-square relationship — the force falls off as the square of the distance — is the key insight. Double the distance, the force drops to one-quarter. Triple the distance, one-ninth. This is the same falloff as light intensity from a point source, and Newton suspected it from geometric arguments about spheres.

The constant G — the gravitational constant — Newton could not determine numerically. It was first measured by Henry Cavendish in 1798, using a torsion balance, seventy-one years after Newton's death.

Deriving Kepler's Laws as a Consequence

The supreme demonstration of the Principia's power was Newton's proof that Kepler's three laws of planetary motion — empirical observations gathered by Kepler from Tycho Brahe's data without any understanding of their cause — follow mathematically from the law of universal gravitation combined with the Second Law of Motion. Kepler's laws were not axioms; they were theorems.

Kepler's Laws — Derived, Not Assumed

Kepler I
The orbit of a planet is an ellipse with the Sun at one focus. Newton proved this follows from the inverse-square law: only 1/r² gravity produces conic sections (ellipses, parabolas, hyperbolas) as orbits.
Kepler II
A line from the Sun to a planet sweeps equal areas in equal times. Newton proved this follows from conservation of angular momentum, which in turn follows from the force always pointing toward the Sun (central force).
Kepler III
The square of a planet's orbital period equals the cube of its semi-major axis: T² ∝ a³. Newton proved this is an exact consequence of the inverse-square law. The constant of proportionality involves G and the Sun's mass.

Opticks — 1704 — The Nature of Light

Published in English (not Latin) in 1704, Opticks is Newton's account of his experiments with light and color — work begun in 1666 with a prism purchased at Stourbridge Fair. Where the Principia was dense deductive geometry, Opticks was empirical and readable — the Royal Society's members could follow it without Newton's mathematical apparatus.

🌈

The Prism Experiment

White sunlight through a prism produces a full spectrum of colors — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet. Newton named these colors. He proved the prism does not add color: it refracts different frequencies by different amounts, revealing colors already present.

🔭

Experimentum Crucis

Newton passed spectrum light through a second prism: each individual color remained unchanged. This "crucial experiment" proved that color is a property of the light ray itself, not an artifact of the glass — overthrowing a century of wrong theory.

🌟

Corpuscular Theory

Newton proposed light consists of particles (corpuscles). This was later challenged by Huygens' wave theory and ultimately superseded by Maxwell and then quantum mechanics (photons). Newton was wrong about the mechanism but right about the spectrum.

👥

Newton's Rings

Circular interference patterns produced when a curved glass surface is placed on a flat one. Newton observed them but could not explain them with his particle theory — they require wave theory. They now bear his name as an irony of science history.

The Calculus — Method of Fluxions

Newton invented what he called the "method of fluxions" in 1665–1666. A fluent is any continuously changing quantity; a fluxion is its rate of change — what we now call a derivative. The notation he used was dot-notation (ẋ for the fluxion of x), which survives today in physics for time derivatives.

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
d/dt[∫ f(t) dt] = f(t)

Differentiation and integration are inverse operations — Newton's most powerful insight, unifying what had seemed to be two unrelated mathematical techniques.

Newton also extended the binomial theorem to fractional and negative exponents — crucial for series expansions — and developed power series methods that allowed functions to be expressed as infinite polynomials. These are now standard tools in every branch of analysis.

📐

Fluxions vs. Differentials

Leibniz invented calculus independently (published 1684) using dy/dx notation — the notation we use today. Newton's fluxion notation (ẋ) survives mainly in physics. The mathematics is identical; the notation was different.

📜

Priority vs. Publication

Newton had the calculus by 1666 but did not publish until 1704 (in an appendix to Opticks). Leibniz published in 1684. Newton's secrecy cost him recognition and gave rise to the bitter priority dispute that consumed the last decades of both men's lives.

🔢

The Binomial Theorem

Newton generalized (a + b)ⁿ to cases where n is any rational number — fractional or negative — producing an infinite series. This was the first major result he achieved at Woolsthorpe in 1665, aged twenty-two.

The Reflecting Telescope — Built by Hand, 1668

Understanding that a prism refracts different colors at different angles — causing chromatic aberration in refracting telescopes — Newton designed a telescope that used a concave mirror rather than a lens to focus light. This eliminated chromatic aberration entirely. He ground the mirrors himself and built the instrument with his own hands in 1668.

The instrument was about 6 inches long but performed comparably to a 40-inch refractor. Newton presented it to the Royal Society in 1671; they were impressed enough to elect him Fellow. The reflecting telescope design — the Newtonian reflector — remains the most common design for amateur astronomy today.
