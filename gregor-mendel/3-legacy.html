The Long Afterlife

He died in 1884, thinking himself forgotten. By 1953 his laws had led, link by link, to the double helix. By 2003 the human genome had been sequenced. Every living thing on Earth is governed by what Mendel found in a monastery garden in Brno.

1900 — Triple Rediscovery

One of the most remarkable coincidences in the history of science: in the spring of 1900, three scientists independently rediscovered Mendel's laws while conducting their own plant hybridization experiments — and each, searching the literature, found the same 1866 paper in a Brno proceedings journal that the world had ignored for thirty-four years.

Mar 1900

Hugo de Vries

The Dutch botanist Hugo de Vries had been conducting hybridization experiments with evening primrose and other plants. He submitted a paper to the French Academy of Sciences in March 1900 describing ratios in his hybrid offspring — without mentioning Mendel. A colleague pointed him toward the 1866 paper. De Vries initially resisted acknowledging Mendel's priority; Carl Correns forced the acknowledgment with his own publication.

Country: Netherlands  ·  Plant: Oenothera
Apr 1900

Carl Correns

The German botanist Carl Correns had independently derived the same segregation ratios from crosses of maize and peas. Unlike de Vries, Correns fully and generously acknowledged Mendel's priority when he found the paper. He was the first to use the phrase "Mendel's laws" in print. His paper, submitted to the German Botanical Society in April 1900, explicitly names Mendel as the discoverer of the principles Correns had reinvented.

Country: Germany  ·  Plant: Maize, peas
Jun 1900

Erich von Tschermak

The Austrian agronomist Erich von Tschermak was the third, and arguably had understood Mendel's paper the least deeply of the three — his initial work was on pea breeding with practical rather than theoretical goals. He too found the 1866 paper independently and acknowledged Mendel. The three simultaneous rediscoveries created what historians call the "Mendelian moment" — the explosive recognition that a complete theory of heredity had been sitting, unread, in a provincial journal since 1866.

Country: Austria  ·  Plant: Peas
1900

What Happened

The year 1900 is now taken as the founding date of Mendelian genetics as a recognized science. Within five years, "genetics" was a recognized discipline with its own journals and societies. Mendel's name was everywhere. He had been dead for sixteen years. His pea notebooks had been burned. The world reconstructed his methods from his published paper alone, and confirmed every claim he had made.

Mendel's death: 1884  ·  Justice: 1900
Building the Science, 1900–1953

The half-century between the rediscovery of Mendel's laws and the discovery of the double helix is one of the most productive periods in the history of science. Each decade added a crucial layer to the structure Mendel had begun.

1909

Johannsen Coins "Gene"

The Danish botanist Wilhelm Johannsen introduced the word gene in 1909 to describe Mendel's "factors" — the discrete heritable units whose existence Mendel had inferred from his ratio data. Johannsen also distinguished between genotype (the genetic makeup of an organism) and phenotype (its observable appearance) — a distinction fundamental to all subsequent genetics. Mendel had the concept; Johannsen gave it the vocabulary.

Terms coined: Gene, genotype, phenotype
1910

Morgan & Chromosomes

Thomas Hunt Morgan at Columbia University began working with the fruit fly Drosophila melanogaster around 1908. By 1910 he had observed that certain traits were linked — they did not assort independently, as Mendel's Second Law predicted. The reason: they were on the same chromosome. Morgan proved that genes are physically located on chromosomes, establishing the chromosomal theory of inheritance. He won the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1933.

Organism: Drosophila  ·  Nobel: 1933
1928–1944

DNA as the Carrier

In 1928, Frederick Griffith showed that something from dead bacteria could transform living bacteria — the first hint of a molecular carrier of hereditary information. In 1944, Oswald Avery, Colin MacLeod, and Maclyn McCarty identified that "something" as DNA, not protein. This was the most important experiment in molecular biology before the double helix — and was initially dismissed by many scientists who could not believe nucleic acids were complex enough to carry genetic information.

Avery et al.: 1944 — "transforming principle" = DNA
25 Apr 1953

Watson, Crick & Franklin

James Watson and Francis Crick published their model of the DNA double helix on 25 April 1953 in Nature — nine pages, with a single figure. The model showed that DNA consisted of two complementary strands wound around each other, with base pairs (A-T, G-C) as the rungs. The structure immediately explained how genetic information could be copied: each strand serves as a template for its partner. Rosalind Franklin's X-ray diffraction data was decisive — and she received no Nobel Prize (she died in 1958, before the award was made in 1962).

Franklin's role: Essential, unacknowledged at award
The Modern Synthesis

Darwin + Mendel = Everything

The Modern Synthesis — developed primarily in the 1930s and 1940s by Ronald Fisher, J.B.S. Haldane, Sewall Wright, Theodosius Dobzhansky, Ernst Mayr, and G.G. Simpson — unified Darwin's natural selection with Mendel's genetics into a single coherent theory of evolution. It answered the question neither man could answer alone.

Darwin had explained why organisms change across generations (selection pressure) and how fast (gradually, over many generations). But he had no mechanism for how variation was maintained or transmitted. Without a correct theory of heredity, his theory had a hole at its center.

The Mechanism Supplied

Mendel's genetics supplied exactly what Darwin needed. Genes, as discrete units, do not blend — they segregate cleanly. Recessive alleles can hide for generations in heterozygotes and resurface unchanged when two carriers mate. Mutation creates new alleles. Natural selection acts on the differential survival of phenotypes, which reflects the underlying genotype. Population genetics — the mathematics developed by Fisher, Haldane, and Wright — showed precisely how allele frequencies change under selection across generations.

The Modern Synthesis is the theoretical foundation on which all of contemporary biology rests. Its two parents were Charles Darwin and Gregor Mendel — who were solving the same problem simultaneously, from different ends, on opposite sides of Europe, and who never knew it.

"Nothing in biology makes sense except in the light of evolution." — Theodosius Dobzhansky, 1973 — the synthesis that joined Darwin's selection to Mendel's laws
Genetics Becomes Medicine

The line from Mendel's monastery garden to the genetics clinic runs straight. Every piece of medical genetics — from carrier testing to cancer risk prediction to gene therapy — is founded on the intellectual structure Mendel built with peas and patience in Brno between 1856 and 1863.

1902

Archibald Garrod

Only two years after the rediscovery, the British physician Archibald Garrod showed that alkaptonuria — a rare metabolic disorder — followed Mendelian recessive inheritance in families. This was the first demonstration that human disease could be inherited according to Mendel's laws. Garrod coined the phrase "inborn errors of metabolism." Mendelian medicine had begun.

Disease: Alkaptonuria  ·  Pattern: Autosomal recessive
1950s–60s

Newborn Screening

Robert Guthrie's development of the Guthrie test for phenylketonuria (PKU) in 1961 created the first population-wide genetic screening program. PKU, a Mendelian recessive disorder of phenylalanine metabolism, causes severe intellectual disability if untreated — and is entirely manageable with dietary intervention if caught early. Mendel's logic of dominant and recessive, applied to biochemistry, saved millions of children from preventable harm.

Disease: PKU  ·  Impact: Millions screened annually
1970s–80s

Carrier Testing

The expansion of carrier testing — for Tay-Sachs disease, sickle cell anemia, cystic fibrosis, and hundreds of other Mendelian disorders — gave couples information about their risk of having affected children before or during pregnancy. Each test depends on Mendel's insight that heterozygous carriers, who show no symptoms, nonetheless carry and transmit the recessive allele. The asymmetry of dominant and recessive, discovered in a pea garden, protects families today.

Basis: Mendelian recessive carrier detection
1990s–now

BRCA & Cancer Genes

The discovery of BRCA1 (1994) and BRCA2 (1995) — genes whose mutations dramatically increase breast and ovarian cancer risk — is a direct descendant of Mendelian thinking. The mutations are inherited in an autosomal dominant pattern: one copy of the mutant allele is sufficient to elevate risk. Genetic testing for BRCA mutations, and the clinical decisions that follow, are Mendelian medicine applied to one of the most feared diseases on Earth.

Pattern: Autosomal dominant  ·  Discovery: 1994–95
CRISPR and the Rewrite

From Observation to Editing

Mendel observed how alleles were transmitted. Watson and Crick revealed the molecular structure that carries them. The Human Genome Project (1990–2003) read the complete sequence of human DNA — all three billion base pairs. And then, in 2012, Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier published a paper describing CRISPR-Cas9 — a molecular tool, borrowed from bacterial immune systems, that can cut DNA at precisely specified locations and allow it to be edited.

CRISPR makes Mendel's passive observation active: we no longer merely read the alleles we inherit — we can, in principle, change them. The same logic Mendel used to predict which peas would be round and which wrinkled now underlies therapies that correct the single-letter mutations responsible for sickle cell disease, beta-thalassemia, and dozens of other Mendelian disorders. Clinical trials are ongoing. Cures are arriving.

The Ethical Weight

The power that flows from Mendel's garden is not morally neutral. Eugenics — the historical misapplication of Mendelian genetics to argue for state-controlled human breeding — represents the darkest chapter in the afterlife of Mendel's work. The eugenics movements of the early twentieth century used Mendelian language to justify forced sterilizations, immigration restrictions, and ultimately the Nazi genocide. The science was distorted, but the scientific authority of Mendelian genetics was claimed as cover.

Mendel himself was a plant breeder operating entirely within the monasticand civic tradition of agricultural improvement. He had no theory of racial hierarchy, no political program. His laws describe how traits are transmitted — nothing about which traits are desirable or who should be permitted to reproduce. The misuse of his work belongs to the people who misused it, not to him. But the misuse was real, and its victims numbered in the millions.

The Burned Notebooks

When Mendel died on 6 January 1884, his successor as abbot of St. Thomas, Anselm Rambousek, ordered Mendel's personal papers cleared from his study. Most of his scientific notebooks, correspondence drafts, and unpublished records were burned. The monastery's records do not explain why — presumably the new abbot considered them of no particular value and wanted the room cleared.

We will never know what was in those notebooks. We know from his published correspondence with von Nägeli that he had conducted experiments on beans, maize, and other plants in addition to peas and hawkweeds. He may have had insights about the limits of his own laws — why hawkweeds didn't behave as expected, for example — that could have accelerated the science. He may have had nothing more than the data we can reconstruct from his letters. The bonfire made the question permanent.

"All that I ask of you is that you will not judge me by what you see, but wait for the result." — Mendel, letter to von Nägeli, 1867 — words that apply to his entire legacy

Posthumous Justice

The Man Who Died Failing

Gregor Mendel died believing that his scientific work had been a failure. The experiments he had devoted seven years of his prime life to had produced a paper that the world had not read. His subsequent work on hawkweeds had contradicted his own earlier findings in ways he could not explain. His administrative career as abbot had been consumed by a tax dispute he had not won. He was, by any external measure of the era, a minor provincial ecclesiastic whose scientific hobby had come to nothing.

The posthumous justice is extraordinary and strange. The man who discovered the mechanism of inheritance — the most fundamental fact about life short of chemistry itself — died thinking he had failed. The world spent the next sixteen years confirming his obscurity. Then, in the space of a single spring, three botanists on three desks in three countries found his paper and understood what it meant.

The Measure of the Work

How do we measure what Mendel gave us? Every crop bred for disease resistance, every cancer drug targeting a specific mutation, every child screened at birth for a metabolic disorder, every person who knows they carry a risk gene and can plan accordingly — all of them are living in the world Mendel made possible. The genome sequencer and the CRISPR scientist and the genetic counselor and the oncologist are all, at some foundational level, applying a framework that a monk built in a garden in Brno because he was curious about peas.

Darwin gave us the why of biological change. Mendel gave us the how of biological continuity. Together — never meeting, never knowing — they gave us the complete picture. The burned notebooks are the only tragedy. Everything else is the slow unfolding of a discovery that was right, and patient, and always going to win.

"My time will come." — Attributed to Mendel, final years — a prophecy he did not live to see fulfilled
Recognition
1911

Mendel Institute Founded

The Mendelianum — now the Mendel Museum — was established at the monastery of St. Thomas in Brno to commemorate Mendel's work. The monastery garden where the pea experiments were conducted was preserved. Today it is a UNESCO Memory of the World site. Visitors can walk the length of the garden where the laws of heredity were found.

Location: St. Thomas Monastery, Brno
1965

Mendel Centenary

The centenary of Mendel's 1865 lectures was marked by international symposia, publications, and formal recognition by scientific bodies worldwide. By the mid-twentieth century, Mendel was firmly established as one of the canonical figures of science — his portrait appearing in every genetics textbook, his name attached to the fundamental laws of the field he founded.

Recognition: Universal, if posthumous
Ongoing

Mendel Prize

The Gregor Mendel Medal, awarded by the German Academy of Sciences Leopoldina (one of the world's oldest scientific academies), recognizes outstanding contributions to genetics. The Mendel Medal of the Genetics Society (UK) similarly honors his name. He is commemorated in the names of laboratories, buildings, and awards on six continents.

Eponyms: Mendelian, Mendelism, Mendel's laws
2022

The 200th Anniversary

In 2022, the bicentennial of Mendel's birth was marked by events in Brno and across the scientific world. The Czech Republic issued commemorative stamps. New analyses of Mendel's surviving correspondence continued. The debate over Fisher's "too good" statistical critique remains unsettled. Mendel's paper remains in print, in print-on-demand, and freely available online — the most influential paper from the most overlooked scientist in history.

Paper availability: Freely accessible worldwide
