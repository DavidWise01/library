Gregor Mendel

Born Johann Mendel on 22 July 1822 in Hynčice, Silesia — a village in what is now the Czech Republic. Died 6 January 1884 in Brno, still believing himself a failure. In between: the discovery that governs every living thing on Earth.

Family & Origins

Born Johann Mendel

On 22 July 1822, in the small farming village of Hynčice (then called Heinzendorf) in the Silesian region of the Austrian Empire — now part of the Czech Republic — a son was born to Anton and Rosine Mendel. They named him Johann. The family scratched a living from a small farm on the border of German-speaking Silesia and Moravian Bohemia, growing fruit trees and keeping a household perpetually one bad harvest from poverty.

Anton Mendel was a veteran of the Napoleonic wars, compelled to spend years of his life doing forced labor on the local lord's estate — a common obligation for peasant landholders. The family's farm was small and the work was hard. But even in this constrained world, education was prized. Johann was a precocious child and his teachers recognized it early.

The Farming Background

The irony of Mendel's origins deserves to be savored. The man who would decode the mathematics of heredity was raised in a family of practical plant breeders — his father cultivated fruit trees and his village had a long tradition of working with the soil. When Mendel later spent seven years crossing pea plants in a monastery garden, he was not abandoning his origins. He was returning to them, but armed with a scientist's precision and a mathematician's eye.

Mendel had one elder sister, Veronika, and one younger, Theresia. The family's poverty was real enough that when Johann needed to continue his education — and the costs began to mount — it was Theresia who sacrificed part of her dowry to help fund his studies. He never forgot this. Years later, as a prosperous abbot, he helped educate all three of Theresia's sons.

Education & the Path to the Monastery
01

Piarist School, Lipník

At age eleven, Johann was sent to the Piarist school in Lipník (Leipnik), some distance from home — a sacrifice for the family. His teachers praised his abilities consistently. He studied philosophy, natural science, mathematics, and Latin. At sixteen he moved to the Gymnasium in Opava (Troppau) for two more years of advanced study.

Years: 1833–1840  ·  Verdict: Exceptional student
02

Olomouc Philosophical Institute

In 1840, at age 18, Mendel enrolled at the two-year philosophical course in Olomouc — the prerequisite for university studies. He was so poor that he could barely eat; he described suffering from "a life-threatening illness" likely caused by chronic hunger and stress. He nonetheless graduated in the top tier of his class. His physics professor Friedrich Franz wrote a glowing recommendation — the same professor who would later introduce him to the Augustinian monastery at Brno.

Years: 1840–1843  ·  Verdict: Top of class, near starvation
03

Entering the Monastery

In 1843, at age 21, Johann Mendel entered the Augustinian monastery of St. Thomas in Brno. He took the name Gregor in honor of St. Gregory. The decision was not purely spiritual: the monastery offered a funded path to education for a young man whose family could not support him. Abbot Cyrill Franz Napp — a remarkable man, scientifically sophisticated — immediately recognized Mendel's gifts and sent him to study in Vienna.

Year: 1843  ·  Takes name: Gregor
04

University of Vienna, 1851–53

Abbot Napp sent Mendel to the University of Vienna to study physics and natural sciences. This proved decisive. Mendel studied under Christian Doppler (of the Doppler Effect) and Franz Unger, a pioneering plant physiologist who believed in the transmutation of species. Vienna gave Mendel the mathematical rigor that would make his plant experiments revolutionary. He returned to Brno in 1853, changed.

Years: 1851–1853  ·  Key teacher: Christian Doppler
The Teacher Who Failed the Teaching Exam — Twice

The single most darkly comic fact in the history of science: Gregor Mendel failed the teacher certification examination twice — and in both cases, his weakest subjects included natural history and geology. The man who would found the science of genetics could not pass a biology exam.

"He became so agitated during the oral examination that he could not demonstrate the knowledge he undoubtedly possessed." — Contemporary account of Mendel's 1850 examination failure

First Failure, 1850

Mendel sat the teaching certification exam in Vienna in 1850, before his university studies there. The written component went acceptably. The oral exam was a disaster — the examiner in natural history, Johann Karl Baumgartner, was reportedly unsympathetic and Mendel's nerves shattered him. The examiner wrote that Mendel had a "lack of insight" and an "unclear manner of speaking." The failure was bitter and humiliating. It was also the prompt that sent him to Vienna for two years of proper university training.

Second Failure, 1856

After two formative years in Vienna, Mendel returned to Brno and attempted the exam a second time. He never finished it. On the first day, the examiner in zoology — a different man, Professor Kner — provoked such a dispute that Mendel withdrew from the examination entirely rather than continue. He submitted a written protest. He never sat the exam again. He taught as a substitute teacher for fourteen years without ever holding a full certificate. He was, ironically, a beloved and effective teacher of both physics and natural history.

The Pea Garden, 1856–1863

In 1856 — the same year he failed his second teaching exam and the same year Darwin was quietly urging forward his species theory in England — Mendel began the experiments that would define his legacy. The monastery garden at St. Thomas was an ideal laboratory: sheltered, well-maintained, approximately 35 meters long. Mendel cultivated up to 29,000 pea plants over seven growing seasons, 1856 to 1863. He planted, crossed, harvested, counted, and recorded with meticulous precision.

Mendel chose Pisum sativum (the garden pea) deliberately. He spent two years first testing that his seed varieties bred true — that each trait was stable across generations before he began crossing them. He identified seven traits that each came in exactly two distinct forms, each dominant or recessive. He was not lucky in his choice: peas were a near-perfect experimental organism, their traits inherited independently (as we now know, because the genes happen to sit on different chromosomes).

The results were stunning, but only if you could read the mathematics. Mendel could. He tabulated thousands of observations and recognized the ratios: 3:1 in the second generation, 1:2:1 in the underlying genotypes. No biologist before him had done this. He was thinking statistically in an era when biology was still largely narrative and descriptive.

Publication, 1866 — and the Long Silence

Versuche über Pflanzenhybriden

Mendel presented his results to the Natural History Society of Brno in two lectures, on 8 February and 8 March 1865. The following year, in 1866, the paper was published in the Society's proceedings: Verhandlungen des naturforschenden Vereines in Brünn — the journal of an obscure provincial scientific society in a provincial city of the Austrian Empire. The paper's title translates as Experiments on Plant Hybridization.

Mendel was thorough: he had 40 reprints made and sent them to prominent scientists across Europe, including several who corresponded with Darwin. He almost certainly sent a copy to the botanist Carl von Nägeli in Munich — who became his main scientific correspondent but famously failed to grasp the importance of what Mendel had found, redirecting him toward hawkweeds (Hieracium), which are apomictic and would have confounded Mendel's results irreparably.

The Silence

The paper was cited only a handful of times in the next 35 years. The reasons for the failure were structural: Mendel's work was too mathematical for botanists of the era, who were not accustomed to statistical reasoning. The journal was obscure and not widely circulated. And crucially, Mendel himself was modest and isolated — he did not push his findings aggressively or travel to international conferences to argue for them.

Darwin almost certainly had access to the paper or to a report of it. His personal library contained Focke's Die Pflanzenmischlinge (1881), which cited Mendel's paper multiple times. Darwin's copy shows no annotations on those pages. Whether he read past them, we cannot know. The two men who together hold the key to life were separated by little more than inattention and circumstance.

Abbot, 1868–1884

In 1868, Mendel was elected Abbot of St. Thomas — the head of his monastery. He was 46 years old. The election honored him and effectively ended his scientific career. Administrative duties consumed his time; ecclesiastical politics consumed his energy. The Austrian government imposed a new tax on monasteries, and Mendel spent his final years locked in a bitter legal dispute with the government over it — a fight he conducted with characteristic stubbornness and which he never won.

1868

Elected Abbot

His election was a recognition of his standing within the Augustinian order. He was a careful and effective administrator and a generous benefactor. But the role ate his science. His breeding experiments never resumed at scale.

Age at election: 46
1870s

Tax Dispute

The Austro-Hungarian government's new religious property tax provoked Mendel into years of bureaucratic warfare. He refused to pay. The government sequestered monastery revenues. He corresponded endlessly with lawyers, bishops, and government officials. He never capitulated — and the dispute was still unresolved at his death.

Duration: ~10 years
1880s

Declining Health

In his final years Mendel suffered from chronic nephritis (kidney disease). He smoked heavily and had grown corpulent. Contemporary accounts describe a melancholy man who felt his scientific work had been ignored and his administrative work futile. He continued his meteorological observations until near the end.

Final hobby: Meteorology & beekeeping
6 Jan 1884

Death

Mendel died of chronic kidney failure on 6 January 1884, aged 61. He was mourned locally as a well-regarded abbot and civic figure. His scientific paper was not mentioned in his obituary. The new abbot, Anselm Rambousek, ordered most of Mendel's scientific papers burned. The pea plant notebooks were lost.

Obituary mentions his science: No
Rediscovery, 1900

Sixteen years after Mendel's death, three botanists independently rediscovered his laws while conducting their own plant hybridization experiments. Hugo de Vries (Netherlands), Carl Correns (Germany), and Erich von Tschermak (Austria) each published papers in 1900 describing results that matched Mendel's. Each, in searching the literature, found Mendel's 1866 paper — and realized he had preceded them all by a generation.

De Vries initially tried to minimize Mendel's priority in his first publication; Correns forced the acknowledgment. The year 1900 is taken as the birth of Mendelian genetics as a recognized scientific discipline. The founder had been dead for sixteen years. His notebooks had been burned. The world had to reconstruct his methods from his published paper alone.

"My time will come." — Attributed to Mendel in his final years, possibly apocryphal, certainly true

The Man Himself
★

Stubborn

Mendel refused to pay the monastery's tax for a decade, knowing he would probably lose. He refused to stop his hawkweed experiments even when von Nägeli's indifference was obvious. He submitted to two failed exams rather than simply abandon the teaching credential. Stubbornness runs through every chapter of his life.

Tax dispute: Never resolved, even at death
★

Meticulous

Mendel spent two full years testing seed varieties for consistency before beginning his crossing experiments. He counted thousands of individual seeds, flowers, and pods by hand, season after season. His published paper contains tables of data so precise that later statisticians argued — controversially — that the numbers were too good to be true.

Too perfect? Fisher's 1936 analysis debated to this day
★

Solitary

Unlike Darwin, who corresponded internationally and had a network of supportive colleagues, Mendel was largely alone. His main scientific correspondent, von Nägeli, never believed in him. He published once on peas, once on hawkweeds, and then stopped. He had no scientific community to sustain him when the world ignored his work.

Key correspondent: Von Nägeli (skeptic)
★

Prescient

Mendel wrote to von Nägeli in 1867: "I am convinced that it will not be long before the whole world acknowledges the results of my work." He died thinking himself a failure. He was right about the world — only wrong about the timeline. It took 34 years, not the few he imagined.

Self-assessment, 1884: A failure. Wrong.
