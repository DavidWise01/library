I — MIT and American Science

Massachusetts Institute of Technology, 1919–1964

MIT in the 1920s was not the institution it would become. It was primarily an engineering school — a place to train men who would build bridges, design machines, and run factories. The mathematics department was subordinate to these practical goals, and the culture was more empirical than theoretical. Wiener found this environment uncongenial in some respects and liberating in others. His theoretical ambitions were occasionally seen as impractical; his collaborations with engineers gave his mathematics a grounding in real physical problems that pure mathematicians of his generation often lacked.

By the 1940s, MIT had been transformed. The demands of World War II had brought in enormous federal research funding and had created an institutional culture oriented toward the most ambitious possible problems at the intersection of science and engineering. The Radiation Laboratory at MIT, which developed radar, employed several thousand scientists and engineers at its peak. The Research Laboratory of Electronics — where Wiener worked — brought together electrical engineers, physicists, mathematicians, and biologists in a genuinely unprecedented way. The culture of interdisciplinary collaboration that produced cybernetics was not accidental: it was the specific product of wartime MIT.

1919–1940

Pre-War MIT

Mathematics as engineering support. Wiener develops his stochastic processes, Brownian motion theory, and harmonic analysis. Builds his reputation in pure mathematics while maintaining connections to applied problems.

1940–1945

WWII Mobilization

The National Defense Research Committee funds the anti-aircraft predictor project. The Radiation Laboratory develops radar. MIT becomes a major defense research institution. Wiener is changed by the work — and by the weapons it produces.

1946–1960

Post-War Research Culture

The Research Laboratory of Electronics becomes the center of interdisciplinary science at MIT. Cybernetics is born, grows, and spreads. The Cold War brings enormous military research funding — which Wiener declines.

1950–1964

The Warning Years

Wiener writes and speaks publicly about the dangers of automation and the ethics of intelligent machines. MIT colleagues consider him eccentric; history considers him prescient.

II — The War and Its Aftermath

World War II: The Project That Changed Him

When the National Defense Research Committee asked Wiener to work on anti-aircraft fire control in 1940, he accepted without hesitation. The problem was scientifically fascinating and the cause was unambiguous — Nazi Germany was a genuine and catastrophic threat, and anything that could help defeat it was legitimate. Wiener worked on the predictor project for five years, living the wartime research culture of enforced urgency, midnight conversations with engineers, and the particular intensity of working on problems where the stakes were human lives.

But the experience marked him in ways he did not fully anticipate. In 1946, as the Cold War began to crystallize and the military began funding the next generation of weapons research at universities, Wiener made a decision that was highly unusual for an American scientist of his era: he publicly refused to do classified weapons research. He wrote a letter — published in The Atlantic — explaining his refusal to respond to a defense contractor's request for results from his wartime work:

"I do not expect to publish any future work of mine which may do damage in the hands of irresponsible militarists."
— Norbert Wiener, "A Scientist Rebels," The Atlantic, January 1947

This was not merely posturing. He meant it. He turned down consulting contracts, declined to participate in classified seminars, and refused to engage with military research programs that most of his MIT colleagues found not only acceptable but desirable. His position was not that weapons research was always wrong — he acknowledged that WWII justified it — but that in the Cold War, with nuclear weapons in play and geopolitical dynamics he regarded as dangerously unstable, the contribution of individual scientists to military capability was more likely to harm than help human welfare.

His colleagues thought him naive. His critics thought him disloyal. History has been kinder to his judgment than to those who simply continued building weapons without asking why.

III — The Macy Conferences, 1946–1953

Where Disciplines Were Dissolved: The Macy Meetings

Between 1946 and 1953, the Josiah Macy Jr. Foundation sponsored a remarkable series of ten interdisciplinary conferences in New York City, organized primarily by the neurologist Warren McCulloch. The subject was, broadly, feedback mechanisms in biology and the social sciences — but the conferences quickly became something larger: the first sustained attempt to develop a common vocabulary for understanding mind, machine, culture, and communication using the concepts of cybernetics and information theory.

Norbert Wiener attended many of the conferences, and his ideas were central to their intellectual agenda. But the Macy Conferences were remarkable precisely because of the breadth of disciplines represented around the table:

Norbert Wiener
Mathematics / Cybernetics
The conceptual anchor. Feedback, control, communication theory.
Warren McCulloch
Neuroscience / Logic
Organizer. Pioneered the logical neuron model with Walter Pitts — the precursor to neural networks.
John von Neumann
Mathematics / Computing
Game theory, computer architecture. Applied mathematical precision to both machines and biology.
Claude Shannon
Information Theory
His 1948 information entropy paper completed the mathematical foundation for cybernetics.
Gregory Bateson
Anthropology / Systems
Applied cybernetics to culture, ecology, schizophrenia, and the theory of mind. Transformed anthropology.
Margaret Mead
Anthropology
Bateson's collaborator. Brought sociological and cross-cultural dimensions to cybernetic analysis.
Walter Pitts
Mathematics / Neuroscience
Self-educated mathematical prodigy. With McCulloch, showed that neural networks could perform logical computation.
Julian Bigelow
Engineering
Wiener's collaborator on the predictor project. Helped build the Institute for Advanced Study computer.

What the Conferences Produced

The Macy Conferences did not produce a single unified theory. What they produced was a shared vocabulary — the concepts of feedback, information, entropy, system, control — and a set of intellectual connections between people working in radically different fields who discovered that they were, at some level of abstraction, working on the same problems. The conversations seeded:

Cognitive Science

Mind as Information Processing

The idea that cognition could be understood as computation — that mental processes were information-processing operations, not mystical vital phenomena — emerged directly from the Macy Conferences. This framing became the foundation of cognitive science.

Artificial Intelligence

The Machine That Thinks

McCulloch and Pitts' logical neurons, von Neumann's computing architectures, and Wiener's feedback loops provided the conceptual and mathematical tools for the AI project that would be formally launched at Dartmouth in 1956.

Systems Theory

Ecology, Culture, Pathology

Bateson and Mead applied cybernetic concepts to ecosystems, family systems, cultural patterns, and mental illness. Systems therapy, ecological thinking, and the concept of "double bind" in schizophrenia all derived from Macy discussions.

Communication Theory

Information and Noise

Shannon's information theory, presented in the context of the Macy discussions, became foundational to linguistics, cryptography, communications engineering, and eventually genomics (genes as information).

"These conferences brought together engineers, mathematicians, neurologists, psychologists, anthropologists, and social scientists, all speaking different disciplinary languages and all discovering, with varying degrees of excitement and alarm, that they might be saying the same thing."
— Common characterization of the Macy Conferences, 1946–1953
IV — Cold War Anxiety

Automation, Unemployment, and the Human Use of Human Beings

By 1950, Wiener had become increasingly alarmed by what he saw as the inevitable social consequences of the technologies he and his colleagues were developing. The second industrial revolution — the automation of judgment and decision-making, not merely of muscle — was coming, and he believed it would cause massive social disruption if society did not prepare for it.

His 1950 book The Human Use of Human Beings stated this concern with unusual directness for an academic text. He argued that automation would displace workers not only in manufacturing but increasingly in professional and administrative roles — wherever repetitive, rule-based judgment was required. He called for serious public discussion of how to manage this transition, and he warned that leaving it to market forces alone would produce social catastrophe.

The 1950 Warning — Still Debated

Wiener wrote in 1950: "The automatic machine... is the precise economic equivalent of slave labor. Any labor which competes with slave labor must accept the economic conditions of slave labor... [The worker's] social adjustment is done for him by the machine." He argued that an economy that used machines wherever they were cheaper than humans would, without deliberate intervention, eliminate not just unpleasant jobs but the economic foundation of the working and middle class. This argument — that automation creates structural unemployment that markets do not self-correct — remains one of the most contested questions in economics and public policy today, seventy-five years later.

Wiener's concerns were not technophobic. He did not oppose automation in principle — he understood its potential to liberate humanity from grinding labor. What he opposed was the abdication of responsibility for its consequences. "The answer, of course, is to have a society based on human values other than buying and selling," he wrote — a sentence that scandalized economists of the 1950s and resonates uncomfortably in the 2020s.

The Cold War Context

Wiener's warnings were delivered into a specific Cold War context in which the automation of weapons, communications, and decision-making was proceeding at maximum speed under the pressure of superpower competition. The United States was building SAGE (Semi-Automatic Ground Environment), a networked system of radar stations and computers designed to provide continental air defense against Soviet bombers — a massive, networked feedback control system of exactly the kind Wiener's cybernetics described. He was, simultaneously, the intellectual father of these systems and one of their most vocal critics. This was not comfortable for anyone involved.

V — The Community

The People Who Made the Moment

Collaborator

Arturo Rosenblueth (1900–1970)

Mexican physician and physiologist. Wiener's closest scientific collaborator. Their joint papers on purposive behavior and teleology defined the conceptual foundations of cybernetics. Wiener dedicated Cybernetics to him.

Collaborator

Julian Bigelow (1913–2003)

Engineer who worked with Wiener on the WWII predictor. Later helped build the von Neumann computer at the Institute for Advanced Study. The practical engineering half of the cybernetics project.

Parallel Theorist

Claude Shannon (1916–2001)

Information theory. The Shannon-Wiener connection is deep: both were working on related problems of communication, noise, and uncertainty. Shannon's entropy and Wiener's entropy are mathematically equivalent.

Contemporary

John von Neumann (1903–1957)

Game theory, computing architecture, quantum mechanics. Von Neumann and Wiener moved in overlapping circles — both attended the Macy Conferences, both worked on the boundary between mathematics and computing. Their temperaments were complementary and their ambitions competing.

Student / Collaborator

Walter Pitts (1923–1969)

Self-taught mathematical prodigy discovered by Wiener. With McCulloch, created the logical neuron model. His tragic later life — alcoholism, destroyed work, early death — is one of the saddest stories in the history of computing.

Intellectual Partner

Warren McCulloch (1898–1969)

Neurologist and philosopher. Organized the Macy Conferences. Co-authored the logical neuron paper with Pitts. His restless intellect ranged from neurophysiology to philosophy of mind to cybernetic psychiatry.
