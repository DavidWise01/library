The Long Afterlife

The Nobel Prize she never received. The book that caricatured her. The slow, still-continuing restoration of what she actually did.

1958 — Present

October 1962 — Stockholm

The Prize She Could Not Receive

On October 18, 1962, the Nobel Assembly at Karolinska Institutet announced that the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine would be awarded jointly to Francis Harry Compton Crick, James Dewey Watson, and Maurice Hugh Frederick Wilkins — "for their discoveries concerning the molecular structure of nucleic acids and its significance for information transfer in living material." The announcement was received, in most quarters, as long-overdue recognition of one of the century's greatest scientific discoveries. Four years had passed since the prize was last awarded for directly related work.

Rosalind Franklin had been dead for four years. The Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine — like all Nobel Prizes — cannot be awarded posthumously. The rules of the Nobel Foundation, established in 1974, made this explicit, but the practice of not awarding posthumously substantially predates the written rule. Franklin died in April 1958. The Nobel was awarded in 1962. She missed eligibility by four years and five months.

The Nobel Rule

The Nobel Prize may not be awarded posthumously except in cases where the laureate dies after the announcement but before the award ceremony. Franklin died in April 1958. Watson, Crick, and Wilkins were awarded the prize in October 1962. There is no mechanism — then or now — to award the prize to Franklin. She is ineligible not because her contribution was insufficient, but because she is dead.

In his Nobel lecture, Watson did not mention Franklin's name. Crick's lecture mentioned her work obliquely. Wilkins acknowledged her contributions most directly — he noted that "without her observations and careful measurements at the start of the project, we would have had no basis for our model." It was more than Watson offered. It was far less than an adequate acknowledgment of Photo 51.

The Nobel Prize could accommodate four laureates in a single category — it has been awarded to three multiple times. Four would have been unusual but not unprecedented. Whether the committee knew the extent of Franklin's contribution, and whether they considered her, is not fully known. What is known is that Franklin's role was not prominently discussed in the nominations or the deliberations that have been made public.

"It would be wrong to suggest that Rosalind Franklin was robbed of the Nobel Prize. It would be equally wrong to suggest that the Prize accurately reflects the magnitude of her contribution."

— Brenda Maddox, biographer, Rosalind Franklin: The Dark Lady of DNA (2002)

1968 — Watson's Account

The Book That Shaped the Myth

James Watson's memoir The Double Helix: A Personal Account of the Discovery of the Structure of DNA was published by Atheneum in 1968. It was a bestseller. It is still in print. It was, at the time, hailed as an unusually honest account of how science actually works — competitive, messy, driven by personality and ambition as much as by pure intellectual curiosity. It remains one of the most widely read books about scientific discovery ever published.

It also contains one of the most influential caricatures in the history of science. Watson's portrayal of Franklin — as "Rosy," a nickname she hated and was never called by anyone she worked with; as dark, difficult, unfeminine, methodologically rigid, someone to be managed and worked around rather than collaborated with — entered public consciousness as a broadly credible portrait. Watson was there. He was the narrator. He was witty and self-deprecating about his own flaws. The framing worked.

The portrait was substantially false. The evidence for this is extensive: the testimony of Anne Sayre, who knew Franklin personally; the testimony of Aaron Klug, who worked with her for five years at Birkbeck; the testimony of colleagues from Paris who had maintained friendships with her for a decade; and most importantly, the primary record — Franklin's own papers, letters, and notebooks, which show a scientist of extraordinary precision and rigor who was functioning effectively in a difficult institutional environment, not the obstinate anti-helix obstructionist of Watson's account.

The "Rosy" Problem

No one who worked with Rosalind Franklin called her "Rosy." Her friends called her Rosalind. Her family called her Ros. Watson's use of "Rosy" — a diminutive she would have found grating — was not an incidental detail. It positioned her as someone to be patronized. Combined with Watson's extended commentary on her appearance and what he described as her failure to "make the best of herself," the portrait was less a description of a colleague than a dismissal of one.

The Pre-Publication Drama

Watson's book was controversial before it appeared. Francis Crick and Maurice Wilkins both objected to portions of it during pre-publication review — Crick primarily on grounds that it misrepresented the sequence of scientific reasoning, Wilkins that it portrayed him unflatteringly. The original publisher, Harvard University Press, declined to publish it after members of the Harvard faculty raised objections. Franklin's family, particularly her brother Colin, objected to the portrayal of Rosalind. None of these objections substantially altered the published text.

Anne Sayre, who had been a close friend of Franklin's, read the book and found it deeply wrong. She began what would become a seven-year project: a biography of Franklin that would correct Watson's account and restore the historical record. Sayre's book, Rosalind Franklin and DNA, published in 1975, was the first sustained public reappraisal of Franklin's role. It was written by a friend, not a scientist — Sayre was a novelist — and it was in places polemical. But its core argument was correct: Franklin had been misrepresented, and her contribution had been inadequately acknowledged.

1975 — Present

The Slow Restoration

The rehabilitation of Rosalind Franklin's reputation has been gradual, cumulative, and — as of the time this volume was written — substantially complete in the scientific community and substantially incomplete in popular culture. The scientific community now broadly understands that Photo 51 was central to the Watson-Crick model, that Franklin's data provided quantitative constraints the Cambridge group did not generate independently, and that the attribution in the 1953 papers was inadequate. Watson's caricature persists in popular imagination.

The Continuation

Aaron Klug: What She Started

Aaron Klug's Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1982 — awarded for "the development of crystallographic electron microscopy and his structural elucidation of biologically important nucleic acid-protein complexes" — is, in a precise sense, a continuation of the work Franklin began. The TMV structural work they did together at Birkbeck established the methodology and many of the fundamental results. The Nobel for structural crystallography of nucleic acid-protein complexes is, in significant part, a posthumous recognition of a research program that Franklin founded and led.

Klug was unambiguous about this in his Nobel lecture. He spoke at length about Franklin: her precision, her intellectual honesty, her refusal to overclaim, her extraordinary experimental ability. He described the collaboration as a genuine partnership. He described how she had established the fundamental framework for understanding helical virus structures that his subsequent work had extended and elaborated. He named her. He did what Watson had not done.

"Rosalind Franklin is often remembered primarily in the context of the DNA story," Klug said. "But her work on the tobacco mosaic virus and on other viruses was, if anything, more complete. She finished what she began. I have spent much of my career finishing what she began with me."

"She saw things precisely and described them precisely. She was not given to speculation — she believed the data should speak. And when the data spoke, it said exactly what she reported."

— Aaron Klug, Nobel Lecture, Stockholm, December 1982

The Downstream

Everything That Followed

The double helix structure of DNA, whose critical dimensional parameters were read from Franklin's diffraction pattern, is the foundation of molecular biology. It is not an exaggeration to say that the entire edifice of modern genetics, genomics, and genetic medicine rests on the structural insight encoded in Photo 51.

Molecular Biology

The Central Dogma

DNA to RNA to protein. The flow of genetic information — the "central dogma" articulated by Crick — is only comprehensible once you understand DNA's double-stranded structure. Each strand is a template for its complement. This is visible in the photograph.

Genomics

Sequencing the Genome

The Human Genome Project, completed in 2003 — fifty years after Photo 51 — sequenced all 3 billion base pairs of human DNA. The base-pairing rules that make sequencing possible are directly derived from the helical structure Franklin measured.

Gene Editing

CRISPR-Cas9

CRISPR gene editing, which won the 2020 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, exploits the double-stranded structure of DNA to find and cut specific sequences with precision. The guide RNA finds its target by complementary base-pairing — the same pairing visible in Photo 51's geometry.

Medicine

Genetic Medicine

Cancer diagnostics, inherited disease screening, mRNA vaccines, gene therapy — the entire field of genetic medicine begins with understanding that DNA is a double helix with specific, measurable geometry. That geometry was first clearly seen in Franklin's camera.

The chain of consequence runs from a laboratory at King's College London in May 1952 — a carefully controlled DNA fiber, a precisely calibrated X-ray beam, 100 hours of exposure time, a film plate — to every genetic medicine administered in every hospital in the world today. The photograph that encodes all of this is stored in the archives. Rosalind Franklin took it at age thirty-one. She died at thirty-seven. She never knew any of this would happen.

What Remains

Her Name on Things That Matter

The retrospective honoring of Rosalind Franklin has accelerated since 2000. A partial accounting of the institutions, ships, and instruments bearing her name illustrates how completely the scientific community has revised its assessment of her significance.

2020 New £50 note features Alan Turing; campaign continues for Franklin on currency

The Rosalind Franklin Institute, established by the UK Research and Innovation agency in 2019 at the Harwell Campus in Oxfordshire, focuses on the development of new technologies for life science research — X-ray imaging, cryo-electron microscopy, mass spectrometry. It is named for a scientist who spent her career mastering the use of precisely these techniques. The naming is not coincidental.

There is a building at Newnham College Cambridge named for her. There is a building at King's College London named for her. There is a crater on Venus named for her — designated Ranklin by the International Astronomical Union. There are scholarships, fellowships, lectureships, and prizes at institutions across the world that carry her name.

"Science and everyday life cannot and should not be separated. Science, for me, gives a partial explanation of life. In so far as it goes, it is based on fact, experience and experiment."

— Rosalind Franklin, letter to her father, 1940

She wrote that letter at nineteen, during her first year at Cambridge. It is the sentence most often quoted from her correspondence — not because it is the most technically sophisticated thing she wrote, but because it captures something essential: a refusal to segregate the intellectual from the lived, the scientific from the human. She believed science was not a domain separate from life but a way of understanding it. She held this belief in a context — a university that did not grant women degrees, a college that made women eat in separate rooms, a scientific community that used her work without adequate credit — that gave her every reason to retreat into pure technique as a way of avoiding the larger insults. She did not retreat. She kept making the argument.

Historical Reckoning

What We Owe the Record

There is a question that history does not have the tools to answer definitively: if Rosalind Franklin had lived — if the ovarian cancer had not arrived in 1956, if she had worked another twenty or thirty years — what would she have done? Her trajectory at Birkbeck suggests she would have extended the structural virology work to animal viruses, to more complex macromolecular assemblies, possibly to the intersection of structure and function in biological systems. Klug, working in the tradition they established together, won the Nobel in 1982. A living Franklin, working alongside him, would likely have shared it.

The structure of the Nobel Prize system means she was not robbed by the Nobel committee in 1962. She was not eligible. The system does not punish women; it simply does not accommodate scientists who die young. But the history of how her work was used — seen, measured, encoded in an image taken with her instrument and her technique, shown to Watson without her knowledge, incorporated into a model that bore no primary attribution to her — is a history that the scientific community has spent sixty years slowly, imperfectly coming to terms with.

The terms of that reckoning are still being negotiated. Watson's published statements on race in the 2000s led to his honorary titles being stripped. His account of DNA's discovery has been substantially revised by scholarship. Crick's later acknowledgments have been noted. The historical record has been substantially corrected. The popular myth lags. The work of people like Anne Sayre and Brenda Maddox and Aaron Klug — arguing, in different registers, for a more accurate account — has cumulatively moved the understanding of what happened in a laboratory in King's College London in May 1952.

What happened was: a scientist prepared a DNA fiber with extraordinary care, mounted it in a humidity-controlled camera, fired X-rays at it for one hundred hours, and developed the film. The image that appeared on the film encoded the structure of the molecule that carries the information for all living things. She measured the image. She extracted the numbers. The numbers were correct. The structure was there, waiting in the pattern she had made it possible to see.

Her name was Rosalind Franklin. The photograph was Photo 51. The structure was the double helix.

"All the DNA work at King's was a bit of a disaster, as far as scientific progress was concerned... Rosalind Franklin's departure opened the way for Crick and Watson to complete their famous paper."

— This version of events, common in the 1960s and 1970s, has been substantially revised. Franklin's work was not a disaster. It was the foundation.
