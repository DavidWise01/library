Photo 51

The image that changed biology — taken by Franklin, seen by Watson, credited to others for a generation.

King's College London — May 1952

The Method

X-Ray Crystallography: Seeing the Invisible

You cannot see a DNA molecule with a conventional microscope. The wavelength of visible light is roughly 400–700 nanometers. A DNA strand is about 2 nanometers wide — two hundred times smaller than the shortest wavelength of light the human eye can perceive. To resolve DNA's structure, you need radiation with a wavelength closer to the dimensions of atoms and bonds: X-rays, with wavelengths of 0.01 to 10 nanometers.

But you cannot simply aim X-rays at a molecule and photograph it. X-rays are scattered by the electron clouds around atoms. When X-rays pass through a crystalline substance — where atoms are arranged in a regular, repeating pattern — the scattered waves interfere with each other. Where waves align in phase, they reinforce. Where they oppose, they cancel. The result is a diffraction pattern: a set of bright spots and arcs on a detector film, arranged in a geometry that encodes the three-dimensional arrangement of atoms in the crystal.

To interpret a diffraction pattern is to work the problem backward: given the pattern of constructive and destructive interference on the film, infer the atomic arrangement that produced it. This is a mathematical and intuitive challenge simultaneously — you must understand the Fourier transform relationship between real space and reciprocal space, interpret the symmetry of the pattern, and apply physical constraints on what kinds of structures are chemically plausible.

Bragg's Law

The relationship between X-ray wavelength (λ), crystal plane spacing (d), and diffraction angle (θ) is given by Bragg's law: nλ = 2d sin θ. When Rosalind Franklin measured the positions of diffraction spots in Photo 51, she was reading off values of d directly — the inter-planar spacings of DNA's structure. The mathematics is exact. The measurements, done carefully, cannot lie.

Why Franklin Was Among the Best

X-ray crystallography of biological macromolecules in the early 1950s was technically brutal. Getting a protein or nucleic acid to form a crystal — or, in the case of DNA fibers, to align in sufficiently ordered arrays — required meticulous specimen preparation. The X-ray equipment was temperamental. Exposure times were long. The diffraction patterns of biological molecules were often weak, diffuse, and difficult to interpret.

Franklin's competitive advantage operated at every stage of this process. She prepared specimens with extraordinary care, controlling humidity and specimen geometry to a degree unusual for the era. She chose exposure parameters with precision. Her pattern interpretation was backed by five years of working with diffuse, disordered patterns in carbon research — exactly the training needed for the messy patterns of DNA fibers. She was, in the language of crystallographers, both a superb technician and a superb analyst.

When Watson saw Photo 51 — he described his reaction as his "jaw dropped and pulse began to race" — he was responding to the quality of the image. It was visibly the best diffraction image of DNA in existence. It was better than anything his Cambridge group had produced. It was better than anything Wilkins had produced. Franklin's specimen preparation had made it possible.

May 1952 — King's College London

The Image

Photo 51 is a black-and-white X-ray diffraction photograph of the B form of DNA, taken by Rosalind Franklin and her research student Raymond Gosling in May 1952. The exposure was approximately 100 hours. The specimen was a fiber of DNA maintained at precisely controlled humidity — the B form appears only when DNA fibers contain sufficient water.

The image shows a characteristic X-shaped pattern of dark reflections, called the "X pattern" or "St Andrew's cross" — a hallmark of helical structures in X-ray crystallography. The shape of this pattern, the positions of the reflections, and the systematic absences (positions where reflections are missing) together encode the structural parameters of DNA directly.

Schematic of Photo 51's diffraction pattern — the X-cross encodes helical structure; the meridional spot at 3.4 Å encodes base-pair rise; the equatorial signal encodes helix diameter and phosphate position.

What the Image Revealed

Franklin measured the pattern with precision. The structural information encoded in Photo 51 was substantial:

3.4 Å Rise per base pair along the helix axis (meridional reflection)
34 Å Distance per complete helical turn (10 base pairs per turn)
~20 Å Diameter of the helix (from equatorial reflections)
Outside Position of phosphate groups — on the exterior of the helix

The phosphate-outside conclusion was critical. The Cambridge model-building group had initially been trying to build DNA with the phosphates on the inside — a configuration that would have been chemically impossible given how tightly packed the interior of the helix was. Franklin's measurements ruled this out definitively. Watson and Crick's eventual correct model has the phosphate groups on the outside, the bases stacked on the inside — exactly as the diffraction pattern demanded.

Franklin had also established, from the pattern's symmetry and the presence of the characteristic cross, that DNA in the B form was helical. She had measured the helix parameters. She was, in early 1953, working toward a complete structural model. She was not far from the answer.

January 1953

The Unauthorized Showing

In January 1953, Maurice Wilkins showed Photo 51 to James Watson. Watson was visiting King's College on other business. Wilkins, without consulting Franklin, without her knowledge, without her permission, took the photograph from a filing cabinet and showed it to Watson in his office.

Watson's account in The Double Helix (1968) makes the moment vivid: "The instant I saw the picture my mouth fell open and my pulse began to race. The pattern was unbelievably simpler than those obtained previously... The black cross of reflections which dominated the picture could arise only from a helical structure."

Watson memorized the key numbers — 3.4 Å rise, 34 Å per turn — and returned to Cambridge. He communicated the measurements to Francis Crick. Combined with another piece of Franklin's data — a Medical Research Council report that included her unit cell dimensions, also shared without her knowledge or consent — Watson and Crick had the quantitative constraints needed to build their model.

The MRC Report

In late 1952, the MRC biophysics committee had circulated an internal report summarizing the work of various units, including Franklin's data on DNA — her unit cell dimensions and water content measurements for B-form DNA. Max Perutz, a member of both the MRC committee and the Cambridge group, gave this report to Watson and Crick. This data, combined with Photo 51, gave them the quantitative framework for their model. Neither Franklin nor Randall had been asked whether this material could be shared.

Was This Theft?

The question has been debated for seventy years. The answer requires distinguishing between legal norms and ethical norms of 1953 scientific practice. Franklin's data was not technically secret — MRC reports circulated within the scientific community, and informal sharing of crystallographic data was common. But the norms of scientific attribution required that use of another researcher's primary data be acknowledged. Watson and Crick's original 1953 Nature paper acknowledges "stimulation from" the King's group. It does not acknowledge that its critical quantitative parameters — the parameters that distinguish the correct model from the many wrong ones — came directly from Franklin's measurements.

Crick, late in life, was more forthcoming than Watson. He acknowledged that Photo 51 had been important, that he had not appreciated at the time how directly they had relied on Franklin's data. Watson, in various interviews, maintained that everything was proceeding in scientific parallel. The historical record — particularly the dates — does not support this. The model was built in weeks after Watson saw the photograph.

Franklin's Own Notes — Early 1953

She Was Getting There

A draft paper discovered after Franklin's death — sometimes called the "unfinished paper" — shows that by early 1953, Franklin had independently concluded that B-form DNA was a double helix with the phosphate groups on the outside. She had established the two-strand structure. She had the dimensions. She was working on the base-pairing details.

The popular narrative — that Franklin was anti-helix, that she resisted the helical model, that she was being cautious to the point of obtuseness — is substantially inaccurate. Franklin was cautious about the A form of DNA, which does not produce as clean a helical cross in diffraction. For the B form, for which Photo 51 was taken, she understood by early 1953 that it was helical. Her notes make this plain.

She was not racing to publish a model. She was working empirically, building the case from measurement. This is a legitimate and indeed admirable scientific style. It was also, in this case, the approach that would allow Watson and Crick — working from her data — to get there first with a bold, model-based publication.

"The results suggest a helical structure (which must be very closely packed) containing probably 2, 3 or 4 co-axial nucleotide chains."

— Franklin's research notes, c. February 1953

Her notes also show that she was reasoning correctly about the phosphate position — outside — and about the water content requirements of the B form. She had the essential structural picture. Watson and Crick published in April 1953. Franklin's complementary paper on DNA X-ray data was published in the same issue of Nature — presented as corroborating the model, when in fact the model had been built using her data.

Birkbeck College — 1953–1958

Beyond DNA: Virus Structure

After transferring to Birkbeck in 1953, Franklin redirected her crystallographic precision entirely to viruses. The tobacco mosaic virus (TMV) work was, scientifically, her most complete achievement — a full structural analysis carried through from specimen preparation to publication, with no one else using her data without credit.

TMV is a rod-shaped virus, roughly 300 nm long and 18 nm wide, consisting of a helical protein coat wound around a single-stranded RNA molecule. Franklin's X-ray work at Birkbeck, combined with Aaron Klug's mathematical analysis, established the helical structure of TMV's protein subunit array — the pitch of the helix, the number of subunits per turn, the radial position of the RNA. This was one of the first complete structural descriptions of a plant virus.

She also worked on turnip yellow mosaic virus (TYMV), demonstrating that the RNA in that virus was located inside the protein shell — not outside as some models had proposed. This was a significant contribution to understanding how genetic material is packaged in viruses. Franklin gave plenary lectures on TMV structure at international crystallography meetings. She was recognized as a leader in the field of structural virology.
