Rosalind Elsie Franklin

The woman whose camera saw the double helix first — and whose name was almost lost to history.

1920 – 1958

London, 1920

A Child Born to Think

Rosalind Elsie Franklin was born on July 25, 1920, in Notting Hill, London, into a prominent Anglo-Jewish family whose prosperity was built on banking and whose culture was built on learning. The Franklins were not a family of passive wealth — they were a family of active minds. Ellis Franklin, her father, had interrupted his own education at Cambridge to serve in the First World War; he returned afterward to work at the family bank while sustaining a deep interest in history and social causes. Muriel Waley Franklin, her mother, was quietly formidable. Between them they instilled in their children — five in all, Rosalind the second — an expectation of intellectual rigor that was not gendered. This was unusual. This mattered.

From earliest childhood, Rosalind distinguished herself. Family legend holds that she was doing arithmetic in her head before most children her age were reading. Her school reports from the age of nine onward identify her as exceptional. She was not simply bright in the way some children are bright — precocious, quick, easily bored. She was constitutionally drawn to precision: to getting things right, exactly right, in ways that could be demonstrated and verified. This temperament would define her career.

She attended St Paul's Girls' School in Hammersmith, one of the few secondary schools in England at that time that took science seriously for girls. She studied physics and chemistry. She won a scholarship. At sixteen she declared she would be a scientist. Her father resisted — not from unkindness but from a belief, common in his generation, that science was not a suitable career for a woman of her station. Her aunt Mamie intervened, arguing for her. Rosalind prevailed.

"Science and everyday life cannot and should not be separated."

— Rosalind Franklin

Newnham College, Cambridge

In 1938, Franklin entered Newnham College, Cambridge — one of only two colleges that admitted women. Cambridge in 1938 was, for women, a peculiar contradiction: they could attend lectures, sit the same examinations as the men, and achieve the same results. What they could not do was receive the same degree. The University of Cambridge did not grant women formal degrees until 1948 — a decade after Franklin arrived and five years after she left. The men who sat beside her in examination halls received BA degrees. She received a certificate of completion. The word "degree" was withheld.

She read physical chemistry under distinguished supervision and excelled. She had close friendships and a fierce independence — she was not socially withdrawn, as later caricatures would suggest, but she was direct, precise, and not given to social performance. She said what she thought. She expected others to do the same. This quality would serve her beautifully in the crystallography laboratories of Paris. It would serve her poorly in the institutional politics of King's College London.

She graduated in 1941. The Second World War had been underway for two years. The context in which she would begin her scientific career was wartime Britain.

1942–1947 — BCURA

Coal and the Birth of a Technique

Franklin spent her war years at the British Coal Utilisation Research Association (BCURA), conducting research into the microstructure of coals. This work is often treated as a biographical footnote — the years before the "real" story. It was nothing of the kind. The coal research was the foundation.

Coal is structurally complex: amorphous, partially crystalline, porous. Understanding its microstructure required understanding how gases and liquids move through it — how the pore geometry controls permeability. Franklin applied X-ray diffraction to this problem, combined with systematic density measurements and gas absorption experiments. She developed a method of classifying coal types by their structural properties — a classification scheme that remained in use for decades. She published five papers on coal microstructure that stand as significant contributions to physical chemistry.

But the deeper achievement was technical. Working with X-rays on disordered, partially crystalline materials requires extraordinary care in specimen preparation and pattern interpretation. You cannot assume perfect crystallinity. You must reason from partial, ambiguous patterns. Franklin learned to do this — learned to extract structural information from imperfect data — in conditions far more demanding than those a crystallographer would face working with well-ordered protein or nucleic acid crystals.

Why the Coal Work Matters

Franklin's coal research trained her in the exact discipline that would make Photo 51 possible: extracting precise structural information from X-ray diffraction patterns of non-ideal specimens. When she later produced the clearest X-ray image of DNA ever taken, it was not luck. It was the accumulation of five years of technical refinement.

She earned her PhD from Cambridge in 1945 for this coal research. She was twenty-five. She had published papers. She had a technique. She needed a laboratory that could use it.

1947–1950 — Laboratoire Central des Services Chimiques

Paris: The Years She Thrived

In 1947, Franklin left England for Paris. She had secured a fellowship at the Laboratoire Central des Services Chimiques de l'État (LCSCS), later known as the LNRS — the French national laboratory of physical chemistry, a serious institution with serious people. She worked under Jacques Mering, one of the leading X-ray crystallographers in Europe.

The Paris years were the happiest of her professional life. Biographer Anne Sayre, who knew Franklin personally, is unambiguous on this point: she was treated as a colleague. Not as a woman-scientist. Not as a curiosity. As a scientist. She ate lunch with her colleagues. She argued with them about data. She was included in conversations. She socialized. She learned to speak French with characteristic precision — she was never merely fluent, she was exact. She hiked in the Alps on weekends.

Under Mering's supervision, she became one of the best X-ray crystallographers in the world — not merely competent, but genuinely exceptional. Her publications from this period on carbon structure are technically sophisticated and methodologically innovative. She developed techniques for analyzing diffuse diffraction patterns — the kind produced by poorly ordered materials — that would become part of the standard toolkit of the field.

The Return

1950

A Turner-Newall Research Fellowship brings Franklin back to England — to King's College London. The circumstances would prove catastrophic.

She would have stayed in Paris. She had friends there, a laboratory that valued her, a city she loved. The fellowship that brought her back to England was a prestigious opportunity — X-ray analysis of DNA fibers at King's College London, one of the most important problems in structural biology. She took it. She could not have known what she was walking into.

1951–1953 — King's College London

The Hostile Institution

The administrative circumstances surrounding Franklin's arrival at King's College London in January 1951 were chaotic in ways that would prove professionally devastating. John Randall, the director of the Medical Research Council's biophysics unit, had written to Franklin describing her role as the lead researcher on X-ray diffraction of DNA. DNA fiber work, X-ray crystallography, specimen preparation — this was her assignment, with research student Raymond Gosling working under her direction.

Maurice Wilkins, a senior researcher at King's who had been doing preliminary DNA X-ray work before Franklin arrived, returned from a sabbatical to find her installed in what he understood to be his territory. Randall had not communicated clearly to Wilkins. The result was a foundational ambiguity: Wilkins believed Franklin was his assistant. She was not. She was an independent MRC researcher, his effective equal. This misunderstanding was never formally resolved. It poisoned everything.

King's College London in 1951 was, for women, an institution that made its priorities clear through architecture. Male researchers and staff ate in the main dining room. Women ate in a different room. This was not custom — it was enforced policy. For Franklin, who had spent three years being treated as a full colleague in Paris, the institutional misogyny of King's was not merely uncomfortable. It was a daily reminder that she was considered lesser by the very institution that employed her.

"Her results were better than ours. That was simply a fact."

— Aaron Klug, on Franklin's X-ray technique

The Work at King's

Despite the institutional hostility, Franklin produced extraordinary science at King's. She identified that DNA existed in two forms — the A form (drier, more crystalline) and the B form (wetter, more extended) — and that the two required different analytical approaches. She systematically pursued the A form while Gosling worked with her on the B form. The B-form diffraction pattern she produced in May 1952 — Photo 51 — was the clearest X-ray image of DNA ever taken. It encoded the structural answer directly.

Franklin was not working toward confirming a theory. She was working empirically: build the best specimen, take the best diffraction pattern, extract the best structural data. She was right to be cautious about model-building. Her standards were higher than those of the model-builders in Cambridge.

The Transfer to Birkbeck

By early 1953, the working relationship between Franklin and Wilkins had deteriorated beyond repair. Franklin had already arranged a transfer to Birkbeck College, where J.D. Bernal led a crystallography group. Watson and Crick published their double helix model in April 1953. Franklin left King's shortly after. She would never know — not in the remaining five years of her life — the full extent to which her work had been used to build that model.

1953–1958 — Birkbeck College

Five Productive Years

At Birkbeck, Franklin finally found a laboratory commensurate with her abilities. J.D. Bernal was one of the great figures of British science — a crystallographer, a polymath, a man who treated his researchers as scientists regardless of gender. Franklin arrived with her technique, her precision, and her ambition redirected entirely away from DNA. She would work on viruses.

The tobacco mosaic virus (TMV) work at Birkbeck was, by any measure, outstanding. TMV is a helical virus — a protein coat wound in a helix around an RNA core. Franklin applied her X-ray crystallography method to TMV specimens with the same meticulous care she had applied to coal and to DNA. She determined key structural parameters of TMV — the helical pitch, the protein subunit arrangement — that constituted one of the first viral structures determined at high resolution.

Her collaborator at Birkbeck was Aaron Klug, a young South African physicist who had recently arrived in England. The Franklin-Klug collaboration was immediately productive: they worked in genuine partnership, each contributing complementary skills. Klug's mathematical analysis of diffraction patterns combined with Franklin's specimen preparation and data quality produced results neither could have achieved alone. Klug later described Franklin as "the best experimentalist I had ever encountered."

She also worked on turnip yellow mosaic virus (TYMV), potato virus X, and other plant viruses. She lectured at international conferences. She traveled to the United States. She was building the career that would have made her — had she lived — a Nobel laureate. She was not a tragic figure in these years. She was a scientist at the height of her powers, doing excellent work, finally surrounded by people who recognized it.

1956–1958

The Last Years

In 1956, Franklin was diagnosed with ovarian cancer. She was thirty-six years old. She underwent surgery and continued working. She traveled to the United States in 1956 and again in 1957. She published papers. She gave talks. She did not stop.

The connection between her cancer and her years of X-ray work is considered by medical historians to be essentially certain. She worked with X-ray equipment without adequate shielding throughout her career — at BCURA, at the LNRS, at King's, at Birkbeck. She took thousands of exposures. The cumulative radiation dose was enormous. Ovarian cancer from radiation exposure does not announce itself. It arrives.

She died on April 16, 1958, at the Royal Marsden Hospital in London. She was thirty-seven years old. She had published over thirty papers. She had determined the structures of two viruses. She had produced, in Photo 51, the clearest X-ray image of DNA in existence. She had not been told — and apparently did not know — that Watson and Crick had used her data without credit. The controversy unfolded after she was gone.

"Rosalind Franklin was a great scientist. But she was also — I must say this — a friend."

— Aaron Klug, Nobel Lecture, 1982

Chronology

A Life in Years

  • 1920July 25: Rosalind Elsie Franklin born in Notting Hill, London. Second of five children of Ellis and Muriel Franklin.
  • 1932Enters St Paul's Girls' School, Hammersmith. Studies physics and chemistry. Already identified as exceptional.
  • 1938Newnham College, Cambridge. Reads physical chemistry. Cambridge will not formally grant women degrees until 1948.
  • 1941Graduates Cambridge with equivalent of First Class — but receives a certificate, not a degree. Women at Cambridge are not granted degrees until seven years later.
  • 1942Joins BCURA (British Coal Utilisation Research Association). Begins research into coal microstructure using X-ray diffraction.
  • 1945PhD from Cambridge for coal microstructure research. Publishes five papers on the physical chemistry of carbons.
  • 1947Moves to Paris. Joins LNRS under Jacques Mering. Learns advanced X-ray crystallography. Treated as a full scientific colleague. The happiest years of her professional life.
  • 1950Turner-Newall Fellowship brings her back to England. Appointed to King's College London to lead DNA X-ray crystallography.
  • 1951Arrives King's College London, January. Administrative confusion with Wilkins begins immediately. She is the lead DNA researcher; he treats her as an assistant.
  • 1952May: Photo 51. The clearest X-ray diffraction image of DNA ever produced. It shows: helical structure, 2 strands, phosphate groups on outside, key dimensions.
  • 1953January: Wilkins shows Photo 51 to Watson without Franklin's knowledge or consent. April: Watson and Crick publish double helix model. Franklin transfers to Birkbeck.
  • 1953–58Birkbeck College. Works with Aaron Klug on tobacco mosaic virus and other viruses. Publishes 17 papers. At the height of her scientific powers.
  • 1956Diagnosed with ovarian cancer. Undergoes surgery. Continues working.
  • 1958April 16: Rosalind Franklin dies at the Royal Marsden Hospital, London. She is thirty-seven years old.
  • 1962Watson, Crick, and Wilkins receive the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for the double helix. The Nobel cannot be awarded posthumously. Franklin is not mentioned.
  • 1968Watson publishes The Double Helix, portraying Franklin dismissively as "Rosy" — a nickname she hated. The caricature enters public consciousness.
  • 1975Anne Sayre publishes Rosalind Franklin and DNA, beginning the serious reappraisal of her role and Watson's account.
  • 1982Aaron Klug wins the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for structural crystallography — work he began in collaboration with Franklin at Birkbeck.
