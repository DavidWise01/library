Volume 1 — The Work

Kitab al-Mukhtasar
fi Hisab al-Jabr
wal-Muqabala

The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing

Written in Baghdad around 830 CE and dedicated to Caliph al-Ma'mun, this book invented a new branch of mathematics. "Al-jabr" — completion — gave the world algebra. Its six canonical equation forms gave the world a framework for solving every polynomial problem up to degree two.

The Source Text

What the Book Is

The full title — al-Kitab al-mukhtasar fi hisab al-jabr wal-muqabala — translates most directly as "The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing." Al-Khwarizmi wrote it in Arabic in Baghdad, probably around 830 CE, and dedicated it to Caliph al-Ma'mun, who had explicitly commissioned the work as a practical aid for legal and commercial calculation.

The book is divided into three major sections. The first is the theoretical core: a classification of six canonical equation forms and a method for solving each. The second section applies algebra to commercial problems — buying, selling, inheritance, partnerships. The third section applies algebra to problems of inheritance law under Islamic jurisprudence (fara'id), which required precise fractional division of estates.

It is not abstract mathematics in the Greek sense. There are no proofs from first principles in Euclidean style, no deductions from axioms. Instead, al-Khwarizmi works through types: here are all the forms a linear or quadratic equation can take, here is a rule for solving each, here are geometric demonstrations to convince you the rule is correct, here are worked examples. This pedagogical structure — classification, rule, demonstration, application — is what made the book enormously influential as a teaching tool.

The book was translated into Latin twice in the 12th century: by Robert of Chester in 1145 and by Gerard of Cremona shortly after. These translations launched algebra into European mathematics.

"That fondness for science, by which God has distinguished the Imam al-Ma'mun, the Commander of the Faithful... has encouraged me to compose a short work on calculating by al-jabr and al-muqabala, confining it to what is easiest and most useful in arithmetic."

— Al-Khwarizmi, Preface to the Algebra, c. 830 CE
Core Operations

Al-Jabr and Al-Muqabala

The two words in the title are the two fundamental operations of his algebra.

Al-Jabr (الجبر) — Completion / Restoration

Literally: "the setting of broken bones." In algebraic terms: the operation of adding a subtracted quantity to both sides of an equation to eliminate negative terms. If you have x² = 40 − 4x, al-jabr moves the subtracted term: x² + 4x = 40. You "restore" the equation to a form with no subtracted terms. This is the fundamental operation of what we would now call "moving terms across the equals sign."

Al-Muqabala (المقابلة) — Balancing / Confronting

Literally: "the placing in opposition." The operation of reducing equal terms on both sides. If you have x² + 10x + 4 = 4 + 2x, al-muqabala reduces the equal constant (4): x² + 10x = 2x, and further reduces equal x-terms: x² + 8x = 0. Together, al-jabr and al-muqabala transform any equation into one of six canonical forms.

Al-Jabr
Move subtracted quantities to the other side. "Restore" the equation.

x² = 40 - 4x
→ x² + 4x = 40
Al-Muqabala
Cancel equal terms on both sides. "Balance" the equation.

x² + 10 + 4 = 4 + 2x
→ x² + 10 = 2x
Together
Applied repeatedly, these two operations reduce any linear or quadratic equation to one of the six canonical forms — then a rule gives the solution directly.
The Classification

The Six Canonical Forms

Al-Khwarizmi had no symbol for zero as a coefficient, and he worked only with positive numbers (negative numbers were not yet accepted as legitimate). Because of this, he needed six separate equation types where we would recognize only one general form (ax² + bx + c = 0). He called the unknowns: mal (property, our x²), jidr (root, our x), and adad (number, our constant).

Type I — Squares equal roots
Amwal taSilu a Juzur
ax² = bx
Example: "A square equals five roots" → x² = 5x → x = 5
Method: Divide both sides by x (al-Khwarizmi: "divide the property by the root") → x = b/a
Modern solution: x = b/a (trivial case: x = 0 or x = b/a)
Type II — Squares equal numbers
Amwal taSilu a Adad
ax² = c
Example: "A square equals nine" → x² = 9 → x = 3
Method: "Divide the number by the coefficient of squares, then take the root"
Modern solution: x = √(c/a)
Type III — Roots equal numbers
Juzur taSilu a Adad
bx = c
Example: "Four roots equal twenty" → 4x = 20 → x = 5
Method: "Divide the number by the number of roots"
Modern solution: x = c/b (pure linear equation)
Type IV — Squares and roots equal numbers
Amwal wa-Juzur taSilu a Adad
ax² + bx = c
Example: "A square plus ten roots equals thirty-nine"
x² + 10x = 39 → the famous example!
Method: Halve the roots (5), square (25), add to number (64), take root (8), subtract half-roots (3) → x = 3
Modern solution: x = -b/2 + √((b/2)² + c) — completing the square
Type V — Squares and numbers equal roots
Amwal wa-Adad taSilu a Juzur
ax² + c = bx
Example: "A square plus twenty-one equals ten roots"
x² + 21 = 10x → x = 3 or x = 7
Method: Halve the roots (5), square (25), subtract number (4), take root (2), result is 5±2
Note: Al-Khwarizmi gives BOTH roots — a remarkably advanced recognition
Type VI — Roots and numbers equal squares
Juzur wa-Adad taSilu a Amwal
bx + c = ax²
Example: "Three roots plus four equals a square"
3x + 4 = x² → x = 4
Method: Same completing-the-square procedure, applied to rearranged form
Modern solution: x = b/2 + √((b/2)² + c) for this form
Geometric Demonstration

Proofs by Completion of the Square

Al-Khwarizmi knew that a rule without justification would not convince scholars trained in Greek geometry. For the three compound types (IV, V, VI), he supplied geometric proofs — visual demonstrations that the algebraic procedure is correct. These proofs are not trivial: they represent a genuine synthesis of Greek geometric method with the new symbolic algebra.

Type IV: x² + 10x = 39

Al-Khwarizmi's most famous example. He draws a square of side x, then attaches four rectangles (each of width 2.5 and length x) to its four sides. The total area is x² + 10x = 39. To complete the large square, he fills in the four small corner squares, each of area 2.5² = 6.25, giving 4 × 6.25 = 25 to add to both sides.

39 + 25 = 64 = 8²

The large square has side 8. Subtracting the corner extensions (each 2.5): x = 8 − 5 = 3. The geometric figure is the proof — the algebra is visible as area.

He actually uses a slightly different arrangement (four half-strips instead of four quarter-strips) but the principle is identical. The point is: the geometric diagram justifies the algebraic rule.

Type V: x² + 21 = 10x — Two Solutions

This case is remarkable because it yields two valid positive solutions: x = 3 and x = 7. Al-Khwarizmi recognizes this explicitly — "the problem has two solutions" — which was an advanced insight for his era.

Halve roots: 5 · Square: 25 · Subtract constant: 25 - 21 = 4 · Root: 2

The geometric proof shows a rectangle of area 10x (the full strip) with x² removed from one end, leaving an area of 21. By completing the square with a (5)² square, the discrepancy resolves to 4, whose root is 2. Both x = 5+2 = 7 and x = 5−2 = 3 satisfy the original equation.

Type VI: bx + c = ax² — "Roots and Numbers Equal Squares"

The third compound type. Here the unknown squared is isolated on one side with both a linear and a constant term on the other.

3x + 4 = x² → rearrange → x² - 3x = 4

Al-Khwarizmi applies the same completing-the-square procedure: halve the coefficient of roots (3/2 = 1.5), square (2.25), add to both sides (6.25), take root (2.5), add half-roots (2.5 + 1.5 = 4).

The geometric proof draws the square x² minus the strip 3x on one side, showing geometrically that adding (3/2)² completes the shape into a perfect square.

Hindu-Arabic Numerals

The Number System That Changed Everything

Alongside the algebra book, al-Khwarizmi wrote a separate treatise on the Hindu numeral system: the positional decimal notation with nine digits and zero that we use today. The original Arabic text is lost. It survives only in a 12th-century Latin translation, which begins:

"Dixit Algoritmi: laudes deo rectori nostro atque defensori dicamus dignas..."
"Thus spake Algoritmi: let us speak praises worthy of God, our guide and defender..."

— Opening of the Latin translation, 12th century CE. The word "Algoritmi" became "algorism" → "algorithm."

The book explained the Indian system of nine digits plus zero — a place-value system where the position of a digit determines its value, rather than requiring different symbols for tens, hundreds, and thousands. This was a revolutionary simplification over Roman numerals, Greek alphabetic numerals, and Babylonian cuneiform. You could now do arithmetic with a finite set of symbols, and the procedures (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, roots) were systematic enough to be described as rules — as algorithms.

0
Zero
The positional zero — a placeholder indicating an empty position — was the key innovation from India. Al-Khwarizmi transmitted this concept to the Islamic mathematical tradition and thence to Europe.
9
Digits
Nine Hindu digits plus zero — the complete system. Al-Khwarizmi described the shapes, values, and positional rules of each, giving Europeans their first systematic account of the decimal system.
10
Base of Counting
Base-10 positional arithmetic. Each position is ten times the value of the position to its right. The principle behind every modern calculator, computer, and accounting system.
1145
Latin Translation Year
Both the arithmetic book and the algebra were translated in the 1140s, launching them into European universities. The arithmetic book entered via its translator as "the method of Algoritmi."
Beyond Algebra

His Other Achievements

Zij al-Sindhind — Astronomical Tables

His first major work (c. 820 CE) was a set of astronomical tables synthesizing Indian astronomical tradition (Sindhind = Sanskrit Siddhanta, meaning "established doctrine") with Ptolemaic Greek astronomy. It included 37 chapters covering solar, lunar, and planetary motion; eclipse calculation; the calculation of time; and trigonometric functions including sine, cosine, and tangent tables.

Adelard of Bath translated the Zij into Latin around 1126 CE, making it one of the first Arabic astronomical texts to reach European astronomers. The tables influenced the Toledan Tables, which in turn influenced European astronomy until the time of Copernicus.

Kitab Surat al-Ard — Book of the Image of the Earth

Under Caliph al-Ma'mun's commission, al-Khwarizmi coordinated a team of 70 geographers to produce an updated world map and accompanying gazetteer. The project corrected and extended Ptolemy's Geography, updating coordinates for 2,402 named localities including cities, seas, mountains, and rivers. His coordinates were better for the eastern world (which Ptolemy knew poorly) than for the western world (which the Islamic geographers knew less well).

The only surviving manuscript of Kitab Surat al-Ard was discovered in Strasbourg in 1878 — nearly a millennium after it was written. It remains a primary source for 9th-century Islamic geographic knowledge.

Kitab al-Tarikh — The Chronicle

A historical text that is largely lost. Known primarily through quotation in later Islamic historical works. It dealt with the history of the early Islamic period and demonstrates that al-Khwarizmi was not only a mathematician but a scholar of history and chronology.

Risala fi Istikhraj Tarikh al-Yahud — Jewish Calendar Treatise

A technical treatment of the Jewish calendar system — its structure, the calculation of intercalary months, and the rules for determining the dates of festivals. This work reflects the multiconfessional intellectual environment of the House of Wisdom, where scholars of different religions collaborated on technical problems.

Kitab al-Rukhama and Kitab al-'Amal bil-Asturlab

Two texts on astronomical instruments: one on sundials (rukhama) and one on the astrolabe. These are among the earliest Arabic texts on instrument design and practical astronomy. They demonstrate al-Khwarizmi's engagement not only with mathematical astronomy but with its physical tools.
