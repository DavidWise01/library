Volume 0 — The Man

Muhammad
ibn Musa
al-Khwarizmi

780 – 850 CE  ·  Khwarezm to Baghdad

He gave mathematics two of its most enduring words. His name became algorithm. His book title became algebra. He worked in a library beside the Tigris River at the center of the known world, and he changed what humans could calculate forever.

Full Name & Identity

The Man Behind the Words

Full Arabic Name Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī
Latin Name Algoritmi — the Latinization from which "algorithm" derives
Born c. 780 CE — Khwarezm region, modern Uzbekistan / Khorezm Province
Died c. 850 CE — Baghdad, Abbasid Caliphate
Active Period 813–833 CE (flourished under Caliph al-Ma'mun)
Institution Bayt al-Ḥikma (House of Wisdom), Baghdad
Fields Mathematics, Astronomy, Geography, Cartography
Patron Caliph al-Ma'mun (r. 813–833 CE), Abbasid dynasty
The name means "Muhammad, son of Moses, native of Khwarezm"
Origins

From the Banks of the Oxus

Al-Khwarizmi was born in the region of Khwarezm — the fertile delta where the Amu Darya (the ancient Oxus River) meets the Aral Sea, in the land that is now Uzbekistan. It was a crossroads civilization: Persian in culture, Zoroastrian and then Islamic in religion, sitting at the junction of the Silk Road routes that connected China to the Mediterranean.

His family's origins may have been Persian-speaking Zoroastrian converts to Islam — a common trajectory for intellectuals absorbed into the early Abbasid empire's cosmopolitan project. The epithet "al-Khwarizmi" simply means "the one from Khwarezm," a practice of geographic naming that identified scholars by their homeland long after they had traveled to the imperial capital.

At some point in his youth or early adulthood — the exact date is unknown — he made the journey to Baghdad, then the largest and most cosmopolitan city on Earth, home to perhaps one million people, the seat of Abbasid power, and the location of the institution that would define his life: the House of Wisdom.

"He was the most learned of his time in the science of the stars and mathematics and the things related thereto."

— Ibn al-Nadim, 10th-century bibliographer, in al-Fihrist

The Scholar

Who He Actually Was

We know al-Khwarizmi almost entirely through his works — he left no memoir, no letters survive, and no contemporary described his personality in detail. What we can reconstruct is the picture of a man of extraordinary synthetic intelligence: someone who could read Indian mathematical texts, Greek geometry, Babylonian astronomical tables, and Persian cartographic traditions, then distill them into entirely new frameworks.

His algebra book opens with explicit religious framing — he praises God and dedicates it to Caliph al-Ma'mun — which was standard practice. But the mathematical content itself is strikingly practical. He was not a theorist in the pure Greek sense. He was solving problems: inheritance division, land measurement, trade calculation. The six canonical equation forms in his algebra were not abstract — they came with worked examples from commercial and legal life.

This combination — rigorous classification married to practical application — is perhaps his defining intellectual signature. He was not trying to prove theorems for their own sake. He was trying to give people a complete, teachable method for solving the problems of everyday quantitative life.

He also worked across disciplines without apparent hierarchy. Astronomy, geography, mathematics — he moved between them fluidly. His astronomical tables (Zij al-Sindhind) synthesized Greek Ptolemaic astronomy with Indian positional astronomy. His geographic work updated Ptolemy's Geography with 2,400 corrected coordinates. The breadth suggests someone who saw the integration of knowledge traditions as itself a worthy intellectual goal.

"The first to write on al-jabr wa'l-muqabala. Al-Khwarizmi wrote on it before any of the ancients of his time."

— al-Qifti, 13th-century historian, in Tarikh al-Hukama

The Name Problem

Some historians have proposed the epithet al-Majusi (the Magian, i.e., Zoroastrian) appears in a few sources near his name — suggesting a Zoroastrian family background. This remains disputed. By the time of his recorded works, he was clearly writing as a Muslim. The question of his precise ethnic and religious background before his move to Baghdad is, at this remove, unanswerable with confidence.

What is not disputed: the intellectual tradition he drew from was genuinely international. He synthesized Greek geometry, Indian numerals and decimal place-value, Babylonian astronomical observation, and Persian administrative traditions into a new unified framework — one that could only have been assembled at Baghdad's House of Wisdom, which specifically collected and translated texts from all these traditions.

Life & Career

A Life in Dates

c. 780 CE
Born in Khwarezm
Born in the Khwarezm region along the Amu Darya River (modern Uzbekistan). The region had been under Arab/Muslim administration since the 710s but retained its Persian cultural character.
c. 800–810 CE
Arrives in Baghdad
Moves to Baghdad during or before the reign of Caliph Harun al-Rashid (786–809) or the subsequent civil war period. Baghdad is already the world's largest city and center of Abbasid power.
813 CE
Al-Ma'mun Becomes Caliph
Al-Ma'mun, the great intellectual patron, wins the Abbasid civil war and becomes Caliph. He immediately expands the House of Wisdom and brings scholars from across the empire to Baghdad.
c. 820 CE
Zij al-Sindhind (Astronomical Tables)
Completes his first major work — a set of astronomical tables synthesizing Indian and Greek astronomy. These tables were later translated into Latin by Adelard of Bath (c. 1126) and influenced European astronomy for centuries.
c. 825 CE
Book on Hindu-Arabic Numerals
Writes his treatise on the Hindu numeral system (including zero and positional notation). The Latin translation of this book — beginning "Dixit Algoritmi..." — gave the word "algorithm" to Western languages.
c. 830 CE
Al-Kitab al-Mukhtasar (The Algebra)
Completes and dedicates to Caliph al-Ma'mun the book on al-jabr wa'l-muqabala — the book whose title gives English the word "algebra." Six canonical equation types, geometric proofs, practical applications.
c. 833 CE
Geographic Survey
Coordinates a team of 70 geographers to produce Kitab Surat al-Ard (Book of the Image of the Earth) — an updated and corrected version of Ptolemy's Geography with 2,402 place coordinates.
833 CE
Al-Ma'mun Dies
Al-Khwarizmi's great patron dies. He continues working under successors al-Mu'tasim and al-Wathiq, but the golden period of his patronage ends.
c. 850 CE
Death in Baghdad
Dies in Baghdad after approximately 70 years of life. His works survive — copied, translated, commented upon — and continue to spread throughout the Islamic world and eventually into Christian Europe.
Bibliography

His Major Works

At least six major works survive in whole or in part, along with several attributed texts of uncertain authorship.

c. 830
Kitab al-Mukhtasar fi Hisab al-Jabr wal-Muqabala
The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing. The algebra. First systematic treatment of quadratic equations. Gave English the word "algebra."
c. 825
Kitab al-Jam' wal-Tafriq bi-Hisab al-Hind
Book on Addition and Subtraction after the Method of the Indians. Introduced Hindu-Arabic numerals (including zero) to the Islamic world. The Latin translation gave us "algorithm."
c. 820
Zij al-Sindhind
A set of astronomical tables synthesizing Indian (Sindhind = Siddhanta) and Ptolemaic astronomy. 37 chapters on calendars, astronomy, and trigonometry. Widely copied.
c. 833
Kitab Surat al-Ard
Book of the Image of the Earth. A world geography listing 2,402 localities with coordinates, correcting and extending Ptolemy's Geography. A product of collaboration with 70 scholars.
c. 825
Kitab al-Tarikh
A historical chronicle. Largely lost — known primarily through references in later Islamic historiography. Dealt with the early Islamic period.
c. 840
Risala fi Istikhraj Tarikh al-Yahud
A treatise on the Jewish calendar. Demonstrates his engagement with multiple calendar systems — Islamic, Julian, Jewish — that he would have needed for his astronomical and chronological work.
Linguistic Legacy

Two Words, One Man

Few scholars have given two words to a language — let alone words as foundational as these.

Algorithm

When his arithmetic text was translated into Latin in the 12th century, the translator rendered his name as Algoritmi. The text began "Dixit Algoritmi..." — "Thus spake Algoritmi." Medieval Europeans who learned the method of Hindu-Arabic calculation began calling it "algorism" — calculation by the method of Algoritmi. Over centuries, the word shifted to algorithm, and its meaning expanded: any step-by-step procedure for solving a problem. By the 20th century, algorithms were the foundation of computer science.

Algebra

His algebra book's full title includes the phrase al-jabr wa'l-muqabala. "Al-jabr" (الجبر) means "restoration" or "completion" — the operation of moving a subtracted quantity to the other side of an equation. "Wa'l-muqabala" means "and balancing" — the reduction of equal terms on both sides. When Robert of Chester translated the book into Latin in 1145, the term algebra entered the Latin mathematical vocabulary directly from the Arabic title. It has never left.

There is a curiosity here: al-jabr also traveled to medieval Spain as a medical term. Bone-setters — who restored (jabara) broken bones to their proper position — were called algebristas in Spanish. The same word described the mathematical operation and the surgical one: both were about restoring something displaced to its proper state.
