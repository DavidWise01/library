Euler's Identity: The Most Beautiful Equation

eiπ + 1 = 0
Euler's Identity · 1748 · Introductio in analysin infinitorum
e
Growth, compounding, natural change
i
Rotation in the complex plane
π
Half a full rotation — the circle
1
The multiplicative identity
0
The additive identity

In a 2004 poll by Physics World, mathematicians and physicists voted Euler's identity the most beautiful equation in all of mathematics. It is not merely beautiful in the aesthetic sense — it is philosophically startling. Five fundamental constants of mathematics, arising from completely different branches of the subject, are bound together in a single equation of perfect economy.

e comes from the study of continuous growth and natural logarithms. i is the square root of −1, the imaginary unit that makes the complex numbers work. π is the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter, discovered by the ancient Greeks. That they are connected at all — and connected this simply — is not obvious. Euler saw it.

"Like a Shakespearean sonnet that captures the essence of love, or a painting that brings out the beauty of the human form that is far more than just skin deep, Euler's equation reaches down into the very depths of existence." — Keith Devlin, Stanford mathematician

Euler's Formula: The Parent Equation

The identity is a special case of the more general formula, which is one of the most used equations in all of physics and engineering:

eix = cos(x) + i · sin(x) Euler's Formula · For any real number x

When x = π, the cosine of π is −1 and the sine of π is 0. The formula collapses to: eiπ = −1, i.e., eiπ + 1 = 0.

The formula expresses something geometrically profound: multiplying by eix is the same as rotating by angle x in the complex plane. Every oscillation in physics — sound waves, light waves, quantum wave functions, alternating current — is computed using this formula. Electrical engineers, quantum physicists, and signal processors all work in Euler's framework every day.

The Basel Problem: π²/6

Number Theory · 1735 Since 1650, when Pietro Mengoli posed it, the Basel Problem had resisted every European mathematician who approached it. The question: what is the exact sum of the reciprocals of the squares of all positive integers?

1 + 1⁄4 + 1⁄9 + 1⁄16 + 1⁄25 + … = π²⁄6 The Basel Problem Solution · Euler, 1735 · Shocking because π appears in a sum of fractions with no circles

Euler solved it in 1735. The proof is a masterwork of mathematical audacity — he treated an infinite polynomial using methods that were, strictly speaking, not yet justified, and the answer was π²/6. Mathematicians across Europe were stupefied. Why should π — a number about circles — appear in a sum of fractions about integers? The answer reveals a deep connection between the geometry of circles and the distribution of numbers that runs through all of modern mathematics.

Euler later generalized this to the Riemann zeta function — the object at the center of the Riemann Hypothesis, the most famous unsolved problem in mathematics to this day.

The Seven Bridges of Königsberg: Birth of Graph Theory

The Problem, 1736

Königsberg, Prussia (now Kaliningrad, Russia) was built across two islands in the Pregel River, connected to the mainland by seven bridges. The citizens had a puzzle: was it possible to walk through the city crossing each bridge exactly once?

Euler did not try to solve the puzzle by trial and error. He recognized that the specific geography was irrelevant — what mattered was the pattern of connections. He abstracted the landmasses to nodes and the bridges to edges, and asked: for a path traversing every edge exactly once to exist, how many edges must connect to each node?

His answer: at most two nodes can have an odd number of connections (the start and end points). In Königsberg, all four landmasses have an odd number of bridges. Therefore, no Eulerian path exists. The proof is exactly one page. It founded the entire discipline of graph theory.

Euler's Number: e = 2.71828…

Analysis · Introduced 1736, formalized thereafter Euler didn't discover the number e — Jacob Bernoulli had encountered it studying compound interest, and Leibniz had mentioned it. But Euler named it, systematized it, connected it to logarithms, and showed it was the natural base for all exponential functions.

e = limn→∞ (1 + 1⁄n)n = 2.71828182845… The number of pure, continuous growth · Irrational and transcendental

The number e is what you get if you compound interest continuously. It is the rate of natural decay and natural growth. It appears in probability (the normal distribution), in calculus (the only function that is its own derivative), in complex analysis, in quantum mechanics, in information theory. Its ubiquity rivals π's.

Euler's Polyhedron Formula

Topology · 1750 – 1752 Consider any convex polyhedron — a tetrahedron, a cube, a dodecahedron. Count its vertices (V), edges (E), and faces (F). Euler discovered:

V − E + F = 2 Euler's Characteristic · True for any convex polyhedron · Foundation of topology

A cube: 8 vertices − 12 edges + 6 faces = 2. A tetrahedron: 4 − 6 + 4 = 2. Always 2. This is not a coincidence — it is a topological invariant, a property that remains constant under continuous deformation. The quantity V − E + F is now called the Euler characteristic, and it is one of the central concepts in algebraic topology.

The f(x) Notation: Mathematics Gets a Language

In 1734, Euler introduced the notation f(x) for a function of x. Before Euler, different mathematicians wrote functions in completely different ways. The modern concept of a function as a rule mapping inputs to outputs — and the notation f(x), g(x), h(x) — is essentially Euler's invention. Every calculus student in the world inherits his notation.

Ten Major Contributions: A Card Survey

01 · Identity

Euler's Identity

eiπ + 1 = 0. Five fundamental constants in one equation. Voted the most beautiful equation in mathematics.

02 · Analysis

Euler's Formula

eix = cos(x) + i·sin(x). The parent equation. Used by every physicist, engineer, and signal processor.

03 · Graph Theory

Königsberg Bridges

Proved the walk impossible. Invented graph theory to do it. Now underlies the internet, circuit design, and logistics.

04 · Number Theory

Basel Problem

Σ1/n² = π²/6. Solved the 85-year-old problem. Opened the path to the Riemann zeta function.

05 · Topology

Polyhedron Formula

V − E + F = 2. The Euler characteristic. Foundation of modern topology and algebraic geometry.

06 · Notation

e, i, π, f(x), Σ

He named or popularized every symbol mathematicians use. The language of mathematics is Euler's vocabulary.

07 · Mechanics

Rigid Body Dynamics

Euler's equations of motion for rotating bodies. Every aerospace engineer computes satellite rotation with his formulas.

08 · Number Theory

Euler's Totient Function

φ(n): counts integers coprime to n. Essential in modern RSA encryption — every secure web transaction uses it.

09 · Analysis

Calculus of Variations

Euler-Lagrange equations. The mathematical framework for all of classical mechanics and much of quantum field theory.

10 · Hydrodynamics

Euler's Fluid Equations

First mathematical description of fluid dynamics. The ancestor of the Navier-Stokes equations, one of the Millennium Problems.
