His Legacy What Euler Left Behind

His notation is the language of mathematics. His graph theory underlies the internet. His zeta function work is connected to the greatest unsolved problem in mathematics. His topology defines the field. He died in 1783. He has not stopped being useful.

The Language of Mathematics

In every branch of mathematics, every day, billions of people use notation that Leonhard Euler introduced or standardized. This is not metaphor. The symbols below — which appear in every textbook, every paper, every calculation — were either invented or made universal by one man from Basel, working in the eighteenth century.

Before Euler standardized these symbols, mathematics was a Babel of competing notations. Different countries, different schools, different mathematicians wrote the same concepts in incompatible ways. Euler's textbooks — the Introductio of 1748, the calculus texts of the 1750s and 1760s — became the international standard because they were clear, comprehensive, and used notation that actually worked. The notation was adopted because it was good.

"Read Euler, read Euler, he is the master of us all." — Pierre-Simon Laplace, mathematician and astronomer, c. 1790

Graph Theory: From Königsberg to the Internet

1736 → Now When Euler proved in 1736 that the Seven Bridges of Königsberg could not be traversed in a single walk, he invented a new kind of mathematics. He had abstracted the physical geography — islands, rivers, bridges — into nodes and edges, and asked a structural question about connectivity. The specific details of Königsberg didn't matter; what mattered was the pattern.

Internet Infrastructure

Routing Algorithms

Every packet of data traveling across the internet is routed through a network graph. The algorithms that find shortest paths — Dijkstra's, A* — are direct descendants of Euler's work on graph traversal.

Social Networks

Network Analysis

Facebook, LinkedIn, Twitter — every social network is a mathematical graph. The mathematics of viral spread, degrees of separation, and influence maximization comes directly from Eulerian graph theory.

Hardware Design

Circuit Board Layout

Designing a circuit board without crossing wires is literally a planarity problem in graph theory. Every CPU, every integrated circuit, owes its layout to Euler's 1736 paper.

Logistics

Traveling Salesman & Routing

The Chinese Postman Problem — finding the shortest route that traverses every edge of a graph — is a direct generalization of Euler's bridge problem. UPS and FedEx use it daily.

Biology

Protein Interaction Networks

Mapping how proteins interact inside cells — one of the frontiers of molecular biology — uses graph theory to model and analyze the network structure.

Epidemiology

Disease Spread Modeling

How a pathogen spreads through a population is modeled as propagation on a contact network graph. Graph theory is essential to pandemic response.

The Riemann Hypothesis: Euler's Unfinished Business

What Euler Started — Riemann Continued — Nobody Has Finished

In solving the Basel Problem, Euler introduced what is now called the Riemann zeta function: the sum of 1/ns for all positive integers n. For s = 2, the answer is π²/6. Euler computed it for all even positive integers. He left the odd integers and complex values of s as open territory.

In 1859, Bernhard Riemann extended Euler's function to complex numbers and made a conjecture about where its zeros lie — the Riemann Hypothesis. The Riemann Hypothesis states that all non-trivial zeros of the zeta function have real part exactly 1/2. It remains unproven.

The Riemann Hypothesis is one of the Clay Mathematics Institute's Millennium Prize Problems — a $1,000,000 prize awaits its proof. It has implications for the distribution of prime numbers, cryptography, and the foundations of number theory. Every attempt to prove it begins with Euler's 1735 work on the Basel Problem.

Euler opened a door that the best mathematical minds of the past 165 years have not been able to close.

Topology: From V − E + F = 2

1750 → Modern geometry Euler's polyhedron formula — V − E + F = 2 — seems at first like a curiosity about counting corners and sides of solid shapes. It is, in fact, one of the deepest insights in mathematics. The quantity V − E + F is an invariant: it doesn't change when you continuously deform the shape. Stretch a cube into a sphere, and V − E + F remains 2.

This invariant is now called the Euler characteristic, and it is fundamental to topology — the mathematics of shapes considered up to continuous deformation. The Euler characteristic distinguishes a sphere (characteristic 2) from a torus — a donut shape (characteristic 0) from a shape with two holes (characteristic −2). Modern topologists classify all surfaces by their Euler characteristics.

Algebraic Topology: Euler's Child

The entire field of algebraic topology — which uses algebraic structures to study topological spaces — can be traced to Euler's 1750 letter to Goldbach announcing his polyhedron formula. The field now includes homology theory, cohomology, homotopy theory, and K-theory. It is one of the most active areas of pure mathematics research.

Euler did not see this coming. He proved a fact about polyhedra. Mathematics decided it was the foundation of a field.

Cryptography: The Totient Function

1763 → Every secure website Euler's totient function φ(n) counts how many integers from 1 to n share no common factor with n. Euler proved a fundamental theorem: aφ(n) ≡ 1 (mod n) whenever a and n are coprime. This is Euler's theorem.

In 1977, Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman used Euler's theorem as the mathematical core of RSA encryption — the first practical public-key cryptosystem. Every HTTPS connection, every encrypted email, every secure online transaction uses RSA or a system derived from it. The mathematical heart of internet security is Euler's 1763 paper.

Mechanics and Engineering

Aerospace

Euler's Rotation Equations

The equations of motion for a rigid rotating body — Euler's equations — are used to compute satellite attitude control, aircraft maneuvers, and gyroscope dynamics. Every spacecraft orientation calculation is Euler's.

Civil Engineering

Euler's Column Formula

The critical load at which a column buckles under compression is given by Euler's formula from 1744. Every structural engineer designing buildings, bridges, or struts uses it.

Fluid Dynamics

Euler's Fluid Equations

Euler's equations of fluid motion (1757) are the foundation of aerodynamics and hydrodynamics. The Navier-Stokes equations — a Millennium Prize Problem — generalize Euler's equations.

Physics

Euler-Lagrange Equations

The variational principle of classical mechanics — the principle of least action — is formalized through the Euler-Lagrange equations. All of theoretical physics uses them.

The Opera Omnia: Still Publishing

1911 → Present The Opera Omnia Euleri — the complete collected works — began publication in 1911. It was expected to take a few decades. As of the 2020s, it has reached 92 volumes and is not yet complete. Euler published so much, in so many areas, with such depth, that a multi-generational international editorial project has spent over a century cataloguing it.

The Euler Archive at the University of the Pacific digitizes and translates his work. The Euler Society holds annual conferences. Papers are still being translated into English for the first time. His work on infinite series, number theory, and combinatorics continues to be studied, cited, and built upon by working mathematicians.

The Verdict of History

In mathematics, there is a standard by which greatness is measured: how much of what followed would have been different if you had not existed? By this measure, Euler stands beside Newton and Archimedes at the very peak.

Remove Euler from history and you lose: the notation system of all mathematics, the foundations of graph theory, the foundations of topology, the path to the Riemann Hypothesis, the mathematical core of internet security, the equations of classical and fluid mechanics, the treatment of complex numbers that makes quantum mechanics possible, and several hundred other results scattered across every field of mathematics.

None of these would be forever lost — other mathematicians would have found their way, eventually, to many of the same places. But the landscape of mathematics would be decades or centuries behind where it is now. And it would speak a different language.

"Euler is the Shakespeare of mathematics — omnipresent, and just as unreachable. We live inside the world he made." — William Dunham, Euler: The Master of Us All, 1999
"He ceased to calculate, and to live." — Marquis de Condorcet, Eloge de M. Euler, 1783
