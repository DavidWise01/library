Leonhard Euler Who He Was · 1707 – 1783

The most prolific mathematician in history: born in Basel, educated by the Bernoullis, blinded in both eyes, and still producing mathematics by dictation until the last hours of his life. He named the symbols the world thinks in.

A Pastor's Son from Basel

Origin · 15 April 1707 Leonhard Euler was born in Basel, Switzerland, the first child of Paul Euler, a Calvinist pastor, and Marguerite Brucker. His father was an amateur mathematician who had studied under Jacob Bernoulli — one of the most distinguished mathematical families in Europe. From the beginning, Euler inhabited a world where mathematics was spoken at the dinner table.

The family moved to Riehen, a village outside Basel, when Leonhard was young. He was sent back to Basel for schooling, boarding with his maternal grandmother, and at thirteen he enrolled at the University of Basel — a prodigy in a city full of them.

"Euler calculated without any apparent effort, just as men breathe, or as eagles sustain themselves in the air." — Francôis Arago, French astronomer

The Bernoulli Connection

Mentor · Johann Bernoulli At the University of Basel, the young Euler came to the attention of Johann Bernoulli — the greatest living mathematician of the age and a man of formidable ego. Johann recognized something rare in the teenager and arranged private Saturday afternoon tutorials. He did not spoon-feed: he gave Euler books and told him to work through difficulties alone, coming to him only with questions he truly could not resolve.

This method forged the mathematician. Euler graduated with a master's degree in philosophy at sixteen, comparing the philosophies of Descartes and Newton. Johann's sons — Daniel and Nicolaus — were Euler's close friends. It was they who would open the door to St. Petersburg and change the course of his life.

Paul Euler

His father. A Calvinist pastor who had studied mathematics under Jacob Bernoulli — planted the seed that became the greatest flowering in the history of mathematics.

Johann Bernoulli

His tutor. The elder Bernoulli recognized Euler's gifts early and gave him rigorous private instruction. Called Euler "the greatest mathematician in the world" late in life.

Daniel Bernoulli

His friend and peer. Daniel invited Euler to join him at the St. Petersburg Academy. Their correspondence continued for decades across the great European courts.

St. Petersburg: The First Act

Russia · 1727 – 1741 Peter the Great had founded the Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg in 1724, determined to import European intellectual capital. When Daniel Bernoulli went in 1725, he wrote to Euler urging him to follow. Euler arrived in 1727, the same week Peter the Great's wife Catherine I died — the Academy teetered on dissolution, and Euler was briefly assigned to the Navy medical service before a mathematical position opened.

But the Academy stabilized, and Euler found his footing with remarkable speed. Within a decade he had solved the Basel Problem (the sum of 1/n², which had defeated all of Europe for nearly a century), published thousands of pages on mechanics, number theory, and music theory, and become the most productive mathematician in the world — at an age when most mathematicians are still finding their legs.

The Loss of the Right Eye — 1738

In 1738, Euler lost the sight in his right eye. The cause is uncertain — fever, overwork, a botched medical procedure. He accepted the loss with characteristic equanimity, remarking that it would reduce distractions. A portrait from this period shows him half-turned, the damaged eye away from the viewer. He kept working.

The Tsar's Mathematician

During his first St. Petersburg period (1727–1741), Euler produced work in mechanics, number theory, infinite series, music theory, cartography, and naval architecture. He was made a full Professor of Mathematics in 1733 at age 26, the same year he married Katharina Gsell, daughter of a Swiss painter at the Russian court. They would have thirteen children, of whom three sons and two daughters survived to adulthood.

Berlin: Twenty-Five Years at Frederick's Court

Prussia · 1741 – 1766 Frederick the Great — "Frederick the Great" — prided himself on being a philosopher-king in the French Enlightenment manner. He preferred Voltaire and the brilliant conversationalists of Paris to a Swiss mathematician who spoke rarely, worked constantly, and held sincere religious beliefs. Frederick called Euler "my great Cyclops" — the insult arrived after the loss of the right eye — and complained that he could not get a word out of him at dinner.

Euler didn't care. He ran the mathematics and physics sections of the Berlin Academy for twenty-five years, supervised its observatory and botanical gardens, advised the government on pensions and lotteries, oversaw the construction of a canal, and — while doing all of this administrative work — produced approximately half of all the mathematics he would ever write.

"He was a one-eyed man in the kingdom of the blind, and he was the king." — Contemporary assessment of Euler at Berlin

Complete Blindness — and Increased Output

St. Petersburg (second period) · 1766 – 1783 Catherine the Great, newly on the Russian throne, wanted Euler back. She offered a salary, a house, money for his family, and a cook. Euler accepted and returned to St. Petersburg in 1766. The following year, a cataract worsened. He had a brief moment of restored sight after surgery, then inflammation closed his remaining eye completely. In 1771, Leonhard Euler went totally blind at sixty-three years old.

What happened next confounded expectations. Euler's output increased. He had always done much of his work in his head; losing his eyes removed an input channel, not the engine. He dictated to his sons and to a young Swiss student, Nicolas Fuss, who became his amanuensis. His memory — already legendary — sharpened under necessity. He could recite the Aeneid from memory and state the first six powers of any number under 100 without hesitation. He composed mathematics at a rate that embarrassed sighted men a third his age.

The Life in Full: A Timeline

1707
Born in Basel, Switzerland, 15 April. Father is a Calvinist pastor with mathematical training under Jacob Bernoulli.
1720
Enrolled at the University of Basel at age thirteen. Studies Saturday afternoons with Johann Bernoulli.
1723
Master's degree at sixteen, comparing Descartes and Newton. Father hopes he will enter the ministry; Bernoulli intervenes.
1727
Arrives St. Petersburg. Catherine I dies that week; Academy nearly dissolved. Euler is briefly assigned to the Russian Navy before a mathematical post opens.
1733
Full Professor of Mathematics at 26. Marries Katharina Gsell. The Swiss mathematician is now the most prolific mathematical mind in Russia.
1735
Solves the Basel Problem in a single year: the sum 1 + 1/4 + 1/9 + 1/16 + ... = π²/6. European mathematicians are astonished.
1736
Publishes the Seven Bridges of Königsberg solution. Invents graph theory to prove it impossible to cross all bridges exactly once.
1738
Loses sight in right eye. Reportedly remarks it will reduce distractions. Continues at full speed.
1741
Invited to the Berlin Academy by Frederick the Great of Prussia. Accepts, partly due to political instability in Russia.
1748
Publishes Introductio in analysin infinitorum — establishing e, i, π, and the notation of modern mathematics. Euler's identity appears here.
1755
Publishes Institutiones calculi differentialis — introduces f(x) notation for functions. Modern calculus textbooks descend directly from this book.
1766
Catherine the Great recalls him to St. Petersburg with a generous offer. Euler accepts and returns to Russia for the last time.
1771
Goes completely blind. His house burns in a fire; he is rescued by a man who carries him out. He loses manuscripts in the blaze but keeps calculating.
1771–1783
The blind years. Dictates to sons and to Nicolas Fuss. Produces hundreds of papers — more per year than most mathematicians produce in a lifetime.
18 September 1783
"He ceased to calculate, and to live." — Condorcet's eulogy. Euler died of a brain hemorrhage while doing mental calculations on Uranus and drinking tea.

The Man Himself

Euler was, by all accounts, genuinely pleasant. He was warm with students, patient as a teacher, and — unlike many great men of his era — modest about his gifts. He held sincere Protestant faith throughout his life and was unmoved by the fashionable deism of the French philosophes. When Diderot reportedly visited the Russian court and tried to undermine religious belief with casual materialism, Euler (the story, perhaps apocryphal, claims) stood up and declared: "Sir, (a + b²)/n = x, therefore God exists. Reply!" The court burst into laughter. Diderot, ignorant of mathematics, was silenced.

He loved his enormous family. He worked with his grandchildren climbing on him. He had, according to every witness, a quality rare in genius: he was happy.

"He was a man who was as simple as a child, tranquil and cheerful, who ate and drank without excess, worked without ceasing, and loved his family tenderly." — Nicolas Fuss, his amanuensis and grandson-in-law

The Notation He Left Behind

Before Euler, mathematical writing was a chaos of competing notations. After Euler, it converged on a single language — his. The symbols below are all Euler introductions or popularizations. Every working scientist and mathematician on Earth uses them daily.

e = 2.71828…

Euler's number. The base of natural logarithms, the rate of pure growth. He named it e in a 1736 manuscript — possibly for "exponential," possibly his own initial.

i = √−1

The imaginary unit. Euler systematized its use and made complex numbers a working tool of mathematics rather than a philosophical scandal.

π (pi)

He didn't invent π — but Euler's use of the symbol in 1736 made it universal. Before Euler, π was written variously as p, c, or nothing at all.

Σ (sigma)

Euler introduced the capital sigma for summation in 1755. Every statistics textbook in the world carries his mark.

f(x)

Function notation. Euler introduced the idea of writing a function as f(x) in 1734 — the modern concept of "function" is essentially his creation.

Δ, ∇, sin, cos

Euler standardized the abbreviations for sine and cosine, introduced Δ for differences, and shaped the notational landscape we inhabit.
