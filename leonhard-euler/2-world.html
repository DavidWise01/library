His World Basel, St. Petersburg, Berlin

Three cities, two empires, one family of mathematicians, and a man who could not stop working even after the world went dark. The circumstances that shaped the most prolific mind in the history of mathematics.

Three Cities, One Mathematician

Leonhard Euler's life was defined by three cities: Basel, where he was born and educated; St. Petersburg, where he did his greatest early work and returned for the final seventeen years of his life; and Berlin, where Frederick the Great tolerated him for twenty-five productive years without quite understanding what he had. Each city represents not just a geography but a political and intellectual climate that shaped what mathematics got written.

1707 – 1727 · Origin

Basel, Switzerland

Reformed Protestant city on the Rhine. Home of the Bernoulli family. The intellectual soil in which Euler germinated. He left at twenty and never returned, but Basel formed his character: industrious, devout, methodical.

1727 – 1741 · First Period

St. Petersburg, Russia

Peter the Great's modernization project. The Academy of Sciences needed mathematicians. Euler arrived young, worked ferociously, lost his right eye, solved the Basel Problem, and invented graph theory — all before forty.

1741 – 1766 · Middle Years

Berlin, Prussia

Frederick the Great's court. Frederick preferred French philosophers and found Euler dull. Euler ran the Academy for twenty-five years and produced approximately half his life's work. He was indifferent to Frederick's opinion.

1766 – 1783 · Final Chapter

St. Petersburg, Russia

Catherine the Great recalled him with a generous offer. He returned, went completely blind, and produced more mathematics per year than ever before. He died here in 1783, mid-calculation.

Peter the Great's Grand Project

Political context · The St. Petersburg Academy The Imperial Academy of Sciences was Peter the Great's instrument for dragging Russia into the European intellectual mainstream. He designed it before he died in 1725; it was formally inaugurated under his widow Catherine I. The early Academy was almost entirely staffed by imported Europeans — mainly Germans and Swiss — paid handsomely to think.

Euler arrived in 1727, invited by Daniel Bernoulli. The political situation was unstable — Catherine I died the same week Euler arrived, and the Academy nearly collapsed in the power struggle that followed. Euler briefly found himself assigned to the Navy medical corps, waiting. Then Anna of Russia stabilized the throne, the Academy reopened, and one of history's great pairings — brilliant young mathematician, well-funded academy — began.

"In Berlin I shall have a king who will be master, while in Russia he will be a servant. I shall therefore remain in Russia." — Attributed remark explaining why Euler preferred St. Petersburg to Berlin

Frederick the Great: The Patron Who Didn't Appreciate His Mathematician

Berlin · 1741 – 1766 Frederick II of Prussia — "Frederick the Great" — was one of the most cultivated monarchs of the Enlightenment. He was a gifted flautist, wrote poetry in French, corresponded with Voltaire, and modeled himself on the philosopher-king ideal of classical antiquity. He was also profoundly dismissive of German culture and of mathematics that he could not follow.

Frederick called Euler "my one-eyed mathematician" and complained privately that Euler never said anything interesting at dinner. He wanted Voltaire at his table, not a Swiss calculator who talked about infinite series. Euler, for his part, was happy. He had resources, a position, and peace to work. He ran the Berlin Academy's mathematical and physical sciences divisions, supervised its botanical gardens and observatory, advised the state on technical matters ranging from the Russian canal system to pension calculations, and published hundreds of papers.

The relationship ended in 1766 when Frederick brought d'Alembert to Berlin as his preferred mathematician and made clear that Euler was an also-ran. Catherine the Great's offer arrived at the right moment. Euler packed up his household — large enough to require a convoy — and returned to Russia.

Catherine the Great: The Patron Who Got It Right

Russia · 1766 Catherine II, Empress of All the Russias, was a genuine Enlightenment figure. She corresponded with Voltaire and Diderot, liberalized parts of Russian law, and had a strong intuition for talent. She wanted Euler back and made an offer he could not refuse: a substantial salary, a house in St. Petersburg, a pension for his wife, and court positions for his sons.

Euler accepted. Catherine honored every commitment and added more — when his house burned in the great fire of 1771, she replaced it personally. She understood, even if she could not follow the mathematics, that she was housing one of the wonders of the age.

The 1771 Fire

Euler's St. Petersburg house caught fire. He was completely blind by this point and had to be carried out by a Swiss craftsman named Peter Grimm who risked his life to save him. Many manuscripts were lost. Euler was uninjured. He resumed work the same week. Catherine the Great funded a complete replacement of the house and its contents.

The Bernoulli Family: His Mathematical Roots

Great-uncle of discipline

Jacob Bernoulli (1655–1705)

Euler's father studied under him. Jacob solved the Basel Problem's predecessor questions. He died two years before Euler was born but shaped the mathematical culture Euler entered.

Teacher & Patron

Johann Bernoulli (1667–1748)

Euler's greatest teacher. Gave him Saturday tutorials. Pushed him hard. Recognized his genius early. Later called him "the greatest mathematician alive" — the student had surpassed the master.

Friend & Peer

Daniel Bernoulli (1700–1782)

Johann's son, Euler's closest friend and contemporary. Invited Euler to St. Petersburg. They corresponded for decades. Their rivalry — affectionate but real — drove both to greater heights.

Peer & Rival

Nicolaus II Bernoulli (1695–1726)

Daniel's brother, also at St. Petersburg. Died tragically young in 1726, one year before Euler arrived. His position opened the door for Euler.

Blindness: The Adaptation

1771 – 1783 · The blind years In 1771, Euler underwent surgery for a cataract in his remaining good eye. The surgery briefly restored some sight, then inflammation destroyed it permanently. At sixty-four, he was completely blind. He had been effectively one-eyed since 1738. Total blindness might have ended any other career.

It did not end Euler's. He dictated his mathematics first to his sons Johann Albrecht and Christoph, then increasingly to Nicolas Fuss, a talented young Swiss mathematician who became his primary amanuensis and later his grandson-in-law. Euler composed entire papers in his head before dictating. His mental arithmetic was already legendary — he was said to be able to perform in his head calculations that required hours with pencil and paper.

In the twelve years of total blindness, Euler produced over 400 papers. The year he went blind, he wrote the complete text of the second edition of his Algebra by dictating it to a servant who had no mathematical training. The servant learned the subject as he transcribed.

The Family Man

Personal life · Katharina Gsell, 1733 Euler married Katharina Gsell in 1733, when he was twenty-six. She was the daughter of Georg Gsell, a Swiss painter employed at the Russian court. Euler was notably domestic for a man of his output. He loved his children and worked with grandchildren playing on his lap — this is not legend but recorded testimony from multiple witnesses. The household was enormous, cheerful, and mathematically saturated.

Katharina died in 1773. After three years of widowhood, Euler married her half-sister Salome Abigail Gsell, largely to ensure the household was managed — he was blind and needed someone who knew how things worked. His family's loyalty and care sustained the final decades of production.

Of his thirteen children, three sons — Johann Albrecht, Karl, and Christoph — survived to adulthood and contributed to his final period of production as secretaries, calculators, and copyists. The mathematical workshop of the blind Euler was a family enterprise.

"Euler retained his mental powers entirely to the end. On the 7th of September 1783 he gave a lesson to one of his grandchildren, then discussed with Lexell his newly computed orbit for Uranus, drank tea, and died." — Nicolas Fuss, eyewitness account of Euler's last day

The St. Petersburg Academy: Home Base

The Imperial Academy of Sciences was, for its era, an extraordinary institution. It paid well, expected results, and left its members largely free to choose their problems. Euler thrived in such conditions. He was not merely a resident genius but an engaged administrator — editing the Academy's journal, supervising map production for the Russian Empire, advising the government on hydraulic systems, and solving practical navigation problems for the Navy.

The breadth was deliberate. Euler did not distinguish between pure and applied mathematics. The same mind that proved the Basel Problem also designed better lenses for telescopes, worked out the mathematics of ship design, and computed the motions of the moon for navigational tables. He saw no border between the two modes of mathematical work.
