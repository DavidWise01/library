George Boole

A cobbler's son from Lincoln who taught himself mathematics, invented a new kind of algebra where the only answers are TRUE and FALSE, and wrote the theoretical foundation for every computer ever built — ninety years before anyone knew that's what he'd done.

The shape of his life

The origin

Born in Lincoln, England, 1815. Son of a cobbler. No money for university. Taught himself Greek, Latin, French, and higher mathematics from books.

The teacher

Opened his own school at 16 to support his family. Taught mathematics while discovering it, reading the masters in their original languages.

The professor

Despite having no degree, appointed first professor of mathematics at Queen's College Cork, Ireland, in 1849. The work was already too good to ignore.

The theorist

Early life — Lincoln
01

Born into the working poor

George Boole was born November 2, 1815, in Lincoln, a cathedral city in the English Midlands, to John Boole, a cobbler, and Mary Ann Joyce.

status working-class, no inherited wealth or connections

so university was financially impossible — everything he learned, he found himself.

+ his father was himself a self-improver, deeply interested in optics and the construction of telescopes — the love of learning was already in the house.

02

A language prodigy first

By his teens Boole had taught himself Latin, Greek, French, and German — partly to read mathematical texts that hadn't been translated into English.

method self-study, local mechanics' institute library, borrowed books

so he could read Lacroix's Differential and Integral Calculus in French — at 14.

+ a local school rector helped him with Latin, but the rest was entirely self-directed. Lincoln's Mechanics' Institute gave him access to scientific journals.

03

School teacher at 16

When his father's business collapsed, George became an assistant teacher to support the family. He opened his own school in Lincoln by 1835.

role sole financial support of his parents and siblings

so he taught all day and did research at night — for more than a decade.

+ he described this period as one of isolation: he had no mathematical community, no colleagues, no feedback. He worked entirely alone.

04

First publications — the Royal Society notices

Despite his isolation, Boole began publishing research papers in the Cambridge Mathematical Journal from 1840, gaining attention from the leading mathematicians of the day.

year first major paper on invariants, 1841

so Arthur Cayley and Augustus De Morgan — giants of the field — began corresponding with him.

+ De Morgan became a close friend and intellectual collaborator. Their correspondence spans decades and touches the deepest questions of logic.

The professor — Cork
05

Queen's College Cork

In 1849, the newly founded Queen's College Cork (now University College Cork) appointed Boole as its first Professor of Mathematics — without a degree.

basis his published research alone, plus recommendations from De Morgan and others

so the work spoke for itself — one of the first examples of pure research merit overriding credentialist gatekeeping.

+ Cork was part of Ireland under British rule — a politically charged institution. Boole navigated this carefully, committed to the education of Irish students regardless of religion.

06

Mary Everest Boole

In 1855, Boole married Mary Everest, then 23 (he was 40). She was the niece of Colonel Sir George Everest, after whom the mountain was named.

she an accomplished mathematician and educational reformer in her own right

so she became a major advocate of his ideas after his death, writing extensively on logic, mathematics education, and pedagogy.

+ They had five daughters in nine years. After Boole's death, Mary raised them alone while writing books on mathematical psychology and teaching method.

07

The Laws of Thought — 1854

Five years into his Cork professorship, Boole published An Investigation of the Laws of Thought — the book that contains everything.

full title An Investigation of the Laws of Thought on Which Are Founded the Mathematical Theories of Logic and Probabilities

so logic became a branch of mathematics. Variables could only be 0 or 1. AND, OR, NOT were algebraic operations.

+ Boole believed this was a mathematical model of how the mind actually worked — a theory of thought, not just notation. He was wrong about that, but magnificently right about everything else.

08

Death at 49

On a wet November day in 1864, Boole walked two miles in heavy rain to deliver a lecture on time, arrived soaking, and taught anyway. He developed pneumonia and died within weeks.

date November 8, 1864 — aged 49

so he left behind a wife, five young daughters, and a body of work that would not be understood for 73 years.

+ One of his daughters, Alicia Boole Stott, became a pioneering geometer who discovered properties of four-dimensional polytopes — genius ran in the family.

"It is not of the essence of mathematics to be conversant with the ideas of number and quantity." — George Boole, An Investigation of the Laws of Thought, 1854
Who he was — the core of it
  • George Boole (1815–1864) — English mathematician, self-educated, son of a cobbler, who became the first Professor of Mathematics at Queen's College Cork and the inventor of Boolean algebra.
  • He had no university degree. Everything he knew, he taught himself from books — in a working-class household in Lincoln, England, while supporting his family as a schoolteacher from age 16.
  • He published The Laws of Thought (1854) — turning Aristotelian logic into algebra where every variable is 0 or 1, and AND, OR, NOT are mathematical operations.
  • He married Mary Everest (1855), niece of the surveyor after whom the mountain is named. She was a mathematician who extended and popularized his ideas after his death.
  • He died in 1864, age 49, of pneumonia — 73 years before Claude Shannon proved his algebra was the language of electrical circuits, and 90+ years before every CPU in the world began running on his logic gates.
What made him different — character and method
09

A rigorous self-critic

Boole was unusually critical of his own work. He revised obsessively, delayed publication until he was satisfied, and openly acknowledged weaknesses in his system — rare virtues in any era.

example his 1847 pamphlet was explicitly superseded by the fuller 1854 book

so the Laws of Thought is a mature, finished work — not a rough draft promoted beyond its station.

+ De Morgan admired this quality especially: the willingness to say "this part isn't right yet" and keep working.

10

Mathematics as philosophy

Boole genuinely believed his algebra was a discovery about the nature of mind — that he had found the mathematical laws governing rational thought. This was his deepest motivation.

wrong cognitive science doesn't support this claim

so he was mistaken about his own work's meaning — but the mathematics he built while chasing that mistaken idea is perfectly correct and extraordinarily powerful.

+ this is one of the most beautiful ironies in the history of science: right result, wrong explanation. The algebra works; the theory of mind does not.

11

The Royal Society Gold Medal

In 1844, the Royal Society awarded Boole a Gold Medal — the highest scientific honor in Britain — for his work on differential equations. He was 29, self-educated, running a school in Lincoln.

significance the mathematical establishment completely accepted him

so by the time he got his Cork professorship in 1849, it was not an upset — it was a recognition already due.

+ the medal was for work that came before his logical algebra — which suggests his genius extended well beyond the Boolean work that made him famous.

12

Alicia Boole Stott — the daughter

Boole's third daughter, Alicia Boole Stott (1860–1940), became a pioneering geometer — she discovered methods to construct four-dimensional polytopes and was awarded an honorary degree by the University of Groningen.

remarkable she had no formal mathematical training either

so the Boole family produced, in two generations, two world-class mathematical minds from the same self-taught, working-class background.

+ Alicia was largely raised by Mary Everest Boole, who made sure all five daughters had access to ideas even in poverty. The cobbler's legacy ran deeper than one book.

Honest limits of what we know
  • Boole's life is well-documented in broad outline — Des MacHale's biography George Boole: His Life and Work (1985) is the standard reference, comprehensive and scholarly.
  • The intimate details of his inner life are harder to recover — his surviving correspondence is substantial but uneven. He was private about feelings and wrote mostly about ideas.
  • The pneumonia story (walking in rain to a lecture) appears consistently in sources and is treated as historical, though some modern biographers flag it as possibly embellished in the retelling.
  • Volume 1 covers his ideas in depth. Volume 2 covers the Victorian world he inhabited. Volume 3 covers the long shadow he cast over computing — the part he never lived to see.
