Her World

Göttingen — the world-center of mathematics. The Weimar opening. The Noether boys. And the letter that ended it all.

Erlangen · Göttingen · 1882–1933

The Exclusion Era

Erlangen and the Women Question

The University of Erlangen's ban on women was not exceptional — it was the norm. In 1900, when Emmy Noether decided to study mathematics, no German university admitted women as full students. Prussia — which contained Berlin, the country's cultural and intellectual capital — would not admit women to its universities until 1908. Bavaria, where Erlangen sat, permitted audit attendance from 1903. That was the opening Emmy Noether walked through.

The attitude was not incidental. It was deliberate policy, supported by formal arguments: that women's intellects were not suited for abstract reasoning; that the presence of women would disrupt the academic community; that a woman's proper sphere was domestic. These arguments were made in faculty meetings, in newspapers, in philosophical journals. They were made by intelligent men, in good faith, in the conviction that the natural order was being correctly described.

Emmy Noether's response to this environment was not political — she did not write polemics or join suffragist organizations. Her response was simply to do mathematics. Her answer to the claim that women were not suited for abstract reasoning was the theory of Noetherian rings. It is the most decisive refutation possible.

"She had absolutely no faculty for intrigue and was thoroughly honest and open in all her dealings with students and colleagues."

— Bartel L. van der Waerden

1915 — The Center of the Universe

Göttingen: Where Mathematics Lived

From roughly 1895 to 1933, the University of Göttingen was the most important center of mathematics and mathematical physics in the world. The list of people who worked, studied, or visited there in that period reads like the periodic table of twentieth-century science: David Hilbert, Felix Klein, Hermann Minkowski, Hermann Weyl, Richard Courant, Max Born, Werner Heisenberg, Erwin Schrödinger, John von Neumann, Paul Dirac. The mathematics of quantum mechanics was essentially invented in Göttingen's seminars and common rooms.

Hilbert's program — to formalize all of mathematics on a rigorous axiomatic foundation — set the agenda for the century. His 1900 list of twenty-three unsolved problems was the discipline's roadmap. His seminar was the place to be. Students came from Russia, America, the Netherlands, Japan, to sit in Göttingen's lecture halls and absorb the mathematics that was being made there in real time.

David Hilbert
The Dominant Force

Greatest mathematician of his generation. Invited Noether to Göttingen, fought for her habilitation, called her "the most significant creative genius." His seminar set the international standard.

Felix Klein
The Organizer

Klein had built Göttingen into a world center. He recognized Noether's talent and co-signed the failed habilitation petition. His Erlangen Program on symmetry groups prefigured her work.

Hermann Weyl
Colleague and Witness

Weyl worked alongside Noether for years. His tribute after her death remains the most eloquent testimony to her genius — and his admission that she had been a finer mathematician than he was.

Richard Courant
The Director

Ran the Göttingen Mathematical Institute from 1920. Created the institutional framework that made Göttingen's golden age possible — before the Nazis destroyed it in 1933.

The 1915 Faculty Fight

The attempt to grant Noether the right to habilitate — to teach officially and receive student fees — required faculty approval. Hilbert and Klein submitted the petition. The philosophy faculty, which then oversaw the qualification process, refused. The debate in the faculty meeting became heated. A professor invoked the returning soldiers: what would they think? Hilbert's response — the bathing establishment remark — became famous immediately and has been quoted ever since as the perfect deflation of a certain kind of institutional stupidity.

The refusal held for four years. During those four years, Emmy Noether taught Hilbert's courses when he was unavailable, listed under his name in the course catalog. The workaround was obvious and was understood to be obvious. The institution maintained its fiction; she maintained her mathematics; the students attended whoever was teaching, because the mathematics was excellent.

Hilbert's Famous Response

"I do not see why the sex of a candidate is an argument against admission to the university. After all, we are a university, not a bathing establishment."

— David Hilbert, faculty meeting, University of Göttingen, 1915

1918–1920 — The Weimar Opening

The Republic and the Habilitation

The German Empire ended on November 9, 1918, with the abdication of Kaiser Wilhelm II. The Weimar Republic that replaced it was, among other things, the first German government to formally commit to equal rights for women. The implications for academic life were significant but not immediate — university traditions moved slowly, and the opposition to women's full academic participation did not simply evaporate with the change of government.

The relevant regulation change came in 1920. Emmy Noether reapplied for habilitation. This time, it was granted. On June 4, 1919, she had already delivered her habilitation lecture — the formal qualifying lecture required by German universities — and the degree was officially conferred in 1919, with full recognition following in 1920. She was thirty-seven years old. She had been doing the work of a professor for more than a decade without the standing of one.

The title she received in 1922 — nichtbeamteter ausserordentlicher Professor, unofficial extraordinary professor — was honorary. It carried no salary. She received a small teaching stipend from student fees. For her most productive mathematical decade, she earned approximately what a junior clerk earned, for work that was quietly remaking the foundations of mathematics.

"Noether was the mathematical center of Göttingen in the years between 1920 and 1933. All roads in algebra led to her seminar."

— Nathan Jacobson, algebraist

The Seminar — 1920s

The Noether Boys

Emmy Noether's lectures were, by all accounts, extraordinary events. She did not lecture in the conventional sense — standing at the board, writing out proofs for students to copy. She thought out loud. She would pace, speak rapidly, erase what she had written, start again, arrive at a result from an unexpected angle. Students who came expecting polished exposition got something more valuable: they saw mathematics being made, the actual process of a great mathematical mind working through a problem in real time.

The students who gathered around her called themselves the "Noether boys" — a term they used proudly, and which she reportedly found amusing. They came from across Europe and beyond. They crammed into her lectures, followed her on walks through Göttingen where she would continue working, sometimes gathering for informal sessions at her apartment. The mathematics being done in her orbit was cutting-edge; the atmosphere was one of genuine intellectual excitement.

Bartel van der Waerden
Dutchman · Moderne Algebra

Attended Noether's lectures and wrote them up as Moderne Algebra (1930–31) — the textbook that spread abstract algebra worldwide. He credits her explicitly and repeatedly.

Helmut Hasse
German · Number Theory

Collaborated with Noether on the theory of algebras over number fields. One of the most significant number theorists of the century, and firmly in her mathematical school.

Pavel Alexandrov
Russian · Topology

The Russian topologist was a close friend, not merely a student. He brought her ideas to Moscow and they influenced the Russian mathematical tradition. He delivered a tribute to her in Moscow after her death.

Ernst Witt
German · Algebra

Attended her final Göttingen seminar. His Witt vectors and contributions to quadratic form theory bear the imprint of Noether's algebraic methods.

Max Deuring
German · Algebra / Number Theory

One of her most devoted students, who followed her approach to the theory of algebras and left an important body of work in algebraic number theory and elliptic curves.

Goro Shimura
Via the lineage

Through Deuring and the Noether-school tradition, the influence reached into the arithmetic of elliptic curves — the mathematics that would eventually prove Fermat's Last Theorem.

Van der Waerden's Moderne Algebra, published in two volumes in 1930 and 1931, deserves special attention. It was the textbook that made abstract algebra a unified discipline accessible to a generation of mathematicians. Van der Waerden wrote it, but he was writing down, in organized form, what he had learned in Noether's lectures. He acknowledged this explicitly. The book's influence was immense. It was translated into multiple languages, went through many editions, and shaped the mathematical education of mid-century physics and mathematics worldwide. Emmy Noether did not write it — but it would not exist without her.

April 7, 1933

The Letter and the Last Class

On April 7, 1933, the Nazi government promulgated the Law for the Restoration of the Professional Civil Service. Its operative clause removed from public employment anyone who was not of "Aryan descent." The euphemism was transparent. It meant Jews.

Within days, Emmy Noether received a letter from the Prussian Ministry of Science, Art, and Public Education. It informed her that she was dismissed from her position effective immediately. The letter was brief and bureaucratic. It referred to the relevant statute. It offered no comment on her qualifications or her work.

What She Did Next

She read the letter. Then she went to teach her class. She taught it. Afterward, she began planning her departure from Germany, with the same quiet practicality she had brought to every obstacle her career had placed in front of her. She wrote to colleagues about positions abroad. She expressed, in letters, concern for her students — particularly those who were also Jewish and would face far worse than dismissal.

The Göttingen Mathematical Institute was effectively destroyed in April 1933. Courant was dismissed. Weyl resigned in protest. Born emigrated. Within months, one of the greatest concentrations of mathematical talent in history had been dispersed — to England, America, and wherever else would take them. A German visitor to Göttingen in 1934 reportedly asked Hilbert, who had retired but remained in the city, how mathematics was going at the institute "now that it has been freed of the Jewish influence." Hilbert answered: "Mathematics at Göttingen? There is no mathematics at Göttingen anymore."

1933–1935 · Pennsylvania

Bryn Mawr and the Tributes at Her Death

Bryn Mawr College was a women's college in the Main Line area west of Philadelphia, founded in 1885. Its mathematics department was not Göttingen, but it offered what she needed: students to teach, colleagues to work with, and an institutional framework that would not fire her because of her heritage. She found American academic life congenial and its informality refreshing.

She lectured weekly at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, the new institution that had gathered some of the most eminent refugees from European fascism — Einstein among them. She and Einstein became friendly. He had known of her work for years; in Göttingen, they had discussed the energy problem in general relativity. Now they were both exiles in the same country, working at adjacent institutions.

She had not quite two years. The unexpected complication from ovarian cyst surgery on April 14, 1935 — she had shown no symptoms before the discovery — ended her life. She was fifty-three years old and had been, in the view of her peers, in the fullest creative period of her mathematical life.

Einstein's Tribute

Albert Einstein wrote to the New York Times. His letter ran on May 4, 1935, three weeks after her death. He wrote: "In the judgment of the most competent living mathematicians, Fräulein Noether was the most significant creative mathematical genius thus far produced, since the higher education of women began." The qualification "since the higher education of women began" was perhaps unavoidable given the norms of the time — but Einstein's underlying assessment was unambiguous. He placed her among the greatest mathematicians of any era.

Hermann Weyl's Tribute

Hermann Weyl delivered a memorial address at Bryn Mawr. It is one of the finest tributes ever paid by one mathematician to another. Weyl, who was himself one of the century's greatest mathematicians, did not hedge: "She was not only the greatest woman mathematician who has ever lived, but the greatest mathematician." And then he added something more personal: he reflected that during the years they worked together, he had sometimes privately rated himself above her — and that looking back, he now believed she had been the more original mathematician all along.

"She was a great mathematician, the greatest, I firmly believe, that her sex has ever produced, and a great woman, simple, warm, and genuine."

— Hermann Weyl, Memorial Address, Bryn Mawr, 1935
